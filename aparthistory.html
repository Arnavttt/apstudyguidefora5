Formal Analysis — FACA Framework
Every question requires formal analysis first

FACA — The 4 Steps of Art Analysis

F — Form

The physical, visual elements: line, shape, color, texture, space, scale, composition, medium, technique. What do you literally see? How is it made?

A — Artwork Info

Title, artist (if known), culture/civilization, date/period, medium/materials, location (original context vs. current). Always establish this first.

C — Context

Historical, religious, political, social, and cultural context. Why was it made? For whom? What was happening in this society at this time?

A — Analysis

How do the formal elements SERVE the context? Connect what you see to why it was made. This is where interpretation happens.

💡 The Key Principle

Every formal choice is a meaning-making choice. A figure shown larger than others signals power (hierarchy of scale). Frontal pose = timelessness and authority. Twisted pose = movement and humanity. Always ask: why did this artist make this formal choice in this context?

Key Formal Terms Every Student Must Know

Hierarchical Scale

Important figures shown larger than others regardless of spatial position. Common in Egyptian, Byzantine, and Mesoamerican art. Signals power, divinity, or social status.

Contrapposto

The natural weight-shift pose with one hip raised. Invented by Greeks. Shows naturalism and the human body as capable of natural movement. Revived in Renaissance.

Chiaroscuro

Dramatic use of light and dark to model form. Associated with Baroque (Caravaggio). Creates volume, drama, and psychological intensity.

Foreshortening

Objects depicted at an angle to create illusion of depth. Shows mastery of perspective. Distorts the "true" shape of a figure to simulate recession in space.

Atmospheric Perspective

Objects farther away appear lighter, bluer, and less detailed. Creates illusion of deep space without geometric perspective. Common in Chinese landscape painting and European painting.

Encaustic / Fresco / Tempera

Encaustic: pigment in wax (Fayum portraits). Fresco: pigment on wet plaster (Sistine Chapel). Tempera: pigment in egg yolk (before oil). Medium shapes appearance and permanence.

Content Area 1 · ~4% of exam
Global Prehistory (30,000–500 BCE)

Key Works

Woman of Willendorf
c. 28,000–25,000 BCE · Limestone · 4.4 inches tall
Exaggerated sexual characteristics (large breasts, belly, hips) → fertility, abundance, or female identity. Lack of face, emphasis on reproductive features. Portable = personal use, not monumental display. Purpose debated: fertility goddess, self-portrait, idealized female form.
Stonehenge
c. 2500–1600 BCE · Salisbury Plain, England · Sarsen sandstone + bluestones
Astronomical alignment with solstices → ritual calendar. Monumental effort suggests organized society, ritual importance. Post-and-lintel construction (trilithons). Transformation from natural material into sacred space through human effort.
Hall of the Bulls (Lascaux)
c. 17,000 BCE · Lascaux, France · Cave painting, mineral pigments
Deep cave location (not living areas) → ritual purpose, not decoration. Twisted perspective on animals (profile body, frontal horns) = composite view showing most recognizable features. Possible hunting magic, shamanic practice, or clan identity marking.
Content Areas 2–4 · ~15% of exam
Ancient Mediterranean

Egyptian Art — Key Conventions

Composite View

Head in profile, eye frontal, shoulders frontal, waist in profile, legs in profile. Shows most recognizable view of each body part. Not an attempt at naturalism — this is about completeness, not perception.

Ka & Permanence

Art made for the Ka (soul) of the deceased. Must last forever → hard stone, standardized poses, damage = danger to the ka. Formula repeated for centuries because deviation was spiritually dangerous.

Hierarchy of Scale

Pharaoh always largest. Enemies shown tiny underfoot. Gods shown huge. Social rank = visual scale. Not about spatial relationships.

Key Works — Ancient Med

Palette of King Narmer
c. 3000–2920 BCE · Egypt · Greywacke · Hierakonpolis
Commemorates unification of Upper and Lower Egypt. Narmer shown in hierarchical scale dominating enemies. Registers (horizontal bands) organize narrative. First example of many Egyptian conventions (composite view, hierarchy, registers). Palette = cosmetic grinding palette used for ritual eye makeup of gods.
Great Pyramids of Giza
c. 2551–2472 BCE · Giza, Egypt · Limestone, granite
Royal tombs for Khufu, Khafre, Menkaure. Perfect geometric form = cosmic order (Ma'at). Aligned to cardinal directions. Massive labor force = royal power. Still the largest stone structures on Earth. Mortuary complex includes sphinx, temples, causeways — entire landscape transformed for the afterlife.
Kritios Boy
c. 480 BCE · Greece · Marble
Transitional work between Archaic and Classical periods. First use of contrapposto in Greek sculpture — natural weight shift, slightly bent knee. Moves away from rigid "Kouros" pose. Marks beginning of naturalistic Greek sculpture. Eyes once had inlaid irises — originally more lifelike than current appearance.
Content Area 5 · ~16% of exam
Early Europe & Colonial Americas (200–1750 CE)

Medieval — Byzantine to Gothic

Byzantine Art

Hierarchical scale, gold backgrounds (heaven, not earthly space), frontal poses (timeless, not momentary), elongated figures (spiritual over physical). Iconoclasm controversy (726–843 CE) — debate over images led to destruction and then revival of icon painting.

Romanesque

Heavy walls, rounded arches, thick piers, small windows. Sculpture returns to church portals. Tympanum over doorways = visual Bible for illiterate. Pilgrimage roads shape art distribution.

Gothic

Pointed arches, flying buttresses (push weight outward), ribbed vaults → walls can be thin → stained glass windows → light = divine presence (theology of light). Chartres Cathedral exemplary. Increasing naturalism in sculpture.

Renaissance — Key Works

Arnolfini Portrait — Jan van Eyck
1434 · Oil on panel · National Gallery, London
Early Netherlandish mastery of oil paint — unprecedented detail in textures (fur, wood, fabric, chandelier). Convex mirror in background shows two witnesses → legal document of marriage? Or posthumous memorial? Hidden symbols debated: dog (fidelity), candle (divine witness), shoes removed (holy ground). Van Eyck's signature on wall = first artist "selfie" in Western art.
School of Athens — Raphael
1509–1511 · Fresco · Vatican, Stanza della Segnatura
Plato (points up = ideal forms) and Aristotle (points down = earthly reality) at center. All major ancient philosophers portrayed with faces of Renaissance contemporaries — Plato = Leonardo, Heraclitus = Michelangelo. One-point perspective directs eye to Plato and Aristotle. Represents synthesis of pagan learning and Christian humanism. Architecture inspired by Roman basilicas (Bramante designed it).

Content Area 7 · ~6% of exam
Indigenous Americas

Key Works

Great Serpent Mound
c. 1000 CE · Adams County, Ohio · Earthwork
Effigy mound: uncoiling serpent (1,348 feet long) with oval (egg or sun?) in mouth. Aligned to solstices and equinoxes. No burials found → ceremonial, not funerary. Transforms landscape itself into monument — art and earth become one. Mississippian or Adena culture, debated.
Sun Stone (Aztec Calendar Stone)
1427–1479 CE · Mexico · Basalt · 11.75 feet diameter
Not a calendar but a cosmological map. Central face = Tonatiuh (sun god). Inner ring = four previous suns (world ages, each destroyed). Outer rings = 20-day signs, year signs, serpents. Demonstrates Aztec belief in cyclical time, cosmic sacrifice (sun requires blood to continue moving), and their role as "People of the Sun."
Content Area 8 · ~6% of exam
Africa

Key Works & Concepts

Seated Man and Woman (Dogon)
c. 18th–20th century · Mali · Wood
Elongated forms emphasize spiritual over physical (opposite of Greek naturalism). Man and woman shown in parallel poses = equal partnership, complementarity. Figures likely used in ancestral shrines. Dogon cosmology: world created through paired, complementary forces.
Benin Plaques (Kingdom of Benin)
16th–17th century CE · Nigeria · Brass (lost-wax casting)
Commemorated military victories and court ceremonies. Hierarchical scale: Oba (king) shown largest. Lost-wax (cire-perdue) casting technique = sophisticated metallurgy. Portuguese soldiers sometimes depicted = evidence of early contact with Europeans. Looted by British in 1897 Punitive Expedition — ongoing repatriation debate.
Context Alert — African Art

Many African works were made for specific ritual functions and should not be evaluated purely by Western aesthetic standards. A "mask" worn in ceremony is not meant to be displayed on a wall. Context (performance, ritual, social function) is inseparable from meaning. The word "primitive" is never appropriate — these works reflect sophisticated cosmological, social, and technical traditions.

Content Areas 9–10 · ~8% of exam
South, East & Southeast Asia · The Pacific

Key Works

Borobudur Temple
c. 750–850 CE · Java, Indonesia · Volcanic stone
Buddhist mandala in architectural form. Nine stacked platforms (6 square, 3 circular, 1 stupa). Pilgrims walk clockwise upward through 2,672 relief panels depicting Buddhist teachings → physical journey = spiritual progress. Top = nirvana (formlessness, circular platform, no reliefs). Architecture as embodied religious instruction.
Nan Madol
c. 1200–1600 CE · Pohnpei, Micronesia · Basalt logs + coral
Artificial island city built entirely on a reef — no natural land. 92 man-made islets connected by canals. Saudeleur dynasty's seat of power. Demonstrates extraordinary engineering and social organization. Basalt logs (up to 50 tons) transported by canoe. Political and religious center; common people forbidden from entering.
Content Area 11 · ~4% of exam
Global Contemporary (1980–present)

Key Themes & Works

Identity & the Body

Kara Walker: cut-paper silhouettes confronting slavery's visual legacy. Cindy Sherman: self-portraits as cultural archetypes (women in media). Yinka Shonibare: Victorian dress + African fabric = colonial critique.

Postcolonialism

Art that addresses the legacy of colonialism, cultural hybridity, and contested identities. Artists work globally, are educated in multiple traditions, and resist being labeled by nationality alone.

Environmental Art

Christo's wrapped landscapes, Smithson's Spiral Jetty (earthwork that changes with water levels), Andy Goldsworthy's ephemeral natural sculptures. Nature as medium AND subject.

LEQ ModelComparing Two Works: Power & Ruler Representation

Prompt: Compare the Palette of King Narmer with one other work of art from a different culture that also uses visual strategies to communicate the power and authority of a ruler.

Chosen comparison: Standard of Ur (Mesopotamia, c. 2600 BCE)


Both the Palette of King Narmer (Egypt, c. 3000 BCE) and the Standard of Ur (Mesopotamia, c. 2600 BCE) use hierarchical scale and composite conventions to naturalize the political authority of their rulers, yet differ significantly in the social contexts for which each object was created.


The Palette of Narmer employs hierarchical scale as its primary communicator of royal power: the pharaoh towers over enemies and attendants, his body rendered in the canonical composite view (profile head, frontal shoulders, profile legs) that would define Egyptian representation for three millennia. This visual formula is not artlessness — it is a deliberate statement that the pharaoh occupies a different ontological category than other humans. Similarly, the "War" panel of the Standard of Ur places the ruler at the far right, seated and largest, receiving prisoners who grow progressively smaller as they approach him, enacting submission through diminishing scale.


The key difference lies in function: the Palette was likely a ceremonial object deposited in a temple as a votive offering, permanently recording Narmer's unification of Egypt for divine audiences. The Standard of Ur was discovered in a royal tomb at Ur — a portable object that traveled to the afterlife with its owner, suggesting it commemorated specific military victories for eternal memory rather than immediate public display. Both works demonstrate that visual hierarchies of scale were not naive — they were sophisticated political technologies for communicating and naturalizing power across ancient cultures.

Practice
MCQ Practice
Q1Formal Analysis

An artwork shows a standing figure with the left leg slightly forward, weight on the right leg, causing a subtle hip tilt. This formal technique is best described as:

  • A) Foreshortening
  • B) Contrapposto
  • C) Chiaroscuro
  • D) Hierarchical scale
Answer: B — Contrapposto (Italian: counterpose) is the natural weight-shift stance first developed in Classical Greek sculpture, most clearly seen in the Kritios Boy (c. 480 BCE). It signals naturalism and humanistic interest in the body.
Q2Context — Egyptian Art

Egyptian sculpture repeatedly used the same formal conventions for over 3,000 years primarily because:

  • A) Egyptian artists lacked the technical skill to innovate
  • B) Egyptian rulers demanded Greek-style naturalism
  • C) Artistic formulas were linked to religious function; deviation was spiritually dangerous
  • D) Trade with Rome discouraged artistic experimentation
Answer: C — Egyptian art's consistency was spiritually motivated. Art served the ka (soul) and had to be perfect and permanent. Established formulas guaranteed the artwork would function correctly. Innovation = risk.
Q3Style Identification

An artwork features dramatic diagonal compositions, intense chiaroscuro, emotional figures in theatrical poses, and large scale intended for church altarpieces. This work is most likely from the:

  • A) Italian Renaissance (1400–1600)
  • B) Baroque (1600–1750)
  • C) Neoclassicism (1750–1850)
  • D) Impressionism (1860–1886)
Answer: B — Baroque art (Caravaggio, Rubens, Bernini) is defined by dramatic chiaroscuro, diagonal composition, emotional intensity, and theatrical religiosity designed to inspire Counter-Reformation faith.

⚡ Perfect Score Checklist

  • Always identify the work completely before analyzing: culture, period, medium, function
  • FACA every work: Form → Artwork info → Context → Analysis
  • Connect formal choices to meaning — never just list elements
  • Hierarchical scale ≠ naturalism: size communicates importance, not spatial position
  • Know the difference between style periods: Baroque (drama/chiaroscuro) vs. Renaissance (harmony/balance) vs. Neoclassical (order/virtue)
  • Non-Western art: function and context first — don't apply Western aesthetic standards
  • Contrapposto = Classical Greek naturalism; composite view = Egyptian completeness
  • LEQ: choose a comparison work you can analyze formally AND contextually
  • Repatriation debate: many works in Western museums were taken under colonial conditions — this is an active issue
  • Never say "primitive" — use "non-Western," "ancient," or name the specific culture
