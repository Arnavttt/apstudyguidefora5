Atomic Structure & Properties

Electron Configuration & Periodic Trends

ELECTRON CONFIGURATION:
1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d¹⁰ 4p⁶ ... // fill lowest energy first (Aufbau)
Hund's Rule: one e⁻ per orbital before pairing in same subshell
Pauli Exclusion: no two electrons same 4 quantum numbers

EXCEPTIONS (memorize):
Cr: [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹ // half-filled 3d is stable
Cu: [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹ // full 3d is stable

Unit 2 · ~7–9% of exam
Chemical Bonding & Molecular Structure

Bond Types & VSEPR

Ionic Bonds

Metal + nonmetal. Electron transfer. Lattice energy — higher charge/smaller radius = stronger lattice. Forms crystalline solids. High mp/bp. Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved.

Covalent Bonds

Nonmetal + nonmetal. Electron sharing. Bond order = # of shared pairs. Higher bond order → shorter bond, stronger bond, higher bond energy.

VSEPR Theory

Electron pairs repel → maximize separation. Lone pairs repel MORE than bonding pairs. Determines molecular geometry (not electron geometry). Know: linear, trigonal planar, bent, tetrahedral, trigonal pyramidal, seesaw, T-shape, octahedral, square planar.

Polarity

Polar bond: ΔEN > 0.4. Polar molecule: polar bonds + asymmetric geometry. Symmetric molecules (CO₂, CCl₄, BF₃) = nonpolar despite polar bonds. Dipole moment = μ = qd.

Resonance

When Lewis structure has equivalent positions for double bonds. Actual structure is average — all bond lengths equal. Delocalized electrons = greater stability (resonance energy).

Formal Charge

FC = valence e⁻ − nonbonding e⁻ − ½(bonding e⁻). Best Lewis structure: minimize FC, negative FC on most electronegative atom.

Unit 3 · ~7–9% of exam
Intermolecular Forces & Properties

IMF Types — Weakest to Strongest

LONDON DISPERSION FORCES (LDF) // all molecules have these
Temporary dipole-induced dipole. Strength ∝ molar mass and surface area.
Larger, more polarizable molecule → stronger LDF

DIPOLE-DIPOLE
Permanent dipoles attract. Only polar molecules. Stronger than LDF for similar-size molecules.

HYDROGEN BONDING // strongest IMF between neutral molecules
H bonded to N, O, or F attracting lone pair on N, O, or F.
Explains: water's high bp, surface tension, ice density anomaly, DNA base pairing.

ION-DIPOLE // strongest overall — why ionic compounds dissolve in water
Ion + polar molecule. Basis of hydration/solvation.
IMF → Properties Rule
  • Stronger IMF = higher boiling point, higher viscosity, higher surface tension, lower vapor pressure
  • Compare molecules of similar size: use dipole-dipole or H-bonding to distinguish
  • Compare molecules same type: use molar mass (LDF) to distinguish
  • Water's anomalously high bp (100°C vs. H₂S −60°C) = hydrogen bonding
Unit 5 · ~7–9% of exam
Chemical Kinetics

Rate Laws & Integrated Rate Laws

RATE LAW: rate = k[A]ᵐ[B]ⁿ
m, n = reaction orders (determined experimentally, NOT from coefficients)
k = rate constant (temperature dependent)

INTEGRATED RATE LAWS:
Zero order: [A] = [A]₀ − kt // linear: [A] vs t
First order: ln[A] = ln[A]₀ − kt // linear: ln[A] vs t
Second order: 1/[A] = 1/[A]₀ + kt // linear: 1/[A] vs t

HALF-LIFE:
First order: t₁/₂ = 0.693/k // constant, independent of [A]₀
Second order: t₁/₂ = 1/(k[A]₀) // depends on [A]₀

Activation Energy & Arrhenius

k = Ae^(−Ea/RT). Higher Ea → slower reaction. Catalyst lowers Ea (provides alternate pathway). ln(k₂/k₁) = (Ea/R)(1/T₁ − 1/T₂).

Reaction Mechanisms

Rate-determining step (slowest step) determines overall rate law. Intermediates: produced then consumed. Catalysts: present before and after. Rate law for mechanism must match experimental.

Collision Theory

Molecules must collide with (1) sufficient energy ≥ Ea and (2) proper orientation. Factors increasing rate: ↑ concentration, ↑ temperature, catalyst, ↑ surface area.

Unit 7 · ~7–9% of exam
Equilibrium

Equilibrium Expressions & Le Chatelier's Principle

EQUILIBRIUM CONSTANT:
Kc = [products]^coeff / [reactants]^coeff // pure solids and liquids omitted
Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn // Δn = moles gas products − moles gas reactants

REACTION QUOTIENT Q:
Q < K → reaction proceeds forward (more products)
Q > K → reaction proceeds reverse (more reactants)
Q = K → at equilibrium

ICE TABLE: Initial → Change → Equilibrium
Change row: use stoichiometric ratios. Solve for x.
Le Chatelier's Principle
  • Add reactant/remove product: shifts right (toward products)
  • Add product/remove reactant: shifts left (toward reactants)
  • Increase pressure: shifts toward fewer moles of gas
  • Increase temperature: shifts toward endothermic side (treats heat as reactant/product)
  • Add catalyst: equilibrium position unchanged — just reaches it faster
  • Add inert gas (constant volume): no shift — partial pressures unchanged
Unit 8 · ~11–15% of exam
Acids, Bases & Buffers

Key Equations

pH = −log[H⁺] pOH = −log[OH⁻] pH + pOH = 14
Ka × Kb = Kw = 1×10⁻¹⁴ (at 25°C)
pKa = −log(Ka) stronger acid = smaller pKa

BUFFER (Henderson-Hasselbalch):
pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])
Buffer resists pH change. Most effective when pH ≈ pKa (equal concentrations).
Buffer capacity: more concentrated = better capacity.

Strong Acids (memorize)

HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄, HClO₃. Fully dissociate. [H⁺] = [acid].

Weak Acids

Ka < 1. Use ICE table. [H⁺] = √(Ka·C) when x << C. Percent ionization = [H⁺]/C × 100%.

Titration Curves

Strong-strong: equivalence point pH = 7. Weak acid-strong base: pH > 7 at equivalence (conjugate base). Half-equivalence: pH = pKa.

Ksp & Solubility

Ksp = [cations]^m[anions]^n. Common ion effect: adding a common ion decreases solubility. pH affects solubility of salts with basic/acidic anions.

Unit 9 · ~7–9% of exam
Thermodynamics
GIBBS FREE ENERGY:
ΔG = ΔH − TΔS // ΔG < 0 → spontaneous
ΔG° = −RT ln K = −nFE°

SIGN RULES:
ΔH < 0 (exothermic) + ΔS > 0 → always spontaneous
ΔH > 0 (endothermic) + ΔS < 0 → never spontaneous
ΔH < 0 + ΔS < 0 → spontaneous at low T
ΔH > 0 + ΔS > 0 → spontaneous at high T

HESS'S LAW: ΔH°rxn = Σ ΔH°f(products) − Σ ΔH°f(reactants)
Reverse reaction: flip sign. Multiply reaction: multiply ΔH.
Unit 9 · ~7–9% of exam
Electrochemistry
GALVANIC CELL: oxidation at anode (−), reduction at cathode (+)
E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode // positive E°cell = spontaneous
ΔG° = −nFE°cell // n = moles of electrons, F = 96485 C/mol
Nernst equation: E = E° − (RT/nF)ln Q = E° − (0.0592/n)log Q (at 25°C)

ELECTROLYSIS: external current forces non-spontaneous reaction
Moles e⁻ = It/F (I = current in amps, t = time in seconds)
⚡ Exam Traps
  • Reaction orders come from EXPERIMENT, never from balanced equation coefficients
  • Temperature ALWAYS shifts equilibrium — pressure shift only matters for gases
  • Catalyst does NOT change K or ΔG° — only changes activation energy and rate
  • At half-equivalence point in titration: pH = pKa (use this to find Ka)
  • In galvanic cell: oxidation at anode. In electrolytic: also oxidation at anode (anode always oxidation)
Practice
MCQ Practice
Q1Kinetics · Rate Law

A reaction A + B → C is first order in A and second order in B. If [A] is doubled and [B] is halved, the rate will:

  • A) Double
  • B) Decrease by half
  • C) Stay the same
  • D) Quadruple
Answer: B — rate = k[A][B]². New rate = k(2[A])([B]/2)² = k·2[A]·[B]²/4 = rate/2. The second-order dependence on B dominates.
Q2Equilibrium · Le Chatelier

For N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) ΔH = −92 kJ/mol. Which change increases NH₃ yield?

  • A) Increasing temperature
  • B) Increasing pressure
  • C) Decreasing pressure
  • D) Adding a catalyst
Answer: B — Haber process insight. 4 mol gas on left → 2 mol on right. Increasing pressure favors fewer gas moles → shifts right → more NH₃. (In practice, temperature is a tradeoff: low T = good yield but slow rate.)

⚡ Perfect Score Checklist

  • Reaction order from experiment, never from coefficients
  • First-order half-life is constant — independent of initial concentration
  • Stronger acid = smaller Ka, smaller pKa
  • pH = pKa at half-equivalence point in weak acid titration
  • ΔG < 0 = spontaneous; E°cell > 0 = spontaneous; K > 1 = products favored — all three equivalent
  • Catalyst: lowers Ea, increases rate, does NOT change K or ΔG°
  • Common ion effect decreases solubility
  • Hydrogen bonding requires H bonded to N, O, or F
  • Anode = oxidation (always) in both galvanic and electrolytic
  • Hess's Law: reverse = flip sign; multiply = scale ΔH
