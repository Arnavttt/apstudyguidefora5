Unit 1 · ~15–22% of exam
Foundations of American Democracy

Constitutional Framework

Popular Sovereignty

Government derives its authority from the consent of the governed. Power ultimately rests with the people, expressed through elections and civic participation.

Limited Government

The Constitution constrains government power through the Bill of Rights, separation of powers, and judicial review. Government can only do what the Constitution permits.

Separation of Powers

Three co-equal branches: Legislative (Congress), Executive (President), Judicial (Courts). Each has distinct powers defined in Articles I, II, III of the Constitution.

Checks and Balances

Each branch can limit the other two. President vetoes → Congress overrides (2/3). Senate confirms appointments. Courts strike laws unconstitutional. Prevents tyranny.

Federalism

Division of power between national and state governments. Enumerated powers (federal), reserved powers (states, 10th Amendment), concurrent powers (both). McCulloch v. Maryland (1819) strengthened federal power.

Republicanism

Indirect democracy — citizens elect representatives who govern. Not a direct democracy. Designed to filter "passions of the majority" through deliberative institutions.

Unit 2 · ~25–36% of exam
Interactions Among the Branches

Congress

House of Representatives

  • 435 members; 2-year terms
  • Proportional by population
  • Originates revenue bills
  • Speaker has significant power
  • More partisan, majoritarian
  • Initiates impeachment

Senate

  • 100 members; 6-year staggered terms
  • 2 per state regardless of population
  • Confirms appointments, ratifies treaties
  • Filibuster (60 votes to end debate)
  • Tries impeachment cases
  • Slower, deliberative by design

How a Bill Becomes a Law

  • Introduced in either chamber
  • Committee markup and vote
  • Floor debate and vote
  • Other chamber repeats
  • Conference committee resolves differences
  • President signs or vetoes (Congress can override 2/3)

Congressional Powers

  • Enumerated: tax, spend, declare war, regulate commerce, coin money
  • Necessary & Proper Clause: "elastic clause" — implied powers
  • Power of the purse (appropriations)
  • Oversight of executive agencies

The Presidency

Formal Powers (Constitutional)

  • Commander-in-chief of military
  • Veto legislation (pocket veto, line-item veto unconstitutional)
  • Nominate federal judges, cabinet, ambassadors
  • Make treaties (2/3 Senate ratification)
  • Grant pardons
  • State of the Union address

Informal Powers

  • Executive orders (bypasses Congress)
  • Executive agreements (like treaties, no Senate approval)
  • Bully pulpit — use media to set agenda
  • Signing statements
  • Pocket veto at end of congressional session

President vs. Congress Tensions

  • War Powers Resolution (1973): president must notify Congress within 48 hrs of military action
  • Divided government slows legislation
  • Senate can block nominations (advice and consent)
  • Budget battles: government shutdowns

The Federal Bureaucracy & Judiciary

Federal Bureaucracy

15 cabinet departments + independent agencies (EPA, FEC, FCC). Implements laws. Iron triangles: agency + congressional committee + interest group = mutually beneficial relationship. Issue networks: broader, more fluid policy communities.

Federal Courts

  • District Courts → Circuit Courts → Supreme Court
  • Judicial review: Marbury v. Madison (1803)
  • Stare decisis: respect precedent
  • Judicial activism vs. restraint
  • Strict vs. loose constructionism
Unit 3 · ~13–18% of exam
Civil Liberties & Civil Rights

Civil Liberties vs. Civil Rights

Civil Liberties

Constitutional protections FROM government interference. The Bill of Rights. First Amendment freedoms, 4th Amendment search/seizure, 5th Amendment self-incrimination, 8th Amendment cruel/unusual punishment.

Civil Rights

Government protection OF equal treatment. 14th Amendment equal protection clause. Civil Rights Act (1964), Voting Rights Act (1965). Government must actively protect against discrimination.

Key Civil Liberties Issues

IssueConstitutional BasisKey Tension
Freedom of Speech1st AmendmentProtected: political speech, symbolic speech, even offensive speech. Not protected: "true threats," incitement to imminent lawless action (Brandenburg test)
Freedom of Religion1st Amendment (two clauses)Free Exercise Clause vs. Establishment Clause. Lemon test for establishment. Government cannot favor or hinder religion.
Search & Seizure4th AmendmentWarrant required with probable cause. Exclusionary rule: illegally obtained evidence inadmissible (Mapp v. Ohio). Exceptions: exigent circumstances, plain view, consent.
Due Process5th & 14th AmendmentsProcedural (fair procedures) vs. substantive (government cannot infringe fundamental rights regardless of procedure)
Right to PrivacyNot explicit; penumbras of 1st, 3rd, 4th, 5th, 9th AmendmentsGriswold v. Connecticut (1965): found right to privacy in "penumbras" of Bill of Rights

Political Socialization — How Beliefs Form

Family

Strongest single predictor of party identification. Most people adopt parents' party ID. Transmission especially strong when both parents share the same party.

Education

Higher education correlates with more liberal views on social issues and more civic participation. Schools transmit civic norms and political knowledge.

Media

Media framing shapes issue salience and opinion. Selective exposure: people consume ideologically consistent media. Horse race journalism emphasizes competition over policy.

Religion, Region, Race

Race: Black Americans overwhelmingly Democratic; White evangelical Christians overwhelmingly Republican. Region: South Republican, Northeast/West Coast Democratic. Religion shapes especially social issue views.

Unit 5 · ~20–27% of exam
Political Participation

Elections & Voting

Electoral College

  • 538 total electors; 270 needed to win
  • Winner-takes-all in 48 states (ME, NE split)
  • Can win popular vote, lose EC (2000, 2016)
  • Favors small states (2 senators regardless of size)
  • Third-party candidates essentially excluded

Voting Barriers & Turnout

  • US has lower turnout than most democracies
  • Registration requirements reduce participation
  • Rational ignorance: cost of voting vs. impact
  • Higher turnout: presidential years, competitive races
  • Motor Voter Act (1993), Voting Rights Act (1965)

Campaign Finance

  • Citizens United (2010): political spending = protected speech
  • Super PACs: unlimited independent expenditures
  • Hard money: regulated direct contributions
  • Soft money: unregulated party/PAC spending
  • FEC oversees campaign finance law

Political Parties

  • Two-party system reinforced by winner-takes-all elections (Duverger's Law)
  • Parties: recruit candidates, mobilize voters, organize government
  • Party platforms shift over time (ideological sorting)
  • Third parties: spoiler effect (Nader 2000, Perot 1992)

Interest Groups

  • Lobbying, campaign contributions, grassroots mobilization, litigation
  • PACs: political action committees
  • Pluralism: competing groups produce democratic outcomes
  • Elite theory: wealthy, organized interests dominate
  • Iron triangles + issue networks (see Unit 2)

Media & Public Opinion

  • Agenda-setting: media determines what people think ABOUT (not what to think)
  • Framing: HOW an issue is presented shapes opinion
  • Priming: issue salience shapes vote choice
  • Social media: echo chambers, misinformation, direct politician-voter communication
Required Content
15 Required SCOTUS Cases
Every single one appears on the exam — know ruling + significance
Marbury v. Madison
1803
Established judicial review
Court can strike down laws that violate the Constitution. Foundation of judicial branch power. "It is emphatically the province of the judicial department to say what the law is."
McCulloch v. Maryland
1819
Congress may create a national bank; states cannot tax federal government
Established implied powers (Necessary & Proper Clause). Federal supremacy over states. "The power to tax is the power to destroy."
Schenck v. United States
1919
Government may restrict speech creating "clear and present danger"
Speech not absolutely protected. "Falsely shouting fire in a crowded theater." Later limited by Brandenburg (1969) to incitement to imminent lawless action.
Brown v. Board of Education
1954
Racial segregation in public schools unconstitutional
Overturned Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) "separate but equal." 14th Amendment requires desegregation. Landmark civil rights ruling. Warren Court unanimous.
Engel v. Vitale
1962
School-sponsored prayer unconstitutional
Establishment Clause prohibits state-sponsored religious activity in public schools. Separation of church and state extended to education.
Baker v. Carr
1962
Federal courts can hear legislative apportionment cases
Established "one person, one vote" principle. Courts can rule on political districting. Led to major reapportionment of state legislatures.
Gideon v. Wainwright
1963
Right to counsel applies to state criminal cases
Incorporated 6th Amendment right to attorney through 14th Amendment. States must provide lawyers for defendants who cannot afford one.
Tinker v. Des Moines
1969
Students retain 1st Amendment rights in school
Black armbands protesting Vietnam War = protected symbolic speech. Schools cannot suppress student expression unless it causes "substantial disruption." "Students do not shed their rights at the schoolhouse gate."
New York Times Co. v. United States
1971
Government cannot impose prior restraint on press ("Pentagon Papers")
Prior restraint (stopping publication before it occurs) requires extraordinary justification. Freedom of press protects publication of classified documents showing government deception.
Wisconsin v. Yoder
1972
Amish families exempt from compulsory education past 8th grade
Free Exercise Clause protects religious practice from neutral, generally applicable laws in some circumstances. Balancing test: government interest vs. religious burden.
Roe v. Wade
1973 / Overturned 2022
Originally: abortion is a fundamental right (14th Amendment privacy). Overturned by Dobbs v. Jackson (2022).
Still on AP exam as required case. Established trimester framework. Dobbs returned abortion regulation to states. Illustrates how SCOTUS precedent can be reversed.
Shaw v. Reno
1993
Racial gerrymandering can violate Equal Protection
Majority-minority districts drawn primarily on race must survive strict scrutiny. Limits race-conscious redistricting. Key case for voting rights and representation.
United States v. Lopez
1995
Gun-Free School Zones Act exceeds Commerce Clause authority
First case in decades to limit federal commerce power. Reaffirmed limits on enumerated powers. Strengthened states' rights. Guns in schools ≠ economic activity.
Citizens United v. FEC
2010
Political spending by corporations and unions = protected free speech
Struck down limits on independent political expenditures. Led to Super PACs. Transformed campaign finance. Critics: money is not speech, corporations are not people.
McDonald v. Chicago
2010
2nd Amendment right to keep arms applies to states
Incorporated 2nd Amendment through 14th Amendment. Extended Heller (2008) which found individual right to bear arms. States cannot ban handguns for self-defense.
Required Content
9 Foundational Documents
Know author, argument, and connection to course concepts
The Declaration of Independence
1776 · Thomas Jefferson
Articulates natural rights (life, liberty, pursuit of happiness), social contract theory (government derives power from consent of the governed), and right to revolution. Influenced by John Locke. Sets out a list of grievances against King George III. Established ideals that later constitutional amendments work to realize.
The Articles of Confederation
1781 · Continental Congress
First governing document. Created a weak central government with no executive, no federal courts, no power to tax, no power to regulate commerce. States retained sovereignty. Failed because it couldn't resolve economic conflicts, fund the government, or respond to Shays' Rebellion. Its failure motivated the Constitutional Convention.
Federalist No. 10
1787 · James Madison
Argues that a large republic is BETTER at controlling "factions" (interest groups) than a small direct democracy. In a large republic, more factions compete and no single faction can dominate. Defends the Constitution against Anti-Federalist charges that a large republic would be tyrannical. Basis for pluralist theory of democracy.
Brutus No. 1
1787 · Robert Yates (Anti-Federalist)
Counter-argument to Federalist Papers. Argues that a large republic will be too distant from citizens and will inevitably consolidate power in the federal government at the expense of states and individual liberty. Warns that Necessary & Proper Clause and Supremacy Clause are dangerous open-ended grants of federal power. Demanded a Bill of Rights.
Federalist No. 51
1788 · James Madison
Explains the logic of separation of powers and checks and balances. "Ambition must be made to counteract ambition." Each branch must have means to resist encroachments by others. Argues that neither majority nor minority can dominate. "If men were angels, no government would be necessary."
Federalist No. 70
1788 · Alexander Hamilton
Argues for a single, energetic executive rather than a plural executive. A single president provides accountability, decisiveness, and unity of action — especially in emergencies. A plural executive creates confusion and diffused accountability. Hamilton's vision of a strong presidency shaped the modern executive branch.
Federalist No. 78
1788 · Alexander Hamilton
Defends the judiciary as the "least dangerous branch" because it has neither the power of the sword (executive) nor the purse (legislative). Argues courts need life tenure to remain independent from political pressure. Lays the groundwork for judicial review by arguing courts must declare laws repugnant to the Constitution void.
Letter from a Birmingham Jail
1963 · Martin Luther King Jr.
Written to "moderate" white clergymen who criticized civil rights protests as "untimely." King argues that "justice too long delayed is justice denied." Distinguishes just laws (which people have a moral responsibility to follow) from unjust laws (which people have a moral responsibility to disobey). Defends non-violent direct action as a legitimate form of political participation when legal channels fail.
The Constitution of the United States
1787 · Constitutional Convention
Supreme law of the land. Seven articles: I (Congress), II (President), III (Courts), IV (States), V (Amendments), VI (Supremacy Clause), VII (Ratification). 27 amendments. Key clauses: Commerce, Necessary & Proper, Supremacy, Equal Protection, Due Process, Free Exercise, Establishment.
Exam Strategy
FRQ Types — 4 Distinct Formats
4 FRQs · 100 min · 50% of score
The 4 AP Gov FRQ Types
  • FRQ 1 — Concept Application: Describe a real-world scenario and explain how it illustrates a course concept
  • FRQ 2 — Quantitative Analysis: Read a graph/chart/map and answer questions about it, then connect to course content
  • FRQ 3 — SCOTUS Comparison: Compare a non-required case to a required SCOTUS case you have studied
  • FRQ 4 — Argument Essay: Take a position on a constitutional question and support it with foundational documents, SCOTUS cases, and course evidence
FRQ Type 4Argument Essay — Model Response

Prompt: Develop an argument explaining whether the expansion of federal government power has, on balance, strengthened or weakened American democracy. Use at least one foundational document, one required SCOTUS case, and one other piece of relevant evidence.

Thesis

While the Anti-Federalist tradition embodied in Brutus No. 1 correctly warned that expanded federal power risks distancing government from citizens, the historical record demonstrates that federal expansion — particularly through the 14th Amendment and subsequent civil rights legislation — has been the primary mechanism by which the Constitution's founding promises were extended to citizens long excluded from their protections, making federal power, properly constrained, indispensable to rather than destructive of American democracy.


Supporting Paragraph (SCOTUS Evidence)

The Supreme Court's ruling in Brown v. Board of Education (1954) illustrates how expanded federal authority can correct the democratic failures of state governments. For nearly sixty years after the Civil War, the "separate but equal" doctrine established in Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) allowed states to legally enforce racial segregation, perpetuating a system of exclusion that denied Black Americans the equal citizenship the 14th Amendment nominally guaranteed. Without the federal judiciary's willingness to override state law, this exclusion would have persisted through local democratic processes that systematically disenfranchised the very citizens they harmed. Brown demonstrates that federal power, exercised through constitutional interpretation, does not inherently threaten democracy — it can be the mechanism by which democracy honors its own stated commitments to equality.

Why This Structure Works
  • Thesis acknowledges the counterargument (Anti-Federalist tradition) before stating the position — this signals sophistication
  • Evidence paragraph connects a specific SCOTUS case to a specific historical failure that federal power corrected
  • Doesn't just say "Brown was important" — explains the constitutional mechanism and the democratic failure it corrected
  • Body paragraph structure: claim → evidence → analysis → so what
FRQ Type 3SCOTUS Comparison — Strategy Guide

The SCOTUS comparison FRQ always gives you a non-required case and asks you to compare it to one of the 15 required cases. The key skills:

Step 1: Identify the constitutional principle in the new case (e.g., free speech, due process, equal protection).
Step 2: Match to a required SCOTUS case involving the same principle.
Step 3: Compare the rulings — did they reach the same conclusion? Why or why not?
Step 4: Explain what the comparison reveals about how that constitutional principle applies across different contexts.

Example: New case involves school dress code as compelled speech → compare to Tinker v. Des Moines (1969). Both involve student speech rights; distinguish by type of speech (symbolic protest vs. dress) and level of disruption.
Practice
MCQ Practice
Q1Federalist 10 · Factions

According to James Madison in Federalist No. 10, which of the following BEST explains why a large republic is preferable to a small direct democracy?

  • A) Direct democracy allows factions to dominate government
  • B) Small republics have fewer factions, making governance easier
  • C) A large republic contains so many diverse factions that no single one can form a tyrannical majority
  • D) Large republics elect representatives from more geographically diverse areas
Answer: C — Madison's core argument in Federalist 10: in a large republic, the variety of interests (factions) means no single faction can easily dominate. This is the foundation of pluralism theory.
Q2SCOTUS · Marbury v. Madison

The significance of Marbury v. Madison (1803) is primarily that it:

  • A) Established the supremacy of federal law over state law
  • B) Established the Supreme Court's power to declare acts of Congress unconstitutional
  • C) Gave Congress implied powers beyond those explicitly listed in the Constitution
  • D) Extended the protections of the Bill of Rights to the states
Answer: B — Marbury v. Madison established judicial review: the power of federal courts to strike down laws that violate the Constitution. This gave the judiciary a co-equal role in American governance.
Q3Congress · Checks and Balances

The Senate's power to confirm presidential nominees to the federal judiciary is an example of which constitutional principle?

  • A) Federalism
  • B) Popular sovereignty
  • C) Checks and balances
  • D) Judicial review
Answer: C — Senate confirmation of judicial nominees is a check on presidential power. The executive nominates; the legislature approves. This prevents one branch from controlling judicial appointments alone.
