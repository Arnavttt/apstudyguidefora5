Narrative Techniques

Point of View

1st person (I/we): intimate, unreliable. 3rd limited: follows one character's consciousness. 3rd omniscient: god-like narrator. 2nd (you): implicates reader. POV shapes what we know and trust.

Unreliable Narrator

Narrator whose account cannot be taken at face value. Clues: contradictions, gaps in memory, self-serving logic, others contradict them. Examples: Humbert Humbert, Nick Carraway, Stevens in Remains of the Day.

Stream of Consciousness

Interior monologue mimicking the flow of thought — unfiltered, associative, shifting. Used to show psychological depth. Woolf, Faulkner, Joyce.

Foreshadowing

Early hints at later events. Creates dramatic irony or suspense. Can be symbolic (weather, objects) or explicit (a character's foreboding).

In Medias Res

Beginning in the middle of action, then filling backstory via flashback. Creates immediate tension, forces the reader to orient themselves.

Flashback / Analepsis

Interruption of the narrative with a past scene. Reveals backstory, motivation, or ironic contrast with the present.

Frame Narrative

A story within a story. The outer narrative frames the inner one. Questions reliability and meaning through layers of narration. Wuthering Heights, Heart of Darkness.

Free Indirect Discourse

Third-person narration that slides into a character's thoughts and speech without quotation marks. Merges narrator and character voice. Common in Austen, Flaubert.

Figurative Language

Metaphor / Extended Metaphor

Direct comparison without like/as. Extended metaphor sustains comparison throughout a passage or poem. "All the world's a stage" (Shakespeare extends through all of life's stages).

Simile

Comparison using like or as. More explicit than metaphor, slightly less intense. "His voice was like gravel being poured."

Personification

Giving human attributes to non-human things. Creates emotional resonance, suggests relationship between humans and the world. "The fog comes on little cat feet" (Sandburg).

Apostrophe

Addressing an absent person, abstract concept, or non-human entity directly. Creates emotional intensity. "O Death, where is thy sting?"

Synecdoche

Part represents the whole (or whole represents part). "All hands on deck" — hands = sailors. Common in poetry for compression.

Metonymy

Substituting a related concept for the thing itself. "The Crown" for monarchy; "the pen is mightier than the sword." Implies deeper cultural associations.

Paradox

Seemingly contradictory statement that reveals a deeper truth. "I must be cruel only to be kind." Demands the reader reconcile the contradiction.

Oxymoron

Two contradictory terms together. "Bittersweet," "living death," "deafening silence." Compressed paradox; highlights tension.

Hyperbole

Deliberate exaggeration for effect. "I've told you a million times." Emphasizes emotional intensity, comic effect, or emphasis.

Understatement / Litotes

Deliberate minimization. Litotes uses negation: "not unkind" = kind. Creates ironic distance or British wit. "It was somewhat unfortunate that he died."

Allusion

Reference to another text, myth, history, or cultural artifact. Layers meaning, builds ethos, assumes reader's cultural literacy. Identify source + effect.

Symbol

Object, person, or place that represents something beyond its literal meaning. Can be universal (light=knowledge) or contextual (the green light in Gatsby = unattainable dream).

Sound Devices (Critical for Poetry)

Alliteration

Repetition of initial consonant sounds. Creates musicality, urgency, or emphasis. "Peter Piper picked..."

Assonance

Repetition of vowel sounds within words. Creates mood — long vowels (slow, mournful); short vowels (sharp, quick). "Hear the mellow wedding bells" (Poe).

Consonance

Repetition of consonant sounds (not just initial). Creates texture and tone.

Onomatopoeia

Words that sound like their meaning. Buzz, crash, hiss, murmur. Creates immediacy; mimics sensation.

Cacophony / Euphony

Cacophony: harsh, jarring sounds (k, g, x, cr, str). Euphony: smooth, pleasant sounds (l, m, n, soft vowels). Sound mirrors meaning.

Enjambment

Line continues without pause to next line. Creates momentum, ambiguity at line breaks. Contrast with end-stopped lines (pause at end). The line break itself is meaningful.

Structural & Tonal Devices

Juxtaposition

Placing contrasting elements side by side. Highlights difference through proximity. Used without comparing directly (unlike antithesis, which uses parallel structure).

Tone

Author's attitude toward subject/audience. Describe precisely: not just "sad" but elegiac, wistful, melancholic. Not just "angry" but sardonic, indignant, scathing. Word choices reveal tone.

Mood / Atmosphere

The feeling evoked in the reader. Tone = author's attitude. Mood = reader's emotional response. Related but distinct.

Volta (in Poetry)

The turn — a shift in tone, argument, or perspective. In sonnets, often at line 9 (Petrarchan) or at the couplet (Shakespearean). Identifying the volta is key to understanding the poem's argument.

Diction

Word choice. Analyze connotation vs. denotation. Latinate words (polysyllabic, formal) vs. Anglo-Saxon (short, direct, raw). Clinical vs. visceral. Elevated vs. colloquial.

Syntax

Sentence structure as meaning. Fragmented syntax = disorientation or emotion. Long, subordinate clauses = complexity, control, or entrapment. Inverted syntax (verb before subject) = emphasis.

TPCASTT — Poetry Analysis Method

TPCASTT Framework
  • T — Title: Before reading, what does the title suggest? Predict meaning, tone, subject.
  • P — Paraphrase: Translate the poem into plain English line by line. Understand the literal meaning before analyzing.
  • C — Connotation: Look at word choice, imagery, figurative language. What feelings/associations do words carry?
  • A — Attitude: What is the speaker's tone? How does the poet feel about the subject?
  • S — Shifts: Where does tone, subject, or perspective change? These shifts are often the most important moments.
  • T — Title (revisit): Now what does the title mean? Has your interpretation changed?
  • T — Theme: What is the poem's central insight about human experience? This is your thesis territory.

Meter & Scansion — What You Actually Need

Iambic Pentameter

5 iambs per line (da-DUM × 5). 10 syllables. Shakespeare's default. When meter is broken (stressed syllable out of place), that break is meaningful — emphasizes a word or creates tension.

Spondee

Two stressed syllables (DUM-DUM). Slows the line, adds weight or emphasis. "Break, break, break" (Tennyson) — weight of grief in each word.

Pyrrhic

Two unstressed syllables. Speeds the line, lightens. Often used as a foil to surrounding stressed feet.

Trochee

Stressed-unstressed (DUM-da). Inverted iamb. Used at line openings for emphasis. "Tiger, tiger, burning bright."

⚡ On the Exam — Don't Get Lost in Scansion

AP Lit FRQ graders care about meaning and interpretation, not perfect scansion notation. Focus on: where does the regular meter break? What word gets unusual stress? What effect does that have? That analysis scores points.

Reading Prose — What to Notice Immediately

Prose Close Reading Checklist
  • Who is narrating? POV, reliability, distance from events
  • What is the syntax doing? Long sentences = control, complexity, entrapment. Short = urgency, clarity, shock.
  • What is the tone/diction? Clinical? Lyrical? Ironic? Detached? What feeling does this create?
  • What details are emphasized? What the narrator notices reveals character and theme.
  • Where does the passage begin and end? The framing often signals the key shift or insight.
  • What is the relationship between characters? Power dynamics, conflict, desire.
  • What is withheld? What the text doesn't say can be as important as what it does.

Poetry FRQModel Response — "Ozymandias" by Shelley

Prompt: In Percy Bysshe Shelley's sonnet "Ozymandias" (1818), the speaker encounters a crumbling statue in a desert wasteland. In a well-written essay, analyze how Shelley uses poetic techniques to convey a complex message about power and legacy.

Thesis (1/1)

Through the framing device of a reported encounter, the ironic juxtaposition of grandiose inscription against desolate landscape, and the poem's formal subversion of the sonnet's conventional triumphalism, Shelley argues that political power is inherently self-deceiving — that the very monuments built to immortalize kings instead become evidence of their impermanence.


Body Paragraph — Evidence + Analysis

The poem's central irony crystallizes in the inscription on the pedestal: "My name is Ozymandias, King of Kings; / Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!" The command to "despair" — intended by Ozymandias to express the crushing superiority of his power — has been entirely inverted by time. Where the king meant for rivals to despair at his might, the reader despairs at the futility of human ambition itself. Shelley reinforces this inversion through the framing of the sonnet's octave and sestet: the octave establishes the physical scene (broken statue, "trunkless legs"), while the sestet delivers the inscription and then expands outward to the "lone and level sands" that "stretch far away." This spatial expansion — from the intimate description of a crumbling face to the vast emptiness of the desert — formally enacts the argument: political power, however enormous it felt, is dwarfed by geological time. The sands that surround the wreckage are not merely a setting; they are Shelley's final rebuttal to every tyrant's claim to permanence.


Sophistication Point

Shelley's critique is directed not merely at a long-dead Egyptian pharaoh but at the political climate of his own moment — an era of post-Napoleonic triumphalism and British imperial expansion. The poem's quiet confidence in time as the great leveler functions as a warning to contemporary powers who share Ozymandias's certainty in their own permanence. This historical dimension transforms a meditation on ruins into an act of political provocation.

Why This Scored 6/6
  • Thesis names specific techniques AND connects them to a complex, arguable claim about meaning
  • Body paragraph quotes specific lines, then explains the ironic mechanism, then connects to the poem's larger argument
  • Formal analysis (octave/sestet structure) is tied to meaning, not just labeled
  • Sophistication paragraph places the poem in historical context, revealing a dimension beyond the literal text

Prose FRQModel Thesis + Analysis Paragraph

Context: A passage from a 20th-century novel in which an adult narrator reflects on a childhood moment of social humiliation. The prompt asks how the author uses narrative techniques to develop the narrator's complex attitude toward the past.

Strong Thesis

Through the interplay of the child's naive perspective and the adult narrator's retrospective irony, free indirect discourse that collapses the distance between past and present self, and the passage's increasingly fragmented syntax at the moment of humiliation, the author reveals that memory is not a neutral record but an act of self-construction — and that the narrator's present identity remains structured around a shame he cannot quite admit to.


Analysis Paragraph — Free Indirect Discourse

The narrative's most revealing technique is its use of free indirect discourse, particularly in the passage's middle section, where the grammatical person becomes unstable: third-person description slides without warning into the child's interior voice, so that the narration simultaneously observes the boy from outside and speaks his thoughts from within. This formal instability — a narrator who cannot maintain distance from his childhood self — is itself the argument. The adult does not describe the childhood humiliation with the detached clarity of someone who has resolved it; he recreates it with the full emotional charge of someone still living inside it. The syntax becomes increasingly truncated as the scene reaches its crisis — complex subordinate clauses giving way to short declarative fragments — formally enacting the way shame short-circuits coherent thought and reduces consciousness to raw sensation. The formal breakdown at the moment of crisis is not stylistic decoration but psychological disclosure.

Literary Argument FRQ
~33% of FRQ score · 40 min suggested
FRQ 3 — What It Is

FRQ 3 gives you a thematic prompt ("Explore how a work of literature uses a character's relationship to their community to develop a complex theme") and asks you to write an argument using a work of literary merit you've studied. This is where your knowledge of major novels pays off. You choose the work — pick one you know deeply.

Literary Argument Thesis Structure

Weak Thesis (scores 0)

In The Great Gatsby, F. Scott Fitzgerald uses symbols to show that the American Dream is corrupt.


Strong Thesis (scores 1/1)

In The Great Gatsby, Fitzgerald uses Gatsby's compulsive return to the green light as an emblem of a peculiarly American pathology: the belief that desire itself — sustained past any possible satisfaction — is more valuable than its object, revealing a culture that has confused the pursuit of happiness with an addiction to longing.


Formula

In [Work], [Author] uses [specific literary element] to argue that [complex, arguable claim about human experience] — revealing [broader insight about the human condition or society].

Literary ArgumentModel Body Paragraph — The Great Gatsby

Prompt: Many works of literature explore a character who is defined by an illusion. Write an essay in which you analyze how a character's inability to relinquish an illusion shapes their fate and contributes to the work's thematic meaning.

Gatsby's ruin is inseparable from the particular quality of his imagination — what Nick calls his "extraordinary gift for hope." Fitzgerald frames this not as naive optimism but as a structural incapacity: Gatsby is constitutionally unable to allow the present to satisfy him, because satisfaction would require him to stop becoming and simply be. The green light at the end of Daisy's dock captures this condition with formal precision: described in the novel's final pages as something that "year by year recedes before us," the light reveals that Gatsby's desire was never really for Daisy herself — a woman he barely knows, repeatedly described through the language of voice and money rather than person. His desire is for the condition of desiring, the condition of pure becoming. Fitzgerald makes this explicit in the scene after Gatsby and Daisy are finally reunited: "there must have been moments... when Daisy tumbled short of his dreams — not through her own fault but because of the colossal vitality of his illusion." The illusion is not about Daisy; it is about Gatsby's need for an object that perpetually recedes. His death, therefore, is not simply a consequence of Daisy's cowardice or Tom's brutality — it is the logical conclusion of a life organized around an impossibility. To let the illusion go would be to become ordinary, and for Gatsby, ordinariness is the only true death.

Skill
Writing a Thesis That Scores

Must Be Arguable

A thesis is not a fact. "Hamlet is indecisive" = not a thesis. "Hamlet's indecision is itself a form of action — a refusal to participate in the cycle of violence his world demands" = thesis.

Must Name a "How"

Identify specific literary techniques. Not "the author uses language to create meaning" but "through the recurring imagery of confinement and the narrator's increasingly fragmented syntax..."

Must Go Beyond "What"

Don't describe the plot or paraphrase the poem. Analyze what it reveals about the human condition, society, psychology, or meaning.

The "Complication" Move

The best theses contain a tension: "While X appears to be about Y, Fitzgerald ultimately argues that Z." The complication signals intellectual depth and sets up the sophistication point.

⚡ Perfect Score Checklist

  • Thesis must be arguable and specific — not a plot summary, not a statement of theme without interpretation
  • Name the technique AND explain its effect — "the use of enjambment" earns nothing without "which creates momentum that mirrors the speaker's loss of control"
  • Volta is the most important moment in a sonnet — always find and analyze it
  • Tone ≠ mood: tone is the author's attitude; mood is the reader's feeling
  • For FRQ 3: choose the work you know BEST, not the most impressive-sounding one
  • Free indirect discourse = narrator and character voices blend; signals unreliability or intimacy
  • Dramatic monologue = there is always an ironic gap between what the speaker says and what the reader understands
  • Syntax analysis is underused and high-scoring — analyze sentence length, structure, and fragmentation
  • Sound devices in poetry serve meaning — always connect cacophony/euphony to emotional effect
  • Sophistication = complexity, not just fancy vocabulary. Show tension, complication, or alternative interpretation.
