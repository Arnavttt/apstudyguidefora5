Major States & Empires 1200–1450

Mongol Empire

Largest contiguous land empire. Genghis → Kublai Khan. Pax Mongolica enabled trade. Destroyed Baghdad (1258). Adopted local cultures (Yuan China, Ilkhanate Islam). Spread Black Death westward.

Mali Empire (West Africa)

Mansa Musa's hajj (1324) — displayed wealth, destabilized Mediterranean gold prices. Timbuktu = Islamic learning center. Built on gold-salt trade. Islam + indigenous traditions coexist.

Byzantine Empire

Eastern Roman legacy. Greek Orthodox Christianity. Preserved classical texts. Fell to Ottoman Turks 1453 — scholars flee to Europe (contributes to Renaissance).

Song China

Champa rice (increased food supply). Gunpowder, printing, compass. Maritime trade. Neo-Confucianism. Paper money. Population growth. Eventually conquered by Mongols (Yuan Dynasty 1271).

Delhi Sultanate / Abbasid Caliphate

Islamic scholarship, trade networks, art. House of Wisdom in Baghdad destroyed by Mongols. Islam spreads along trade routes — merchants → rulers convert populations.

Aztec (Mexica) & Inca Empires

Aztec: chinampas, tribute system, Tenochtitlan. Inca: mit'a labor system, quipus, road network, no writing. Both fall rapidly to Spanish (1521, 1532) — disease + internal divisions.

Period 2 · 1450–1750 · ~20% of exam
Age of Exploration, Gunpowder Empires & Global Trade

Columbian Exchange — Most Important Concept in Period 2

Columbian Exchange
  • Old World → Americas: Horses, cattle, pigs, sheep, wheat, rice, sugarcane, smallpox, measles, influenza → devastating disease mortality (up to 90% in some regions)
  • Americas → Old World: Potatoes, maize, tomatoes, cacao, tobacco, silver → population growth in Europe, economic transformation
  • Significance: Silver from Americas destabilizes Chinese and Spanish economies. Disease enables conquest. New foods transform diets globally.

Atlantic Slave Trade

Atlantic Slave Trade (c. 1500–1800s)
Triangular trade: Europe → Africa (manufactured goods) → Americas (enslaved people) → Europe (sugar, tobacco, cotton). ~12.5 million Africans transported; ~2 million died in Middle Passage. Created plantation economy in Americas. Devastated West African societies. Profits funded European industrialization. Coerced labor systems: encomienda, chattel slavery, indentured servitude.

Imperialism & Its Effects

Motives (MEN)

Markets and raw materials. European nationalism and competition. National pride + "civilizing mission" (Social Darwinism, racism). Also: strategic bases, missionary activity.

Scramble for Africa (1880s)

Berlin Conference (1884–85): Europeans divide Africa without African input. By 1914, only Ethiopia (Adwa 1896) and Liberia remain independent. Colonial economies produce raw materials for Europe.

Responses to Imperialism

Sepoy Mutiny (India, 1857): revolt against British rule → direct crown control. Boxer Rebellion (China, 1900). Meiji Restoration (Japan, 1868): industrialize to resist. Ethiopian victory at Adwa (1896).

Economic Effects

Plantation agriculture, cash crops (rubber, cotton, palm oil) replace food crops. Famines increase. Deindustrialization of colonized regions (India's textile industry destroyed). Demographic disruption.

Period 4 · 1900–Present · ~25% of exam
The 20th Century: Wars, Decolonization & Globalization

World Wars & Their Consequences

WWI (1914–1918)
MAIN causes: Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, Nationalism. Trench warfare, poison gas. ~20 million dead. Collapse of Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, German empires. Sykes-Picot divides Middle East (sets up future conflicts). Treaty of Versailles (humiliates Germany → seeds WWII). Influenza pandemic 1918.
WWII (1939–1945)
Rise of fascism (Great Depression, WWI humiliation). Holocaust — 6 million Jews + millions others. ~70–85 million dead. Atomic bombs on Japan. Decolonization accelerates. Cold War begins. United Nations founded. Marshall Plan rebuilds Europe. Bretton Woods = US dollar as world reserve currency.

Decolonization (1945–1970s)

India (1947)

Gandhi's nonviolent resistance. Partition into India + Pakistan. Massive violence during partition. Model for nonviolent movements globally.

Africa (1950s–70s)

"Wind of Change" — Britain/France grant independence. Pan-Africanism. Many new nations face artificial borders, Cold War proxy conflicts, economic dependency (neocolonialism).

Cold War Impact

Superpowers (US/USSR) compete for influence in newly independent states. Korea, Vietnam, Angola, Afghanistan become proxy wars. Non-Aligned Movement.

Globalization (1980s–present)

Fall of Soviet Union (1991). WTO, free trade agreements. China's economic rise. Internet + communication technology. Climate change as global challenge. Migration and refugee crises.

WHAP Themes
The 5 AP World Themes (SPICE-T)

S — Social

Class, gender, family, ethnic/racial groups, inequality. How societies organize themselves and relationships between groups.

P — Political

States, governments, empires, revolutions, nationalism. How power is organized, exercised, and contested.

I — Interactions

Trade, migration, warfare, diplomacy between civilizations. Environment-human interactions.

C — Culture

Belief systems, religion, philosophy, art, science. How ideas spread and change across cultures.

E — Economy

Agriculture, labor, trade, commerce, industrialization. Production, distribution, and exchange of goods.

T — Technology

Innovations in agriculture, military, communication, transportation. How technology drives historical change.

Exam Strategy
Essay Types
AP World FRQ Structure
  • DBQ (Document Based Question): 7 documents, analyze for argument, sourcing, contextualization, HAPP (Historical situation, Audience, Purpose, Point of view). Must use 6+ docs, source 3+, contextualize, and show complexity.
  • LEQ (Long Essay Question): Thesis + evidence for a comparative/continuity-change/causation prompt. Must have complex thesis showing relationships, not just listing facts.
  • SAQ (Short Answer Question): 3 parts (a, b, c). No thesis needed. Specific, precise answers. About 3–6 sentences each.

⚡ Perfect Score Checklist

  • Columbian Exchange = biggest turning point in Period 2 — disease, silver, foods all transform the world
  • Contextualization = the broader historical context BEFORE the prompt period (what led to this?)
  • Sourcing (HAPP): always consider who made the document, when, why, and for whom
  • Gunpowder empires all used new military technology to consolidate large territories
  • Silver from Americas → China → inflation → Ming weakness
  • Meiji Japan = successful modernization in response to imperialism; others failed
  • Complexity point: show multiple causes, diverse perspectives, or connect across time periods
  • Every LEQ thesis must have a line of reasoning — say HOW and WHY, not just what
