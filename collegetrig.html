4.2
The Unit Circle & Fundamental Identities
Foundation

📊The Unit Circle — Definition

Unit Circle: x² + y² = 1, centered at origin, radius = 1
  • A point P = (x, y) on the unit circle defines all 6 trig functions.
  • Since r = 1 and s = rθ, in the unit circle arc length = radian measure of the central angle.
FunctionUnit Circle DefinitionReciprocal FunctionRestriction
sin θycsc θ = 1/yy ≠ 0
cos θxsec θ = 1/xx ≠ 0
tan θy/xcot θ = x/yx ≠ 0, y ≠ 0

📊Fundamental Identities (4.2)

Reciprocal Identities
  • csc θ = 1/sin θ   |   sec θ = 1/cos θ   |   cot θ = 1/tan θ
Quotient Identities
  • tan θ = sin θ / cos θ   |   cot θ = cos θ / sin θ
Pythagorean Identities
  • sin²θ + cos²θ = 1
  • tan²θ + 1 = sec²θ (divide Pythagorean by cos²θ)
  • 1 + cot²θ = csc²θ (divide Pythagorean by sin²θ)
Even and Odd Functions
  • Even (cosine, secant): cos(−θ) = cos θ  |  sec(−θ) = sec θ
  • Odd (sine, cosecant, tangent, cotangent): sin(−θ) = −sin θ
Periodic Properties
  • sin(θ + 2π) = sin θ  |  cos(θ + 2π) = cos θ  →  Period = 2π
  • tan(θ + π) = tan θ  |  cot(θ + π) = cot θ  →  Period = π
Example (4.2): Given sin θ = 2/5 and cos θ = √21/5, find all six trig functions
1
sin θ = 2/5    csc θ = 5/2
2
cos θ = √21/5    sec θ = 5/√21 = 5√21/21
3
tan θ = sin/cos = (2/5)/(√21/5) = 2/√21 = 2√21/21
4
cot θ = √21/2

Right Triangle Trigonometry (SOHCAHTOA)
Unit 1

📊SOHCAHTOA — The 6 Trig Ratios

For a right triangle with angle θ (not the right angle), opposite side (opp), adjacent side (adj), hypotenuse (hyp):
  • SOH: sin θ = opp/hyp    csc θ = hyp/opp
  • CAH: cos θ = adj/hyp    sec θ = hyp/adj
  • TOA: tan θ = opp/adj    cot θ = adj/opp
Example (4.3): Right triangle with hyp = 13, one leg = 5, other leg = 12
1
sin θ = 5/13    csc θ = 13/5
2
cos θ = 12/13    sec θ = 13/12
3
tan θ = 5/12    cot θ = 12/5

📊Angles of Elevation & Depression (4.3/4.8)

Definitions
  • Angle of elevation: angle measured UPWARD from horizontal to the line of sight.
  • Angle of depression: angle measured DOWNWARD from horizontal to the line of sight.
  • These two angles are alternate interior angles — they are equal when the horizontal lines are parallel.
Example: A surveyor measures a 22° angle of elevation to the top of a building. Transit is 5 ft above ground, 300 ft from building. Find building height.
1
Draw: right triangle with adjacent = 300 ft, angle = 22°.
2
tan 22° = x/300  →  x = 300·tan 22° = 121.2 ft
3
Add instrument height: 121.2 + 5 = 126.2 ft
Example: Kite flies at 30 ft height with 65 ft string. Find the angle with the ground.
1
sin θ = opp/hyp = 30/65
2
θ = sin⁻¹(30/65) = 27.6°

📊Trig Functions of Any Angle

For any angle θ with terminal side passing through (x, y), r = √(x²+y²):
  • sin θ = y/r  |  cos θ = x/r  |  tan θ = y/x
  • csc θ = r/y  |  sec θ = r/x  |  cot θ = x/y
Example (4.4): P = (−3, −4) on terminal side of θ
1
r = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5
2
sin θ = −4/5  |  cos θ = −3/5  |  tan θ = 4/3
3
csc θ = −5/4  |  sec θ = −5/3  |  cot θ = 3/4

📊Reference Angles

Reference Angle θ' = the acute angle formed between the terminal side of θ and the x-axis
  • Q I: θ' = θ
  • Q II: θ' = 180° − θ (or π − θ)
  • Q III: θ' = θ − 180° (or θ − π)
  • Q IV: θ' = 360° − θ (or 2π − θ)
  • For θ > 360° or θ < −360°: first find coterminal angle in [0°, 360°), then find reference angle.
Example: Find exact value of sin 300° using reference angles
1
300° is in Q IV (sin is negative there)
2
θ' = 360° − 300° = 60°
3
sin 60° = √3/2
4
sin 300° = −√3/2 (negative in Q IV)

📊y = A sin(Bx + C) + D — All Four Parameters

ParameterMeaningFormulaEffect
AAmplitude|A|Vertical stretch. If A < 0: reflection over x-axis.
BFrequency—Period = 2π/|B|. Larger B → shorter period.

Properties of sin x (parent function)
  • Domain: (−∞, ∞)  |  Range: [−1, 1]  |  Period: 2π  |  Odd function (origin symmetry)
Properties of cos x (parent function)
  • Domain: (−∞, ∞)  |  Range: [−1, 1]  |  Period: 2π  |  Even function (y-axis symmetry)
Example (4.5): Determine amplitude, period, phase shift for y = 4 sin(2x − π)
1
Amplitude: |A| = 4
2
Period: 2π/|B| = 2π/2 = π
3
Phase shift: C/B = π/2 to the RIGHT (since form is 2x − π = 2(x − π/2))
4
5 key points over one period starting at x = π/2: (π/2, 0), (3π/4, 4), (π, 0), (5π/4, −4), (3π/2, 0)
Example (4.5 Day 2): Tidal Model — depth varies from 5 ft (low) to 13 ft (high), low tide at 4 AM, high tide at 10 AM
1
Amplitude: A = (max − min)/2 = (13 − 5)/2 = 4
2
Midline (vertical shift): D = (max + min)/2 = 9
3
Period: 12 hours (low to high = half period = 6 hrs) → B = 2π/12 = π/6
4
Phase shift: low tide at 4 AM → shift right by 4 → C/B = 4, so C = 4·(π/6) = 2π/3... adjust for model
5
y = 4 sin(π/6 · x − 2π/3) + 9

📊Graphing y = A tan(Bx + C) — Steps from 4.6

Steps for graphing y = A tan(Bx + C)
1
Find asymptotes: Set Bx + C = −π/2 and Bx + C = π/2 and solve.
2
Find x-intercept: midway between the two asymptotes.
3
Find key points: ¼ way between each asymptote and the x-intercept — these have y-coordinates of −A and +A respectively.
4
Sketch one full period using these 3 points, then extend as needed.
Example (4.6): y = 2 tan(x/2) on [−π, 3π]
  • Period = π/|B| = π/(1/2) = 2π
  • Asymptotes: x/2 = ±π/2 → x = ±π
  • Next asymptote at x = 3π. x-intercept at x = π.
  • Key points: (0, 0), (π/2, 2), (3π/2, −2) (for next period)
Graphing sec and csc using sin/cos
  • Strategy: First sketch the sin or cos parent curve in light pencil.
  • sec x = 1/cos x: draw U-shapes opening UP where cos = 1, and DOWN-facing U's where cos = −1.
  • Asymptotes occur where sin x = 0 (for csc) or cos x = 0 (for sec).
  • The trig curve TOUCHES the peaks and troughs; the sec/csc curves are TANGENT there.

Inverse Trigonometric Functions
Unit 2

📊The Three Inverse Trig Functions — Restricted Domains & Ranges

Inverse FunctionInput DomainOutput RangeNotes
y = sin⁻¹(x) = arcsin(x)[−1, 1][−π/2, π/2]Must restrict sin to [−π/2, π/2] for 1-to-1
y = cos⁻¹(x) = arccos(x)[−1, 1][0, π]Must restrict cos to [0, π] for 1-to-1
y = tan⁻¹(x) = arctan(x)All reals (−∞, ∞)(−π/2, π/2)Must restrict tan to (−π/2, π/2)
Inverse Properties — When They Cancel
  • sin(sin⁻¹ x) = x  for x ∈ [−1, 1]
  • sin⁻¹(sin x) = x  ONLY for x ∈ [−π/2, π/2] — be careful with domain!
  • cos(cos⁻¹ x) = x  for x ∈ [−1, 1]
  • cos⁻¹(cos x) = x  ONLY for x ∈ [0, π]
  • tan(tan⁻¹ x) = x  for all x
  • tan⁻¹(tan x) = x  ONLY for x ∈ (−π/2, π/2)
Domain Trap (4.7)
  • sin⁻¹(sin(π/4)) = π/4 ✓ (π/4 is in [−π/2, π/2])
  • sin⁻¹(sin(5π/4)) ≠ 5π/4 ✗ (5π/4 is NOT in [−π/2, π/2])
  • sin(5π/4) = −√2/2, so sin⁻¹(sin(5π/4)) = sin⁻¹(−√2/2) = −π/4

Verifying Trigonometric Identities
Unit 3

📊Rules for Verifying Identities

The Golden Rules
  • Work only ONE side — usually the more complex side. Transform it to look like the other side.
  • Never move terms across the = sign (no cross-multiplication while verifying).
  • Never multiply both sides by the same quantity.
  • Common strategies: factor, substitute a Pythagorean identity, convert everything to sin/cos, find common denominators, multiply by conjugate.
Example (5.1): Verify sec x · cot x = csc x
1
Work left side only.
2
sec x · cot x = (1/cos x) · (cos x/sin x)
3
= 1/sin x
4
= csc x ✓
Example (5.1): Verify sin x · tan x + cos x = sec x
1
Left side: sin x · (sin x/cos x) + cos x
2
= sin²x/cos x + cos x
3
= sin²x/cos x + cos²x/cos x (common denominator)
4
= (sin²x + cos²x)/cos x
5
= 1/cos x = sec x ✓
Example (5.1): Verify cos x/(1 + sin x) + (1 + sin x)/cos x = 2 sec x
1
Left side: combine fractions with LCD = cos x(1 + sin x).
2
= [cos²x + (1 + sin x)²] / [cos x(1 + sin x)]
3
= [cos²x + 1 + 2 sin x + sin²x] / [cos x(1 + sin x)]
4
= [1 + 1 + 2 sin x] / [cos x(1 + sin x)] (since cos²x + sin²x = 1)
5
= 2(1 + sin x) / [cos x(1 + sin x)]
6
= 2/cos x = 2 sec x ✓
📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. When verifying a trig identity, you should:
  • A) Cross-multiply to clear fractions
  • B) Work both sides simultaneously toward each other
  • C) Work only ONE side, transforming it to equal the other side
  • D) Substitute a specific value to check
C is correct. Verifying an identity means showing it is true for ALL values — working one side only. Cross-multiplying assumes the equation is true (which is what you're trying to prove), making the logic circular.

📊Half-Angle Formulas

Half-Angle Identities (sign determined by quadrant of α/2)
  • sin(α/2) = ±√((1 − cos α)/2)
  • cos(α/2) = ±√((1 + cos α)/2)
  • tan(α/2) = (1 − cos α)/sin α = sin α/(1 + cos α)
  • The ± is determined by the quadrant of the angle α/2, not α.

1

2
cos 112.5° is in Q II (90° to 180°) → cosine is NEGATIVE
3
cos(225°/2) = −√((1 + cos 225°)/2)
4
cos 225° = −√2/2
5
= −√((1 − √2/2)/2) = −√((2 − √2)/4) = −√(2 − √2)/2

Solving Trigonometric Equations
Unit 3

📊Types of Trig Equations (from 5.5)

General Strategy
  • 1. Solve for trig function values on [0, 2π]
  • 2. Since period is 2π (or π for tan/cot), general solution adds + 2kπ (or + kπ).
  • 3. Always check for extraneous solutions when squaring or using identities.
Example: Linear — Solve 5 sin θ − 3 sin θ = √3 on [0, 2π]
1
2 sin θ = √3 → sin θ = √3/2
2
sin is positive in Q I and Q II
3
θ = π/3 (Q I) and θ = 2π/3 (Q II)
4
General solution: θ = π/3 + 2kπ or θ = 2π/3 + 2kπ
Example: Quadratic — Solve 2cos²θ + cos θ − 1 = 0 on [0, 2π]
1
Factor: (2cos θ − 1)(cos θ + 1) = 0
2
cos θ = 1/2 → θ = π/3, 5π/3
3
cos θ = −1 → θ = π
4
Solutions: π/3, π, 5π/3
Example: Using Identities — Solve 2sin²x − cos x − 1 = 0 on [0, 2π]
1
Replace sin²x with 1 − cos²x:
2
2(1 − cos²x) − cos x − 1 = 0
3
−2cos²x − cos x + 1 = 0 → 2cos²x + cos x − 1 = 0
4
(2cos x − 1)(cos x + 1) = 0
5
cos x = 1/2 → x = π/3, 5π/3  |  cos x = −1 → x = π
Example: Factoring — Solve sin 2x = sin x on [0, 2π]
1
Use double-angle: 2 sin x cos x = sin x
2
2 sin x cos x − sin x = 0
3
sin x(2 cos x − 1) = 0
4
sin x = 0 → x = 0, π  |  cos x = 1/2 → x = π/3, 5π/3
5
Solutions: 0, π/3, π, 5π/3

  • B) θ = π/2, 3π/2 only
  • C) θ = 0, π, π/4, 5π/4
  • D) θ = 0, π (from tan θ(cos θ − 1) = 0, where cos θ = 1)
D is correct. Factor: tan θ(cos θ − 1) = 0. tan θ = 0 → θ = 0, π. cos θ = 1 → θ = 0. Solutions: θ = 0, π. (Note: tan θ = sin θ/cos θ is undefined where cos θ = 0, so π/2 and 3π/2 are excluded from the domain.)

Law of Sines: a/sin A = b/sin B = c/sin C
  • Or equivalently: sin A/a = sin B/b = sin C/c
  • Always: Find the third angle first using A + B + C = 180°.
  • If the law gives sin B > 1, no triangle exists.
Example (6.1 AAS): A = 30°, B = 45°, a = 32 ft
1
Find C: C = 180° − 30° − 45° = 105°
2
b/sin B = a/sin A → b = 32·sin 45°/sin 30° = 32·(√2/2)/(1/2) = 45.3 ft
3
c = 32·sin 105°/sin 30° = 61.8 ft

📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. In the SSA ambiguous case, if sin B > 1, then:
  • A) Two triangles exist
  • B) One right triangle exists
  • C) No triangle exists — impossible
  • D) The triangle is equilateral
C: No triangle. The sine function only outputs values in [−1, 1]. If sin B > 1, the calculation is impossible — the given side is too short to reach the opposite side.
2. For triangle ABC with A = 30°, a = 12, b = 5 (SSA), the number of triangles is:
  • A) 0
  • B) 1
  • C) 2
  • D) Cannot be determined
B: 1 triangle. sin B = 5·sin30°/12 = 2.5/12 = 0.2083. B ≈ 12° or B ≈ 168°. Check B = 168°: A + B = 30° + 168° = 198° > 180° — impossible. Only one triangle exists.
Perfect Score Checklist
  • Unit circle: sin θ = y, cos θ = x, tan θ = y/x on unit circle of radius 1
  • Pythagorean identities: sin²+cos²=1; tan²+1=sec²; 1+cot²=csc²
  • ASTC (CAST rule): Q1 All, Q2 Sin, Q3 Tan, Q4 Cos positive
  • Reference angle: always positive, always acute, always with the x-axis
  • y = A sin(Bx+C)+D: Amplitude=|A|, Period=2π/|B|, Phase shift=C/B, Midline=D
  • Inverse trig ranges: arcsin [−π/2,π/2]; arccos [0,π]; arctan (−π/2,π/2)
  • Verifying identities: work ONE side only — never cross the equal sign
  • sin(A+B) = sinA cosB + cosA sinB (the '+' version keeps all positives)
  • cos(A+B) = cosA cosB − sinA sinB (note the MINUS for +)
  • sin 2θ = 2 sinθ cosθ; cos 2θ has THREE equivalent forms — pick the right one
  • Half-angle sign: determined by quadrant of α/2, not α
  • Solve trig equations: isolate the function, find reference angle, apply ASTC
  • Quadratic trig equations: factor or substitute Pythagorean identity to reduce
  • Law of Sines: use for AAS, ASA, SSA
  • SSA ambiguous case: if sin B > 1 → no triangle; if sin B < 1 → check for 1 or 2 triangles
