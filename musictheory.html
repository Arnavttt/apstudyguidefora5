Unit 1 · Foundation
Music Notation Fundamentals

Staff, Clefs & Ledger Lines

Treble Clef (G clef)

Curls around G4 (second line from bottom). Lines (low to high): E G B D F — "Every Good Boy Does Fine." Spaces: F A C E (face). Used for higher-pitched instruments, right hand piano.

Bass Clef (F clef)

Dots bracket F3 (fourth line from bottom). Lines: G B D F A — "Good Boys Do Fine Always." Spaces: A C E G — "All Cars Eat Gas." Left hand piano, low strings, tuba.

Alto/Tenor Clef (C clef)

C clef middle C on different lines. Alto clef: middle C on middle (3rd) line — viola. Tenor clef: middle C on 4th line — cello, trombone (upper range). Used to avoid excessive ledger lines.

Accidentals

Sharp (♯): raises pitch one half step. Flat (♭): lowers one half step. Natural (♮): cancels previous accidental. Double sharp (𝄪): raises two half steps. Double flat (𝄫): lowers two half steps. Apply for entire measure unless canceled.

Scales & Key Signatures

Major & Minor Scales — Patterns

MAJOR SCALE: W W H W W W H (whole-half step pattern)
C major: C D E F G A B C (all white keys)
// Every major scale = same interval pattern starting from different pitch

NATURAL MINOR: W H W W H W W
A natural minor: A B C D E F G A (all white keys)
// Relative minor = 6th scale degree of major scale

HARMONIC MINOR: natural minor with raised 7th
A harmonic minor: A B C D E F G♯ A
// Creates leading tone → stronger pull to tonic. V7 chord usable.

MELODIC MINOR: ascending: raised 6th + 7th; descending: natural minor
A melodic minor: A B C D E F♯ G♯ A (asc) / A G F E D C B A (desc)

Key Signature Tricks
  • Sharps order: F C G D A E B — "Father Charles Goes Down And Ends Battle"
  • Flats order: B E A D G C F — reverse of sharps (or: "Battle Ends And Down Goes Charles' Father")
  • Major key with sharps: name of key = one half step above last sharp
  • Major key with flats: name of key = second-to-last flat
  • Relative minor: down a minor 3rd (or up a major 6th) from major tonic = relative minor tonic
Unit 3 · Critical Skill
Intervals

Interval Quality & Size

SIZE: count every letter name (including both endpoints)
C→E = 3rd (C D E), C→G = 5th (C D E F G)

MAJOR SCALE INTERVALS (from tonic):
2nd: Major 2nd (M2 = 2 half steps)
3rd: Major 3rd (M3 = 4 half steps)
4th: Perfect 4th (P4 = 5 half steps)
5th: Perfect 5th (P5 = 7 half steps)
6th: Major 6th (M6 = 9 half steps)
7th: Major 7th (M7 = 11 half steps)
8th: Perfect Octave (P8 = 12 half steps)

QUALITY RULES:
Perfect − 1 hs = diminished | Perfect + 1 hs = augmented
Major − 1 hs = minor | Minor − 1 hs = diminished | Major + 1 hs = augmented

INVERSIONS:
Interval + its inversion = 9. Quality: P↔P, M↔m, d↔A

Consonance vs. Dissonance

Consonant: Unison, octave, P5, P4 (in context), M/m 3rd, M/m 6th. Dissonant: M/m 2nd, M/m 7th, tritone (d5/A4 = devil's interval). Dissonances need resolution.

Tritone

Augmented 4th = diminished 5th = 6 half steps = exactly half an octave. Most dissonant interval. Divides octave in half. Creates tension that demands resolution. Found in dominant 7th chord (F and B in G7 in C major).

Unit 4 · Core Harmony
Chords & Inversions

Triads & Seventh Chords

TRIAD TYPES:
Major (M): M3 + m3 from root (e.g., C E G)
Minor (m): m3 + M3 from root (e.g., C E♭ G)
Diminished (d): m3 + m3 (e.g., C E♭ G♭)
Augmented (A): M3 + M3 (e.g., C E G♯)

SEVENTH CHORD TYPES:
Major 7th (MM7): M triad + M7 (e.g., C E G B)
Dominant 7th (Mm7): M triad + m7 (e.g., G B D F — V7 in C major)
Minor 7th (mm7): m triad + m7
Half-dim 7th (dm7 / ø7): d triad + m7
Fully dim 7th (dd7 / °7): d triad + d7 (all m3s stacked)

INVERSIONS:
Root position: root on bottom (5/3 figured bass = omit)
First inversion: 3rd on bottom (6/3 figured bass = "6")
Second inversion: 5th on bottom (6/4 figured bass)
For 7th chords: third inversion = 7th on bottom (4/2 or "2")

Unit 5 · Written Composition
Voice Leading (SATB)
SATB Voice Ranges
  • Soprano: B3–G5
  • Alto: G3–D5
  • Tenor: C3–G4
  • Bass: E2–C4
Voice Leading Rules — MEMORIZE THESE
  • No parallel 5ths or octaves — voices cannot move in the same direction to a P5 or P8
  • No direct (hidden) 5ths or octaves in outer voices (soprano/bass) if soprano leaps
  • Resolve tendency tones: Leading tone (7̂) must resolve up to tonic (8̂). Chordal 7th must resolve down by step.
  • Avoid voice crossing — voices should maintain their relative positions
  • Prefer contrary motion — voices moving in opposite directions is strongest
  • Double the root in root position chords (usually). In first inversion, usually double the soprano or bass note.
  • Avoid doubling the 7th — it would force awkward resolution
  • Bass moves by step or leap; inner voices prefer stepwise motion
  • Avoid large leaps in inner voices — especially augmented intervals
Unit 1 · Fundamentals
Rhythm & Meter
NOTE VALUES:
Whole → Half → Quarter → Eighth → Sixteenth → Thirty-second
Each = half the value of previous. Dotted note = 1.5× its value.

SIMPLE METER: top # = beats per measure; bottom = beat unit
4/4 = 4 quarter-note beats. 3/4 = 3 quarter-note beats. 2/4 = 2 quarter-note beats.
Simple meter: each beat divides into 2.

COMPOUND METER: each beat divides into 3
6/8 = 2 dotted-quarter beats (felt in 2, counted 1-2-3 4-5-6)
9/8 = 3 dotted-quarter beats. 12/8 = 4 dotted-quarter beats.

SYNCOPATION: accent on normally weak beat or off-beat
HEMIOLA: temporary 3-against-2 metric displacement

Common Chord Progressions

FUNCTIONAL PROGRESSION:
Tonic → Predominant → Dominant → Tonic
I → IV or ii → V → I (strongest cadential motion)

COMMON PROGRESSIONS:
I − V − vi − IV (pop music backbone: C G Am F)
I − IV − V − I (standard blues/folk)
ii − V − I (jazz standard progression)
I − vi − IV − V (50s doo-wop progression)

SECONDARY DOMINANTS:
V/V = dominant of the dominant (e.g., D major chord in C major)
Creates temporary tonicization — brief pivot to new key center
Unit 6 · Analysis
Musical Form & Analysis

Binary Form (AB)

Two sections, each often repeated (||: A :||: B :||). Simple binary: B grows from A material. Rounded binary: B ends with return of A material. Common in Baroque dances (minuets, gavottes).

Ternary Form (ABA)

Three sections — B contrasts with A, then A returns. A is complete, B contrasts (different key, mood, material). Common in da capo arias, marches, scherzo movements.

Sonata Form

Exposition (P theme - tonic; T; S theme - dominant; coda), Development (fragmentation, modulation, tension), Recapitulation (both themes in tonic). Hallmark of Classical symphony movements.

Rondo Form

ABACADA... Refrain (A) alternates with contrasting episodes. Common in final movements (rondeau). Cheerful, dance-like character. Episodes in related keys.

Theme & Variations

Theme stated, then repeated with changes (rhythm, harmony, texture, key, mode). Beethoven Op. 109, Copland Appalachian Spring, Handel Harmonious Blacksmith.

Phrase Structure

Period: antecedent phrase (ends HC) + consequent phrase (ends PAC). Parallel period: both phrases begin with same material. Contrasting period: consequent differs. Phrase = 4–8 measures.

Aural Skills
AP Music Theory Exam Structure
  • Section I Written: Multiple Choice (45 questions, 45 min) + MCQ Aural (30 questions, 45 min)
  • Section II Written: FRQ — voice leading, composition, analysis, harmonization
  • Section II Aural: Melodic dictation, harmonic dictation, sight singing

Melodic Dictation

Listen for: starting pitch, rhythmic pattern, stepwise vs. leap, interval quality. Process: notate rhythm first, then pitches. Use scale degree numbers to track tonal context. Repeat listenings: big picture → details → check.

Harmonic Dictation

Listen for: bass movement (steps vs. leaps = inversions), cadences (PAC, HC, deceptive). Identify Roman numerals and inversions. Common patterns: I-IV-V-I, ii-V-I, deceptive V-vi. Bass line is often most distinctive voice.

Sight Singing Tips

Use solfège (do re mi...) or scale degrees (1 2 3...). Establish tonal center first. Scan for difficult intervals, plan them ahead. Subdivide complex rhythms. Maintain steady tempo even if pitch uncertain. Sing confidently — hesitation loses points.

Interval Recognition

Associate each interval with a familiar melody: m2 = Jaws, M2 = Happy Birthday (2nd note), m3 = Greensleeves, M3 = When the Saints, P4 = Wedding March, P5 = Star Wars, m6 = The Entertainer, M6 = My Bonnie, m7 = Star Trek, M7 = Take On Me.

⚡ Perfect Score Checklist

  • No parallel 5ths or octaves — most common voice-leading error on exam
  • Leading tone (7̂) resolves UP to tonic — never double the leading tone
  • Chordal 7th resolves DOWN by step
  • V7 in minor requires harmonic minor — raise the 7th scale degree
  • PAC: root position V or V7 → root position I, soprano ends on 1̂
  • 6/4 chords are unstable: I⁶⁄₄ is a dissonant chord — use only as cadential (resolving to V)
  • Sharps order: F C G D A E B. Flats: reverse of that.
  • Relative minor = same key signature as major, starts on 6̂
  • Harmonic minor raises 7̂; melodic minor raises 6̂ and 7̂ ascending only
  • In compound meter, count in dotted notes: 6/8 = 2 beats, each a dotted quarter
