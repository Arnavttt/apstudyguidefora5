Physics 1 · Unit 1
Kinematics
~12–18% of Physics 1

Big 4 Kinematic Equations constant acceleration only

THE BIG 4 (constant acceleration):
v = v₀ + at // no displacement
x = x₀ + v₀t + ½at² // no final velocity
v² = v₀² + 2a(x − x₀) // no time
x = x₀ + ½(v₀ + v)t // no acceleration

VARIABLE DEFINITIONS:
v₀ = initial velocity · v = final velocity · a = acceleration · t = time · x = displacement

Projectile Motion

KEY PRINCIPLE: horizontal and vertical motions are INDEPENDENT

Horizontal: aₓ = 0 → x = v₀ₓt (constant velocity)
Vertical: aᵧ = −g = −9.8 m/s² (or −10 m/s² for estimates)

At maximum height: vᵧ = 0
Range (level ground): R = v₀² sin(2θ)/g
// 45° gives maximum range; angles that add to 90° give same range
⚡ Exam Trap — Graphs
  • Slope of x-t graph = velocity; slope of v-t graph = acceleration
  • Area under v-t graph = displacement (not distance!)
  • Object at rest: x-t flat. Moving at constant v: x-t straight line.
  • Negative acceleration ≠ slowing down (could be speeding up in negative direction)
Physics 1 · Unit 2
Newton's Laws & Forces
~20–25% of Physics 1

Newton's Three Laws

1st Law — Inertia

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion unless acted on by a net external force. ΣF = 0 → a = 0

2nd Law — F = ma

ΣF = ma

Net force equals mass × acceleration. Always draw FBD first. Sum forces in each direction separately.

3rd Law — Action-Reaction

Forces come in equal and opposite pairs acting on DIFFERENT objects. FAB = −FBA. They don't cancel (different objects).

Free Body Diagrams — Exam Requirement

FBD Rules — Always Do This First
  • Draw object as a dot or box. Draw every force as an arrow FROM the object.
  • Label each force (N, W, T, fₖ, etc.). Include direction.
  • For inclined planes: use tilted axes (x parallel to ramp, y perpendicular).
  • Decompose forces: Wₓ = mg sin θ (along ramp), Wᵧ = mg cos θ (perpendicular to ramp).
  • AP FRQ explicitly states: "draw a free-body diagram" — you must draw one to get those points.
Physics 1 · Unit 3
Work, Energy & Power
~15–20% of Physics 1

Work

W = F·d·cos θ

θ = angle between F and displacement. Work is zero if F ⊥ displacement (like normal force, circular motion).

Kinetic Energy

KE = ½mv²

Work-Energy Theorem: W_net = ΔKE = KE_f − KE_i

Gravitational PE

ΔPEg = mgh

h = height above reference point (choose any). Only change in PE matters.

Spring PE

PEs = ½kx²

x = displacement from equilibrium. k = spring constant.

Power

P = W/t = Fv·cos θ

Units: watts (W) = J/s. Average power: P = ΔE/Δt

Conservation of Energy

ΔKE + ΔPE + ΔE_therm = W_ext

In isolated system with no friction: KE + PE = constant. Friction converts mechanical energy to thermal energy.

Physics 1 · Unit 4
Momentum & Impulse
~15–18% of Physics 1

Momentum

p = mv

Vector quantity. Units: kg·m/s. Direction = direction of velocity.

Impulse

J = FΔt = Δp

Impulse equals change in momentum. Area under F-t graph = impulse.

Conservation of Momentum

p_before = p_after (no ext force)

Always conserved in collisions. Apply in vector form.

Elastic Collision

KE conserved AND momentum conserved

Bouncing without energy loss. Rare in real life.

Inelastic Collision

Momentum conserved. KE NOT conserved.

Objects stick together: perfectly inelastic. KE lost → thermal/sound/deformation.

Center of Mass

x_cm = Σ(mᵢxᵢ) / Σmᵢ

Net external force on system = total mass × a_cm

⚡ Common Mistake — Momentum is a Vector

In 2D collisions or when objects move in opposite directions, momentum can cancel. Always define a positive direction and assign signs. Don't just sum magnitudes.

Physics 1 · Unit 5–6
Circular Motion & Rotation
~12–15% of Physics 1

Centripetal Acceleration

aᶜ = v²/r

Direction: always toward center of circle. Centripetal force is the NET force directed inward.

Centripetal Force

Fc = mv²/r = mω²r

Not a new force — it IS the net inward force (gravity, tension, friction, etc.)

Torque

τ = rF sin θ = rF⊥

r = distance from pivot. F sin θ = perpendicular component of force. Causes angular acceleration.

Rotational Equilibrium

Στ = 0 (no net torque)

Clockwise torques = counterclockwise torques. Used for lever problems.

Angular Momentum

L = Iω = mvr (point mass)

Conserved when net torque = 0. Figure skater spins faster when arms pulled in: I↓, ω↑

Moment of Inertia

Point: I = mr²Rod (center): I = (1/12)mL²Solid sphere: I = (2/5)mr²
Physics 1 · Unit 10
Simple Harmonic Motion, Waves & Sound
~12–15% of Physics 1

Wave Speed

v = fλ

v = speed (m/s), f = frequency (Hz), λ = wavelength (m). Speed depends on medium, not source.

Period & Frequency

T = 1/fPendulum: T = 2π√(L/g)Spring: T = 2π√(m/k)

Standing Waves — String

λₙ = 2L/n (n = 1,2,3...)fₙ = nv/(2L)

n=1: fundamental. Nodes at fixed ends.

Standing Waves — Open Pipe

λₙ = 2L/n (all harmonics)

Antinodes at open ends. Same formula as string.

Standing Waves — Closed Pipe

λₙ = 4L/n (n = 1,3,5 only)

Node at closed end, antinode at open. ODD harmonics only.

Doppler Effect

Observed f increases when source approaches

Source moves toward you: wavelengths compress → higher pitch. Moving away: lower pitch.

Physics 1 · Unit 7
Electric Charge & Force
~5–10% of Physics 1

Coulomb's Law

F = kq₁q₂/r²

k = 9×10⁹ N·m²/C². Like charges repel, opposite attract. Force direction along line joining charges.

Conservation of Charge

Total charge of an isolated system is conserved. Charge is quantized: q = ne where e = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C

Physics 2 · Unit 1
Fluids
~15–18% of Physics 2

Density

ρ = m/V

Units: kg/m³. Water: ρ = 1000 kg/m³. Density determines if object floats.

Pressure

P = F/A · P = P₀ + ρgh

Pressure increases with depth. Pascal's Law: pressure applied at one point transmits equally throughout.

Buoyancy — Archimedes

Fb = ρ_fluid · V_submerged · g

Buoyant force = weight of fluid displaced. Object floats when Fb ≥ Fg.

Continuity Equation

A₁v₁ = A₂v₂

For incompressible fluid. Narrow pipe → faster fluid (conservation of mass).

Bernoulli's Equation

P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant

Fast fluid → lower pressure. Explains lift on airfoil, curveball. Incompressible, non-viscous flow.

Physics 2 · Unit 2
Thermodynamics
~12–15% of Physics 2

Ideal Gas Law

PV = nRT

n = moles, R = 8.314 J/(mol·K), T in Kelvin. Also: PV = NkT (k = Boltzmann constant)

First Law of Thermo

ΔU = Q − W

Q = heat added to system (+). W = work done BY system (+). ΔU = change in internal energy.

Thermodynamic Processes

Isothermal: ΔT=0, ΔU=0, Q=WAdiabatic: Q=0, ΔU=−WIsobaric: ΔP=0, W=PΔVIsochoric: ΔV=0, W=0

Second Law & Entropy

Heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold. Entropy of an isolated system increases (or stays same). No perfectly efficient engine.

Efficiency

e = W_out/Q_in = 1 − Q_cold/Q_hotCarnot: e_max = 1 − T_cold/T_hot
Physics 2 · Unit 3
Electric Fields, Potential & Magnetism
~25–30% of Physics 2

Electric Field

E = kq/r² (point charge)F = qE

Direction: away from +, toward −. E points from high to low potential.

Electric Potential

V = kq/r (point charge)ΔUE = qΔV

Scalar quantity. Equipotential lines ⊥ electric field lines.

Capacitors

C = Q/V · U = ½CV²Parallel plates: C = ε₀A/d

Series: 1/C = Σ1/Cᵢ. Parallel: C = ΣCᵢ

Magnetic Force

F = qv × B (magnitude: qvB sinθ)F = IL × B (on current)

Use right-hand rule. F ⊥ both v and B → circular motion.

Magnetic Field Sources

Long wire: B = μ₀I/(2πr)

Right-hand rule: curl fingers in direction of current → B field direction wraps around.

Electromagnetic Induction

ε = −ΔΦ_B/Δt (Faraday)Φ_B = BA cos θ

Changing B flux induces EMF. Lenz's Law: induced current opposes change in flux.

Physics 2 · Unit 4
DC Circuits
~15–20% of Physics 2

Ohm's Law

V = IR

V = voltage (volts), I = current (amps), R = resistance (ohms). Only valid for ohmic resistors.

Power

P = IV = I²R = V²/R

Series Circuit

R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃Same current everywhereV = V₁ + V₂ + V₃

Parallel Circuit

1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂Same voltage across eachI = I₁ + I₂ + I₃

Kirchhoff's Laws

Junction rule: ΣI_in = ΣI_outLoop rule: ΣV = 0 around loop

Capacitors in Circuits

Charging: current decreases → 0

Fully charged capacitor: no current flows through it. Acts like open switch in steady state.

⚡ Parallel Resistors — Total R is Less Than Smallest

Adding a resistor in parallel always DECREASES total resistance. In parallel, voltage is same across each; current splits. In series, current is same; voltage splits.

Physics 2 · Unit 6
Geometric & Physical Optics
~12–15% of Physics 2

Snell's Law

n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂

n = index of refraction. Light bends toward normal entering denser medium. Critical angle: sin θᶜ = n₂/n₁

Mirror / Lens Equation

1/f = 1/dₒ + 1/dᵢm = −dᵢ/dₒ = hᵢ/hₒ

f > 0: converging (concave mirror, convex lens). f < 0: diverging.

Double-Slit Interference

Bright: d sin θ = mλ (m=0,±1,±2...)Dark: d sin θ = (m+½)λ

Single-Slit Diffraction

Dark: a sin θ = mλ (m=±1,±2...)

a = slit width. Wider central maximum with narrower slit.

Physics 2 · Unit 7
Modern Physics
~10–15% of Physics 2

Photoelectric Effect

KE_max = hf − φ

h = 6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·s. φ = work function. No electrons below threshold frequency. Einstein won Nobel Prize for this.

Photon Energy

E = hf = hc/λ

c = 3×10⁸ m/s. Higher frequency = higher energy photon.

de Broglie Wavelength

λ = h/p = h/(mv)

Every particle has a wavelength. Explains electron diffraction and atomic orbitals.

Radioactive Decay

N(t) = N₀ e^(−λt)t₁/₂ = ln(2)/λ = 0.693/λ

Alpha (α): loses 4 mass, 2 charge. Beta (β⁻): neutron → proton + electron. Gamma (γ): no mass/charge change.

Mass-Energy Equivalence

E = mc²

Nuclear reactions convert tiny mass → enormous energy. Binding energy holds nucleus together.

MCQ Practice
Q1Kinematics · Graphs

A car accelerates from rest at 3 m/s² for 4 seconds, then moves at constant speed for 3 seconds. What is the total displacement?

  • A) 36 m
  • B) 48 m
  • C) 60 m
  • D) 72 m
Answer: C) 60 m
Phase 1 (accelerating): x = ½at² = ½(3)(16) = 24 m. Final speed = (3)(4) = 12 m/s.
Phase 2 (constant): x = vt = 12(3) = 36 m. Total = 24 + 36 = 60 m.
Q2Energy · Conservation

A 2 kg ball is released from rest at height h = 5 m. Ignoring friction, what is its speed at the bottom?

  • A) 5 m/s
  • B) 10 m/s
  • C) 7 m/s
  • D) 14 m/s
Answer: B) 10 m/s
Conservation of energy: mgh = ½mv². Mass cancels: v = √(2gh) = √(2×10×5) = √100 = 10 m/s.
Q3Circuits (P2)

Two resistors, 4Ω and 12Ω, are connected in parallel. What is the equivalent resistance?

  • A) 16 Ω
  • B) 8 Ω
  • C) 3 Ω
  • D) 6 Ω
Answer: C) 3 Ω
1/R = 1/4 + 1/12 = 3/12 + 1/12 = 4/12 → R = 3 Ω. Parallel resistance is always less than the smallest resistor.

Physics FRQ Strategy
  • Draw labeled diagrams: FBD, energy diagrams, circuit diagrams — even if not asked, they help you organize thinking
  • Start every mechanics problem by defining a coordinate system with a positive direction
  • Write the general equation first (F=ma), then substitute — don't just write numbers
  • Show all algebra steps. Correct setup with arithmetic error gets partial credit; answer with no work gets zero
  • Include UNITS on every answer. "10" earns less than "10 m/s"
FRQ 1Newton's Laws — Atwood Machine

Two blocks are connected by a massless string over a frictionless pulley. Block A (m = 3 kg) hangs on the left; Block B (m = 5 kg) hangs on the right.

Part A: Draw a free-body diagram for each block.
Part B: Find the acceleration of the system.
Part C: Find the tension in the string.
Part A: FBD
Block A: T upward, m_A·g downward
Block B: T upward, m_B·g downward

Part B: Apply Newton's 2nd Law (B descends, A ascends)
For block B: m_B·g − T = m_B·a
For block A: T − m_A·g = m_A·a
Add equations: (m_B − m_A)g = (m_A + m_B)a
a = (5−3)(10)/(5+3) = 20/8 = 2.5 m/s²

Part C: Tension
T = m_A(g + a) = 3(10 + 2.5) = 3(12.5) = 37.5 N

⚡ Perfect Score Checklist

  • FBD first, always — AP explicitly awards points for correct FBDs
  • Mass ≠ Weight: W = mg. Mass in kg, weight in N
  • Centripetal force is not a new force — it IS the net inward force
  • Work by normal force = 0 (perpendicular to motion). Gravity on circular motion = 0 at equator
  • Elastic collision: KE AND momentum conserved. Inelastic: only momentum conserved.
  • Pressure increases with depth: P = P₀ + ρgh
  • Closed pipe: only odd harmonics (1,3,5...). Open pipe and strings: all harmonics
  • Current through capacitor = 0 in steady state
  • Parallel resistors: equivalent R < smallest R
  • Lenz's Law direction: induced current opposes the change in flux that created it
  • Photoelectric effect: frequency matters, not intensity for ejecting electrons
  • Show units in every answer — graders dock points for missing units
