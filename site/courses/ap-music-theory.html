Q1
The treble clef (G clef) circles around the line that represents
  • A) F4
  • B) the note G4 (the G above middle C)
  • C) Middle C (C4)
  • D) D4
B is correct. the note G4 (the G above middle C)
Q2
A major scale is constructed using the pattern of whole and half steps
  • A) H-W-W-W-H-W-W
  • B) W-W-W-H-W-W-H
  • C) W-W-H-W-W-W-H (starting from the tonic)
  • D) W-H-W-W-H-W-W
C is correct. W-W-H-W-W-W-H (starting from the tonic)
Q3
A minor scale (natural minor) uses the pattern
  • A) W-W-H-W-W-W-H (same as major)
  • B) H-W-W-H-W-W-W
  • C) W-H-W-W-W-H-W
  • D) W-H-W-W-H-W-W
D is correct. W-H-W-W-H-W-W
Q4
The key signature with four sharps (F#, C#, G#, D#) corresponds to the key of
  • A) A major
  • B) D major
  • C) B major
  • D) E major (or C# minor)
D is correct. E major (or C# minor)
Q5
A perfect fifth interval spans
  • A) 5 half steps
  • B) 8 half steps
  • C) 6 half steps
  • D) 7 half steps (e.g., C to G)
D is correct. 7 half steps (e.g., C to G)
Q6
A tritone (augmented fourth / diminished fifth) consists of
  • A) 7 half steps
  • B) 4 half steps
  • C) 6 half steps — exactly half an octave, creating strong dissonance
  • D) 5 half steps
C is correct. 6 half steps — exactly half an octave, creating strong dissonance
Q7
In 4/4 time (common time), the quarter note receives
  • A) one beat, and there are four beats per measure
  • B) two beats
  • C) half a beat
  • D) four beats and a whole note gets two
A is correct. one beat, and there are four beats per measure
Q8
A triad in root position has the root in the bass, with intervals of
  • A) a third and a sixth above the root
  • B) a third and a fifth above the root (e.g., C-E-G)
  • C) a fourth and a sixth above the root
  • D) a second and a fourth above the root
B is correct. a third and a fifth above the root (e.g., C-E-G)
Q9
The dominant chord (V) in a major key typically contains
  • A) scale degrees 4, 6, and 1 (the subdominant)
  • B) scale degrees 2, 4, and 6 (the supertonic)
  • C) scale degrees 5, 7, and 2 (a major triad, often with an added seventh)
  • D) scale degrees 1, 3, and 5 (the tonic triad)
C is correct. scale degrees 5, 7, and 2 (a major triad, often with an added seventh)
Q10
An authentic cadence (V → I) is considered
  • A) a half cadence ending on the dominant
  • B) a plagal cadence (IV → I)
  • C) an imperfect cadence with an inverted chord
  • D) a strong, conclusive cadence that creates a sense of resolution and closure
D is correct. a strong, conclusive cadence that creates a sense of resolution and closure
Q11
Parallel fifths (consecutive perfect fifths between two voices) are avoided in
  • A) only harmonic minor progressions
  • B) common-practice voice leading because they weaken the independence of the voices
  • C) all music including 20th-century styles
  • D) only keyboard music
B is correct. common-practice voice leading because they weaken the independence of the voices
Q12
The term "soprano" in SATB four-part writing refers to
  • A) the lowest male voice
  • B) a solo female voice of any range
  • C) the highest voice part, with typical range about C4 to G5
  • D) the second-highest voice (alto)
C is correct. the highest voice part, with typical range about C4 to G5
Q13
In Roman numeral analysis, a chord labeled "viio" in a major key is
  • A) a diminished triad built on the seventh scale degree (e.g., B-D-F in C major)
  • B) a major triad on the seventh degree
  • C) an augmented triad on the leading tone
  • D) a dominant seventh chord
A is correct. a diminished triad built on the seventh scale degree (e.g., B-D-F in C major)
Q14
Compound meter has beats that are divided into
  • A) four beats per measure always
  • B) unequal divisions of five or seven
  • C) groups of three (e.g., 6/8 has two main beats each divided into three eighth notes)
  • D) groups of two (like simple meter)
C is correct. groups of three (e.g., 6/8 has two main beats each divided into three eighth notes)
Q15
Modulation in tonal music refers to
  • A) a change of tonal center (key) that is confirmed by a cadence in the new key
  • B) a change of tempo within the same key
  • C) the use of chromatic notes without key change
  • D) alternating between two modes (major/minor)
A is correct. a change of tonal center (key) that is confirmed by a cadence in the new key
Q16
Secondary dominants are chords that
  • A) replace the dominant in a cadence
  • B) use only notes from the current scale
  • C) appear only in minor keys
  • D) temporarily tonicize a non-tonic chord by acting as the V (or V7) of that chord within the original key
D is correct. temporarily tonicize a non-tonic chord by acting as the V (or V7) of that chord within the original key
Q17
The harmonic minor scale differs from the natural minor scale by
  • A) raising both the 6th and 7th degrees
  • B) using the same pitch material as major
  • C) raising the 7th scale degree by a half step, creating a leading tone and a V major chord
  • D) lowering the 6th scale degree
C is correct. raising the 7th scale degree by a half step, creating a leading tone and a V major chord
Q18
A suspension (4-3, 7-6, 9-8) in counterpoint involves
  • A) changing the bass while the upper voices hold
  • B) holding a note from the previous chord into a new harmony where it creates dissonance, then resolving down by step
  • C) moving a voice up by step to create tension
  • D) skipping to a dissonant note then holding
B is correct. holding a note from the previous chord into a new harmony where it creates dissonance, then resolving down by step
Q19
Enharmonic equivalents are pitches that
  • A) are exactly one octave apart
  • B) share the same letter name but different accidentals in the same octave
  • C) are a tritone apart
  • D) sound identical (same frequency) but are written differently (e.g., C# and Db)
D is correct. sound identical (same frequency) but are written differently (e.g., C# and Db)
Q20
A Picardy third refers to
  • A) a modulation to the relative major at the end
  • B) a chromatic passing tone in the bass
  • C) the use of a major tonic chord (raised 3rd) at the final cadence of a piece in minor key
  • D) the use of a minor chord in a major-key ending
C is correct. the use of a major tonic chord (raised 3rd) at the final cadence of a piece in minor key
