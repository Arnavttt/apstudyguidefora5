Evidence for Evolution

Learn on this site
Fossil, Anatomical, and Molecular Evidence

Evidence for evolution includes the fossil record (transitional forms), comparative anatomy (homologous and vestigial structures), comparative embryology, and molecular biology (DNA sequence similarities).

Common misconception: Students think evolution is "just a theory" in the colloquial sense; in science, theory means a well-substantiated explanation supported by extensive evidence from multiple independent lines.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to cite multiple lines of evidence for evolution and explain what each reveals about common ancestry.

Homologous and Analogous Structures

Homologous structures share a common evolutionary origin (same structural plan) but may have different functions (human arm, bat wing, whale flipper); analogous structures have similar functions but different evolutionary origins (bird wing vs. insect wing).

Common misconception: Students confuse homologous with analogous; homologous = common origin (indicates phylogenetic relationship); analogous = similar function but different origin (indicates convergent evolution, not common ancestry).

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to classify structural similarities as homologous or analogous.

Molecular Phylogenetics

Comparing DNA, RNA, and protein sequences between species allows scientists to construct phylogenetic trees and estimate the timing of evolutionary divergence; more sequence similarity = more recent common ancestry.

Common misconception: Students think molecular data always confirms morphological phylogenies; molecular and morphological analyses sometimes conflict, requiring integration of multiple data types.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to interpret a phylogenetic tree; FRQs ask how molecular clock data can estimate divergence times.

Key Vocabulary
  • Evolution — A change in allele frequencies in a population over generations; driven by natural selection, genetic drift, mutation, and gene flow.
  • Homologous Structures — Anatomical features of different species sharing a common evolutionary origin; indicates descent from a common ancestor.
  • Analogous Structures — Features of different species with similar functions but different evolutionary origins; the result of convergent evolution.
  • Vestigial Structure — A reduced or nonfunctional structure that was functional in an ancestor (e.g., human coccyx, whale pelvis); evidence of evolutionary history.
  • Phylogenetic Tree — A branching diagram representing evolutionary relationships and common ancestry among organisms based on shared derived characteristics.
Objectives
  • Identify and explain multiple lines of evidence for evolution.
  • Distinguish homologous from analogous structures and explain what each implies about evolution.
  • Interpret a phylogenetic tree to determine relatedness and common ancestry.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to use evidence from multiple sources (fossil, molecular, anatomical) to support evolution.
  • MCQs ask whether a structural similarity is homologous or analogous.
  • Questions ask students to read phylogenetic trees and identify the most recent common ancestor.
Practice before moving on
  • Classify each pair as homologous or analogous: (a) human arm/whale flipper, (b) bat wing/insect wing, (c) human eye/octopus eye.
  • Explain what it means if two species share 97% DNA sequence identity.

Natural Selection and Adaptation

Learn on this site
Darwin's Mechanism of Natural Selection

Natural selection has four postulates: (1) variation exists in a population; (2) variation is heritable; (3) individuals with favorable traits survive and reproduce more; (4) favorable alleles increase in frequency over generations.

Common misconception: Students think natural selection is directed or purposeful; it is entirely undirected — traits are "selected" only in the sense that they affect differential reproductive success.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to apply all four postulates to a specific example (e.g., antibiotic resistance in bacteria).

Types of Natural Selection

Directional selection shifts the phenotype distribution toward one extreme; stabilizing selection favors intermediate phenotypes and reduces variation; disruptive selection favors both extremes, increasing variation.

Common misconception: Students think evolution always produces new species; selection can maintain existing variation (stabilizing) or drive gradual change without producing speciation.

How it can be tested: MCQs show a bell curve before and after selection and ask students to identify the type of selection.

Sexual Selection and Other Evolutionary Mechanisms

Sexual selection favors traits that improve mating success (e.g., peacock tails); genetic drift (especially in small populations — founder effect, bottleneck) and gene flow also change allele frequencies.

Common misconception: Students confuse natural selection and genetic drift; natural selection is deterministic (based on fitness), while genetic drift is random chance — important in small populations.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to distinguish natural selection from genetic drift using a specific population example.

Key Vocabulary
  • Natural Selection — The process by which heritable traits that improve survival and reproduction become more common in a population over generations.
  • Directional Selection — Natural selection that favors one extreme phenotype, shifting the population mean in that direction.
  • Stabilizing Selection — Natural selection that favors intermediate phenotypes, reducing variation around the mean.
  • Genetic Drift — Random fluctuations in allele frequencies in small populations; more pronounced in small populations and not related to fitness.
  • Bottleneck Effect — A reduction in genetic diversity following a drastic reduction in population size; a form of genetic drift.
Objectives
  • State Darwin's four postulates of natural selection.
  • Distinguish directional, stabilizing, and disruptive selection using graphical examples.
  • Compare natural selection and genetic drift in terms of direction, cause, and population size effects.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to apply natural selection postulates to a specific scenario.
  • MCQs show phenotype distribution graphs before and after selection.
  • Questions ask students to distinguish natural selection from genetic drift.
Practice before moving on
  • Apply all four postulates of natural selection to the evolution of antibiotic resistance in bacteria.
  • Draw bell curve diagrams for each type of selection (directional, stabilizing, disruptive); label the selected phenotypes.

Population Genetics and Hardy-Weinberg

Learn on this site
Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

The Hardy-Weinberg principle states that allele and genotype frequencies remain constant in a population that is large, randomly mating, with no mutation, migration, or natural selection.

Common misconception: Students memorize H-W but don't understand its use; the equilibrium model identifies what would happen if evolution did NOT occur — deviations from H-W indicate that evolution IS occurring.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to use H-W equations (p + q = 1; p² + 2pq + q² = 1) to calculate allele and genotype frequencies.

Calculating H-W Frequencies

If the frequency of the recessive phenotype (aa) is known, q² can be determined; q = √q², p = 1 - q; then p², 2pq, and q² give the expected genotype frequencies.

Common misconception: Students square the wrong quantity; q is the allele frequency, q² is the genotype (homozygous recessive) frequency — you must find q from q² (take the square root) before calculating p.

How it can be tested: MCQs and FRQs frequently present q² (recessive phenotype frequency) and require students to work backward to find allele and genotype frequencies.

Violations of H-W and Microevolution

Any violation of H-W conditions (selection, mutation, gene flow, genetic drift, non-random mating) causes allele frequencies to change — that is, evolution (microevolution) occurs.

Common misconception: Students think only natural selection causes evolution; all five violations of H-W can independently drive allele frequency change.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to identify which H-W assumption is violated in a given scenario and predict the evolutionary consequence.

Key Vocabulary
  • Hardy-Weinberg Principle — A model stating that allele and genotype frequencies remain constant in a non-evolving (ideal) population; deviations indicate evolution.
  • p — The frequency of the dominant allele in a population; ranges from 0 to 1.
  • q — The frequency of the recessive allele; p + q = 1 for a two-allele system.
  • Microevolution — Changes in allele frequencies within a population over generations; includes directional selection, drift, mutation, and gene flow.
  • Gene Flow — The movement of alleles between populations through migration; can increase or decrease genetic diversity in a population.
Objectives
  • State the five conditions required for Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium.
  • Use H-W equations to calculate allele and genotype frequencies from phenotype data.
  • Identify which H-W condition is violated and predict the evolutionary consequence.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs present the frequency of a recessive phenotype and ask for allele and genotype frequencies.
  • MCQs ask which condition is violated in a scenario and how allele frequencies will change.
  • Questions use H-W as a null model to detect whether evolution is occurring.
Practice before moving on
  • In a population of 1000, 160 are homozygous recessive (aa). Calculate p, q, and the expected number of heterozygotes.
  • Identify which H-W assumption is violated: (a) random mating breaks down; (b) a drought kills most of the population; (c) immigrants join the population.

Speciation and Macroevolution

Learn on this site
Biological Species Concept and Reproductive Isolation

The biological species concept defines species as populations whose members can interbreed and produce viable fertile offspring; reproductive isolation (prezygotic and postzygotic barriers) prevents gene flow between species.

Common misconception: Students think reproductive isolation always requires geographic separation; sympatric speciation (in the same geographic area) can also occur through polyploidy or disruptive selection.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to classify a reproductive barrier as prezygotic or postzygotic and explain its role in speciation.

Allopatric vs. Sympatric Speciation

Allopatric speciation occurs when a geographic barrier separates a population, allowing independent evolution; sympatric speciation occurs without geographic isolation, often through polyploidy (common in plants).

Common misconception: Students think speciation always takes a very long time; polyploidy can cause instantaneous speciation — a single generation can produce a reproductively isolated polyploid individual.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to classify a speciation scenario as allopatric or sympatric; FRQs ask about the role of geographic isolation.

Macroevolution and Patterns of Evolution

Macroevolution describes large-scale evolutionary change including adaptive radiation, convergent evolution, coevolution, and mass extinctions; the pace of evolution can be gradualistic or punctuated (punctuated equilibrium).

Common misconception: Students think evolution is always slow and gradual; punctuated equilibrium proposes that species remain relatively unchanged for long periods interspersed with rapid change.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to distinguish convergent evolution from divergent evolution; FRQs ask about the evidence for punctuated equilibrium in the fossil record.

Key Vocabulary
  • Biological Species Concept — Defines a species as a group of populations whose members can interbreed and produce fertile, viable offspring.
  • Reproductive Isolation — Biological barriers (prezygotic or postzygotic) that prevent two populations from exchanging genes and leading to speciation.
  • Allopatric Speciation — Speciation that occurs when populations are separated by a geographic barrier, allowing independent evolutionary divergence.
  • Sympatric Speciation — Speciation without geographic isolation; common via polyploidy in plants.
  • Adaptive Radiation — The rapid diversification of a single ancestral lineage into many ecologically diverse species, often following colonization of a new environment.
Objectives
  • Define reproductive isolation and distinguish prezygotic from postzygotic barriers.
  • Compare allopatric and sympatric speciation mechanisms.
  • Describe adaptive radiation and give an example.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask about speciation mechanisms and reproductive isolation barriers.
  • MCQs distinguish convergent from divergent evolution and adaptive radiation.
  • Questions ask about the evidence for and implications of punctuated equilibrium.
Practice before moving on
  • Classify as prezygotic or postzygotic: (a) different mating seasons; (b) hybrid infertility; (c) geographic separation; (d) hybrid inviability.
  • Explain how polyploidy in plants can cause instantaneous sympatric speciation.
