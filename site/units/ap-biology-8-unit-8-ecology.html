Unit 8 of AP® Biology

Ecology

Ecology studies interactions among organisms and their environments at population, community, and ecosystem levels.

Unit learning gateway

  • Population ecology describes how populations grow and are regulated.
  • Community ecology studies species interactions including competition, predation, and symbiosis.
  • Energy flows through ecosystems while matter cycles.
  • Biodiversity and ecosystem stability are linked; human activities threaten both.

Population Ecology

Learn on this site
Population Growth Models

Exponential growth (J-curve) occurs when resources are unlimited; logistic growth (S-curve) occurs when population growth is limited by carrying capacity (K) — the maximum sustainable population size.

Common misconception: Students think all populations follow logistic growth; exponential growth is realistic for short periods (e.g., bacteria in fresh culture); logistic growth applies when resources are limited.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to compare exponential and logistic growth models and identify factors that create the S-shaped curve.

Density-Dependent and Independent Factors

Density-dependent limiting factors (predation, disease, competition) increase in severity as population density increases; density-independent factors (weather, disasters) affect population regardless of density.

Common misconception: Students think all population regulation is density-dependent; weather events (drought, frost) are density-independent — they kill the same proportion of a population regardless of how many individuals there are.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to classify a limiting factor; FRQs ask how a combination of factors regulates a real population.

Life History Strategies

K-selected species (large, slow-reproducing, few offspring with high parental care) are adapted to stable environments near K; r-selected species (small, fast-reproducing, many offspring with little care) exploit unstable environments.

Common misconception: Students think K-selected species are always "better"; r and K selection are trade-offs adapted to different environments — neither is superior in all contexts.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to classify a species as r- or K-selected based on life history traits.

Key Vocabulary
  • Carrying Capacity (K) — The maximum sustainable population size that an environment can support with its available resources.
  • Logistic Growth — Population growth that slows as the population approaches carrying capacity; produces an S-shaped curve.
  • Exponential Growth — Unrestricted population growth at a constant rate; produces a J-shaped curve; occurs when resources are unlimited.
  • Density-Dependent Factor — A limiting factor whose effect increases with population density (e.g., predation, disease, food competition).
  • r-Selected Species — Species with high reproductive rates, small body size, and short lifespan; adapted to unstable environments with fluctuating resources.
Objectives
  • Compare exponential and logistic growth models.
  • Classify limiting factors as density-dependent or density-independent.
  • Describe life history trade-offs (r vs. K selection).
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to draw and compare growth curves and explain what limits logistic growth.
  • MCQs ask about limiting factors and their density-dependence.
  • Questions ask students to classify a species as r- or K-selected from its life history traits.
Practice before moving on
  • Graph exponential and logistic growth for a population; explain why logistic growth decelerates near K.
  • Classify each as r- or K-selected: (a) elephant, (b) mosquito, (c) oak tree, (d) dandelion.

Community Ecology

Learn on this site
Species Interactions

Species interactions include competition (−/−), predation (+/−), mutualism (+/+), commensalism (+/0), and parasitism (+/−); these interactions shape community structure and evolution.

Common misconception: Students confuse commensalism and mutualism; in commensalism, one species benefits and the other is neither helped nor harmed; in mutualism, both benefit.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to classify an interaction by its +/− effects; FRQs ask how removing a key species would affect community structure.

Keystone Species and Trophic Cascades

A keystone species has a disproportionately large effect on community structure relative to its abundance; removing a keystone predator can cause a trophic cascade that alters the entire community.

Common misconception: Students think keystone species are always predators; while many are predators, some keystone species are ecosystem engineers (beavers) or mutualists (mycorrhizal fungi).

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to predict the effects of removing a keystone species from a food web.

Ecological Succession

Primary succession begins on bare rock (no soil); secondary succession begins on disturbed land where soil remains; pioneer species facilitate establishment of more complex communities.

Common misconception: Students think succession always ends in the same climax community; the endpoint of succession depends on the local climate and disturbance regime — some ecosystems are maintained in intermediate successional states.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to distinguish primary from secondary succession; FRQs ask students to explain the role of pioneer species.

Key Vocabulary
  • Competition — An interaction in which two species use the same limited resource; both are harmed (−/−); competitive exclusion states that two species with the same niche cannot coexist long-term.
  • Predation — An interaction in which one organism (predator) kills and eats another (prey); (+/−); drives coevolution of predator and prey.
  • Mutualism — A symbiotic interaction benefiting both species (+/+); e.g., mycorrhizal fungi and plant roots, nitrogen-fixing bacteria and legumes.
  • Keystone Species — A species that has a disproportionately large effect on community structure relative to its abundance; its removal causes major community changes.
  • Ecological Succession — The directional change in community composition over time after a disturbance; primary or secondary depending on whether soil remains.
Objectives
  • Classify species interactions by their +/− effects on each species.
  • Explain the concept of keystone species and trophic cascades.
  • Distinguish primary from secondary succession and explain the role of pioneer species.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask about competition exclusion, predator-prey dynamics, and succession.
  • FRQs ask students to predict outcomes when a keystone predator is removed.
  • Questions test the difference between primary and secondary succession.
Practice before moving on
  • Predict the community consequences if wolves were removed from a forest ecosystem.
  • Classify each interaction: (a) clownfish/sea anemone; (b) bees and flowers; (c) tapeworm in intestine; (d) oak trees competing for light.

Ecosystem Ecology and Energy Flow

Learn on this site
Trophic Levels and Energy Flow

Energy flows from producers (autotrophs) through primary, secondary, and tertiary consumers (heterotrophs); approximately 10% of energy is transferred between trophic levels — the 10% rule — the rest is lost as heat.

Common misconception: Students think matter is lost between trophic levels like energy; matter cycles but energy does not — energy flows one-way through ecosystems and is ultimately lost as heat.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to calculate energy available at each trophic level; MCQs ask why ecosystems support fewer top predators than producers.

Biogeochemical Cycles

Carbon cycles through photosynthesis (CO2 → organic carbon) and respiration/decomposition (organic carbon → CO2); nitrogen cycles through nitrogen fixation (N2 → NH4+), nitrification, denitrification, and assimilation.

Common misconception: Students think nitrogen in the atmosphere is directly available to plants; most plants cannot use N2 directly — it must first be fixed by nitrogen-fixing bacteria.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to trace carbon or nitrogen through the biogeochemical cycle and identify the role of decomposers.

Human Impacts on Ecosystems

Eutrophication occurs when excess nutrients (N, P from fertilizers) cause algal blooms and oxygen depletion; habitat destruction, invasive species, and climate change (ocean acidification from CO2) threaten ecosystem stability.

Common misconception: Students think eutrophication directly causes death by the nutrients; excess nutrients → algal bloom → algae decomposition → bacterial respiration → oxygen depletion → hypoxic zone killing fish.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to trace the causal chain from fertilizer runoff to dead zones.

Key Vocabulary
  • 10% Rule — Only about 10% of energy stored in one trophic level is transferred to the next; 90% is lost as heat through cellular respiration.
  • Biogeochemical Cycle — The cyclic movement of a chemical element between biotic and abiotic components of an ecosystem (e.g., carbon cycle, nitrogen cycle).
  • Nitrogen Fixation — The conversion of atmospheric N2 to ammonia (NH3 or NH4+) by nitrogen-fixing bacteria (e.g., Rhizobium in legume root nodules); makes nitrogen available to plants.
  • Eutrophication — Excessive nutrient enrichment of a water body, causing algal blooms, oxygen depletion, and loss of aquatic life.
  • Net Primary Productivity (NPP) — The rate of organic matter production by autotrophs minus the energy used in their own respiration; the energy available to consumers.
Objectives
  • Apply the 10% rule to calculate energy available at each trophic level.
  • Trace carbon and nitrogen through their biogeochemical cycles.
  • Explain the causal chain from fertilizer runoff to eutrophication.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to calculate energy transfer between trophic levels.
  • MCQs ask about the role of decomposers in biogeochemical cycling.
  • Questions trace human-caused eutrophication as a systems problem.
Practice before moving on
  • Given 10,000 kcal of solar energy captured by producers, calculate the energy available at the 3rd trophic level.
  • Trace a nitrogen atom from the atmosphere to a plant protein and back to N2.

Biodiversity and Conservation

Learn on this site
Levels and Importance of Biodiversity

Biodiversity has three levels: genetic diversity (within species), species diversity (number and abundance of species), and ecosystem diversity (variety of habitats); higher biodiversity generally increases ecosystem stability and resilience.

Common misconception: Students think biodiversity is only about species number; genetic diversity within species is equally critical for long-term survival and adaptability to changing environments.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to explain how each level of biodiversity contributes to ecosystem function.

Threats to Biodiversity

Major threats include habitat destruction (HIPCO: Habitat loss, Invasive species, Pollution, Climate change, Overexploitation); habitat fragmentation creates isolated populations vulnerable to genetic drift and local extinction.

Common misconception: Students rank threats as equally serious in all ecosystems; habitat destruction is the dominant cause of biodiversity loss globally, followed by invasive species.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to identify the greatest threat to biodiversity; FRQs ask how habitat fragmentation reduces genetic diversity.

Conservation Biology

Conservation strategies include protecting habitat corridors between fragmented patches, reducing invasive species, and addressing climate change; the minimum viable population (MVP) concept estimates the smallest population that can persist long-term.

Common misconception: Students think larger reserves are always better without considering shape; a compact reserve minimizes edge effects and habitat fragmentation — a large reserve with corridors connecting patches is ideal.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to design a conservation plan for a fragmented population using ecological principles.

Key Vocabulary
  • Biodiversity — The variety of life at all levels: genetic diversity, species diversity, and ecosystem diversity.
  • Habitat Fragmentation — The division of continuous habitat into smaller, isolated patches; increases edge effects and reduces effective population size.
  • Invasive Species — A non-native species introduced to a new environment where it lacks natural predators or competitors, often causing ecological damage.
  • Minimum Viable Population (MVP) — The smallest population size that has a reasonable probability of surviving long-term; below this size, genetic drift and demographic stochasticity become serious threats.
  • Habitat Corridor — A strip of habitat connecting isolated patches, allowing gene flow and migration between fragmented populations.
Objectives
  • Explain the three levels of biodiversity and why each is important.
  • Identify and rank the major threats to global biodiversity.
  • Describe conservation strategies to preserve biodiversity in fragmented habitats.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask about the threats to biodiversity and how habitat fragmentation reduces genetic diversity.
  • MCQs ask about minimum viable populations, habitat corridors, and invasive species effects.
  • Questions ask students to design a conservation strategy for a specific ecological scenario.
Practice before moving on
  • Design a conservation plan for a population of 100 endangered wolves in a fragmented forest.
  • Explain why invasive species often cause more harm in island ecosystems than in continental ones.
