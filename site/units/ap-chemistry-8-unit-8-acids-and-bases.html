Acid-Base Theories and Equilibria

Learn on this site
Brønsted-Lowry Theory

Acids donate protons (H⁺); bases accept protons. Conjugate acid-base pairs differ by one H⁺.

Common misconception: Amphiprotic species (like H₂O, HCO₃⁻) can act as either acid or base depending on the partner.

How it can be tested: Identify conjugate acid-base pairs and write proton-transfer equations.

Lewis Theory

Lewis acids accept electron pairs; Lewis bases donate electron pairs. Broader than Brønsted-Lowry.

Common misconception: All Brønsted-Lowry acids are Lewis acids, but not all Lewis acids are Brønsted-Lowry acids.

How it can be tested: Identify Lewis acid and Lewis base in coordinate bond formation.

Water Autoionization

Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C; pH + pOH = 14 at 25°C.

Common misconception: Kw changes with temperature; pH = 7 is neutral only at 25°C.

How it can be tested: Use Kw to interconvert [H⁺], [OH⁻], pH, and pOH.

Key Vocabulary
  • Brønsted-Lowry acid — Species that donates a proton (H⁺) in a reaction.
  • Brønsted-Lowry base — Species that accepts a proton (H⁺) in a reaction.
  • conjugate acid-base pair — Two species differing by one proton; acid and its conjugate base.
  • Lewis acid — Electron pair acceptor.
  • autoionization of water — H₂O + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + OH⁻; Kw = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C.
Objectives
  • Identify Brønsted-Lowry conjugate acid-base pairs.
  • Distinguish Lewis acid-base from Brønsted-Lowry definitions.
  • Use Kw to relate [H⁺] and [OH⁻].
How this can be tested
  • Identifying conjugate pairs in acid-base reactions.
  • Recognizing Lewis acid-base interactions in coordinate chemistry.
  • Calculating pH from [H⁺] and vice versa.

Strong and Weak Acids/Bases

Learn on this site
Strong Acids and Bases

Strong acids (HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, HClO₄, H₂SO₄) dissociate 100%; [H⁺] = initial acid concentration.

Common misconception: H₂SO₄ is diprotic; only the first proton is fully dissociated in dilute solutions.

How it can be tested: Calculate pH directly from concentration for strong acids and bases.

Weak Acid Equilibria

Weak acids partially dissociate; use Ka and ICE table to find [H⁺] and pH.

Common misconception: Ka is not the same as percent dissociation; both must be calculated separately.

How it can be tested: Set up Ka expression and ICE table to solve weak acid pH problems.

Weak Bases and Kb

Weak bases partially accept protons; use Kb and ICE table to find [OH⁻] and pH.

Common misconception: For conjugate acid-base pairs: Ka × Kb = Kw; stronger acid has weaker conjugate base.

How it can be tested: Use Kb to calculate [OH⁻] then convert to pH for weak base solutions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Ka — Acid dissociation constant; larger Ka means stronger acid.
  • Kb — Base dissociation constant; larger Kb means stronger base.
  • percent dissociation — Fraction of weak acid or base that ionizes, expressed as percent.
  • strong acid — Acid that dissociates completely in aqueous solution; Ka >> 1.
  • weak acid — Acid that only partially dissociates; Ka < 1.
Objectives
  • Calculate pH of strong acid and base solutions.
  • Use Ka and ICE tables to find pH of weak acid solutions.
  • Relate Ka and Kb for conjugate pairs using Kw.
How this can be tested
  • Multi-step weak acid pH calculations using ICE tables.
  • Calculating percent dissociation of a weak acid.
  • Comparing acidity of two weak acids from Ka values.

Buffers

Learn on this site
Buffer Composition

A buffer contains a weak acid and its conjugate base (or weak base and its conjugate acid) in significant amounts.

Common misconception: Not every mixture of acid and base creates a buffer — stoichiometry must leave significant amounts of both components.

How it can be tested: Identify whether a solution is a buffer from its composition.

Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation

pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]) calculates buffer pH quickly.

Common misconception: Henderson-Hasselbalch is an approximation valid when [A⁻]/[HA] is between 0.1 and 10.

How it can be tested: Use Henderson-Hasselbalch to calculate buffer pH and to design buffers at a target pH.

Buffer Capacity

Buffer capacity is greatest when [HA] = [A⁻] (pH = pKa); large concentrations increase capacity.

Common misconception: A buffer is overwhelmed when strong acid or base added exceeds its buffering components.

How it can be tested: Predict how addition of strong acid or base changes buffer pH.

Key Vocabulary
  • buffer — Solution that resists pH change upon addition of small amounts of strong acid or base.
  • Henderson-Hasselbalch equation — pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]); calculates pH of a buffer.
  • buffer capacity — Amount of acid or base a buffer can neutralize before significant pH change.
  • pKa — Negative log of Ka; represents pH at which [HA] = [A⁻] in a buffer.
  • conjugate base — Species formed after an acid donates its proton; the A⁻ component of a buffer.
Objectives
  • Identify buffer solutions from composition.
  • Apply Henderson-Hasselbalch to calculate buffer pH.
  • Explain buffer capacity and its dependence on concentration and ratio.
How this can be tested
  • Calculating pH after adding strong acid/base to a buffer.
  • Designing a buffer at a target pH using Henderson-Hasselbalch.
  • Comparing buffer capacities of different concentrations.

Titrations

Learn on this site
Titration Curve Shapes

Strong acid-strong base: sharp equivalence point at pH 7. Weak acid-strong base: equivalence point above pH 7 (conjugate base is basic).

Common misconception: The equivalence point is where moles of acid = moles of base added, not necessarily where pH = 7.

How it can be tested: Sketch titration curves and identify equivalence points and half-equivalence points.

Half-Equivalence Point

At the half-equivalence point in a weak acid titration, pH = pKa because [HA] = [A⁻].

Common misconception: Henderson-Hasselbalch directly gives pH = pKa at the half-equivalence point.

How it can be tested: Use half-equivalence point to determine Ka from a titration curve.

Indicators

Acid-base indicators are weak acids where HIn and In⁻ have different colors; they change color near their pKa.

Common misconception: A good indicator has a pKa within ±1 unit of the equivalence point pH.

How it can be tested: Select appropriate indicator based on expected equivalence point pH.

Key Vocabulary
  • equivalence point — Point in a titration where moles of acid exactly equal moles of base.
  • half-equivalence point — Point where half the acid has been neutralized; pH = pKa for weak acid titration.
  • indicator — Weak acid or base that changes color at a characteristic pH.
  • endpoint — Observed color change of indicator; ideally matches the equivalence point.
  • titrant — Solution of known concentration added from a burette during a titration.
Objectives
  • Sketch and interpret strong and weak acid-base titration curves.
  • Identify and use the half-equivalence point to determine Ka.
  • Select an appropriate indicator for a given titration.
How this can be tested
  • Interpreting titration curves at equivalence and buffer regions.
  • Calculating Ka from pH at half-equivalence point.
  • Predicting equivalence point pH for weak acid/base titrations.
