Economic Systems and Development Strategies

Learn on this site
Market vs. Command Economies

Market economies rely on price signals and private ownership; command (planned) economies have state ownership and central planning. Most AP® countries have mixed economies, but China retains significant state direction despite market opening.

Common misconception: China's economy is often called "state capitalism" — market mechanisms operate but the state owns major industries and directs credit through state banks.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare China's economic model to the UK's market economy; MC on state capitalism features.

Import Substitution Industrialization (ISI)

ISI is a development strategy that protects domestic industries from foreign competition through tariffs and subsidies; Mexico and Nigeria used ISI in the mid-20th century to build domestic manufacturing capacity.

Common misconception: ISI produced industrialization but also inefficiency, inflation, and debt crises — Mexico's 1982 debt crisis forced a shift toward export-oriented and market-oriented policies.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain how Mexico transitioned from ISI to export-led growth; MC on ISI consequences.

Export-Led Growth and Trade

Export-led growth strategies focus on developing internationally competitive industries; China used this approach from 1978, building export manufacturing that drove three decades of ~10% annual GDP growth.

Common misconception: China's export-led growth model required suppressing labor costs (low wages, restrictions on labor organizing) and currency management.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare China's export-led model with Nigeria's oil-dependent development strategy; MC on comparative development paths.

Globalization and Its Political Consequences

Learn on this site
Globalization and Economic Integration

Globalization involves the increasing integration of national economies through trade, investment, and technology; the UK joined the EEC (1973) and later left the EU (Brexit 2020); Mexico joined NAFTA (1994, now USMCA); China joined the WTO (2001).

Common misconception: Globalization creates winners and losers within countries — it can raise aggregate prosperity while increasing inequality and displacing workers in import-competing sectors.

How it can be tested: FRQ: how did Mexico's NAFTA membership affect different groups within Mexico?

Brexit: Globalization Backlash

UK's 2016 Brexit referendum and 2020 exit from the EU reflects a backlash against globalization's costs — immigration, sovereignty loss, and regional inequality. The leave vote was concentrated in areas that had not benefited from EU membership.

Common misconception: Brexit represents a case where democratic majorities can reverse integration — but the costs of exiting a deep economic union are substantial and long-lasting.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain the causes and consequences of Brexit for UK governance and economy.

China's Role in Global Economy

China is the world's second-largest economy and largest exporter; the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) extends Chinese economic influence through infrastructure investment across Asia, Africa, and Europe.

Common misconception: China's BRI creates debt dependency in recipient countries — critics call it "debt-trap diplomacy," though research shows varied evidence for deliberate debt entrapment.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain how China's economic power affects its political relationships globally.

Key Vocabulary

  • NAFTA/USMCA
  • Brexit
  • Belt and Road Initiative (BRI)
  • WTO

Regime Change and Democratization

Learn on this site
Democratic Transitions

Democratic transitions occur when authoritarian regimes move toward competitive elections and civil liberties; Mexico's gradual transition (1977–2000) and Nigeria's transitions (1999 return to civilian rule) are AP® examples.

Common misconception: Transitions can occur through elite negotiation (pacted transitions), popular uprising, or military withdrawal; the mode of transition affects the stability of the resulting democracy.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare the transition processes in Mexico and Nigeria.

Democratic Consolidation

Consolidation occurs when democracy becomes "the only game in town" — when all major political actors accept electoral defeat and do not try to subvert democratic rules.

Common misconception: Mexico is considered a consolidated democracy; Nigeria's democracy remains fragile — military intervention remains a concern and electoral violence persists.

How it can be tested: MC on indicators of democratic consolidation; FRQ on challenges to consolidation in Nigeria.

Authoritarian Persistence and Backsliding

Some authoritarian regimes persist through adaptation (China's CCP has survived by delivering economic growth and managing information). Some democracies backslide — Russia transitioned from competitive democracy in the 1990s to competitive authoritarianism under Putin.

Common misconception: Democratic backsliding often happens gradually through legal means — amending constitutions, controlling courts, restricting press — rather than through sudden coups.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain how Russia's political system changed from the 1990s to the present; MC on backsliding mechanisms.

Key Vocabulary
  • democratic transition
  • democratic consolidation
  • authoritarian persistence
  • democratic backsliding
  • pacted transition
Objectives
  • Explain the factors that enable or hinder democratic transitions
  • Describe Mexico's gradual democratic transition from 1977 to 2000
  • Analyze Russia's democratic backsliding under Putin
How this can be tested

  • MC on democratic consolidation indicators
  • Short-answer on authoritarian adaptation strategies in China
Practice before moving on

Comparing Development and State-Society Relations

Learn on this site
Welfare States and Social Policy

The UK operates a comprehensive welfare state including the NHS (universal healthcare), public education, unemployment insurance, and pensions; this reflects a social democratic political tradition.

Common misconception: China has expanded social insurance but access is unequal between urban and rural populations (hukou system); coverage is less comprehensive than the UK's.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare social policy and welfare provision in UK vs. China; MC on NHS as a political institution.

Corruption and State Quality

Corruption — the use of public power for private gain — undermines state capacity, economic development, and democratic legitimacy. Nigeria and Russia rank low on Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index.

Common misconception: Anti-corruption campaigns can themselves become tools of political control — China's Xi Jinping anti-corruption drive (2012-present) has removed political rivals alongside genuine corrupt officials.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain how corruption affects governance in Nigeria; MC on anti-corruption campaigns in China.

Gender, Representation, and Inclusion

Representation of women and minorities in political institutions varies: UK had its first female PM (Thatcher, 1979); Nigeria has very low female legislative representation; Iran strictly limits women's roles in the judiciary and religious governance.

Common misconception: Formal representation (being in office) is distinct from substantive representation (policy outcomes that improve women's lives) — a country can have high female representation and still maintain gender-unequal policies.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare women's political representation across AP® countries; MC on barriers to women's representation in Iran and Nigeria.

  • corruption
  • Transparency International
  • gender representation
