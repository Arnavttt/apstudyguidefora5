Ecosystem Structure

Learn on this site
Biotic and Abiotic Factors

Ecosystems include living (biotic) and nonliving (abiotic) components that interact to sustain life.

Common misconception: Abiotic factors like temperature and pH are not living but strongly control biotic communities.

How it can be tested: Identify biotic vs. abiotic components and predict how changes affect the ecosystem.

Trophic Levels

Energy moves from producers (autotrophs) through primary, secondary, and tertiary consumers; only ~10% transfers per level.

Common misconception: The 10% rule is an approximation; actual efficiency varies by ecosystem.

How it can be tested: Calculate energy available at each trophic level given primary productivity.

Food Webs

Food webs show multiple feeding relationships; removing one species can cascade through the web.

Common misconception: Food chains are simplified; real systems are webs with many interconnections.

How it can be tested: Predict consequences of removing a keystone species from a food web.

Key Vocabulary
  • producer (autotroph) — Organism that makes its own food via photosynthesis or chemosynthesis.
  • consumer (heterotroph) — Organism that obtains energy by eating other organisms.
  • decomposer — Organism (fungi, bacteria) that breaks down dead organic matter, returning nutrients to soil.
  • trophic level — Position in a food chain based on feeding relationships.
  • keystone species — Species with disproportionately large ecological impact relative to its abundance.
Objectives
  • Describe energy flow through trophic levels using the 10% rule.
  • Distinguish producers, consumers, and decomposers.
  • Predict cascading effects of species removal from a food web.
How this can be tested
  • Calculating energy available at higher trophic levels.
  • Interpreting food web diagrams.
  • Identifying keystone species and their removal effects.

Energy Flow and Productivity

Learn on this site
Gross and Net Primary Productivity

GPP is total photosynthetic energy fixed; NPP = GPP − respiration by plants; NPP is energy available to consumers.

Common misconception: NPP, not GPP, determines how much energy is available to consumers.

How it can be tested: Calculate NPP from GPP and respiration data; compare productivity across biomes.

Ecosystem Energy Budget

Energy enters as sunlight, flows through trophic levels, and exits as heat; no energy is recycled (unlike matter).

Common misconception: Unlike matter, energy does not cycle — it flows in one direction and is lost as heat.

How it can be tested: Explain why energy must continually enter an ecosystem while matter cycles.

Productivity Limits

Temperature, light, water, and nutrient availability limit productivity in terrestrial and aquatic biomes.

Common misconception: Tropical rainforests have high NPP due to warmth and rain; tundra has low NPP despite sunlight in summer.

How it can be tested: Identify the limiting factor for productivity in a described biome.

Key Vocabulary
  • gross primary productivity (GPP) — Total energy fixed by photosynthesis per unit time.
  • net primary productivity (NPP) — Energy available to consumers: NPP = GPP − plant respiration.
  • biomass — Total mass of living organic material in an area.
  • limiting factor — Abiotic factor that most constrains growth or productivity.
  • secondary productivity — Rate of energy storage by consumers (animals); lower than NPP due to inefficiency.
Objectives
  • Calculate NPP from GPP and respiration values.
  • Explain why energy flows unidirectionally while matter cycles.
  • Identify limiting factors for productivity in different biomes.
How this can be tested
  • Calculating energy available at each trophic level from NPP data.
  • Comparing productivity across biomes and explaining the differences.
  • Free-response: calculating biomass supported at higher trophic levels.

Biogeochemical Cycles

Learn on this site
Carbon Cycle

Carbon cycles through photosynthesis (atmosphere → biosphere), respiration and decomposition (biosphere → atmosphere), and ocean absorption.

Common misconception: Human burning of fossil fuels adds ancient stored carbon to the active cycle, disrupting the balance.

How it can be tested: Trace carbon through the cycle and identify human impacts on each pathway.

Nitrogen Cycle

N₂ is fixed by bacteria (or lightning) to NH₃/NH₄⁺, nitrified to NO₃⁻, used by plants, and denitrified back to N₂.

Common misconception: Plants cannot use N₂ directly; nitrogen fixation is the critical entry point.

How it can be tested: Sequence the steps of nitrogen cycling and identify the organisms responsible.

Phosphorus Cycle

Phosphorus cycles from rock (weathering) → soil/water → organisms → decomposition; there is no atmospheric reservoir.

Common misconception: Unlike carbon and nitrogen, phosphorus has no significant atmospheric form.

How it can be tested: Explain why phosphorus is a limiting nutrient and how it enters aquatic systems.

Key Vocabulary
  • nitrogen fixation — Conversion of atmospheric N₂ to ammonia (NH₃) by bacteria such as Rhizobium.
  • nitrification — Bacterial conversion of NH₃ to nitrite then nitrate (plant-available form).
  • denitrification — Bacterial conversion of nitrate back to N₂ gas; removes nitrogen from soil.
  • weathering — Physical and chemical breakdown of rock releasing minerals (including phosphorus).
  • eutrophication — Excessive nutrient (N, P) enrichment of water causing algal blooms and oxygen depletion.
Objectives
  • Trace carbon through the major reservoirs and pathways.
  • Sequence nitrogen cycling steps and name responsible organisms.
  • Explain why phosphorus lacks an atmospheric reservoir and is often limiting.
How this can be tested
  • Identifying steps where human activity disrupts nutrient cycles.
  • Explaining eutrophication as a consequence of nutrient cycle disruption.
  • Comparing carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus cycles.

Aquatic and Terrestrial Biomes

Learn on this site
Terrestrial Biomes

Biomes are defined by climate (temperature and precipitation); major types include tropical rainforest, desert, grassland, temperate forest, taiga, and tundra.

Common misconception: Biomes are defined by climate, not geography — similar climates produce similar biomes on different continents.

How it can be tested: Match climate conditions (precipitation × temperature graphs) to biome types.

Aquatic Biomes

Marine biomes (open ocean, coral reefs, estuaries) and freshwater biomes (streams, lakes, wetlands) differ in salinity, productivity, and biodiversity.

Common misconception: Estuaries are among the most productive ecosystems due to nutrient inputs from both freshwater and marine sources.

How it can be tested: Compare productivity and biodiversity of marine vs. freshwater biomes.

Freshwater Zones

Lakes have littoral (near-shore), limnetic (open water), and profundal (deep, dark) zones; stratification changes seasonally.

Common misconception: Thermal stratification in summer limits nutrient mixing between surface and deep layers.

How it can be tested: Describe lake zones and explain how seasonal turnover affects nutrient distribution.

Key Vocabulary
  • biome — Large-scale community defined by characteristic climate and vegetation.
  • estuary — Transition zone where freshwater and saltwater mix; highly productive.
  • littoral zone — Shallow near-shore area of a lake with rooted aquatic plants.
  • thermocline — Layer of rapid temperature change with depth in a stratified lake.
  • upwelling — Vertical movement of cold, nutrient-rich water to the ocean surface; supports high productivity.
Objectives
  • Match climate graphs to terrestrial biome types.
  • Compare productivity of marine, freshwater, and terrestrial biomes.
  • Explain lake stratification and seasonal turnover.
How this can be tested
  • Identifying biomes from climate data.
  • Explaining why estuaries are highly productive.
  • Describing how thermal stratification affects lake oxygen and nutrients.
