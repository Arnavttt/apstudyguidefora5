GDP and National Income Accounting

Learn on this site
What GDP Measures

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given year. It measures economic output, not well-being — it excludes non-market production (household work) and ignores distribution of income.

Common misconception: GDP counts only FINAL goods to avoid double-counting — intermediate goods (steel used in a car) are not counted separately because their value is embedded in the final car's price.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to identify what is and is not counted in GDP.

Expenditure Approach: C + I + G + NX

GDP = Consumption (C) + Investment (I) + Government Spending (G) + Net Exports (NX = X − M). Investment in GDP means business spending on physical capital (equipment, construction), not stock purchases. Government spending excludes transfer payments.

Common misconception: Transfer payments (Social Security, welfare) are NOT included in G — they are not payment for current production, just redistribution of income.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to calculate GDP from a table using the expenditure approach.

Real vs. Nominal GDP and the GDP Deflator

Nominal GDP is measured in current prices; real GDP adjusts for inflation using a base year price level. Real GDP growth indicates actual output growth; nominal GDP growth can reflect inflation. The GDP deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100.

Common misconception: Real GDP, not nominal GDP, is used to compare output across years — if nominal GDP rose 10% but prices rose 8%, real growth was only about 2%.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests the distinction between real and nominal GDP; FRQ asks you to calculate real GDP or the GDP deflator.

Key Vocabulary
  • GDP — Market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a year
  • final goods — Goods sold to the end user; counted in GDP to avoid double-counting intermediate inputs
  • nominal GDP — GDP measured at current prices, not adjusted for inflation
  • real GDP — GDP adjusted for inflation using a base year price level; measures actual output
  • GDP deflator — Price index: (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100; measures economy-wide inflation
Objectives
  • Explain what GDP measures and what it excludes.
  • Calculate GDP using the expenditure approach (C + I + G + NX).
  • Distinguish real from nominal GDP and calculate the GDP deflator.
How this can be tested

  • FRQ calculates GDP from a table using the expenditure approach.
  • MCQ and FRQ distinguish nominal and real GDP.
Practice before moving on

  • Explain why intermediate goods are excluded from GDP.

The Business Cycle

Learn on this site
Phases of the Business Cycle

The business cycle describes recurring fluctuations in economic activity: expansion (rising output and employment), peak (maximum output), contraction/recession (falling output), and trough (minimum output). A recession is typically defined as two consecutive quarters of negative real GDP growth.

Common misconception: The business cycle is not regular or predictable — its timing and severity vary. Students sometimes assume cycles are fixed-length.

How it can be tested: MCQ identifies business cycle phases from a graph or description.

Output Gap

The output gap is the difference between actual GDP and potential GDP (what the economy would produce at full employment). A negative output gap (recessionary gap) means the economy is producing below potential; a positive output gap (inflationary gap) means it is producing above potential.

Common misconception: Potential GDP is not the maximum possible output — it is the output level consistent with full employment (the natural rate of unemployment), which still includes frictional and structural unemployment.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to identify the type of output gap and its implications for policy.

Leading, Lagging, and Coincident Indicators

Leading indicators (stock prices, building permits, consumer confidence) change before the economy does. Lagging indicators (unemployment rate, interest rates) change after. Coincident indicators (real GDP, personal income) change at the same time as the economy.

Common misconception: The unemployment rate is a lagging indicator — it continues to rise even after a recession ends because firms wait to hire until they are confident the recovery is sustained.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to classify a given economic statistic as leading, lagging, or coincident.

Key Vocabulary
  • business cycle — Recurring fluctuations in economic activity through expansion, peak, contraction, and trough
  • recession — Two consecutive quarters of negative real GDP growth
  • output gap — Difference between actual GDP and potential GDP
  • recessionary gap — Negative output gap: actual GDP below potential GDP
  • inflationary gap — Positive output gap: actual GDP above potential GDP
Objectives
  • Identify and describe the four phases of the business cycle.
  • Explain the difference between a recessionary gap and an inflationary gap.
  • Classify economic indicators as leading, lagging, or coincident.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ identifies business cycle phases from a graph.
  • FRQ identifies an output gap and recommends policy.
  • MCQ classifies economic indicators.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw a business cycle graph and label expansion, peak, contraction, trough, potential GDP, and output gaps.
  • Explain why unemployment is a lagging indicator.

Unemployment

Learn on this site
Types of Unemployment

Frictional unemployment is short-term job searching between positions. Structural unemployment occurs when skills don't match available jobs (often due to technology or industry shifts). Cyclical unemployment is caused by recessions — demand for labor falls with economic output. Seasonal unemployment follows predictable seasonal patterns.

Common misconception: Only cyclical unemployment is the target of stabilization policy. Frictional and structural unemployment always exist even at "full employment" — trying to eliminate them would cause inflation.

How it can be tested: MCQ gives a scenario and asks you to classify the type of unemployment.

Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU)

The natural rate of unemployment (NRU) is the unemployment rate when the economy is at full employment — it includes frictional and structural unemployment but not cyclical. The NRU is typically estimated at 4–5% in the U.S.

Common misconception: Full employment does NOT mean zero unemployment — the NRU is typically 4–5%. Students often think full employment = 0% unemployment.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to identify whether the economy is at, above, or below the NRU and what that implies for policy.

Unemployment Rate and Labor Force

Unemployment rate = (Unemployed / Labor Force) × 100. The labor force includes employed and actively job-seeking people; it excludes discouraged workers (who stopped looking), retirees, and students. Discouraged workers understate the true unemployment problem.

Common misconception: The official unemployment rate understates joblessness by excluding discouraged workers and part-time workers seeking full-time work.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to calculate unemployment rate or determine who is/is not counted in the labor force.

Key Vocabulary
  • frictional unemployment — Short-term unemployment while searching for a job between positions
  • structural unemployment — Unemployment due to skills mismatch — workers' skills don't match available jobs
  • cyclical unemployment — Unemployment caused by recessions; falls when economy recovers
  • natural rate of unemployment (NRU) — The unemployment rate at full employment; includes frictional and structural only
  • discouraged workers — People who stopped looking for work; not counted in the official unemployment rate
Objectives
  • Classify unemployment as frictional, structural, or cyclical using a scenario.
  • Explain the natural rate of unemployment and why it is not zero.
  • Calculate the unemployment rate and identify who is excluded from the labor force.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ classifies unemployment type from a scenario.
  • FRQ identifies NRU and output gap implications.
  • MCQ calculates unemployment rate or labor force participation.
Practice before moving on
  • List four types of unemployment and give an example of each.
  • Explain why the official unemployment rate understates true joblessness.

Inflation and Price Indices

Learn on this site
What Inflation Measures

Inflation is a sustained rise in the general price level. It reduces purchasing power — $100 buys less when prices rise. The Consumer Price Index (CPI) measures inflation by tracking the price of a fixed basket of goods typical of an urban consumer.

Common misconception: Inflation is measured as a RATE OF CHANGE of prices, not the price level itself — 5% inflation means prices are rising 5% per year, not that prices are high.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to calculate the inflation rate from CPI data.

CPI Calculation and Limitations

CPI = (Cost of basket in current year / Cost of basket in base year) × 100. Inflation rate = ((CPI₂ − CPI₁) / CPI₁) × 100. The CPI overstates inflation because it ignores substitution (consumers switch to cheaper goods), quality improvements, and new products.

Common misconception: The CPI uses a FIXED basket — it ignores the fact that consumers substitute away from goods that get expensive. This makes it overstate the true cost of living increase.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to calculate CPI and inflation rate; MCQ asks about CPI limitations.

Effects of Inflation and Deflation

Unexpected inflation hurts savers, lenders, and those on fixed incomes; it benefits borrowers and debtors. Deflation (falling prices) can be more damaging than inflation — it leads consumers to delay purchases, reducing aggregate demand.

Common misconception: Moderate inflation (2%) is considered healthy by central banks — deflation is more dangerous because it creates deflationary spirals where falling prices lead to falling output.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks who is helped and hurt by unexpected inflation.

Key Vocabulary
  • inflation — A sustained increase in the general price level, reducing purchasing power
  • CPI — Consumer Price Index — measures inflation by tracking the price of a fixed consumer goods basket
  • deflation — A sustained decrease in the general price level
  • purchasing power — The quantity of goods and services a unit of money can buy
  • real interest rate — Nominal interest rate minus the inflation rate; measures the true cost of borrowing
Objectives
  • Calculate CPI and the inflation rate from price data.
  • Explain two limitations of the CPI as an inflation measure.
  • Identify who is helped and hurt by unexpected inflation.
How this can be tested
  • FRQ calculates CPI and inflation rate.
  • MCQ identifies CPI limitations.
  • MCQ asks who benefits or is harmed by unexpected inflation.
Practice before moving on
  • Calculate the CPI and inflation rate given basket price data for two years.
  • Explain why deflation is considered more dangerous than moderate inflation.
