Balance of Payments

Learn on this site
Current Account

The current account records exports and imports of goods and services, income payments, and transfers. A current account surplus means a country exports more than it imports (net exporter); a deficit means it imports more than it exports (net importer). The U.S. runs a persistent current account deficit.

Common misconception: Trade balance and current account are related but not identical — the current account also includes income payments (dividends, interest) and transfers, not just trade in goods.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to classify transactions as current account or capital account.

Capital/Financial Account

The capital/financial account records flows of financial assets: foreign direct investment (FDI), portfolio investment (stocks, bonds), and official reserve transactions. A current account deficit is always offset by a capital account surplus — the balance of payments must sum to zero.

Common misconception: The balance of payments always balances — a trade deficit (current account) is automatically financed by foreign capital inflows (capital account surplus). They are two sides of the same coin.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to explain the relationship between the current account deficit and capital account surplus.

Balance of Payments Identity

Current account + Capital account = 0. If a country has a $500B current account deficit, it must have a $500B capital account surplus — foreigners are investing $500B in the country. This is why trade deficits are associated with foreign ownership of domestic assets.

Common misconception: A trade deficit is not inherently bad — it reflects foreigners willingness to invest in the country. But it means the country is consuming more than it produces, financed by foreign saving.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to apply the balance of payments identity.

Key Vocabulary
  • balance of payments — Record of all economic transactions between a country and the rest of the world
  • current account — Records exports, imports, income, and transfers; surplus = net exporter
  • capital/financial account — Records flows of financial assets and investments between countries
  • trade deficit — Imports exceed exports; a negative trade balance
  • foreign direct investment (FDI) — Investment in which a firm acquires ownership or control of a foreign business
Objectives
  • Distinguish the current account from the capital/financial account.
  • Explain why the balance of payments sums to zero.
  • Explain the relationship between a trade deficit and capital inflows.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ classifies transactions as current or capital account.
  • FRQ explains balance of payments identity.
  • MCQ applies BOP = 0 to determine capital account from current account.
Practice before moving on
  • Classify five international transactions as current account or capital account.
  • If the U.S. runs a $600B current account deficit, what must be true of the capital account?

Exchange Rates

Learn on this site
Nominal Exchange Rates

The exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another. If the dollar appreciates (strengthens), it buys more foreign currency; exports become more expensive for foreigners (fall) and imports become cheaper for Americans (rise). Appreciation causes the trade deficit to widen.

Common misconception: Dollar appreciation makes U.S. exports more expensive for foreigners — counterintuitive but important. Students sometimes think a strong dollar is always good for trade.

How it can be tested: MCQ and FRQ ask you to trace the effects of currency appreciation or depreciation.

Currency Market (Forex)

In the foreign exchange (forex) market, currencies are bought and sold. Demand for dollars comes from foreigners wanting to buy U.S. goods, services, or assets. Supply of dollars comes from Americans wanting to buy foreign goods or invest abroad. The equilibrium determines the exchange rate.

Common misconception: Dollar demand in the forex market equals demand for U.S. exports and U.S. assets — understanding what drives each component is key to predicting exchange rate changes.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to draw the foreign exchange market and shift supply or demand.

Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)

Purchasing power parity (PPP) says exchange rates should adjust until a basket of goods costs the same in all countries. If the U.S. has higher inflation than Japan, the dollar should depreciate relative to the yen. PPP holds in the long run but not reliably in the short run.

Common misconception: PPP is a long-run equilibrium concept — short-run exchange rates are driven by interest rate differentials, speculation, and capital flows, not just relative prices.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to apply PPP logic to predict long-run currency movements.

Key Vocabulary
  • exchange rate — The price of one currency in terms of another
  • appreciation — An increase in the value of a currency relative to another; makes exports more expensive
  • depreciation — A decrease in the value of a currency; makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive
  • forex market — The foreign exchange market where currencies are bought and sold
  • purchasing power parity (PPP) — Theory that exchange rates adjust to equalize the price of a basket of goods across countries
Objectives
  • Explain the effects of dollar appreciation on exports, imports, and the trade balance.
  • Draw the foreign exchange market and identify what shifts supply and demand for dollars.
  • Apply purchasing power parity to predict long-run currency movements.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ traces effects of currency appreciation/depreciation.
  • FRQ draws forex market and shifts supply/demand.
  • MCQ applies PPP logic.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw the forex market for dollars; show a shift caused by rising U.S. interest rates.
  • Trace: dollar appreciates → exports → imports → trade balance.

Exchange Rate Policy and Macro Connections

Learn on this site
Fixed vs. Flexible Exchange Rates

Countries can maintain fixed exchange rates (pegged to another currency or gold) by buying/selling their own currency in the forex market, or allow flexible (floating) rates determined by market forces. The U.S. uses a floating rate; China managed its exchange rate for decades.

Common misconception: A fixed exchange rate requires a country to hold large foreign exchange reserves to intervene in the market — it sacrifices monetary policy independence.

How it can be tested: MCQ distinguishes fixed from flexible exchange rate regimes.

Interest Rates and Exchange Rates

Higher domestic interest rates attract foreign capital, increasing demand for the currency and causing appreciation. This is the interest rate-exchange rate link: expansionary monetary policy (lower rates) → dollar depreciates → exports rise → AD shifts right (partially).

Common misconception: The exchange rate effect partially reinforces monetary policy — lower rates depreciate the currency, boosting net exports and adding to the AD stimulus from lower investment rates.

How it can be tested: FRQ may ask you to trace the full effect of monetary policy including the exchange rate channel.

Net Export Effect of Macro Policy

Expansionary fiscal policy → higher interest rates (crowding out) → dollar appreciates → exports fall / imports rise → NX falls → partially offsets the fiscal stimulus. This is the net export effect (or open economy crowding out).

Common misconception: In an open economy, expansionary fiscal policy is less effective than a closed economy model predicts because higher interest rates appreciate the currency and reduce net exports.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to trace the complete open-economy effect of fiscal policy.

Key Vocabulary
  • fixed exchange rate — Exchange rate pegged to another currency; requires foreign reserve intervention to maintain
  • floating exchange rate — Exchange rate determined by market supply and demand; used by the U.S.
  • net export effect — Fiscal expansion raises interest rates → appreciates currency → reduces NX, partially offsetting stimulus
  • capital inflow — Foreign funds entering a country to buy assets; increases demand for the currency
  • open economy crowding out — In an open economy, fiscal expansion also crowds out net exports via exchange rate appreciation
Objectives
  • Compare fixed and flexible exchange rate regimes.
  • Trace the complete effect of expansionary monetary policy including the exchange rate channel.
  • Explain the net export effect of expansionary fiscal policy in an open economy.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ distinguishes fixed from flexible exchange rates.
  • FRQ traces monetary policy through both interest rate and exchange rate channels.
  • FRQ traces open-economy effect of fiscal policy.
Practice before moving on
  • Trace: expansionary fiscal policy → interest rates → exchange rate → NX → net AD effect.
  • Explain why a fixed exchange rate requires giving up independent monetary policy.

Trade Deficits, Surpluses, and Globalization

Learn on this site
Trade Deficits: Causes and Consequences

Trade deficits occur when imports exceed exports; they are financed by capital inflows (foreigners investing in the deficit country). A trade deficit can reflect strong domestic growth (high income → high imports) or low competitiveness. It is not inherently harmful but does mean the country is a net debtor internationally.

Common misconception: Trade deficits are not always caused by unfair foreign trade practices — they are often the result of high domestic spending relative to domestic saving.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to explain what causes trade deficits and their relationship to saving and investment.

Saving, Investment, and Trade Balance

Trade deficit = domestic investment − domestic saving (including government saving). If I > S (domestic saving), the gap is filled by foreign saving (capital inflows). A government budget deficit reduces national saving, all else equal, widening the trade deficit — the "twin deficits" hypothesis.

Common misconception: The trade balance is ultimately a macroeconomic phenomenon tied to saving and investment patterns, not just trade policy — tariffs alone cannot close a trade deficit if the saving-investment gap persists.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to explain the twin deficits connection.

Globalization and the Open Economy

Globalization increases trade, capital flows, and labor mobility across borders. It raises average living standards through specialization and comparative advantage but can create domestic losers (import-competing industries). Multinational corporations, global supply chains, and financial integration define the modern open economy.

Common misconception: Globalization's distributional effects are real — even if aggregate GDP rises, workers in import-competing industries can be permanently harmed. AP® macro focuses on aggregate effects but recognizes distributional trade-offs.

How it can be tested: FRQ may ask you to evaluate the macroeconomic effects of increased international integration.

Key Vocabulary
  • trade deficit — Imports exceed exports; the current account is negative; must be financed by capital inflows
  • twin deficits — Theory linking government budget deficits to trade deficits through reduced national saving
  • national saving — Private saving plus government saving (budget surplus) or minus government borrowing (deficit)
  • comparative advantage — The basis for mutually beneficial trade even when one country is absolutely more productive
  • globalization — Increasing integration of national economies through trade, capital flows, and labor mobility
Objectives
  • Explain the relationship between saving, investment, and the trade balance.
  • Describe the twin deficits hypothesis and evaluate its validity.
  • Evaluate the macroeconomic costs and benefits of globalization.
How this can be tested
  • FRQ explains twin deficits and saving-investment identity.
  • MCQ asks what causes trade deficits.
  • FRQ evaluates globalization effects on a specific economy.
Practice before moving on
  • Explain why reducing a government budget deficit could reduce a trade deficit.
  • Explain how a tariff can improve the trade balance in the short run but may not close it in the long run.
