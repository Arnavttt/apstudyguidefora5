Staff, Clefs, and Pitch Notation

Learn on this site
Reading the Staff

The staff has five lines and four spaces; pitch names depend on which clef is used. Treble clef circles G4; bass clef marks F3.

Common misconception: Students mix up which line the clef marks; treble clef circles the second line (G), not the first.

How it can be tested: Identify any note on treble or bass staff; write a given pitch name on the staff.

Ledger Lines and Middle C

Ledger lines extend the staff above and below; middle C (C4) sits on a ledger line just below the treble staff and just above the bass staff.

Common misconception: Students forget that C4 is on a ledger line in both treble and bass clef, not on a main staff line.

How it can be tested: Identify notes on ledger lines; write C4 in both clefs.

Key Vocabulary
  • treble clef — G clef: circles around the second line, identifying it as G4
  • bass clef — F clef: two dots around the fourth line, identifying it as F3
  • ledger line — Short line added above or below the staff for notes outside its range
  • middle C — C4 — the C in the middle of the piano keyboard; written on a ledger line in both treble and bass clef
Objectives
  • Identify any note on treble and bass staff
  • Write notes in treble and bass clef from letter names
  • Explain the function of ledger lines
How this can be tested
  • Identify pitch names from notation
  • Write given pitches on a staff
  • Recognize clef symbols and their reference pitches
Practice before moving on
  • Write all C pitches on a grand staff
  • Name every note in a short melody in treble and bass clef

Major Scales and Key Signatures

Learn on this site
Major Scale Construction

A major scale follows W-W-H-W-W-W-H. Starting on any pitch and applying this pattern gives that major scale.

Common misconception: Students forget the exact placement of the half steps (between 3-4 and 7-8). Counting semitones rather than using the pattern leads to errors.

How it can be tested: Build any major scale from a given starting pitch; identify the key from a key signature.

Circle of Fifths and Key Signatures

Moving clockwise around the circle of fifths adds sharps; counter-clockwise adds flats. The mnemonic for sharps: F C G D A E B; for flats: B E A D G C F.

Common misconception: Students confuse the order of sharps and flats; each new key adds exactly one sharp/flat in a specific order.

How it can be tested: Identify the major key from any key signature; write the correct key signature given a key name.

Key Vocabulary
  • whole step — Two half steps; skips one key on the keyboard
  • half step — The smallest interval; adjacent keys on the keyboard
  • key signature — Sharps or flats placed at the beginning of a staff to indicate the prevailing key
  • circle of fifths — Diagram showing all 12 major (and minor) keys arranged by the number of sharps/flats
Objectives
  • Construct major scales using the W-W-H-W-W-W-H pattern
  • Identify any major key from its key signature
  • Write correct key signatures for any major key
How this can be tested
  • Write the key signature for F major
  • Identify the key from 3 sharps
  • Spell a D major scale
Practice before moving on
  • Write all major scales starting from C, G, D, A, E, B, F, B♭, E♭, A♭, D♭, G♭
