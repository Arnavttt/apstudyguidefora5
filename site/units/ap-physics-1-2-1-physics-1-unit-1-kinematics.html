Describing Motion: Position, Velocity, Acceleration

Learn on this site
Displacement vs. Distance

Displacement is the vector change in position (Δx = x_f − x_i); distance is the total path length (scalar). An object returning to its start has zero displacement but non-zero distance.

Common misconception: Students use distance and displacement interchangeably; only displacement is a vector and can be negative.

How it can be tested: Given a position-time graph, identify intervals where displacement and distance differ.

Average vs. Instantaneous Velocity

Average velocity = Δx/Δt (slope of secant on x-t graph). Instantaneous velocity = slope of tangent on x-t graph. Speed = |velocity|.

Common misconception: Students think average speed equals average velocity; they agree only for constant-speed motion in one direction.

How it can be tested: Calculate average velocity over an interval from a graph; estimate instantaneous velocity from tangent slope.

Acceleration

Acceleration a = Δv/Δt is the rate of change of velocity. Positive a does not always mean speeding up — an object moving in the negative direction with positive a is slowing down.

Common misconception: Students equate positive acceleration with speeding up; acceleration direction relative to velocity direction determines speeding up vs. slowing down.

How it can be tested: From a v-t graph, identify where an object is speeding up, slowing down, and momentarily at rest.

Key Vocabulary
  • displacement — Vector change in position: Δx = x_f − x_i
  • average velocity — v_avg = Δx/Δt; slope of secant on x-t graph
  • instantaneous velocity — Slope of tangent on x-t graph at a specific moment
  • acceleration — a = Δv/Δt; rate of change of velocity
Objectives
  • Compute displacement and distance from a path description
  • Read velocity and acceleration from kinematic graphs
  • Determine when an object is speeding up or slowing down
How this can be tested
  • Multiple representations tested: graphs, diagrams, equations
  • Graph interpretation in both MC and FR
  • Speeding up/slowing down reasoning required in FR justification
Practice before moving on
  • Given an x-t graph, find average velocity from t=0 to t=4 s
  • From a v-t graph, identify when the object is at rest and when it is speeding up

Kinematic Equations for Constant Acceleration

Learn on this site
The Big Four Kinematic Equations

For constant acceleration: (1) v=v₀+at, (2) x=x₀+v₀t+½at², (3) v²=v₀²+2aΔx, (4) x=x₀+(v+v₀)/2·t. Choose the equation missing the unknown you do not need.

Common misconception: Students try to memorize all four; instead, identify which variable is missing (not given, not asked) and choose the equation without it.

How it can be tested: Identify the given and unknown quantities, select the appropriate kinematic equation, and solve.

Free Fall

Near Earth's surface, all objects fall with a=−g=−9.8 m/s² (taking up as positive). Horizontal velocity is unaffected. Neglect air resistance unless stated.

Common misconception: Students use g=+9.8 m/s² without a sign convention and get wrong directions; always define a positive direction first.

How it can be tested: Apply kinematic equations to free-fall problems with defined sign conventions.

Graphical Relationships

Slope of x-t graph = velocity. Slope of v-t graph = acceleration. Area under v-t graph = displacement. Area under a-t graph = Δv.

Common misconception: Students confuse slope and area; slope gives rate of change, area gives accumulated quantity.

How it can be tested: Extract displacement and acceleration from v-t and a-t graphs using slope and area.

Key Vocabulary
  • kinematic equations — Four equations valid for constant-acceleration motion
  • free fall — Motion under gravity alone; a=−9.8 m/s² (up positive)
  • area under v-t graph — Equals displacement over that time interval
  • slope of v-t graph — Equals acceleration at that moment
Objectives
  • Select and apply kinematic equations for given scenarios
  • Solve free-fall problems with correct sign conventions
  • Use graphical methods to find displacement and acceleration
How this can be tested
  • Kinematic equations tested in MC and FR
  • Graph area/slope questions appear frequently
  • Free-fall problems include vertical launch and dropped objects
Practice before moving on
  • A ball is thrown upward at 15 m/s; find max height and time to return
  • From a v-t graph, find displacement from t=0 to t=5 s using area

Projectile Motion

Learn on this site
Independence of Horizontal and Vertical Motion

A projectile has constant horizontal velocity (aₓ=0) and constant downward acceleration (aᵧ=−g). The two directions are completely independent.

Common misconception: Students think horizontal speed decreases during projectile flight; only air resistance (not gravity) affects horizontal velocity.

How it can be tested: Decompose projectile motion into independent horizontal and vertical components; solve each separately.

Range and Time of Flight

Time of flight determined entirely by vertical component. Horizontal range R=vₓ·t. For symmetric launch/landing at same height: t=2v₀sin θ/g, R=v₀²sin(2θ)/g.

Common misconception: Students add horizontal and vertical components vectorially to find range; range is just vₓ × total time.

How it can be tested: Calculate range, max height, and time of flight for angled projectiles.

Relative Velocity

Velocity of A relative to B = v_A − v_B. Used for boats crossing rivers, planes in wind. Add vectors using component method.

Common misconception: Students forget that relative velocity requires vector subtraction, not just magnitude comparison.

How it can be tested: Find velocity of object A relative to reference frame B using vector addition/subtraction.

Key Vocabulary
  • projectile — Object launched with initial velocity and subject only to gravity
  • horizontal component — v_x = v₀cos θ; constant throughout flight
  • vertical component — v_y = v₀sin θ − gt; changes due to gravity
  • range — Horizontal distance traveled during flight: R = v_x · t
Objectives
  • Decompose initial velocity into components
  • Find time of flight, max height, and range
  • Apply relative velocity for observers in different reference frames
How this can be tested
  • Projectile problems in FR require component decomposition explicitly shown
  • Range formula and angle-for-max-range tested in MC
  • Relative velocity in both MC and FR
Practice before moving on
  • A ball launched at 30° above horizontal at 20 m/s; find range and max height
  • A boat can travel 5 m/s in still water; river flows at 3 m/s; find actual velocity

Circular Motion and Reference Frames

Learn on this site
Uniform Circular Motion

An object moving at constant speed in a circle has centripetal acceleration a_c=v²/r directed toward the center. The net force equals ma_c=mv²/r (centripetal force).

Common misconception: Students think there is a centrifugal force pushing outward; in an inertial frame only the centripetal force (inward) exists. "Centrifugal force" is a fictitious force felt in the rotating frame.

How it can be tested: Calculate centripetal acceleration and force for circular motion; identify the physical source of the centripetal force.

Period and Frequency

Period T = 2πr/v = time for one revolution. Frequency f = 1/T = revolutions per second (Hz). Speed v = 2πr/T = 2πrf.

Common misconception: Students confuse period with frequency; T is seconds per cycle, f is cycles per second.

How it can be tested: Calculate T, f, and v for circular motion; convert between them.

Non-Uniform Circular Motion

If speed changes, there is a tangential component of acceleration in addition to the centripetal component. The net acceleration is the vector sum of centripetal and tangential.

Common misconception: Students add centripetal and tangential accelerations as scalars; they are perpendicular and must be added as vectors.

How it can be tested: Identify when both centripetal and tangential acceleration are present and find their resultant.

Key Vocabulary
  • centripetal acceleration — a_c = v²/r directed toward center of circle
  • centripetal force — Net inward force = mv²/r; provided by tension, gravity, friction, or normal force
  • period — T = time for one full revolution; T = 2πr/v
  • frequency — f = 1/T; revolutions per second
Objectives
  • Calculate centripetal acceleration and force for circular motion
  • Find period, frequency, and speed for circular motion
  • Identify the source of centripetal force in physical scenarios
How this can be tested
  • Centripetal force problems: identify source (tension, gravity, friction)
  • Period and frequency conversions in MC
  • Free-body diagrams for circular motion in FR
Practice before moving on
  • Find centripetal acceleration of a 2 kg ball on a 0.5 m string at 4 m/s
  • A car rounds a curve of radius 40 m at 20 m/s; find centripetal acceleration and required friction
