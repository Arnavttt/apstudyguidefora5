Torque

Learn on this site
Torque Definition

τ = rF sinθ where r is the moment arm length, F is the force, and θ is the angle between r and F. Torque is the rotational equivalent of force.

Common misconception: Students use r as the distance to the force application point without accounting for the angle; τ = r × F × sinθ (or lever arm × F).

How it can be tested: Calculate torque for various force directions using τ = r·F·sinθ or lever arm × F.

Lever Arm

The lever arm (or moment arm) is the perpendicular distance from the rotation axis to the line of action of the force. τ = lever arm × F.

Common misconception: Students confuse the distance to where force is applied with the lever arm; the lever arm is the perpendicular distance to the force's line of action.

How it can be tested: Find the lever arm geometrically and calculate torque for angled forces.

Sign of Torque

Choose a sign convention: counterclockwise (CCW) positive, clockwise (CW) negative (or reverse). Must be consistent throughout a problem. Net torque = Στ determines rotation direction.

Common misconception: Students forget to assign signs to torques; a torque that produces CCW rotation must be opposite in sign to one producing CW rotation.

How it can be tested: Assign correct signs to all torques and sum them to find net torque.

Key Vocabulary
  • torque — τ = rFsinθ; rotational effect of a force, unit N·m
  • lever arm — Perpendicular distance from axis to force's line of action
  • CCW positive convention — Counterclockwise torques are positive; clockwise are negative
  • net torque — Στ = algebraic sum of all torques on an object
Objectives
  • Calculate torque from force and distance using the angle formula
  • Identify the lever arm for angled forces
  • Sum torques with correct signs to find net torque
How this can be tested
  • Torque calculations in both MC and FR
  • Lever arm must be identified geometrically in FR
  • Sign of torque crucial for equilibrium problems
Practice before moving on
  • A 30 N force at 90° acts 0.5 m from pivot; find τ
  • A 40 N force at 30° from horizontal acts 0.8 m from pivot; find τ

Rotational Equilibrium

Learn on this site
Conditions for Static Equilibrium

For static equilibrium: (1) ΣF=0 (translational equilibrium) and (2) Στ=0 (rotational equilibrium). Both must hold. The torque equation can be taken about any convenient pivot point.

Common misconception: Students apply only ΣF=0; rotational equilibrium (Στ=0) is an additional independent condition required for static equilibrium.

How it can be tested: Apply both ΣF=0 and Στ=0 to solve for unknown forces in static equilibrium problems.

Choosing the Pivot Point

Choose the pivot where an unknown force acts to eliminate it from the torque equation. This simplifies the algebra significantly.

Common misconception: Students take torques about an arbitrary point that includes unknowns; strategic pivot choice eliminates unknowns.

How it can be tested: Select the pivot to minimize unknowns in the torque equation; explain why the choice simplifies the solution.

Uniform Beam Problems

A uniform beam has its weight acting at its geometric center (CM). For beam problems: identify all forces (weight, supports, applied loads), write ΣF=0 and Στ=0.

Common misconception: Students forget the beam's own weight at the center; every object has weight acting at its CM.

How it can be tested: Include the weight of the beam at its midpoint when setting up equilibrium equations for beam problems.

Key Vocabulary
  • translational equilibrium — ΣF=0 in all directions
  • rotational equilibrium — Στ=0 about any pivot
  • static equilibrium — Both ΣF=0 and Στ=0; object at rest
  • uniform beam weight — Acts at geometric center; always include in torque sum
Objectives
  • Apply both equilibrium conditions to solve for unknown forces
  • Choose strategic pivot points to simplify torque equations
  • Solve uniform beam problems with multiple support forces
How this can be tested
  • Beam equilibrium is a classic AP® Physics FR problem
  • Must write both ΣF=0 and Στ=0 explicitly
  • Pivot choice strategy tested in MC and FR
Practice before moving on
  • A 4 m uniform beam (20 kg) rests on two supports at ends; find support forces
  • A 6 m beam supported at left end and 1 m from right end with 100 N load at right end

Rotational Kinematics and Dynamics

Learn on this site
Angular Kinematic Quantities

Angular position θ (radians), angular velocity ω (rad/s), angular acceleration α (rad/s²). Equations mirror linear kinematics: ω=ω₀+αt; θ=ω₀t+½αt²; ω²=ω₀²+2αθ.

Common misconception: Students mix linear and angular equations; angular equations use θ, ω, α; linear use x, v, a. Use arc length s=rθ and v=rω to connect them.

How it can be tested: Apply angular kinematic equations to rotating objects; convert between angular and linear quantities using s=rθ and v=rω.

Newton's Second Law for Rotation

Στ_net = Iα where I is the moment of inertia (I=Σmr² for point masses). The angular equivalent of F=ma.

Common misconception: Students use F=ma for rotational problems; for rotation, use Στ=Iα, where I depends on mass distribution.

How it can be tested: Apply Στ=Iα to find angular acceleration from net torque and moment of inertia.

Moment of Inertia

I = Σmᵢrᵢ² for point masses. For extended objects: disk I=½MR², solid sphere I=2/5MR², rod about center I=1/12ML². Larger I means harder to angularly accelerate.

Common misconception: Students think I=MR² for all objects; I depends on how mass is distributed relative to the axis.

How it can be tested: Identify the correct I formula for the shape, calculate I, and use in Στ=Iα.

Key Vocabulary
  • angular velocity — ω = Δθ/Δt; rad/s
  • angular acceleration — α = Δω/Δt; rad/s²
  • moment of inertia — I = Σmr²; resistance to angular acceleration
  • Στ=Iα — Rotational Newton's second law
Objectives
  • Apply angular kinematic equations to rotating systems
  • Apply Στ=Iα to find angular acceleration
  • Calculate moment of inertia for point mass systems
How this can be tested
  • Angular kinematics tested in MC using τ=Iα
  • Moment of inertia tables not needed — AP® gives I formulas
  • Converting between linear and angular with v=rω
Practice before moving on
  • A disk (I=2 kg·m²) has τ=6 N·m applied; find α
  • A point mass (0.5 kg) at 0.4 m from axis; calculate I

Angular Momentum

Learn on this site
Angular Momentum

L = Iω for a rigid body rotating about a fixed axis. Unit: kg·m²/s. L is a vector (direction given by right-hand rule): curl fingers in rotation direction, thumb points in L direction.

Common misconception: Students compute L=Iω and forget that L is a vector; direction is determined by the right-hand rule for the axis of rotation.

How it can be tested: Calculate angular momentum and state its direction using the right-hand rule.

Conservation of Angular Momentum

If the net external torque is zero, angular momentum is conserved: L_i = L_f (Iω_i = Iω_f). When I decreases (e.g., arms pulled in), ω increases to maintain L.

Common misconception: Students think ω is conserved; it is L=Iω that is conserved. If I changes, ω changes oppositely.

How it can be tested: Apply conservation of angular momentum to spinning skater, collapsing star, and rolling problems.

Angular Impulse

τ_net·Δt = ΔL (angular impulse-momentum theorem). Analogous to J=FΔt=Δp. A net torque over time changes angular momentum.

Common misconception: Students forget that angular impulse (not torque alone) changes L; just as force changes linear momentum, torque over time changes angular momentum.

How it can be tested: Apply angular impulse-momentum theorem to find change in angular momentum from a torque applied over time.

Key Vocabulary
  • angular momentum — L = Iω; rotational analogue of linear momentum
  • conservation of L — If Στ_ext=0, then L_i=L_f; Iω=constant
  • right-hand rule — Curl fingers in rotation direction; thumb = L direction
  • angular impulse — τ·Δt = ΔL; rotational analogue of FΔt=Δp
Objectives
  • Calculate angular momentum of rotating objects
  • Apply conservation of angular momentum when I changes
  • Use angular impulse-momentum theorem
How this can be tested
  • Conservation of L tested in skater/diver problems in MC and FR
  • L direction (right-hand rule) in MC
  • Angular impulse in FR
Practice before moving on
  • A skater with I=4 kg·m² at ω=3 rad/s pulls arms in to I=1 kg·m²; find new ω
  • Apply τ=5 N·m for 2 s to a stationary disk; find final L
