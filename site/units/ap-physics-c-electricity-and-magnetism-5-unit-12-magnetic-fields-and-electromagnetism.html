Faraday's Law and Electromagnetic Induction

Learn on this site
Faraday's Law

ε = -dΦ_B/dt; induced EMF equals the negative rate of change of magnetic flux. Flux: Φ_B = ∫B·dA.

Common misconception: The negative sign in Faraday's law is Lenz's law — the induced EMF opposes the change that caused it.

How it can be tested: Calculate induced EMF from changing flux; apply Lenz's law for direction.

Motional EMF

A conductor of length L moving at velocity v in field B generates EMF = BLv (perpendicular case). Current flows if circuit is closed.

Common misconception: Motional EMF is the work done per unit charge by the magnetic force on moving charges in the conductor (F = qv × B).

How it can be tested: Calculate motional EMF for conductors moving in B fields.

Lenz's Law

The induced current creates a magnetic field that opposes the change in flux. If flux is increasing, induced B opposes original B; if decreasing, induced B reinforces original B.

Common misconception: Lenz's law says induced current opposes the change in flux, not the flux itself — if flux is decreasing, induced current tries to maintain it.

How it can be tested: Apply Lenz's law to determine induced current direction in various scenarios.

Key Vocabulary
  • Magnetic flux — Φ_B = ∫B·dA = BA cosθ for uniform B (Wb = T·m²)
  • Faraday's law — ε = -dΦ_B/dt; induced EMF from changing flux
  • Motional EMF — ε = BLv for conductor moving perpendicular to B
  • Lenz's law — Induced current opposes change in flux (consequence of energy conservation)
  • Eddy currents — Induced currents in bulk conductors due to changing B; cause heating and braking
Objectives
  • Calculate induced EMF using Faraday's law for various flux changes.
  • Find motional EMF for a moving conductor in a B field.
  • Apply Lenz's law to determine induced current direction.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: find induced EMF and current for a loop entering a B field region.
  • Motional EMF: find current and force on a sliding bar in a magnetic field.
  • Lenz's law: determine direction of induced current in several scenarios.
Practice before moving on

Inductance and RL Circuits

Learn on this site
Self-Inductance

L = NΦ/I; induced EMF: ε = -L(dI/dt). For a solenoid: L = μ₀n²V = μ₀n²Al. Units: henry (H = V·s/A).

Common misconception: Self-inductance L depends on geometry only (like capacitance C) — not on current or applied EMF.

How it can be tested: Calculate L for solenoids; use ε = -L(dI/dt) for inductors.

RL Circuit Transients

Charging: I(t) = (ε/R)(1 - e^{-Rt/L}) = I_max(1 - e^{-t/τ}); τ = L/R. Discharging: I(t) = I₀e^{-t/τ}.

Common misconception: At t = 0, an inductor opposes change in current (acts like open circuit); at t = ∞, acts like a short circuit (wire).

How it can be tested: Derive and apply the RL transient equations.

Energy in an Inductor

U = ½LI²; stored in the magnetic field. Energy density in B field: u = B²/(2μ₀). Total energy in solenoid: U = ½LI² = B²Vol/(2μ₀).

Common misconception: Energy in an inductor is stored in the magnetic field throughout the volume, not at the terminals.

How it can be tested: Calculate energy stored in an inductor and energy density of a B field.

Key Vocabulary
  • Self-inductance L — L = NΦ/I; units henry (H); opposes current changes
  • Inductor EMF — ε = -L(dI/dt); inductor opposes changing current
  • RL time constant — τ = L/R; larger L or smaller R → slower transient
  • RL charging — I(t) = I_max(1-e^{-t/τ}); current increases exponentially toward I_max
  • Energy in inductor — U = ½LI²; magnetic field energy density u = B²/(2μ₀)
Objectives
  • Calculate self-inductance for solenoids.
  • Derive and apply RL transient equations for charging and discharging.
  • Calculate energy stored in an inductor and in a B field.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: derive I(t) for RL charging from the differential equation.
  • Calculate L for a toroid and find energy stored at given I.
  • Compare RL and RC transients: similarities and differences.
Practice before moving on
  • Derive the RL charging equation by solving the KVL ODE.
  • Calculate magnetic energy density for several B values.

Maxwell's Equations

Learn on this site
Gauss's Law for E and B

∮E·dA = Q_enc/ε₀ and ∮B·dA = 0. The first shows electric monopoles exist; the second shows magnetic monopoles do not.

Common misconception: The divergence of B is always zero — there are no magnetic monopole charges in nature.

How it can be tested: State Gauss's law for both E and B and explain the physical difference.

Faraday's Law and Ampere-Maxwell Law

Faraday: ∮E·dl = -dΦ_B/dt. Ampere-Maxwell: ∮B·dl = μ₀I_enc + μ₀ε₀dΦ_E/dt. The displacement current μ₀ε₀dΦ_E/dt is Maxwell's addition.

Common misconception: Displacement current is not actual current — it is an equivalent term for changing electric flux, producing a B field as if it were a real current.

How it can be tested: State all four Maxwell's equations and identify each's physical meaning.

Electromagnetic Waves

Maxwell's equations predict EM waves traveling at c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀) ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s. E and B are perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation; in phase.

Common misconception: E and B in an EM wave are in phase (both reach max at the same time), not 90° out of phase.

How it can be tested: State the wave speed prediction from Maxwell's equations and describe E and B in an EM wave.

Key Vocabulary
  • Gauss E — ∮E·dA = Q_enc/ε₀; electric monopoles exist
  • Gauss B — ∮B·dA = 0; no magnetic monopoles
  • Faraday's law — ∮E·dl = -dΦ_B/dt; changing B induces E
  • Ampere-Maxwell — ∮B·dl = μ₀I_enc + μ₀ε₀dΦ_E/dt; changing E induces B
  • Displacement current — ε₀dΦ_E/dt; Maxwell's addition; allows EM waves to propagate
Objectives
  • State all four Maxwell's equations in integral form.
  • Explain the physical meaning of the displacement current.
  • Derive the wave speed c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀) from Maxwell's equations.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: write and name all four Maxwell's equations.
  • Calculate c from μ₀ and ε₀ numerically.
  • Conceptual: explain why a changing E field produces B even with no current.
Practice before moving on
  • Memorize all four Maxwell's equations in integral form.
  • Explain displacement current in a charging capacitor.

LC and RLC Circuits

Learn on this site
LC Oscillations

An ideal LC circuit oscillates: q(t) = Q₀cos(ωt); ω = 1/√(LC). Energy alternates between capacitor (½CV²) and inductor (½LI²). Analogous to SHM.

Common misconception: LC oscillations are analogous to mechanical SHM: L↔m, C↔1/k, q↔x, I↔v. All SHM results apply.

How it can be tested: Derive the LC oscillation equation and find ω, T.

RLC Circuits

Adding R to an LC circuit causes damped oscillations: q(t) = Q₀e^{-Rt/(2L)} cos(ω't); ω' = √(1/(LC)-(R/2L)²). R dissipates energy.

Common misconception: The RLC circuit is the electrical analog of a damped mechanical oscillator — same differential equation structure.

How it can be tested: Classify RLC damping regime; describe effect of R on oscillations.

Resonance in RLC

In a driven RLC circuit, maximum current at resonance ω_d = ω₀ = 1/√(LC). Quality factor Q = ω₀L/R. Impedance: Z = √(R² + (ωL-1/(ωC))²).

Common misconception: At resonance, impedance Z = R (minimum); maximum current flows. Below resonance: capacitive; above: inductive.

How it can be tested: Calculate resonance frequency, impedance, and Q for an RLC circuit.

Key Vocabulary
  • LC angular frequency — ω = 1/√(LC); analogous to √(k/m) in SHM
  • LC energy — U = ½LI² + ½q²/C = const; alternates between L and C
  • RLC damping — R causes exponential decay; ω' < ω₀
  • Resonance frequency — ω₀ = 1/√(LC); maximum current in driven RLC
  • Q factor — Q = ω₀L/R; sharpness of resonance; higher Q → sharper peak
Objectives
  • Derive the LC oscillation equation and solve for q(t).
  • Classify RLC damping and describe effect on oscillations.
  • Calculate resonance frequency and Q factor for RLC circuit.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: derive LC oscillation ODE and identify ω.
  • RLC: find condition for critical damping.
  • Resonance: plot I vs. ω_d and identify key features.
Practice before moving on
  • Compare LC and SHM analogies systematically.
  • Calculate Q and bandwidth for a given RLC resonant circuit.

  • A) u = 4/(8π×10⁻⁷) = 4/(2.513×10⁻⁶) = 1.59×10⁶ J/m³
  • B) u ≈ 1.59 MJ/m³ — enormous energy density
  • C) MRI machine stores this energy in the superconducting solenoid
  • D) u = B²/(2μ₀) = (2)²/(2 × 4π×10⁻⁷)
D is correct. u = B²/(2μ₀) = (2)²/(2 × 4π×10⁻⁷)
Q10
An RLC circuit has R = 20 Ω, L = 0.5 H, C = 500 μF driven at resonance. Find I_max for ε₀ = 50 V peak.
  • A) V_L and V_C are both large but cancel: V_L = I_max ω₀L, V_C = I_max/(ω₀C)
  • B) Q factor = ω₀L/R = (200)(0.5)/20 = 5; voltage across L or C = QV_source
  • C) At resonance: Z = R = 20 Ω (reactive terms cancel)
  • D) I_max = ε₀/Z = 50/20 = 2.5 A
C is correct. At resonance: Z = R = 20 Ω (reactive terms cancel)
