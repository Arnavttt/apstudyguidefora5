Defining and Classifying Psychological Disorders

Learn on this site
Defining Abnormality

Psychological disorders are typically defined by the 4 D's: deviance from cultural norms, dysfunction impairing daily life, distress to the individual, and danger to self or others; the DSM-5 (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual) provides standardized diagnostic criteria.

Common misconception: Deviance alone does not define disorder—behavior must be dysfunctional or distressing; cultural context matters enormously in what counts as deviant.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests the criteria for defining psychological disorders and understanding the DSM's purpose.

Anxiety Disorders

Anxiety disorders include generalized anxiety disorder (persistent worry), panic disorder (recurring panic attacks), phobias (irrational fear of specific stimuli), PTSD (trauma-based anxiety), and OCD (obsessive thoughts driving compulsive behaviors).

Common misconception: OCD is no longer classified as an anxiety disorder in DSM-5—it has its own category, though it shares anxiety features; this is a frequently tested distinction.

How it can be tested: MCQ distinguishes anxiety disorder types; FRQ may ask you to explain a case using learning theory or the biopsychosocial model.

Mood and Psychotic Disorders

Major depressive disorder involves persistent sad mood, loss of interest, sleep/appetite changes, and suicidal ideation; bipolar disorder involves alternating mania and depression; schizophrenia involves positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions) and negative symptoms (flat affect, poverty of speech).

Common misconception: Depression is not simply sadness—it is a clinical syndrome with biological, cognitive, and social components; schizophrenia is not multiple personality disorder (dissociative identity disorder is a separate condition).

How it can be tested: MCQ distinguishes mood disorders and schizophrenia; FRQ asks about biological and psychological causes.

Key Vocabulary
  • DSM-5
  • Generalized anxiety disorder
  • Schizophrenia
  • Major depressive disorder
  • Bipolar disorder
Objectives

  • Distinguish anxiety disorder types and their key features.
  • Compare major depressive disorder, bipolar disorder, and schizophrenia.

Psychological Treatments

Learn on this site
Psychoanalytic and Humanistic Therapies

Psychoanalytic therapy uses free association and dream analysis to uncover unconscious conflicts; modern psychodynamic therapy focuses on relational patterns; person-centered therapy (Rogers) uses active listening and unconditional positive regard to facilitate growth.

Common misconception: Psychoanalytic therapy is long, expensive, and has limited empirical support; person-centered therapy has better research support for mild-to-moderate distress.

How it can be tested: MCQ distinguishes therapy types; FRQ may ask you to apply a therapy approach to a case.

Behavioral and Cognitive Therapies

Behavior therapies apply learning principles: systematic desensitization (gradual exposure) treats phobias; token economies use operant reinforcement; cognitive therapy (Beck) targets distorted negative thought patterns; CBT combines cognitive and behavioral approaches and has the strongest empirical support.

Common misconception: Cognitive therapy is not the same as "positive thinking"—it involves systematic identification and testing of specific distorted thoughts, not just encouraging positive reframing.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to describe how CBT would treat a specific disorder.

Biomedical Treatments

Drug treatments include antidepressants (SSRIs, SNRIs), antipsychotics (dopamine antagonists), anti-anxiety medications (benzodiazepines), and mood stabilizers (lithium for bipolar); ECT (electroconvulsive therapy) treats severe depression; drug treatments address symptoms, not underlying causes.

Common misconception: Antidepressants are not "happy pills"—they do not produce euphoria in people without depression; they work by reducing the imbalance of neurotransmitters associated with depressive episodes.

How it can be tested: MCQ matches medication types to disorders; FRQ evaluates treatment approaches.

Key Vocabulary
  • Systematic desensitization
  • Cognitive-behavioral therapy
  • Antidepressants
  • ECT
  • Person-centered therapy
Objectives
  • Distinguish psychoanalytic, humanistic, behavioral, and cognitive therapy approaches.
  • Explain how CBT targets distorted thinking patterns.
  • Match biomedical treatments to the disorders they target and explain the mechanisms.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ distinguishes therapy approaches.
  • FRQ applies CBT to a case study.
  • MCQ matches medications to disorders.
Practice before moving on
  • Research evidence-based treatments for depression and identify the most effective combination.
  • Design a systematic desensitization hierarchy for a specific phobia.

Stress, Health, and Coping

Learn on this site
Stress and the Body

The general adaptation syndrome (Selye) describes three stages of stress response: alarm (fight-or-flight), resistance (maintaining response), and exhaustion (resources depleted); chronic stress suppresses the immune system (psychoneuroimmunology) and increases vulnerability to illness.

Common misconception: Stress does not always harm health—short-term acute stress can enhance immune function; it is chronic prolonged stress that suppresses immunity.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests GAS stages; FRQ connects stress to health outcomes.

Cognitive Appraisal and Stressors

Cognitive appraisal (Lazarus) determines whether an event is stressful based on whether it is perceived as threatening and whether one has resources to cope; the Social Readjustment Rating Scale (Holmes and Rahe) showed that both positive and negative life changes create stress.

Common misconception: Not all major life events are equally stressful for all people—cognitive appraisal of the event determines its impact, not the objective severity alone.

How it can be tested: FRQ applies cognitive appraisal to explain why the same event affects different people differently.

Coping Strategies and Resilience

Problem-focused coping addresses the stressor directly; emotion-focused coping manages emotional response to the stressor; positive emotions and social support (buffering hypothesis) protect against stress effects; hardiness (commitment, control, challenge) characterizes stress-resilient individuals.

Common misconception: Emotion-focused coping is not less effective than problem-focused coping—for uncontrollable stressors, emotion-focused strategies may be more adaptive.

How it can be tested: MCQ distinguishes coping strategies; FRQ recommends appropriate coping for specific stressor types.

Key Vocabulary
  • General adaptation syndrome
  • Cognitive appraisal
  • Problem-focused coping
  • Emotion-focused coping
  • Social support
Objectives
  • Trace the general adaptation syndrome stages and connect to health outcomes.
  • Apply cognitive appraisal theory to explain individual differences in stress responses.
  • Distinguish problem-focused and emotion-focused coping and identify appropriate contexts.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests GAS stages.
  • FRQ applies cognitive appraisal to case scenarios.
  • MCQ distinguishes coping strategies.
Practice before moving on
  • Research one study connecting social support to reduced illness risk.
  • Identify a recent personal stressor and classify your coping response as problem- or emotion-focused.

Positive Psychology and Well-being

Learn on this site
Positive Psychology

Positive psychology (Seligman) studies strengths, virtues, and factors enabling flourishing rather than only treating disorders; key concepts include flow (Csikszentmihalyi—optimal experience of complete absorption), gratitude, and character strengths.

Common misconception: Positive psychology does not deny the reality of suffering—it argues that psychological science should equally study what makes life worth living, not only what causes distress.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests positive psychology concepts; FRQ may ask you to apply well-being research.

Happiness and the Adaptation Level Phenomenon

Research shows happiness is influenced by genetic set point, life circumstances, and voluntary activities; the adaptation level phenomenon shows we adapt to new circumstances (good or bad) and return toward our happiness baseline; lottery winners and accident survivors converge over time.

Common misconception: Material wealth above poverty has diminishing returns for happiness—beyond meeting basic needs, additional income has progressively smaller effects on well-being.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests adaptation level phenomenon; FRQ applies it to decision-making.

Health Behaviors and Prevention

Health behaviors (exercise, nutrition, sleep, not smoking) are among the most powerful determinants of physical health; locus of control—belief in internal (control over one's health) vs. external (health determined by fate) control—predicts health behavior engagement.

Common misconception: Internal locus of control predicts better health behaviors but not always better health outcomes—some health outcomes are genuinely outside individual control.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests locus of control; FRQ connects health behaviors to psychological factors.

Key Vocabulary
  • Positive psychology
  • Flow
  • Adaptation level phenomenon
  • Internal locus of control
  • Well-being
Objectives
  • Explain the adaptation level phenomenon and its implications for decision-making.
  • Apply flow theory to identify activities that produce optimal experience.
  • Connect locus of control to health behavior and outcomes.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests adaptation level phenomenon.
  • FRQ applies well-being research to scenarios.
  • MCQ tests locus of control.
Practice before moving on
  • Research Csikszentmihalyi's concept of flow and identify one personal flow experience.
  • Evaluate the evidence for a genetic happiness set point.
