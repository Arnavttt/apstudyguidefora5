French and Indian War and Growing Colonial Tensions

Learn on this site
Causes and Course of the French and Indian War

The French and Indian War (1754–1763) was the North American theater of the Seven Years' War; Britain and American colonists fought against France and its Native allies over control of the Ohio River Valley and westward expansion.

Common misconception: The war is called "French and Indian" from the British colonial perspective—France and its Native allies were the enemy; most Native nations sided with France because French traders were less threatening to their land than British settlers.

How it can be tested: SAQ asks you to explain how the war reshaped British-colonial relations.

Proclamation of 1763 and Colonial Resentment

Britain's Proclamation of 1763 forbade colonial settlement west of the Appalachians to prevent costly conflicts with Native peoples; colonists who had fought in the war resented being denied western lands they considered their reward.

Common misconception: The Proclamation was pragmatic British policy, not anti-colonial hostility, but colonists interpreted it as proof Britain cared more about costs than colonial interests.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates the Proclamation as a cause of growing colonial resentment.

New British Taxes and Colonial Resistance

Britain, deeply in debt after the war, passed the Stamp Act (1765) and Townshend Acts (1767) to have colonists pay for their own defense; colonists argued "no taxation without representation" since they had no seats in Parliament.

Common misconception: Colonists paid less in taxes than people living in Britain—"no taxation without representation" was a constitutional argument about consent and rights, not simply about tax burdens.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses colonial protest documents to analyze the constitutional arguments against British taxation.

The American Revolution

Learn on this site
Ideological Foundations of Independence

Thomas Paine's Common Sense (1776) argued in plain language that monarchy was irrational and American independence was natural; the Declaration of Independence drew on Enlightenment ideas of natural rights to justify separation from Britain.

Common misconception: The Declaration of Independence was a political document justifying independence, not a governing document—it did not abolish slavery despite Jefferson's initial draft language criticizing it.

How it can be tested: DBQ frequently uses Declaration of Independence and Common Sense to analyze revolutionary ideology.

Military Course of the Revolution

American forces were often outgunned but benefited from French alliance (1778), knowledge of terrain, and British strategic errors; key turning points included Saratoga (1777), which secured French military alliance, and Yorktown (1781), which effectively ended the war.

Common misconception: The Revolution was not a popular war—historians estimate only about one-third of colonists actively supported independence, one-third remained loyal, and one-third were neutral.

How it can be tested: SAQ asks you to evaluate factors that enabled American victory despite British military superiority.

Social Consequences of Revolution: Loyalists, Women, and Slavery

The Revolution drove 80,000 Loyalists from their homes; women's wartime roles (Abigail Adams, camp followers) did not translate to formal political rights; enslaved people who supported either side hoped for freedom, but the Revolution largely reinforced slavery in the South.

Common misconception: Revolutionary rhetoric about liberty created expectations the new nation failed to fulfill for women, enslaved people, and the poor.

How it can be tested: LEQ and SAQ evaluate which groups benefited and which were excluded from revolutionary ideals.

Key Vocabulary
  • Common Sense
  • Declaration of Independence
  • Battle of Saratoga
  • Loyalists
  • Abigail Adams
Objectives
  • Explain the ideological arguments in Common Sense and the Declaration of Independence.
  • Identify military turning points and explain why America won despite British advantages.
  • Evaluate which groups were included in and excluded from revolutionary ideals.
How this can be tested
  • DBQ uses revolutionary documents for ideological analysis.
  • SAQ evaluates military factors enabling American victory.
  • LEQ assesses social consequences of Revolution.
Practice before moving on
  • Annotate key passages of the Declaration of Independence identifying Enlightenment ideas.
  • Compare the wartime experiences of Patriot women, Loyalist women, and enslaved women.

Articles of Confederation and Constitutional Convention

Learn on this site
Articles of Confederation: Strengths and Weaknesses

The Articles of Confederation (1781) created a loose confederation where states retained sovereignty; Congress could not tax, regulate commerce, or enforce laws; the Northwest Ordinance (1787) was its major success, organizing the Old Northwest Territory.

Common misconception: The Articles were deliberately weak because the founders feared concentrated power after their experience with British tyranny—their weakness was a feature, not just a bug.

How it can be tested: SAQ and LEQ evaluate whether the Articles period (1781–1789) was a "critical period" of failure or a reasonable experiment.

Shays' Rebellion and the Call for a Stronger Government

Shays' Rebellion (1786–87) was an armed uprising of Massachusetts farmers facing debt and foreclosure; it alarmed propertied elites who feared that democracy was leading to mob rule and convinced key leaders that a stronger national government was necessary.

Common misconception: Shays' Rebellion was not a revolution—it was a property dispute—but elites used it to argue that the Articles were inadequate for maintaining social order.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses Shays' Rebellion documents to analyze critiques of the Articles.

Constitutional Convention and Great Compromise

The Constitutional Convention (1787) created a stronger federal government; key compromises included the Great Compromise (bicameral legislature balancing large and small state interests) and the Three-Fifths Compromise (enslaved persons counted as 3/5 of a person for representation).

Common misconception: The Three-Fifths Compromise was not a recognition of partial humanity—it was a political bargain that gave slaveholding states more congressional representation and electoral votes.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates Constitutional compromises and their consequences.

Key Vocabulary
  • Articles of Confederation
  • Northwest Ordinance
  • Shays' Rebellion
  • Great Compromise
  • Three-Fifths Compromise
Objectives
  • Identify key weaknesses of the Articles and explain why they were deliberately designed.
  • Analyze how Shays' Rebellion influenced the call for a Constitutional Convention.
  • Evaluate Constitutional compromises and their consequences, especially the Three-Fifths Compromise.
How this can be tested
  • SAQ compares Articles period as success vs. failure.
  • DBQ uses Shays' Rebellion documents for argument analysis.
  • LEQ evaluates Constitutional compromises.
Practice before moving on
  • List three weaknesses of the Articles and explain why each mattered.
  • Explain how the Three-Fifths Compromise affected Southern political power in Congress.

The Early Republic: Federalists, Anti-Federalists, and Washington's Presidency

Learn on this site
Federalists vs. Anti-Federalists

Federalists (Hamilton, Madison, Jay) argued in the Federalist Papers that a strong central government with checks and balances would protect liberty; Anti-Federalists (Patrick Henry, George Mason) feared tyranny from a distant central government and demanded a Bill of Rights.

Common misconception: Anti-Federalists were not simply opponents of good government—they had principled concerns based on colonial experience with distant British authority.

How it can be tested: DBQ frequently uses Federalist Papers and Anti-Federalist writings to assess constitutional debates.

Hamilton's Economic Program

Alexander Hamilton's financial plan (1790–91) included assumption of state debts, a national bank, protective tariffs, and excise taxes to establish federal credit and promote manufacturing; Jefferson and Madison opposed it as unconstitutional and favoring wealthy merchants over farmers.

Common misconception: The debate over Hamilton's bank was the first major constitutional controversy—Hamilton used "implied powers" while Jefferson used "strict construction."

How it can be tested: SAQ and LEQ evaluate the Hamilton-Jefferson debate as reflecting fundamental constitutional differences.

Whiskey Rebellion and Washington's Farewell

The Whiskey Rebellion (1794) tested federal authority when western Pennsylvania farmers resisted the excise tax; Washington used federal troops to suppress it, establishing that the new government could enforce its laws; Washington's Farewell Address warned against permanent foreign alliances and political factions.

Common misconception: The Whiskey Rebellion suppression proved federal power was real, unlike under the Articles; Washington used more force than the actual threat required, establishing a precedent for federal authority.

How it can be tested: SAQ asks you to connect the Whiskey Rebellion to the establishment of federal authority.

Key Vocabulary
  • Federalist Papers
  • Anti-Federalists
  • Bill of Rights
  • Hamilton's financial plan
  • Whiskey Rebellion
Objectives
  • Compare Federalist and Anti-Federalist arguments about the Constitution.
  • Evaluate Hamilton's economic program and Jefferson's constitutional objections.
  • Analyze the Whiskey Rebellion as a test of federal authority.
How this can be tested
  • DBQ uses Federalist Papers and Anti-Federalist writings.
  • SAQ evaluates Hamilton-Jefferson debate.
  • LEQ connects early republic events to constitutional development.
Practice before moving on
