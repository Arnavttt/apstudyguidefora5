Unit 6 of AP® World History: Modern

Consequences of Industrialization, c. 1750–1900

Industrialization transformed economies and societies worldwide, fueling capitalism, new ideologies, imperial expansion into Africa and Asia, mass migration, and significant environmental change.

The Spread of Industrialization

Learn on this site
From Britain to the World

Britain industrialized first due to coal and iron reserves, a colonial empire for raw materials and markets, and favorable banking and legal institutions. By the mid-1800s industrialization spread to Western Europe, the United States, and Japan through technology transfer, investment, and deliberate government policy.

Common misconception: Industrialization was not automatic or inevitable — Japan industrialized rapidly through state-directed investment (Meiji Restoration), proving that non-Western societies could industrialize on their own terms.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to explain why Britain industrialized first or to compare industrial development paths across regions.

Capitalism and the Market Economy

Industrial capitalism was characterized by private ownership of the means of production, wage labor, market competition, and the pursuit of profit. The factory system replaced cottage industries, creating an urban working class (proletariat) separated from the land and dependent on wages.

Common misconception: Capitalism in this era was largely unregulated — students often apply modern assumptions about labor laws to a period before they existed.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to explain how capitalism changed the relationship between workers and the means of production.

Responses to Industrialization: Socialism and Labor Movements

Socialism — from utopian varieties (Robert Owen) to Marxist scientific socialism — argued that capitalism exploited workers and called for collective or state ownership of production. Labor unions and strikes were workers's primary tools to improve wages and conditions; Marxism predicted a worldwide proletarian revolution.

Common misconception: Marx's Communist Manifesto (1848) predicted revolution would begin in the most industrialized countries — it actually occurred in agrarian Russia, which Marx did not predict.

How it can be tested: MCQ distinguishes capitalism, socialism, and communism; FRQ asks you to explain worker responses to industrial conditions.

Global Imperialism in Africa and Asia

Learn on this site
Causes of the New Imperialism

European imperialism after 1870 was driven by industrial capitalism's demand for raw materials and new markets, Social Darwinist and racial ideologies justifying domination, nationalist competition among European powers, and new military technologies (Maxim gun, steamships). The Berlin Conference (1884–85) partitioned Africa among European powers with minimal African input.

Common misconception: The "civilizing mission" was ideology, not benevolent policy — European powers used it to justify extraction and control while denying African and Asian agency.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to identify economic, ideological, or political causes of imperialism; FRQ asks you to evaluate which cause was most important.

Methods of Imperial Control

European powers used several methods of control: direct rule (France in West Africa), indirect rule (Britain in Nigeria through local chiefs), sphere of influence (China), and protectorate (Egypt under Britain). Colonies provided raw materials, absorbed manufactured exports, and served as strategic military bases.

Common misconception: Not all imperial control was direct — Britain often ruled vast territories cheaply through local intermediaries, which students overlook when they assume all colonialism was the same.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to compare direct and indirect rule using specific colonial examples.

African and Asian Resistance

Colonized peoples resisted imperial rule through armed revolt (Zulu wars, Indian Rebellion of 1857, Boxer Rebellion in China), passive resistance, and preservation of cultural practices. Most resistance was ultimately suppressed by industrial military advantage, but it demonstrated that colonialism was never passively accepted.

Common misconception: The Indian Rebellion of 1857 is also called the Sepoy Mutiny — students should know both names and understand it as a broad anti-British uprising, not just a military mutiny.

How it can be tested: MCQ and FRQ ask you to explain forms of anti-colonial resistance and evaluate their effectiveness.

Key Vocabulary
  • New Imperialism
  • Berlin Conference
  • direct rule
  • indirect rule
  • Social Darwinism
Objectives
  • Identify economic, ideological, and political causes of the New Imperialism.
  • Distinguish direct rule from indirect rule with colonial examples.
  • Explain forms of anti-colonial resistance and why most failed.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests causes of imperialism and methods of colonial control.
  • FRQ asks you to evaluate which cause of imperialism was most significant.
  • SAQ asks you to analyze a primary source defending or opposing empire.
Practice before moving on
  • Map Africa before and after the Berlin Conference — list which country claimed each territory.
  • Compare British indirect rule in Nigeria to French direct rule in West Africa.

Migration Patterns in the Industrial Era

Learn on this site
Global Migration Flows

Industrialization drove mass migration: Europeans emigrated to the Americas and Australia seeking land and opportunity; Chinese and Indian laborers migrated as indentured workers to plantations in the Caribbean, Southeast Asia, and South Africa after the abolition of slavery. Internal rural-to-urban migration filled industrial cities.

Common misconception: Indentured labor after 1834 was not slavery — but the conditions (multi-year contracts, physical coercion, geographic displacement) made it a close successor that historians debate extensively.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to classify migration types and explain push-pull factors; FRQ asks you to compare indentured labor migration with earlier slave migration.

Indentured Labor and the Coolie Trade

After Britain abolished slavery (1833), plantation owners recruited Indian and Chinese laborers under multi-year indenture contracts. Workers were transported to Trinidad, Guyana, Mauritius, Fiji, and Malaysia. Conditions were harsh and workers had little legal recourse, though indenture was formally voluntary unlike chattel slavery.

Common misconception: The "coolie" trade affected millions of Asian workers but is often omitted from narratives that jump from slavery to free labor — AP® World History emphasizes this as a transitional labor system.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to compare forms of coerced and semi-coerced labor across the 19th century.

Push and Pull Factors of Industrial Migration

Push factors from Europe included land scarcity, famine (Irish Famine 1845–52), religious persecution, and political instability. Pull factors to the Americas included available land, higher wages, and political freedom. Chain migration through social networks accelerated migration flows once established.

Common misconception: European emigrants were not universally welcomed — nativist movements in the U.S. (Chinese Exclusion Act 1882, restrictions on Southern/Eastern Europeans) show that receiving societies contested mass immigration.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to identify push and pull factors; FRQ asks you to analyze how migration transformed both sending and receiving societies.

Key Vocabulary
  • indentured labor
  • chain migration
  • push factor
  • pull factor
  • diaspora
Objectives
  • Explain economic and social push-pull factors driving 19th-century migration.
  • Compare indentured labor migration with the earlier transatlantic slave trade.
  • Analyze how migration transformed sending and receiving societies.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests migration flows, push-pull factors, and labor systems.
  • FRQ compares coerced labor forms across the period.
  • SAQ analyzes a primary source on migration experiences.
Practice before moving on
  • Map major 19th-century migration routes and label the push and pull factors for each.
  • Compare the experiences of Irish emigrants to the U.S. and Indian indentured laborers in Trinidad.

Environmental Consequences of Industrialization

Learn on this site
Deforestation and Resource Extraction

Industrial demand for timber, coal, rubber, and metals drove massive deforestation and extraction in Europe, the Americas, and colonies. Britain's coal-fueled factories depleted domestic forests; colonial rubber plantations in the Congo and Southeast Asia transformed tropical ecosystems.

Common misconception: Environmental destruction was not an unintended side effect — it was integral to the logic of industrial capitalism and imperial resource extraction, which maximized short-term profit without accounting for ecological costs.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to connect industrial capitalism and imperialism to specific environmental consequences.

Urbanization and Pollution

Industrial cities became centers of severe air and water pollution. Coal smoke caused "London fog" — actually smog — that killed thousands. Factories discharged waste into rivers; overcrowded tenements lacked sanitation, spreading cholera and typhus. These conditions sparked early public health movements.

Common misconception: Industrial pollution was not evenly distributed — working-class and poor neighborhoods bore the worst environmental burdens, an early form of what we now call environmental injustice.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to identify consequences of industrialization; FRQ may ask you to evaluate the social costs of industrial urban growth.

Early Environmentalism and Conservation

Reactions to industrial environmental destruction generated early conservation movements: U.S. national parks (Yellowstone 1872), the writings of John Muir, and European public health reforms. These movements were limited — they often protected wilderness for elite recreation while ignoring urban industrial pollution affecting the poor.

Common misconception: Early conservation was not environmentalism in the modern sense — it was often motivated by aesthetic, nationalist, or elite recreational interests rather than ecological science.

How it can be tested: AP® World History FRQ asks you to explain both the environmental costs of industrialization and the nature of early responses to those costs.

Key Vocabulary
  • deforestation
  • urbanization
  • smog
  • cholera
  • conservation movement
Objectives
  • Explain specific environmental consequences of industrial production and colonial resource extraction.
  • Analyze how industrial pollution created public health crises in urban areas.
  • Describe early conservation responses and evaluate their limitations.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests environmental consequences of industrialization.
  • FRQ connects industrial capitalism and imperialism to environmental change.
  • SAQ analyzes a primary source on urban industrial conditions.
Practice before moving on
  • Research the Great London Smog of 1952 as a continuation of industrial-era air pollution.
  • Compare deforestation in industrial Britain to rubber extraction in the Belgian Congo.
