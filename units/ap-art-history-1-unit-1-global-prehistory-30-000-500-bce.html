Prehistoric Art Overview

Learn on this site
Cave Paintings and Early Figurines

Cave paintings at Lascaux (France, c. 17,000 BCE) and Altamira (Spain, c. 14,000 BCE) feature animals and handprints made with mineral pigments; they may relate to ritual, storytelling, or marking territory.

Common misconception: Students assume cave paintings were purely decorative; evidence suggests ritual or shamanistic purposes, though we cannot know with certainty.

How it can be tested: Describe formal qualities of a work; explain potential cultural function; compare prehistoric works across regions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Lascaux — Cave site in France with Paleolithic paintings of horses, aurochs, and other animals (c. 17,000 BCE)
  • Venus of Willendorf — Small limestone figurine (c. 28,000–25,000 BCE) with exaggerated female features; possible fertility symbol
  • megalith — Large stone used in prehistoric monument construction; from Greek "megas" (great) + "lithos" (stone)
  • Stonehenge — Prehistoric monument in England (c. 3000–1500 BCE); aligned with solstices; likely ceremonial and funerary

Q1
The Venus of Willendorf figurine's exaggerated physical features are most often interpreted as
  • A) a realistic portrait of a specific woman
  • B) a child's toy
  • C) a warning marker for territory
  • D) evidence of fertility symbolism or concepts of female power in prehistoric culture
D is correct. evidence of fertility symbolism or concepts of female power in prehistoric culture
Q2
Stonehenge is aligned with the solstices, suggesting its primary function was
  • A) ceremonial or religious, possibly related to the agricultural cycle and ancestor veneration
  • B) astronomical research and navigation
  • C) defensive military fortification
  • D) storage of grain and livestock
A is correct. ceremonial or religious, possibly related to the agricultural cycle and ancestor veneration
Q3
Formal analysis of a prehistoric cave painting would focus on
  • A) line, shape, pigment, scale, and composition — the visual elements and how they create meaning
  • B) the identity of the artist and their biography
  • C) the market value of the artwork
  • D) the literary sources that inspired the images
A is correct. line, shape, pigment, scale, and composition — the visual elements and how they create meaning
Q4
The term "prehistoric" in art history means
  • A) art created by hunter-gatherers only
  • B) before the invention of writing — a period known through material culture rather than written records
  • C) art made more than 10,000 years ago regardless of writing
  • D) art with no recognizable subject matter
B is correct. before the invention of writing — a period known through material culture rather than written records
Q5
The Paleolithic cave paintings at Lascaux and Altamira are significant because they
  • A) were made by Neanderthals rather than modern humans
  • B) show evidence of writing systems
  • C) demonstrate sophisticated artistic skill and symbolic thought in early modern humans
  • D) are the earliest human artifacts ever found
C is correct. demonstrate sophisticated artistic skill and symbolic thought in early modern humans

Unit 1 Review — 10 questions0 / 10 correct
Q1
The Hall of the Bulls at Lascaux (France, c. 17,000 BCE) is significant because it
  • A) demonstrates sophisticated compositional skill and represents one of the earliest large-scale painted narratives
  • B) was the first artwork ever created by humans
  • C) uses linear perspective to suggest space
  • D) depicts a specific historical battle
A is correct. demonstrates sophisticated compositional skill and represents one of the earliest large-scale painted narratives
Q2
Prehistoric megalithic monuments such as Stonehenge were most likely used for
  • A) communal ceremonial or funerary purposes related to seasonal cycles
  • B) defensive military purposes
  • C) astronomical research by scientists
  • D) agricultural storage
A is correct. communal ceremonial or funerary purposes related to seasonal cycles
Q3
The use of mineral pigments (ochre, manganese) in cave paintings demonstrates
  • A) that painting was a commercial activity
  • B) that early humans had no symbolic thinking
  • C) early humans' ability to plan, prepare materials, and apply systematic artistic technique
  • D) that prehistoric artists had formal training
C is correct. early humans' ability to plan, prepare materials, and apply systematic artistic technique
Q4
Prehistoric portable objects (figurines, beads, carved bones) differ from cave paintings in that they
  • A) were only made by women
  • B) could be carried and exchanged, suggesting possible social and trade functions across communities
  • C) are always more recent than cave paintings
  • D) have no artistic merit compared to cave paintings
B is correct. could be carried and exchanged, suggesting possible social and trade functions across communities
Q5
The "handprints" at Cueva de las Manos (Argentina, c. 9000–13,000 BCE) are interpreted as
  • A) simple decoration with no meaning
  • B) calendar marks tracking seasons
  • C) ownership marks on territory
  • D) assertions of individual presence and identity — "I was here" — as well as possible communal ritual
D is correct. assertions of individual presence and identity — "I was here" — as well as possible communal ritual
Q6
Comparing global prehistoric art reveals
  • A) that all prehistoric cultures were in contact with each other
  • B) identical artistic styles across all regions
  • C) that only European cultures created sophisticated early art
  • D) universal human tendencies toward symbolic thinking, animal imagery, and marking of significant spaces
D is correct. universal human tendencies toward symbolic thinking, animal imagery, and marking of significant spaces
Q7
The formal element most important in distinguishing a mammoth from a horse in a cave painting is
  • A) outline/contour line and the distinctive silhouette shape of each animal
  • B) color, since mammoths were always painted red
  • C) scale, since large animals are always shown larger
  • D) shading, which clearly distinguishes species
A is correct. outline/contour line and the distinctive silhouette shape of each animal
Q8
Neolithic art (after c. 10,000 BCE) differs from Paleolithic art primarily in
  • A) abandoning all animal imagery
  • B) adopting linear perspective
  • C) being entirely non-representational
  • D) reflecting settled agricultural societies — including architecture, pottery, and weaving alongside continued figure-making
D is correct. reflecting settled agricultural societies — including architecture, pottery, and weaving alongside continued figure-making
Q9
The study of objects without written records relies on
  • A) carbon-dating all artistic materials
  • B) archaeology, formal analysis, anthropological comparison with living cultures, and scientific dating methods
  • C) reading the artist's journal and letters
  • D) applying one universal theory of meaning to all objects
B is correct. archaeology, formal analysis, anthropological comparison with living cultures, and scientific dating methods
Q10
A Neolithic burial site with grave goods (tools, figurines, food) suggests
  • A) evidence of warrior culture
  • B) that burial was rare in prehistoric times
  • C) belief in an afterlife and the spiritual significance of material objects for the deceased
  • D) early capitalism and property rights
C is correct. belief in an afterlife and the spiritual significance of material objects for the deceased
