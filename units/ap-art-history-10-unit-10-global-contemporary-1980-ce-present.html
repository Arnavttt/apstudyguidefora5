Contemporary Art: Identity, Globalization, and New Media

Learn on this site
Identity and Postcolonial Art

Contemporary art since the 1980s has centered on questions of identity: race (Jean-Michel Basquiat, Kara Walker, Kerry James Marshall), gender and sexuality (Cindy Sherman, Zanele Muholi), diaspora and postcolonialism (Yinka Shonibare, Cai Guo-Qiang, El Anatsui). These artists use appropriation, photography, installation, and performance to challenge Western art historical canons and address ongoing legacies of colonialism.

Common misconception: Students assume contemporary art is purely conceptual with no craft; many contemporary artists possess exceptional technical skill alongside conceptual depth.

How it can be tested: Analyze the use of identity in contemporary art; explain postcolonial critique; describe how artists use non-traditional media to challenge art historical assumptions.

Installation Art, Biennials, and Digital Media

Installation art creates immersive environments the viewer inhabits — examples include Judy Chicago's "The Dinner Party" (1974–1979), Kara Walker's "A Subtlety" (2014), and Ai Weiwei's "Sunflower Seeds" (2010). International biennials (Venice, São Paulo, Gwangju) have created a global infrastructure for contemporary art. Digital art (net art, AI art, NFTs) raises new questions about originality and authorship.

Common misconception: Students assume the art world is centered exclusively in New York and London; since the 1990s, biennials across Asia, Africa, and Latin America have shifted the art world's geography.

How it can be tested: Describe the biennial as an art world institution; explain what installation art demands of its viewer; analyze how digital art challenges definitions of the art object.

Key Vocabulary
  • installation art — An art form creating an immersive three-dimensional environment for the viewer to inhabit; often site-specific and temporary
  • appropriation — The use of pre-existing imagery, objects, or styles in new artworks, often to critique or recontextualize the original
  • postcolonialism — A critical framework examining the cultural and artistic legacies of European colonialism
  • biennial — A large-scale international contemporary art exhibition held every two years; Venice Biennale (est. 1895) is the oldest
  • diaspora art — Art by artists living outside their ancestral homeland, often negotiating between multiple cultural identities
  • NFT — Non-fungible token — a blockchain-based certificate of ownership for a digital artwork; raises questions of originality and value

  • A) introducing abstract expressionism to American art
  • B) being the first artist to exhibit street art in a gallery

Q2
Kara Walker's large-scale silhouettes address
  • A) the Victorian silhouette tradition without political commentary
  • B) the history of American slavery and racial violence — using the decorative silhouette form to depict traumatic scenes, creating jarring contrast between elegant form and brutal content
  • C) the experience of African immigration to the United States in the 20th century

  • A) creating a global platform presenting non-Western artists alongside Western artists — decentralizing New York and London as sole arbiters of important art
