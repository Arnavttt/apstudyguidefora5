Egyptian Conventions and the Afterlife

Egyptian art follows strict conventions: composite view (head in profile, eye frontal, shoulders frontal, legs in profile), hierarchy of scale, and registers. These conventions served religious functions, especially funerary contexts — art ensured the continued existence of the deceased.

Common misconception: Students see Egyptian conventions as "primitive" inability to depict space; they are deliberate choices serving religious and symbolic functions, not failed attempts at naturalism.

How it can be tested: Identify Egyptian conventions in a work; explain their function; compare Egyptian and Greek approaches to the human figure.

Key Vocabulary
  • composite view — Egyptian figure convention showing the most characteristic view of each body part simultaneously
  • hierarchy of scale — Depicting important figures larger, regardless of spatial logic
  • ka — The ancient Egyptian concept of a spiritual life force, housed in the body and associated tomb images
  • ziggurat — Stepped pyramid-shaped temple of ancient Mesopotamia (e.g., Ur-Nammu's ziggurat)
  • kouros — Greek standing nude male youth figure from the Archaic period; influenced by Egyptian posture

  • Explain why Egyptian art maintained conventions for 3,000 years

Q1
The Egyptian convention of the "composite view" shows the human body
  • A) from a single consistent viewpoint
  • B) with naturalistic foreshortening
  • C) as a flat silhouette with no internal detail
  • D) with the head in profile, eyes frontal, shoulders frontal, and legs in profile to show all parts most completely
D is correct. with the head in profile, eyes frontal, shoulders frontal, and legs in profile to show all parts most completely
Q2
The Sphinx at Giza combines
  • A) the head of a pharaoh with the body of a lion — symbolizing royal power and divine protection
  • B) the head of a falcon with the body of a human
  • C) the head of a bull with the wings of an eagle
  • D) the head of a god with the body of a cow
A is correct. the head of a pharaoh with the body of a lion — symbolizing royal power and divine protection
Q3
Classical Greek sculpture (5th century BCE) is characterized by
  • A) realistic individual portraiture (that's Roman)
  • B) idealized proportions (Canon of Polyclitus), contrapposto stance, and calm expression (severe style giving way to naturalism)
  • C) rigid frontal poses and stylized forms (that's Archaic)
  • D) extreme emotional expression (that's Hellenistic)
B is correct. idealized proportions (Canon of Polyclitus), contrapposto stance, and calm expression (severe style giving way to naturalism)
Q4
Roman veristic portraiture differs from Greek idealism in that Roman portraits
  • A) always show subjects in their youth and idealized form
  • B) depict only gods and mythological heroes
  • C) were carved only from marble
  • D) emphasize realistic, individualized features — wrinkles, age, and specific identity — asserting ancestral authority
D is correct. emphasize realistic, individualized features — wrinkles, age, and specific identity — asserting ancestral authority
Q5
The Colosseum (Rome, 72–80 CE) demonstrated Roman engineering mastery through
  • A) the same techniques used in the Parthenon
  • B) concrete construction, the barrel vault, the groin vault, and a sophisticated system of arches supporting tiered seating
  • C) Greek post-and-lintel construction throughout
  • D) purely decorative stone cladding with no structural function
B is correct. concrete construction, the barrel vault, the groin vault, and a sophisticated system of arches supporting tiered seating

  • A) is the earliest known oil painting

Q2
The Great Pyramids at Giza were built as
  • A) temples for public worship of the sun god Ra
  • B) administrative centers for tax collection
  • C) astronomical observatories
  • D) royal tombs to house the mummified bodies of pharaohs and ensure their journey to the afterlife

Q3
The Lamassu (Assyria, c. 883–859 BCE) guardian figures have five legs because
  • A) the fifth leg was added for stability
  • B) when viewed from the front they appear stationary (two legs) and from the side they appear walking (four legs)
  • C) each leg represents a different Assyrian god
  • D) they were carved by two different sculptors

Q4

  • A) Alexander the Great's military victories
  • B) the Panathenaic procession — citizens of Athens approaching the gods — combining religious and civic celebration
  • C) the Trojan War narrative
  • D) the genealogy of the Olympian gods

  • A) aqueducts carrying water into the city

Q7

  • A) ideal mathematical proportions for the human body, most famously realized in the "Doryphoros" (Spear-Bearer)
  • B) rules for Greek temple construction and column orders

Q8

  • A) using only marble rather than bronze
  • B) showing more extreme emotion, dramatic poses, and psychological complexity (pathos)

B is correct. showing more extreme emotion, dramatic poses, and psychological complexity (pathos)
Q9

Q10
