Neoclassicism and the Academy

Neoclassicism (c. 1750–1830) revived Greco-Roman subject matter, style, and moral clarity in response to the Rococo's frivolity. Jacques-Louis David's "Oath of the Horatii" (1784) exemplifies this: heroic subject, clarity of line, architectural composition, and moral message tied to republican virtue.

Common misconception: Students equate "neoclassical" with "old-fashioned"; Neoclassicism was politically radical — it opposed the decadent aristocratic Rococo with republican virtue and civic duty.

How it can be tested: Identify key works of Neoclassicism, Romanticism, and Realism; explain their political/social contexts; describe formal differences.

Key Vocabulary
  • Neoclassicism — Late 18th–early 19th century movement reviving Greek and Roman forms, associated with Enlightenment ideals and republican politics
  • Romanticism — Early 19th century movement emphasizing emotion, nature, the sublime, and individualism in reaction to rationalism
  • Realism — Mid-19th century movement depicting everyday working-class life without idealization (Courbet, Daumier)
  • Impressionism — French movement (1860s–80s) capturing momentary light with loose brushwork and everyday subjects (Monet, Renoir, Degas)
  • chiaroscuro — Strong contrast between light and dark areas, used to create volume and dramatic effect (Caravaggio, Rembrandt)
Objectives
  • Identify formal features of major 19th–20th century movements
  • Explain how each movement responded to the previous one
  • Analyze a work by Cézanne, Picasso, or Pollock using formal analysis
How this can be tested
  • Compare Monet's garden series with a David Neoclassical work — what changed?
  • Identify the cultural context that produced Cubism
Practice before moving on
  • What political events shaped Neoclassicism and Romanticism?

Q1
The Impressionists were controversial when they first exhibited because
  • A) they depicted only industrial and ugly subjects
  • B) they completely abandoned color in favor of black and white
  • C) they used photography instead of painting
  • D) their loose brushwork, unconventional subjects, and lack of academic finish violated the standards of the Paris Salon
D is correct. their loose brushwork, unconventional subjects, and lack of academic finish violated the standards of the Paris Salon
Q2
Pablo Picasso and Georges Braque developed Cubism (c. 1907–1914) to
  • A) produce purely decorative geometric patterns
  • B) return to classical Greek ideals of beauty
  • C) depict objects from multiple viewpoints simultaneously, fragmenting form to show full three-dimensionality on a flat surface
  • D) imitate African masks without any conceptual innovation
C is correct. depict objects from multiple viewpoints simultaneously, fragmenting form to show full three-dimensionality on a flat surface
Q3
Surrealism (founded 1924) drew on __________ to access the unconscious.
  • A) Freudian psychoanalysis and dream imagery, depicting irrational, dreamlike juxtapositions
  • B) Marxist political theory and class struggle
  • C) ancient mythology and religious symbolism
  • D) mathematical theories of space and time
A is correct. Freudian psychoanalysis and dream imagery, depicting irrational, dreamlike juxtapositions
Q4
Abstract Expressionism (1940s–50s, New York) is characterized by
  • A) geometric abstraction based on mathematical systems
  • B) large-scale canvases, gestural or color-field abstraction, and emphasis on the act of painting as expressive
  • C) precise geometric forms and flat color planes (Minimalism)
  • D) collaged newspaper headlines and commercial imagery (Pop Art)
B is correct. large-scale canvases, gestural or color-field abstraction, and emphasis on the act of painting as expressive
Q5
The advent of photography (1839) affected Western painting by
  • A) freeing painters from the obligation to record reality, contributing to abstraction, Impressionism, and avant-garde experimentation
  • B) eliminating portrait painting completely within 20 years
  • C) having no effect — painters ignored photography entirely
  • D) causing the immediate death of academic painting
A is correct. freeing painters from the obligation to record reality, contributing to abstraction, Impressionism, and avant-garde experimentation
Exit ticket
  • Compare Romanticism's emotion with Neoclassicism's reason
  • Explain why Courbet's Realism was politically controversial
  • How did Cézanne bridge Impressionism and Cubism?

Q2

  • A) it portrayed the victims of war with pathos rather than glorifying military violence — an anti-heroic perspective
  • B) it was the first painting to depict a modern firearm
  • C) it celebrated the French invaders as liberators
  • D) it showed no human figures, only destroyed buildings

Q3
Gustave Courbet's "A Burial at Ornans" (1849) was controversial because
  • A) it was painted on an extremely small canvas
  • B) it depicted ordinary provincial people at a burial with the scale and seriousness reserved for epic history painting
  • C) it showed naked figures in a public setting
  • D) it depicted a violent political assassination
B is correct. it depicted ordinary provincial people at a burial with the scale and seriousness reserved for epic history painting

  • A) it was the first French painting of a female nude
  • B) it depicted a historical goddess in an irreverent context
  • C) it used photographic techniques rather than traditional painting
  • D) it depicted a nude woman as a modern prostitute who stares boldly at the viewer, challenging academic idealization of the nude

Q5
Paul Cézanne is considered a "bridge" to Modernism because
  • A) he was the first painter to use photographs as references
  • B) his analysis of form through geometric simplification and multiple viewpoints directly influenced Cubism and 20th-century abstraction

Q6

  • A) the influence of Cubism and Picasso's work directly
