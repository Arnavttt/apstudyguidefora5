Easter Island Moai and Chiefly Power

The moai of Rapa Nui (Easter Island, c. 1100–1680 CE) are monolithic carved stone figures representing deified ancestors (aringa ora — "living faces"). Erected on stone platforms (ahu) facing inward to protect the community, their mana (spiritual power) was believed to bless the living.

Common misconception: Students assume moai were religious idols for worship; they are ancestor figures whose mana protects the living community.

How it can be tested: Describe the function of moai as ancestor figures; explain the role of mana; analyze how moai communicate chiefly authority.

Māori Art: Ta Moko and the Wharenui

Māori culture of New Zealand expresses identity through ta moko (facial tattoo) encoding an individual's genealogy, rank, and achievements in a unique pattern. The wharenui (meeting house) is conceived as the body of an ancestor — ridge beam as spine, rafters as ribs — covered with carved panels (poupou) depicting ancestral narratives.

Common misconception: Students assume ta moko is merely decorative; it is a biographical text encoding genealogical information that others can "read" to understand the wearer's lineage.

How it can be tested: Explain ta moko as biographical text; describe the wharenui as an embodied ancestor; compare Pacific concepts of art with Western fine art categories.

Key Vocabulary
  • moai — Monolithic ancestor figures carved from volcanic tuff on Easter Island, erected on stone platforms (ahu)
  • mana — In Polynesian culture, spiritual power or authority — chiefs and ancestors possess mana transmittable through objects and images
  • ta moko — Māori facial tattoo encoding genealogy, rank, and personal identity; each design is unique to the individual
  • wharenui — A Māori meeting house conceived as the body of an ancestor; a central space for community gathering and ceremony
  • tapa — Cloth made from beaten bark used across Polynesia for clothing, exchange, and ceremonial decoration
  • malangan — Complex carved ceremonial objects of New Ireland (Papua New Guinea) made for mortuary feasts; destroyed after use

  • A) they were defensive fortifications against seaborne invaders

  • A) purely decorative body art with no symbolic content
  • B) a punishment marking for criminals and outcasts
  • C) a religious symbol identical across all tribal members

  • A) they are made specifically for mortuary ceremonies and destroyed after use — their value lies in the act of making and ritual use, not in permanence
  • B) they are the oldest surviving Pacific artworks preserved for centuries
  • C) they are purely decorative objects for permanent display

Q5
Tapa cloth in Polynesian cultures functioned as
  • A) a material exclusively used for wrapping sacred objects
  • B) a prestige material used in ceremonial exchange, clothing royalty, marking life transitions, and as a medium for geometric decoration
