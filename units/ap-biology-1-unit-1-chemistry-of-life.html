Properties of Water and Carbon Chemistry

Learn on this site
Water's Unique Properties

Water's polar covalent bonds and hydrogen bonding give it cohesion (water-water attraction), adhesion (water-surface attraction), high specific heat, and the ability to act as a universal solvent.

Common misconception: Students confuse cohesion and adhesion; cohesion is water sticking to water (surface tension), while adhesion is water sticking to other surfaces (capillary action in plants).

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to connect a water property to a biological function (e.g., high specific heat → temperature buffering in oceans).

Carbon Chemistry and Functional Groups

Carbon forms four covalent bonds, enabling long chains, rings, and branched structures; functional groups (hydroxyl, carbonyl, carboxyl, amino, phosphate, sulfhydryl) determine molecule properties.

Common misconception: Students think functional groups are just labels; they determine solubility, reactivity, and which macromolecule family a molecule belongs to.

How it can be tested: MCQs show a functional group and ask which macromolecule it belongs to or what property it confers.

Dehydration Synthesis and Hydrolysis

Polymers form when monomers are joined by dehydration synthesis (water removed); hydrolysis breaks polymers by adding water.

Common misconception: Students reverse the two reactions; dehydration removes water to form bonds, hydrolysis adds water to break them.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask which reaction forms or breaks a polymer; FRQs ask students to connect digestion to hydrolysis.

Key Vocabulary
  • Hydrogen Bond — A weak attraction between a partially positive hydrogen and a partially negative atom (N, O, or F); responsible for water's unique properties.
  • Cohesion — The attraction of water molecules to each other via hydrogen bonds; creates surface tension.
  • Polarity — An unequal distribution of charge in a molecule due to electronegativity differences; makes water an excellent solvent.
  • Functional Group — A specific grouping of atoms that gives an organic molecule its characteristic chemical properties.
  • Dehydration Synthesis — A reaction that joins monomers into polymers by removing a water molecule at the bond site.
Objectives
  • Explain how hydrogen bonding creates water's unique properties.
  • Identify major functional groups and the properties they confer.
  • Distinguish dehydration synthesis from hydrolysis.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs connect a water property to a specific biological example.
  • MCQs show molecular structures with functional groups for identification.
  • Questions test dehydration synthesis vs. hydrolysis in polymer formation and digestion.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw a water molecule showing partial charges; list all four properties that result from hydrogen bonding.
  • Build a dipeptide from two amino acids using dehydration synthesis; identify the peptide bond formed.

Carbohydrates and Lipids

Learn on this site
Carbohydrate Structure and Function

Carbohydrates consist of C, H, O in a 1:2:1 ratio; monosaccharides (glucose, fructose) are monomers; polysaccharides (starch, glycogen, cellulose, chitin) serve as energy storage or structural support.

Common misconception: Students confuse starch and cellulose — both are glucose polymers, but different glycosidic bond angles make cellulose indigestible and structurally rigid while starch is digestible.

How it can be tested: MCQs compare starch vs. cellulose structure-function; FRQs ask about the relationship between monomer structure and polymer function.

Lipid Structure and Function

Lipids are hydrophobic molecules including fats (glycerol + 3 fatty acids), phospholipids (glycerol + 2 fatty acids + phosphate head), and steroids; they store energy, form membranes, and act as signaling molecules.

Common misconception: Students think all fats are bad; saturated fats have straight tails that pack tightly (solid at room temp), while unsaturated fats have kinked tails (liquid at room temp) — structure determines function.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask about phospholipid bilayer structure and how it determines membrane properties.

Membrane Phospholipids and Cholesterol

Phospholipids have hydrophilic heads and hydrophobic tails; they spontaneously form bilayers in water; cholesterol in animal membranes buffers fluidity across temperature ranges.

Common misconception: Students think cholesterol is always harmful; in membranes, cholesterol regulates fluidity — it prevents membranes from becoming too rigid at low temperatures or too fluid at high temperatures.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask how changes in fatty acid saturation or cholesterol affect membrane fluidity.

Key Vocabulary
  • Monosaccharide — The simplest carbohydrate monomer (e.g., glucose, fructose, galactose); building block of polysaccharides.
  • Glycosidic Bond — The covalent bond formed between monosaccharides via dehydration synthesis; the orientation (α vs. β) determines the polymer's properties.
  • Saturated Fatty Acid — A fatty acid with no double bonds; straight tail that allows dense packing; solid at room temperature.
  • Phospholipid — A lipid with a hydrophilic phosphate head and two hydrophobic fatty acid tails; the main structural component of cell membranes.
  • Membrane Fluidity — The ability of phospholipids to move within the bilayer; affected by fatty acid saturation and cholesterol content.
Objectives
  • Compare the structure and function of starch, glycogen, and cellulose.
  • Explain why phospholipids spontaneously form bilayers in water.
  • Predict how changes in fatty acid saturation affect membrane fluidity.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs compare carbohydrate polymers by bond type and function.
  • FRQs ask about phospholipid bilayer structure and how composition affects membrane properties.
  • Questions test the relationship between saturated/unsaturated fatty acids and membrane fluidity.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw a phospholipid and label the hydrophilic head and hydrophobic tails; explain bilayer formation.
  • Compare starch and cellulose: same monomer, different bond → different structure and function.

Proteins and Enzymes

Learn on this site
Protein Structure

Proteins are amino acid polymers; primary structure is the amino acid sequence; secondary structure forms alpha helices and beta sheets via hydrogen bonds; tertiary structure forms 3D shape via R-group interactions; quaternary involves multiple polypeptides.

Common misconception: Students think proteins have only three levels; quaternary structure is only present in proteins with more than one polypeptide chain (e.g., hemoglobin).

How it can be tested: FRQs ask how a change in primary structure (mutation) can alter protein function through changes in higher-level structure.

Enzyme Catalysis

Enzymes are proteins that lower activation energy by binding substrates at the active site; the induced fit model explains how enzyme shape changes slightly to better bind the substrate.

Common misconception: Students think enzymes are used up in reactions; they are biological catalysts that are released unchanged and can catalyze many reactions.

How it can be tested: MCQs show an enzyme activity vs. temperature or pH graph; FRQs ask how a change in conditions alters enzyme activity.

Enzyme Inhibition

Competitive inhibitors block the active site; noncompetitive inhibitors bind an allosteric site and change the enzyme's shape; allosteric regulation can activate or inhibit enzymes through conformational changes.

Common misconception: Students confuse competitive and noncompetitive inhibition; competitive inhibitors can be overcome by adding more substrate, while noncompetitive inhibitors cannot.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to design an experiment to distinguish competitive from noncompetitive inhibition.

Key Vocabulary
  • Polypeptide — A chain of amino acids linked by peptide bonds; one or more polypeptides fold into a functional protein.
  • Active Site — The specific region of an enzyme where the substrate binds and catalysis occurs; its shape is complementary to the substrate.
  • Induced Fit — The model of enzyme action where the enzyme changes shape slightly to better accommodate the substrate after initial binding.
  • Competitive Inhibitor — A molecule that blocks the active site by mimicking the substrate; effect can be overcome by increasing substrate concentration.
  • Denaturation — The loss of a protein's functional 3D shape due to extreme pH, temperature, or chemicals; disrupts non-covalent bonds.
Objectives
  • Describe the four levels of protein structure and the bonds involved at each level.
  • Explain how enzymes lower activation energy and accelerate reactions.
  • Compare competitive and noncompetitive enzyme inhibition.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask how a mutation in the gene encoding an enzyme could alter its function.
  • MCQs show enzyme activity graphs vs. pH or temperature with inhibitors present.
  • Questions test the effect of temperature, pH, and inhibitors on enzyme-catalyzed reaction rates.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw an enzyme activity vs. temperature graph; predict what happens above and below the optimum.
  • Explain how competitive inhibition of an enzyme could be overcome in a cell.

Nucleic Acids and Review of Macromolecules

Learn on this site
DNA and RNA Structure

Nucleotides consist of a sugar (deoxyribose in DNA, ribose in RNA), a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base (A, T, G, C in DNA; A, U, G, C in RNA); DNA is double-stranded with antiparallel strands; RNA is usually single-stranded.

Common misconception: Students forget that RNA uses uracil instead of thymine and ribose instead of deoxyribose; these differences are structurally significant and appear in every AP® exam.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to identify differences between DNA and RNA; FRQs ask students to explain how structure relates to function.

DNA Base Pairing and Chargaff's Rules

Adenine pairs with thymine (2 H-bonds) and guanine with cytosine (3 H-bonds) in DNA; Chargaff's rules state that %A = %T and %G = %C in any double-stranded DNA sample.

Common misconception: Students forget the number of hydrogen bonds between base pairs; A-T has 2 H-bonds, G-C has 3 — this affects DNA stability and melting temperature.

How it can be tested: MCQs give the percentage of one base and ask for the others; FRQs ask about implications for double helix stability.

Comparing Macromolecules

All four macromolecule classes (carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids) are built on carbon backbones; their specific monomers, bonds, and functional groups determine their biological roles.

Common misconception: Students treat the four macromolecule groups as completely separate; they share chemical principles (C-H-O backbones, covalent bonds, functional groups) while differing in composition and 3D structure.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to compare two macromolecule classes on structure, bond type, monomer, and function.

Key Vocabulary
  • Nucleotide — The monomer of nucleic acids; consists of a 5-carbon sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base.
  • Antiparallel — The orientation of the two DNA strands running in opposite 5'→3' directions; required for proper base pairing.
  • Complementary Base Pairing — A pairs with T (in DNA) or U (in RNA); G always pairs with C; the basis of DNA replication and transcription.
  • Chargaff's Rules — In double-stranded DNA: %A = %T and %G = %C; total purines = total pyrimidines.
  • Purine — A double-ring nitrogenous base: adenine or guanine; always pairs with pyrimidines (C, T, or U) in the double helix.
Objectives
  • Describe the structural differences between DNA and RNA.
  • Apply Chargaff's rules to calculate base percentages in a DNA sample.
  • Compare the four macromolecule classes by monomer, bond type, and function.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs give DNA base percentages and ask for the percentage of complementary bases.
  • FRQs ask students to compare DNA and RNA structure and relate differences to their roles.
  • Questions ask students to distinguish macromolecule families by structure and function.
Practice before moving on
  • Given 30% adenine in a DNA sample, calculate the % of each remaining base using Chargaff's rules.
  • Create a comparison table of all four macromolecules: monomer, polymer bond, function, example.
