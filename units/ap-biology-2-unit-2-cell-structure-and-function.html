Unit 2 of AP® Biology

Cell Structure and Function

Cells are the fundamental unit of life; their structures are specialized for function, and membrane transport maintains homeostasis.

Unit learning gateway

  • All cells share a plasma membrane, ribosomes, and DNA.
  • Eukaryotic cells have membrane-bound organelles; prokaryotic cells do not.
  • The endomembrane system synthesizes, modifies, and packages proteins.
  • Membrane transport (diffusion, osmosis, active transport) maintains cellular homeostasis.

Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic Cells

Learn on this site
Prokaryotic Cell Structure

Prokaryotes lack membrane-bound organelles and a true nucleus; DNA is in a nucleoid region; they have ribosomes, a plasma membrane, and most have a cell wall.

Common misconception: Students think prokaryotes have no internal structure; they have ribosomes, DNA, and a cytoplasm, just no membrane-bound nucleus or organelles.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to distinguish prokaryote from eukaryote features; FRQs ask about antibiotic targets specific to prokaryotic structures.

Eukaryotic Cell Organelles

Eukaryotes have membrane-bound organelles; the nucleus stores DNA; mitochondria produce ATP; chloroplasts (in plants) perform photosynthesis; the rough ER and Golgi process proteins; lysosomes recycle cellular components.

Common misconception: Students confuse the rough and smooth ER; rough ER has ribosomes and synthesizes membrane and secretory proteins, while smooth ER synthesizes lipids and detoxifies chemicals.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask which organelle performs a specific function; FRQs ask students to trace the path of a secreted protein through organelles.

Comparing Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic Cells

Both cell types share a plasma membrane, ribosomes, and DNA, reflecting common ancestry; eukaryotic organelles likely arose through endosymbiosis (mitochondria and chloroplasts from engulfed bacteria).

Common misconception: Students think ribosomes are only in eukaryotes; all cells have ribosomes — they differ in size (70S in prokaryotes, 80S in eukaryotes) and this difference is exploited by antibiotics.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to cite evidence for endosymbiotic theory explaining the origin of mitochondria.

Key Vocabulary
  • Prokaryote — A unicellular organism without a membrane-bound nucleus or organelles; bacteria and archaea.
  • Nucleoid — The region of a prokaryotic cell where the DNA is concentrated; not enclosed by a membrane.
  • Endomembrane System — The network of membranes in eukaryotic cells including the nuclear envelope, ER, Golgi, and vesicles that work together in protein and lipid synthesis and secretion.
  • Endosymbiotic Theory — The hypothesis that mitochondria and chloroplasts evolved from ancient prokaryotes engulfed by a host cell; supported by their double membranes, 70S ribosomes, and circular DNA.
  • Ribosome — The organelle that synthesizes proteins; 70S in prokaryotes (targeted by many antibiotics), 80S in eukaryotes.
Objectives
  • List the features shared by all cells.
  • Identify the function of major eukaryotic organelles.
  • Explain the evidence supporting endosymbiotic theory.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask about organelle function and prokaryote vs. eukaryote distinctions.
  • FRQs trace the path of a secretory protein from ribosome to plasma membrane.
  • Questions ask students to explain endosymbiotic theory and its supporting evidence.
Practice before moving on
  • Trace the path of an insulin molecule from its gene in the nucleus to its release from the cell.
  • List three pieces of evidence supporting the endosymbiotic origin of mitochondria.

Cell Membranes: Structure and the Fluid Mosaic Model

Learn on this site
Fluid Mosaic Model

The plasma membrane is a fluid phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins (integral) and surface proteins (peripheral); it is selectively permeable, allowing some molecules through while blocking others.

Common misconception: Students think the membrane is a rigid barrier; it is fluid — phospholipids and proteins can move laterally, which is essential for membrane function.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to explain how membrane composition affects fluidity and permeability.

Membrane Proteins

Integral membrane proteins span the bilayer and include transport proteins (channels, carriers), receptor proteins (signal transduction), and enzymes; peripheral proteins associate with the membrane surface.

Common misconception: Students confuse channel proteins with carrier proteins; channels provide open passageways for specific ions/molecules, while carriers change shape to move molecules across the membrane.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to identify the function of a membrane protein from its structure or location.

Cell Wall and Extracellular Matrix

Plant cells have a cellulose cell wall outside the membrane (providing structural support); animal cells have an extracellular matrix (ECM) of glycoproteins and collagen that supports cells and mediates communication.

Common misconception: Students think cell walls and ECM are the same; cell walls are rigid barriers providing support, while the ECM is more flexible and involved in cell signaling and adhesion.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask about the composition and function of plant cell walls vs. animal ECM.

Key Vocabulary
  • Fluid Mosaic Model — The model describing the plasma membrane as a fluid bilayer with diverse proteins embedded in or attached to it.
  • Integral Protein — A membrane protein embedded in the phospholipid bilayer; includes transmembrane proteins that span the entire bilayer.
  • Selective Permeability — The property of the plasma membrane that allows some molecules to pass freely while restricting others based on size, polarity, and charge.
  • Channel Protein — A transport protein forming a hydrophilic pore through which specific molecules or ions can move by facilitated diffusion.
  • Glycoprotein — A membrane protein with attached carbohydrate chains; involved in cell recognition, signaling, and adhesion.
Objectives
  • Describe the fluid mosaic model and its key features.
  • Distinguish between integral and peripheral membrane proteins and their functions.
  • Explain how membrane composition determines selective permeability.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask how changes in phospholipid composition or temperature affect membrane fluidity.
  • MCQs test the functions of different types of membrane proteins.
  • Questions ask students to explain why small, nonpolar molecules cross membranes more easily than large, polar ones.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw the fluid mosaic model; label integral protein, peripheral protein, phospholipid, cholesterol.
  • Explain why a cell in a cold environment might increase unsaturated fatty acids in its membrane.

Membrane Transport

Learn on this site
Passive Transport: Diffusion and Osmosis

Diffusion is the net movement of molecules from high to low concentration (no energy required); osmosis is the diffusion of water through a selectively permeable membrane from low to high solute concentration.

Common misconception: Students describe osmosis as water moving toward high water concentration, which is equivalent but confusing; osmosis moves water from low solute concentration to high solute concentration.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to predict the direction of osmosis given solute concentrations; MCQs test tonicity vocabulary.

Facilitated Diffusion and Active Transport

Facilitated diffusion uses membrane proteins to move molecules down their concentration gradient (no ATP); active transport moves molecules against their gradient and requires ATP (e.g., Na+/K+ pump).

Common misconception: Students assume all transport through proteins requires energy; facilitated diffusion uses proteins but goes with the gradient and requires no ATP.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask whether a transport scenario is passive or active based on direction and energy use; FRQs ask about the Na+/K+ pump mechanism.

Bulk Transport: Endo- and Exocytosis

Endocytosis (phagocytosis, pinocytosis, receptor-mediated endocytosis) brings material into the cell in a vesicle; exocytosis expels vesicle contents out of the cell.

Common misconception: Students think endocytosis only occurs in white blood cells; all eukaryotic cells use endocytosis and exocytosis for large molecules that cannot cross the membrane.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to distinguish types of endocytosis; FRQs ask about how exocytosis is used in secretion.

Key Vocabulary
  • Osmosis — The diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane from low solute to high solute concentration.
  • Tonicity — The relative solute concentration of a solution compared to the cell's interior; determines the direction of osmotic water movement.
  • Hypertonic — A solution with a higher solute concentration than the cell; causes cells to lose water (shrink/crenate in animals, plasmolyze in plants).
  • Active Transport — Movement of molecules against their concentration gradient using membrane proteins and ATP; e.g., the sodium-potassium pump.
  • Exocytosis — A process by which a vesicle fuses with the plasma membrane and releases its contents outside the cell; used for secretion.
Objectives
  • Predict the direction of osmosis given two solutions separated by a semipermeable membrane.
  • Distinguish passive from active transport based on gradient direction and energy use.
  • Describe the process and biological roles of endocytosis and exocytosis.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs give solute concentrations on both sides of a membrane and ask for water movement direction and cell behavior.
  • MCQs ask whether a specific transport scenario requires ATP.
  • Questions ask students to apply tonicity to plant and animal cell experiments.
Practice before moving on
  • Predict the outcome (swell, shrink, no change) for an animal cell and plant cell placed in each: distilled water, isotonic solution, 10% NaCl.
  • Explain the mechanism of the Na+/K+ pump and why it requires ATP.

Cell Communication and Signal Transduction

Learn on this site
Types of Cell Signaling

Cells communicate through paracrine (local), autocrine (self), endocrine (distant via hormones), and direct contact (gap junctions, plasmodesmata) signaling; all require a signal molecule and a target receptor.

Common misconception: Students think all cell signaling uses hormones; paracrine signaling involves local molecules like growth factors and neurotransmitters that don't enter the bloodstream.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to classify a signaling scenario by type; FRQs describe a signaling pathway and ask students to predict the response.

Signal Transduction Pathway

Signal transduction involves three stages: reception (signal binds receptor), transduction (cascading molecular changes, often involving second messengers like cAMP), and response (gene expression change or protein activation).

Common misconception: Students think the signal molecule enters the cell; most signaling molecules bind surface receptors and trigger internal changes without entering — only steroid hormones (being lipid-soluble) cross the membrane to bind intracellular receptors.

How it can be tested: FRQs trace a complete signal transduction pathway and identify the role of each component.

Apoptosis and Cell Death Signaling

Apoptosis is programmed cell death triggered by internal or external signals; it eliminates damaged cells and shapes developing tissues without causing inflammation.

Common misconception: Students think apoptosis is the same as necrosis; apoptosis is controlled and programmed (no inflammation), while necrosis is uncontrolled cell death from injury.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask why apoptosis is important in development and immunity; FRQs ask how a disrupted apoptosis pathway contributes to cancer.

Key Vocabulary
  • Receptor Protein — A protein that binds a specific signaling molecule (ligand) and triggers a cellular response.
  • Signal Transduction — The conversion of an extracellular signal into an intracellular response through a cascade of molecular interactions.
  • Second Messenger — A small molecule (e.g., cAMP, Ca2+) that amplifies a signal inside the cell after a surface receptor is activated.
  • Apoptosis — Programmed cell death; cells deliberately dismantle themselves in a controlled way to eliminate damaged or unneeded cells.
  • Ligand — A signaling molecule that binds specifically to a receptor, triggering a conformational change and downstream response.
Objectives
  • Describe the three stages of signal transduction.
  • Explain why most signals do not need to enter the cell to produce a response.
  • Connect apoptosis to development and cancer prevention.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to trace a signal transduction pathway from ligand to cellular response.
  • MCQs ask which molecule is a second messenger and what role it plays.
  • Questions ask students to explain how disrupted apoptosis leads to tumor formation.
Practice before moving on
  • Trace the complete pathway of a hydrophilic hormone (e.g., epinephrine) from binding to cellular response.
  • Explain why a mutation that prevents apoptosis could lead to cancer development.
