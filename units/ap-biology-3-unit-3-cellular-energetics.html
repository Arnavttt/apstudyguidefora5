Unit 3 of AP® Biology

Cellular Energetics

Photosynthesis converts light energy to chemical energy; cellular respiration releases that energy as ATP for cellular work.

Unit learning gateway

  • Photosynthesis: light reactions capture energy; Calvin cycle fixes CO2 into G3P.
  • Cellular respiration: glycolysis, pyruvate oxidation, Krebs cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation.
  • ATP is produced by substrate-level and oxidative phosphorylation via ATP synthase.
  • The electron transport chain uses a proton gradient (chemiosmosis) to drive ATP synthesis.

Enzymes and Energy in Metabolism

Learn on this site
Free Energy and Spontaneous Reactions

Reactions with negative ΔG (exergonic) release free energy and are spontaneous; reactions with positive ΔG (endergonic) require an input of energy; cells couple exergonic ATP hydrolysis to drive endergonic reactions.

Common misconception: Students confuse "spontaneous" with "fast"; a spontaneous reaction has a negative ΔG but may proceed very slowly — enzymes speed up spontaneous reactions without changing ΔG.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask whether a reaction is exergonic or endergonic given ΔG; FRQs ask how ATP coupling drives endergonic cellular processes.

ATP Structure and Function

ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is the cell's energy currency; hydrolysis of the terminal phosphate bond releases ~7.3 kcal/mol and drives cellular work (mechanical, transport, chemical).

Common misconception: Students think ATP stores energy in its chemical bonds; energy is released when the phosphate group is transferred, not simply in the bond itself.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask about ATP hydrolysis and the types of work it powers; FRQs ask how ATP drives muscle contraction or active transport.

Enzyme Regulation in Metabolism

Metabolic pathways are regulated by feedback inhibition (the end product inhibits an early enzyme), allosteric regulation (effector molecules bind non-active sites to activate or inhibit), and by controlling enzyme concentration.

Common misconception: Students think feedback inhibition is always negative (inhibitory); allosteric regulation can also activate enzymes when activators bind and change shape to increase activity.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to explain how feedback inhibition prevents overproduction of a metabolic product.

Key Vocabulary
  • Exergonic Reaction — A reaction that releases free energy (ΔG < 0) and can proceed spontaneously; e.g., cellular respiration.
  • Endergonic Reaction — A reaction that requires an input of free energy (ΔG > 0); e.g., protein synthesis, photosynthesis.
  • ATP Hydrolysis — The reaction that breaks the terminal phosphate bond of ATP to produce ADP + Pi, releasing energy to drive cellular work.
  • Feedback Inhibition — A metabolic regulation mechanism where the end product of a pathway inhibits an enzyme early in the same pathway.
  • Allosteric Regulation — The regulation of enzyme activity by a molecule binding to a site other than the active site, causing a conformational change.
Objectives
  • Distinguish exergonic from endergonic reactions using ΔG.
  • Explain how ATP hydrolysis drives endergonic reactions.
  • Describe feedback inhibition and its role in metabolic regulation.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask whether a specific cellular reaction is endergonic or exergonic.
  • FRQs ask how a cell uses ATP coupling to drive biosynthesis.
  • Questions ask students to apply feedback inhibition to a metabolic pathway diagram.
Practice before moving on
  • Explain why ATP synthesis (ADP + Pi → ATP) is endergonic and how cells drive it.
  • Draw a metabolic pathway with 4 steps; add a feedback inhibition arrow in the correct position.

Photosynthesis

Learn on this site
Light Reactions in the Thylakoid

Light reactions in the thylakoid membrane split water (photolysis), produce ATP (via photophosphorylation), and generate NADPH; electrons flow through Photosystem II → electron transport chain → Photosystem I → NADP+.

Common misconception: Students think light reactions occur in the stroma; the light reactions (including photosystems and ATP synthase) are embedded in the thylakoid membranes.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to trace electron flow through the light reactions and explain how ATP is produced.

Calvin Cycle in the Stroma

The Calvin cycle uses CO2, ATP, and NADPH to produce G3P (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate); three CO2 are fixed per turn, using RuBisCO; G3P is the precursor for glucose and other organic molecules.

Common misconception: Students think glucose is the direct product of the Calvin cycle; G3P is the direct product — glucose is assembled from G3P afterward.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to explain how limiting light or CO2 would affect G3P and RuBP levels in the Calvin cycle.

Factors Affecting Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis rate is affected by light intensity, CO2 concentration, temperature, and water availability; the compensation point is where photosynthesis rate equals respiration rate (no net gas exchange).

Common misconception: Students think more light always increases photosynthesis indefinitely; at high light intensity, the Calvin cycle becomes the limiting factor (CO2 fixation rate limits G3P production).

How it can be tested: MCQs interpret photosynthesis rate graphs vs. light intensity; FRQs ask what limits photosynthesis under specific conditions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Photolysis — The light-driven splitting of water in Photosystem II, releasing O2, electrons, and H+ ions; O2 is released as a byproduct.
  • NADPH — An electron carrier produced in the light reactions; carries high-energy electrons to the Calvin cycle.
  • Calvin Cycle — The light-independent reactions in the stroma that fix CO2 into G3P using ATP and NADPH.
  • RuBisCO — The enzyme that catalyzes carbon fixation in the Calvin cycle, binding CO2 to RuBP.
  • G3P (Glyceraldehyde-3-Phosphate) — The three-carbon sugar produced by the Calvin cycle; used to build glucose and other organic compounds.
Objectives
  • Trace electron flow through the light reactions, from water to NADPH.
  • Explain the three stages of the Calvin cycle: carbon fixation, reduction, and regeneration.
  • Predict how changes in light intensity or CO2 affect the rate of photosynthesis.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to trace electrons from water through the photosystems to NADPH.
  • MCQs ask about the products of the light reactions and Calvin cycle.
  • Questions ask students to predict effects of experimental changes on G3P and RuBP levels.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw a diagram of a chloroplast; map where the light reactions and Calvin cycle occur.
  • Explain why plants appear green: which wavelengths of light are absorbed vs. reflected?

Cellular Respiration

Learn on this site
Glycolysis

Glycolysis occurs in the cytoplasm; one glucose (6C) is split into two pyruvate (3C), producing 2 ATP (net) and 2 NADH; it occurs in the absence of oxygen (anaerobic) and is the first step in both aerobic respiration and fermentation.

Common misconception: Students confuse the gross and net ATP from glycolysis; 4 ATP are produced but 2 are invested, yielding 2 net ATP from glycolysis.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask about the products of glycolysis; FRQs ask about glycolysis in aerobic vs. anaerobic contexts.

Krebs Cycle and Pyruvate Oxidation

Pyruvate enters the mitochondrial matrix; pyruvate oxidation converts it to acetyl-CoA (releasing CO2 and NADH); the Krebs cycle turns twice per glucose, producing 2 ATP, 6 NADH, 2 FADH2, and releasing CO2.

Common misconception: Students forget that CO2 is released in both pyruvate oxidation and the Krebs cycle, not just in one place; this is where the carbon atoms from glucose are released as CO2.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to identify where in respiration each type of molecule is produced and consumed.

Oxidative Phosphorylation and Chemiosmosis

The electron transport chain (ETC) in the inner mitochondrial membrane oxidizes NADH and FADH2, pumping H+ into the intermembrane space; H+ flows back through ATP synthase, driving ATP synthesis (chemiosmosis).

Common misconception: Students think the ETC directly produces ATP; NADH and FADH2 pass electrons to the ETC, which creates a proton gradient, and ATP synthase uses that gradient to synthesize ATP.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to explain why cyanide (which blocks the ETC) stops ATP production even though glycolysis and the Krebs cycle continue.

Key Vocabulary
  • Glycolysis — The breakdown of glucose (6C) to two pyruvate (3C) in the cytoplasm; net yield of 2 ATP and 2 NADH; works aerobically and anaerobically.
  • Acetyl-CoA — The 2-carbon product of pyruvate oxidation that enters the Krebs cycle; formed in the mitochondrial matrix.
  • Krebs Cycle — A cyclic series of reactions in the mitochondrial matrix that oxidize acetyl-CoA, producing NADH, FADH2, ATP, and CO2.
  • Chemiosmosis — The synthesis of ATP using the energy of an H+ (proton) gradient across a membrane; drives ATP synthase in mitochondria and chloroplasts.
  • ATP Synthase — The enzyme that catalyzes ATP synthesis as protons flow through it down their electrochemical gradient; found in the inner mitochondrial membrane and thylakoid membrane.
Objectives
  • Identify the products (ATP, NADH, FADH2, CO2) at each stage of cellular respiration.
  • Explain chemiosmosis and how it drives ATP synthesis.
  • Predict why blocking the ETC stops ATP production even if glycolysis continues.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to trace the fate of carbon atoms from glucose through respiration.
  • MCQs ask how many ATP are produced at each stage and why oxygen is needed.
  • Questions ask students to explain chemiosmosis and the role of ATP synthase.
Practice before moving on
  • Trace one glucose molecule through all stages of cellular respiration; count NADH, FADH2, and ATP at each step.
  • Explain why cells that cannot perform oxidative phosphorylation must rely on fermentation.

Fermentation and Metabolic Connections

Learn on this site
Fermentation

Fermentation regenerates NAD+ from NADH without oxygen, allowing glycolysis to continue; lactic acid fermentation produces lactate (in muscle cells); alcoholic fermentation produces ethanol and CO2 (in yeast).

Common misconception: Students think fermentation produces ATP; it does not directly produce any additional ATP — it only regenerates NAD+ so glycolysis can continue generating 2 ATP.

How it can be tested: MCQs compare lactic acid vs. alcoholic fermentation; FRQs ask why fermentation is necessary for glycolysis to continue in the absence of oxygen.

Metabolic Connections: Fat and Protein Catabolism

Fats are catabolized via beta-oxidation to acetyl-CoA, entering the Krebs cycle (more ATP per gram than glucose); amino acids are deaminated and enter the pathway at various points.

Common misconception: Students think only glucose is used in respiration; other macromolecules enter at different points (fats as acetyl-CoA, proteins after amino acid processing).

How it can be tested: FRQs ask how fats or proteins can enter cellular respiration and at which stage.

Regulation of Cellular Respiration

Key enzymes in glycolysis (phosphofructokinase) and the Krebs cycle are regulated by ATP, ADP, AMP, and NADH levels; high ATP inhibits respiration (feedback inhibition), preventing energy waste.

Common misconception: Students think cells respire at a constant rate; respiration is tightly regulated to match ATP demand — when ATP is high, the pathway slows down.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask how ATP/ADP ratios affect respiration rate; FRQs ask about phosphofructokinase regulation.

Key Vocabulary
  • Fermentation — An anaerobic process that regenerates NAD+ by transferring electrons from NADH to an organic molecule; enables glycolysis to continue producing 2 ATP without oxygen.
  • Lactic Acid Fermentation — Fermentation that reduces pyruvate to lactate, regenerating NAD+; occurs in muscle cells during intense exercise.
  • Alcoholic Fermentation — Fermentation that reduces pyruvate to ethanol and CO2, regenerating NAD+; performed by yeast.
  • Beta-Oxidation — The catabolic process that breaks down fatty acids into acetyl-CoA units for entry into the Krebs cycle.
  • Phosphofructokinase — A key regulatory enzyme of glycolysis; inhibited by high ATP and citrate, activated by high AMP; controls the rate of glycolysis.
Objectives
  • Explain why fermentation is necessary when oxygen is unavailable.
  • Describe how fats and proteins enter cellular respiration.
  • Explain how ATP levels regulate the rate of cellular respiration.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask why fermentation is needed and what it produces.
  • FRQs ask where fats and proteins enter the respiratory pathway.
  • Questions test phosphofructokinase regulation as a model of metabolic control.
Practice before moving on
  • Explain why an athlete who depletes ATP rapidly during a sprint relies on lactic acid fermentation.
  • Explain why fats yield more ATP per gram than carbohydrates.
