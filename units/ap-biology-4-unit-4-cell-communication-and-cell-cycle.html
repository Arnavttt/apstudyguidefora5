Unit 4 of AP® Biology

Cell Communication and Cell Cycle

Precise cell cycle regulation ensures accurate DNA replication and division; errors in this control lead to cancer.

Unit learning gateway

  • The cell cycle includes interphase (G1, S, G2) and mitotic phase (mitosis + cytokinesis).
  • Checkpoints ensure DNA is replicated correctly before division proceeds.
  • Cyclins and CDKs regulate checkpoint progression.
  • Cancer results from mutations in genes controlling the cell cycle.

Cell Signaling Pathways Review

Learn on this site
Reception, Transduction, Response

All signal transduction pathways follow the same basic logic: a ligand binds a receptor (reception), triggers a cascade of molecular changes often amplified by second messengers (transduction), and produces a cellular response.

Common misconception: Students forget that signal amplification occurs during transduction; one activated receptor can activate many downstream molecules, greatly amplifying the original signal.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to trace a specific signaling pathway (e.g., epinephrine → cAMP → protein kinase → target) and identify each component.

G-Protein Coupled Receptors

GPCRs are surface receptors that activate G proteins upon ligand binding; the G protein then activates adenylyl cyclase, producing cAMP as a second messenger, which activates protein kinase A.

Common misconception: Students confuse the steps in the GPCR pathway; the order is: ligand → GPCR → G protein → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → protein kinase A → response.

How it can be tested: MCQs trace the GPCR pathway and ask which step is affected by a given drug or mutation.

Signal Transduction and the Cell Cycle Connection

Growth factors bind receptors and activate signal transduction pathways that promote entry into the cell cycle (G1 phase); loss of growth factor signaling or its control contributes to cancer.

Common misconception: Students think growth factors directly cause cell division; they activate signaling pathways that ultimately affect cyclin and CDK levels, which then control cell cycle entry.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask how a mutation in a growth factor receptor constitutively activates the cell cycle.

Key Vocabulary
  • Ligand — A signaling molecule that binds to a specific receptor, triggering a conformational change and downstream response.
  • G-Protein — A membrane-associated protein that is activated by a GPCR and relays the signal to adenylyl cyclase or other effectors.
  • cAMP — Cyclic AMP; a second messenger produced by adenylyl cyclase from ATP; activates protein kinase A and other downstream targets.
  • Protein Kinase — An enzyme that phosphorylates (adds a phosphate group to) target proteins, activating or inactivating them and propagating the signal.
  • Signal Amplification — The process by which a small initial signal (few ligand-receptor interactions) produces a large cellular response through cascading activation of many molecules.
Objectives
  • Trace the steps of a GPCR signaling pathway from ligand binding to response.
  • Explain how signal amplification occurs during transduction.
  • Connect growth factor signaling to cell cycle entry.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs trace the epinephrine or other GPCR signaling pathway.
  • MCQs ask which component of the pathway is affected by a specific drug.
  • Questions ask how amplification works in the transduction stage.
Practice before moving on
  • Trace the complete pathway: epinephrine binds β-receptor → G protein → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → PKA → glycogen phosphorylase activation.
  • Explain how one epinephrine molecule can cause millions of glucose molecules to be released.

The Cell Cycle

Learn on this site
Phases of the Cell Cycle

The cell cycle consists of interphase (G1: growth; S: DNA synthesis; G2: growth and preparation for division) and the mitotic phase (M: mitosis + cytokinesis).

Common misconception: Students think S phase means the cell is dividing; S phase is DNA replication (in interphase), not cell division — division occurs in M phase.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask what occurs in each phase; FRQs ask how disruption of a specific phase would affect subsequent cell division.

Mitosis: Prophase through Telophase

Mitosis produces two genetically identical daughter cells; stages are prophase (chromosomes condense, spindle forms), metaphase (chromosomes align at plate), anaphase (sister chromatids separate), and telophase (nuclear envelopes re-form).

Common misconception: Students confuse anaphase I (homologs separate in meiosis) with anaphase in mitosis (sister chromatids separate); these are fundamentally different events.

How it can be tested: MCQs show a cell in a specific mitotic stage; FRQs ask students to identify the stage and predict the cell's chromosome number.

Cytokinesis

Cytokinesis divides the cytoplasm; in animal cells, a cleavage furrow pinches the cell; in plant cells, a cell plate forms between daughter cells.

Common misconception: Students think mitosis includes cytokinesis; mitosis refers only to nuclear division — cytokinesis is the subsequent division of the cytoplasm.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask about the difference in cytokinesis between plant and animal cells.

Key Vocabulary
  • Interphase — The period of the cell cycle (G1, S, G2) during which the cell grows and replicates DNA before division.
  • S Phase — The phase of interphase in which DNA is replicated; each chromosome becomes two sister chromatids.
  • Mitosis — Nuclear division producing two genetically identical daughter nuclei; includes prophase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase.
  • Sister Chromatids — The two identical copies of a chromosome joined at the centromere after DNA replication; separated in anaphase of mitosis.
  • Cytokinesis — The division of the cytoplasm following mitosis; produces two complete daughter cells.
Objectives
  • Describe what happens in each phase of the cell cycle (G1, S, G2, M).
  • Identify and describe the four stages of mitosis.
  • Compare cytokinesis in animal vs. plant cells.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs show diagrams of mitotic stages and ask for identification and chromosome number.
  • FRQs ask how blocking a specific phase of the cell cycle would affect division.
  • Questions compare mitosis and meiosis in terms of chromosome number outcomes.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw a cell in each stage of mitosis (assume 2n = 4); label key structures.
  • Calculate how many chromosomes and chromatids are present at each stage of mitosis in a human cell (2n = 46).

Cell Cycle Regulation

Learn on this site
Checkpoints and Cyclins

The cell cycle is regulated at three checkpoints (G1, G2, and M spindle assembly) by cyclin/CDK complexes; cyclins rise and fall cyclically, activating CDKs that phosphorylate target proteins to advance the cycle.

Common misconception: Students think checkpoints just detect errors passively; they are active regulatory gates — if conditions are not met (e.g., DNA damage, incomplete replication), the cycle is actively stopped.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to explain how a checkpoint would respond to DNA damage detected in G2.

Proto-Oncogenes and Tumor Suppressors

Proto-oncogenes promote cell division (when mutated to oncogenes, they drive uncontrolled division); tumor suppressor genes (e.g., p53, Rb) brake the cell cycle (when inactivated, checkpoints fail).

Common misconception: Students think one mutation causes cancer; cancer requires multiple mutations in both oncogenes (gain-of-function) and tumor suppressors (loss-of-function) accumulating over time.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask whether a cancer-associated gene is an oncogene or tumor suppressor; FRQs ask how mutations in each lead to uncontrolled cell growth.

Cancer Biology

Cancer cells exhibit uncontrolled proliferation (lost cell cycle control), avoidance of apoptosis, angiogenesis (new blood vessel formation), and eventually metastasis (spread to other tissues).

Common misconception: Students think cancer is a single disease; it is a collection of diseases sharing the hallmark of uncontrolled cell division due to accumulated mutations in cell cycle regulatory genes.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to connect a specific mutation (e.g., inactivated p53) to hallmarks of cancer.

Key Vocabulary
  • Cell Cycle Checkpoint — A regulatory point in the cell cycle where the cycle is halted if conditions are not met (e.g., DNA damage, unreplicated DNA, unattached spindle fibers).
  • Cyclin — A regulatory protein whose concentration rises and falls during the cell cycle; activates CDKs to phosphorylate targets and drive cell cycle progression.
  • Proto-Oncogene — A normal gene that promotes cell division; when mutated to an oncogene, it drives uncontrolled cell proliferation.
  • Tumor Suppressor Gene — A gene that inhibits cell division or promotes apoptosis; loss of function allows uncontrolled proliferation (e.g., p53, Rb).
  • Metastasis — The spread of cancer cells from the primary tumor to other locations in the body through the blood or lymphatic system.
Objectives
  • Explain how cyclin/CDK complexes regulate cell cycle checkpoints.
  • Compare the roles of proto-oncogenes and tumor suppressor genes in cancer.
  • Describe the hallmarks of cancer cells.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to explain how p53 responds to DNA damage and what happens if p53 is mutated.
  • MCQs ask whether a gene is an oncogene or tumor suppressor based on its mutational effect.
  • Questions ask students to connect cell cycle dysregulation to each hallmark of cancer.
Practice before moving on
  • Explain why losing both copies of a tumor suppressor gene (Rb) causes cancer but losing one oncogene usually does not.
  • Describe what would happen to a cell if its G2 checkpoint protein were permanently active.

Meiosis Overview and Comparison to Mitosis

Learn on this site
Meiosis I: Reductional Division

Meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes, reducing the chromosome number from diploid (2n) to haploid (n); crossing over during prophase I creates genetic recombination.

Common misconception: Students confuse meiosis I and II; in meiosis I, homologs separate (reducing chromosome number); in meiosis II, sister chromatids separate (like mitosis).

How it can be tested: MCQs ask what is separated in each meiotic division; FRQs ask how meiosis generates genetic diversity.

Meiosis II and Meiosis vs. Mitosis

Meiosis II separates sister chromatids (like mitosis) in haploid cells, producing 4 genetically unique haploid daughter cells from one diploid parent cell.

Common misconception: Students think meiosis II involves DNA replication; no replication occurs between meiosis I and II — each cell enters meiosis II with haploid unreplicated chromosomes.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to compare mitosis and meiosis in terms of: number of divisions, chromosome number of products, and sources of genetic variation.

Sources of Genetic Variation in Meiosis

Three mechanisms generate variation: independent assortment of homologs (2^23 possible human gamete types), crossing over (recombination) in prophase I, and random fertilization.

Common misconception: Students underestimate the variation generated; independent assortment alone produces over 8 million different gamete combinations in humans, and crossing over adds virtually unlimited additional variation.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask how crossing over increases genetic diversity; FRQs ask students to explain each source of genetic variation and its mechanism.

Key Vocabulary
  • Meiosis — A form of cell division that produces 4 haploid daughter cells with genetic diversity; consists of meiosis I (reductional) and meiosis II (equational).
  • Crossing Over — The exchange of corresponding segments between homologous non-sister chromatids during prophase I of meiosis; generates new allele combinations (recombinants).
  • Independent Assortment — The random alignment and separation of homologous chromosome pairs during meiosis I; each pair orients independently, creating diverse gametes.
  • Haploid — A cell containing one set of chromosomes (n); the chromosome number after meiosis I.
  • Homologous Chromosomes — Chromosomes of the same size, shape, and gene sequence but with potentially different alleles; one from each parent; separate in meiosis I.
Objectives
  • Compare meiosis I and meiosis II in terms of what separates and the chromosome number outcome.
  • Explain crossing over and its role in generating genetic diversity.
  • Compare mitosis and meiosis in outcomes: number of cells, ploidy, genetic identity.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask what separates in each meiotic division.
  • FRQs compare the two divisions of meiosis and compare meiosis to mitosis.
  • Questions ask about sources of genetic variation specific to meiosis.
Practice before moving on
  • Create a side-by-side comparison of mitosis and meiosis: divisions, DNA replication, products, ploidy, genetic diversity.
  • Explain how independent assortment alone can produce millions of unique gametes.
