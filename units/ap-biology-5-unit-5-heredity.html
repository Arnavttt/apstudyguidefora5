Heredity

Meiosis and Mendelian genetics explain how traits are inherited; non-Mendelian patterns reveal the complexity of inheritance.

Unit learning gateway

  • Mendel's laws describe segregation and independent assortment of alleles.

Mendel's Laws and Monohybrid Crosses

Learn on this site
Law of Segregation

Alleles separate during gamete formation; each gamete receives one allele for each gene; when gametes fuse, the offspring receive one allele from each parent.

Common misconception: Students confuse segregation with independent assortment; segregation describes the separation of two alleles for one gene; independent assortment describes the random sorting of genes on different chromosomes.

How it can be tested: MCQs and FRQs use Punnett squares to predict monohybrid cross outcomes; FRQs ask students to explain how Mendel's law of segregation relates to meiosis.

Dominant and Recessive Alleles

A dominant allele masks the phenotypic effect of a recessive allele when both are present; an organism's phenotype is determined by its genotype (allele combination).

Common misconception: Students think dominant alleles are "better" or more common; dominance only describes the expressed phenotype, not frequency or fitness.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask for the probability of phenotypes from a cross; FRQs ask students to determine a parent's genotype from offspring ratios.

Test Crosses and Probability

A test cross (unknown genotype × homozygous recessive) reveals whether the unknown parent is homozygous dominant (100% dominant phenotype) or heterozygous (50% each phenotype).

Common misconception: Students confuse genotype ratios with phenotype ratios; a 3:1 phenotype ratio comes from a 1:2:1 genotype ratio in a monohybrid cross of two heterozygotes.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to use test cross results to determine the genotype of a parent.

Key Vocabulary
  • Allele — An alternative form of a gene; diploid organisms have two alleles per gene, one from each parent.
  • Law of Segregation — Mendel's first law: the two alleles for a gene separate during gamete formation, each gamete receiving one allele.
  • Homozygous — Having two identical alleles for a gene (e.g., AA or aa).
  • Heterozygous — Having two different alleles for a gene (e.g., Aa); the dominant allele is expressed.
  • Test Cross — A cross between an organism of unknown genotype and a homozygous recessive individual to determine the unknown genotype.
Objectives
  • Explain Mendel's law of segregation and connect it to meiosis.
  • Predict phenotype and genotype ratios from a monohybrid cross.
  • Use a test cross to determine an unknown genotype.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs calculate the probability of a specific genotype or phenotype from a cross.
  • FRQs ask students to use offspring phenotype ratios to determine parental genotypes.
  • Questions connect segregation to the mechanism of meiosis I.
Practice before moving on
  • Perform a Punnett square for Tt × Tt; calculate genotype ratio and phenotype ratio.
  • Design a test cross to determine if a black cat (B_) is BB or Bb.

Dihybrid Crosses and Independent Assortment

Learn on this site
Law of Independent Assortment

Genes on different chromosomes (or far apart on the same chromosome) assort independently during meiosis; this produces a 9:3:3:1 phenotype ratio in a dihybrid cross between two double heterozygotes.

Common misconception: Students apply independent assortment to all gene pairs; it applies only to genes on different chromosomes (or far apart); linked genes violate this rule.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to set up a dihybrid cross and calculate the probability of a specific phenotypic class.

Dihybrid Punnett Squares and Probability

A 4×4 Punnett square for a dihybrid cross generates 16 equally probable genotype combinations; probability rules (product rule for "and," sum rule for "or") can calculate outcomes without a full square.

Common misconception: Students draw 4×4 squares for every dihybrid cross; the product rule (multiply independent probabilities) is faster and more accurate.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask for the probability of a specific phenotype; FRQs ask students to use probability rules for a dihybrid cross.

Linked Genes and Recombination Frequency

Genes on the same chromosome (linked genes) do not assort independently; recombination frequency (crossover percentage) is used to map gene distance on a chromosome.

Common misconception: Students think linked genes always travel together; crossing over separates linked genes — the closer they are, the less recombination and the more tightly they are linked.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to use recombination frequencies to construct a simple genetic map.

Key Vocabulary
  • Law of Independent Assortment — Mendel's second law: genes on different chromosomes assort into gametes independently of one another.
  • Dihybrid Cross — A cross tracking two different traits simultaneously; a cross of two double heterozygotes (AaBb × AaBb) yields a 9:3:3:1 phenotype ratio.
  • Product Rule — The probability of two independent events both occurring equals the product of their individual probabilities; used to calculate dihybrid outcomes.
  • Gene Linkage — The tendency of genes located on the same chromosome to be inherited together; they violate Mendel's law of independent assortment.
  • Recombination Frequency — The percentage of offspring showing recombinant phenotypes in a test cross; used to estimate the distance between linked genes on a chromosome.
Objectives
  • Set up and interpret a dihybrid cross using the product rule.
  • Explain why linked genes violate the law of independent assortment.
  • Use recombination frequencies to construct a genetic map.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs calculate the probability of a specific dihybrid phenotype class.
  • FRQs ask students to use recombination data to determine gene distance.
  • Questions distinguish independent assortment from genetic linkage.
Practice before moving on
  • Use the product rule to calculate the probability of producing a plant with a specific two-trait phenotype.
  • Given recombination frequencies of 20% (A-B), 15% (B-C), and 35% (A-C), construct a genetic map.

Non-Mendelian Inheritance Patterns

Learn on this site
Incomplete Dominance and Codominance

In incomplete dominance, the heterozygote phenotype is intermediate between the two homozygote phenotypes (e.g., red × white = pink flowers); in codominance, both alleles are fully expressed simultaneously (e.g., AB blood type).

Common misconception: Students confuse incomplete dominance with blending inheritance; incomplete dominance is still particulate — the alleles do not blend permanently but segregate in subsequent crosses.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to distinguish incomplete dominance from codominance; FRQs ask about ABO blood type inheritance.

Polygenic Inheritance and Pleiotropy

Polygenic traits (skin color, height) are controlled by multiple genes and show continuous variation; pleiotropy occurs when a single gene affects multiple phenotypic traits (e.g., sickle cell anemia).

Common misconception: Students confuse polygenic with pleiotropy; polygenic = many genes → one trait; pleiotropy = one gene → many traits.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to identify whether a trait pattern suggests polygenic inheritance; FRQs ask about pleiotropic effects of a specific mutation.

Sex-Linked Inheritance

Genes on the X chromosome are X-linked; females (XX) can be carriers of recessive X-linked traits; males (XY) express any X-linked allele since they have only one X chromosome.

Common misconception: Students think sex-linked means caused by sex chromosomes directly; X-linked traits can be autosomal in females (two copies) but hemizygous in males.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to determine the probability of a sex-linked trait appearing in offspring given parental genotypes; MCQs ask why X-linked recessive traits appear more often in males.

Key Vocabulary
  • Incomplete Dominance — A pattern where the heterozygote has a phenotype intermediate between the two homozygote phenotypes.
  • Codominance — A pattern where both alleles in a heterozygote are fully expressed simultaneously; e.g., AB blood type shows both A and B antigens.
  • Polygenic Inheritance — Control of a phenotype by two or more genes; produces a continuous range of phenotypes in the population.
  • Pleiotropy — The ability of one gene to affect multiple, seemingly unrelated phenotypic traits.
  • Sex-Linked Gene — A gene located on a sex chromosome (usually the X chromosome); X-linked recessive traits appear more frequently in males (XY).
Objectives
  • Distinguish incomplete dominance from codominance with examples.
  • Explain how polygenic inheritance produces continuous variation.
  • Solve a sex-linked trait inheritance problem.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs distinguish incomplete dominance, codominance, and multiple alleles.
  • FRQs ask about the inheritance of sex-linked recessive traits and carrier status.
  • Questions ask students to identify a pattern of inheritance from pedigree data.
Practice before moving on
  • A red (RR) × white (WW) snapdragon cross produces all pink (RW) offspring; explain the inheritance pattern.
  • A carrier mother (X^H X^h) × normal father (X^H Y): calculate the probability of hemophilia in sons.

Chromosomal Basis of Inheritance

Learn on this site
Chromosomal Disorders

Nondisjunction during meiosis results in gametes with extra or missing chromosomes; Down syndrome (trisomy 21), Turner syndrome (45, X), and Klinefelter syndrome (47, XXY) are examples.

Common misconception: Students think all chromosomal disorders are equally severe; the phenotypic effect depends on which chromosome is affected — aneuploidy of most autosomes is lethal, while sex chromosome aneuploidy is often tolerable.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to identify chromosomal disorders; FRQs ask about the mechanism (nondisjunction) and the parental origin of the extra chromosome.

Karyotyping and Chromosomal Analysis

A karyotype arranges chromosomes by size and shape to detect aneuploidy, translocations, and other chromosomal abnormalities; it can be prepared from amniotic fluid (amniocentesis) or chorionic villi sampling.

Common misconception: Students think karyotyping detects all genetic disorders; it detects chromosomal number and large structural changes but not point mutations or small deletions.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask what a karyotype can and cannot detect; FRQs ask students to interpret a karyotype image.

Epigenetics

Epigenetic changes alter gene expression without changing DNA sequence; DNA methylation silences genes, while histone acetylation activates them; imprinting is the epigenetic silencing of one parental allele.

Common misconception: Students think DNA sequence is the only determinant of gene expression; epigenetic marks are heritable and environmentally influenced, showing that genes and environment interact.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to explain how epigenetic modifications affect gene expression.

Key Vocabulary
  • Nondisjunction — Failure of homologous chromosomes or sister chromatids to separate properly during meiosis, resulting in gametes with abnormal chromosome numbers.
  • Aneuploidy — Having an abnormal number of chromosomes (e.g., trisomy = 2n+1, monosomy = 2n-1).
  • Karyotype — An ordered display of all chromosomes in a cell arranged by size and centromere position; used to detect chromosomal abnormalities.
  • Epigenetics — Heritable changes in gene expression that do not involve changes to the DNA sequence; includes DNA methylation and histone modification.
  • Genomic Imprinting — The epigenetic silencing of one parental allele (maternal or paternal) in the offspring; the expressed allele depends on which parent contributed it.
Objectives
  • Explain how nondisjunction produces trisomy or monosomy.
  • Describe what a karyotype can and cannot detect.
  • Explain how DNA methylation and histone acetylation regulate gene expression.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask which chromosomal disorder results from a specific nondisjunction event.
  • FRQs ask students to trace the mechanism of nondisjunction and its gametic consequences.
  • Questions ask about epigenetic mechanisms and how environment can influence gene expression.
Practice before moving on
  • Explain why nondisjunction in meiosis I produces a different result than nondisjunction in meiosis II.
  • Explain how DNA methylation can silence a gene without changing its nucleotide sequence.
