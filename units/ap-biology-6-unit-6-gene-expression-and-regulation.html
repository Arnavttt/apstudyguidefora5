Unit 6 of AP® Biology

Gene Expression and Regulation

DNA is transcribed to RNA and translated into proteins; gene expression is regulated at multiple levels in both prokaryotes and eukaryotes.

Unit learning gateway

  • DNA replication is semiconservative and uses a template strand.
  • Transcription produces mRNA from a DNA template; translation produces protein from mRNA.
  • Gene regulation allows cells to respond to the environment and differentiate.
  • Mutations in regulatory sequences or coding regions can have major phenotypic effects.

DNA Replication

Learn on this site
Semiconservative Replication

DNA replication is semiconservative: each new double helix contains one original strand and one newly synthesized strand; DNA helicase unwinds the helix, primase adds RNA primers, and DNA polymerase synthesizes new strands.

Common misconception: Students forget that DNA polymerase can only add nucleotides in the 5'→3' direction; the lagging strand is synthesized discontinuously as Okazaki fragments.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to describe the role of each enzyme in DNA replication; MCQs test the direction of synthesis.

Leading and Lagging Strands

The leading strand is synthesized continuously in the 5'→3' direction; the lagging strand is synthesized in fragments (Okazaki fragments) in the opposite direction; DNA ligase joins the fragments.

Common misconception: Students think both strands are synthesized continuously; only the leading strand is continuous — the lagging strand requires multiple RNA primers and DNA ligase to seal gaps.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask which strand is leading vs. lagging; FRQs ask students to explain why the lagging strand is synthesized discontinuously.

DNA Repair

Proofreading by DNA polymerase and mismatch repair mechanisms reduce the error rate; mutations that escape repair can be passed to daughter cells.

Common misconception: Students think all errors in replication cause mutations; most errors are caught by proofreading and repair systems, making the actual mutation rate very low.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask about the consequence of defective DNA repair; FRQs ask how an error in proofreading would affect mutation rate.

Key Vocabulary
  • Helicase — An enzyme that unwinds and separates the two DNA strands at the replication fork by breaking hydrogen bonds.
  • DNA Polymerase — The enzyme that synthesizes new DNA strands; adds nucleotides in the 5'→3' direction only; also proofreads newly synthesized DNA.
  • Okazaki Fragments — Short segments of DNA synthesized on the lagging strand during replication; later joined by DNA ligase.
  • DNA Ligase — An enzyme that seals gaps between Okazaki fragments by forming phosphodiester bonds.
  • Semiconservative Replication — Each new DNA double helix consists of one original parental strand and one newly synthesized strand.
Objectives
  • Describe the role of each enzyme involved in DNA replication.
  • Explain why the lagging strand is synthesized discontinuously.
  • Explain the importance of proofreading and DNA repair.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to identify enzymes and their roles in DNA replication.
  • MCQs ask about the directionality of DNA synthesis and which strand is leading vs. lagging.
  • Questions ask about the consequences of mutations in DNA repair enzymes.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw a replication fork; label the leading and lagging strand, identify where each enzyme acts.
  • Explain why DNA polymerase cannot start a new strand and why primase is needed.

Transcription and RNA Processing

Learn on this site
Transcription

Transcription uses RNA polymerase to synthesize mRNA from a DNA template in the 5'→3' direction; the process has three stages: initiation (RNA polymerase binds promoter), elongation (mRNA strand grows), and termination.

Common misconception: Students confuse the template strand with the coding strand; the template strand is read 3'→5' to produce mRNA 5'→3'; the coding (non-template) strand has the same sequence as the mRNA (with T replaced by U).

How it can be tested: MCQs ask for the mRNA sequence from a given DNA template strand; FRQs ask about transcription factors and promoter regions.

RNA Processing in Eukaryotes

Eukaryotic pre-mRNA is processed: a 5' cap and poly-A tail are added for protection and ribosome recognition; introns (non-coding) are spliced out and exons (coding) are joined by spliceosomes.

Common misconception: Students think prokaryotes also process mRNA; in prokaryotes, transcription and translation are coupled and occur simultaneously — no splicing occurs.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to explain alternative splicing and how one gene can produce multiple proteins.

Types of RNA

mRNA carries the genetic message from nucleus to ribosome; tRNA brings amino acids to the ribosome (anticodon base-pairs with codon); rRNA forms part of the ribosome structure.

Common misconception: Students confuse codon and anticodon; the codon is on the mRNA and the anticodon is on the tRNA — they base pair to ensure correct amino acid placement.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask about the functions of mRNA, tRNA, and rRNA; FRQs ask students to predict the amino acid sequence from a codon table.

Key Vocabulary
  • Promoter — A DNA sequence upstream of a gene where RNA polymerase binds to initiate transcription.
  • Intron — A non-coding sequence in pre-mRNA that is removed (spliced out) before the mRNA is translated.
  • Exon — A coding sequence in pre-mRNA that is retained after splicing and translated into protein.
  • Alternative Splicing — The splicing of pre-mRNA in different ways to produce multiple protein variants from a single gene.
  • Codon — A three-nucleotide sequence on mRNA that specifies a particular amino acid or stop signal during translation.
Objectives
  • Describe the three stages of transcription and the role of RNA polymerase.
  • Explain RNA processing in eukaryotes: 5' cap, poly-A tail, and splicing.
  • Distinguish the functions of mRNA, tRNA, and rRNA.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask for the mRNA sequence from a DNA template.
  • FRQs ask how alternative splicing increases proteomic diversity.
  • Questions ask students to use the genetic code table to determine amino acid sequences.
Practice before moving on
  • Given a DNA template strand 3'-TACGCCATT-5', write the mRNA sequence and identify the amino acids using a codon table.
  • Explain how alternative splicing allows one human gene to produce multiple protein isoforms.

Translation and the Genetic Code

Learn on this site
Translation: Initiation, Elongation, Termination

Translation occurs at ribosomes; initiation begins when the small ribosomal subunit binds the mRNA start codon (AUG) and the initiator tRNA; elongation adds amino acids; termination occurs at a stop codon (UAA, UAG, UGA).

Common misconception: Students think ribosomes make proteins independently; ribosomes are the machinery but require mRNA (instructions), tRNA (amino acid delivery), and various protein factors to function.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to trace translation from mRNA to polypeptide; MCQs ask which event occurs at each stage.

The Genetic Code

The genetic code is a nearly universal set of 64 codons (61 amino acid-coding + 3 stop); it is degenerate (redundant) — multiple codons can specify the same amino acid, providing some protection against mutation.

Common misconception: Students confuse degenerate with ambiguous; degenerate means multiple codons → one amino acid (all codons have only one meaning); ambiguous would mean one codon → multiple amino acids (which does not occur).

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to use a codon table; FRQs ask how a synonymous mutation (same amino acid) differs in effect from a missense mutation.

Mutations and Their Effects

Point mutations include missense (wrong amino acid), nonsense (stop codon), and silent (same amino acid); frameshift mutations (insertions or deletions) shift the reading frame and usually alter all downstream amino acids.

Common misconception: Students underestimate frameshift mutations; they shift every codon after the insertion/deletion, usually producing a nonfunctional protein — generally more severe than point mutations.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to predict the effect of a specific mutation on the amino acid sequence and protein function.

Key Vocabulary
  • Start Codon — AUG; codes for methionine and signals the beginning of translation; the ribosome assembles at this codon.
  • Stop Codon — UAA, UAG, or UGA; does not code for an amino acid; signals the ribosome to release the polypeptide.
  • Missense Mutation — A point mutation that changes one codon to specify a different amino acid.
  • Nonsense Mutation — A point mutation that converts an amino acid codon to a stop codon, truncating the protein.
  • Frameshift Mutation — An insertion or deletion of one or two nucleotides that shifts the reading frame of all codons downstream.
Objectives
  • Describe the three stages of translation.
  • Explain the degeneracy of the genetic code and its protective effect.
  • Compare the effects of missense, nonsense, and frameshift mutations on protein function.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to translate a mRNA sequence using a provided codon table.
  • MCQs ask for the effect of a specific type of mutation on the protein product.
  • Questions ask students to compare mutations and predict severity of phenotypic effect.
Practice before moving on
  • Translate the mRNA sequence: 5'-AUG-CCU-GAA-UUU-UAG-3' using a codon table.
  • Compare the likely effect on protein function of: (a) a silent mutation, (b) a missense mutation in the active site, (c) a frameshift mutation near the N-terminus.

Gene Regulation

Learn on this site
Prokaryotic Gene Regulation: The Operon

The lac operon is a prokaryotic regulatory system; when lactose is absent, a repressor protein binds the operator and blocks transcription; when lactose is present, the allolactose inducer binds the repressor, releasing it and allowing transcription.

Common misconception: Students think the lac operon is "on" by default; in the absence of lactose, the repressor is active and blocks transcription — the operon is off by default.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to predict the transcriptional state of the lac operon under different nutrient conditions.

Eukaryotic Gene Regulation

Eukaryotic gene expression is regulated at multiple levels: chromatin remodeling (histone acetylation/deacetylation), transcription factors binding enhancers, mRNA stability, and post-translational modification.

Common misconception: Students think eukaryotic regulation works like the operon model; eukaryotic regulation is more complex, involving multiple layers and physical separation of transcription and translation.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to explain how an enhancer and transcription factor can regulate a gene located far away on the chromosome.

Gene Expression and Cell Differentiation

Although all cells of an organism contain the same genome, different genes are expressed in different cell types; differentiation is caused by differential gene expression regulated by transcription factors.

Common misconception: Students think differentiated cells have different DNA; all somatic cells contain the same DNA — only the pattern of gene expression differs.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask why liver and muscle cells with the same DNA have different proteins; FRQs ask how transcription factors regulate differentiation.

Key Vocabulary
  • Lac Operon — A prokaryotic regulatory unit containing the lacZ, lacY, and lacA genes controlled by a single promoter and operator; regulated by a repressor protein.
  • Repressor Protein — A protein that binds the operator of an operon and blocks RNA polymerase, turning off transcription.
  • Enhancer — A regulatory DNA sequence, often far from the gene, where transcription activators bind to increase transcription.
  • Transcription Factor — A protein that binds to specific DNA regulatory sequences and influences the rate of transcription.
  • Differential Gene Expression — The process by which different cell types express different genes from the same genome, leading to cellular differentiation.
Objectives
  • Explain how the lac operon is regulated in the presence and absence of lactose.
  • Describe the multiple levels of eukaryotic gene regulation.
  • Explain how differential gene expression leads to cell differentiation.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to predict lac operon transcription states given different nutrient conditions.
  • MCQs ask about enhancers, transcription factors, and chromatin remodeling.
  • Questions ask how cell differentiation can occur if all cells have the same DNA.
Practice before moving on
  • Predict the transcriptional state of the lac operon: (a) no lactose, no glucose; (b) lactose present, no glucose; (c) both present.
  • Explain how a transcription factor can regulate a gene whose enhancer is thousands of base pairs away.
