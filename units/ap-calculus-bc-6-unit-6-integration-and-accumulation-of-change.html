Riemann Sums and the Definite Integral

Learn on this site
Riemann Sums

A Riemann sum approximates ∫_a^b f(x)dx by summing areas of rectangles: left, right, or midpoint. As n→∞ the sum approaches the exact integral.

Common misconception: Students think left/right sums give exact values; they are approximations whose accuracy improves with more subintervals.

How it can be tested: Calculate left and right Riemann sums from a table of values and compare to the exact integral.

Definite Integral as a Limit

∫_a^b f(x)dx = lim_{n→∞} Σf(x_i*)Δx. The definite integral gives the net signed area between f and the x-axis.

Common misconception: Students think the integral always gives positive area; it gives signed area — portions below the x-axis are negative.

How it can be tested: Evaluate definite integrals geometrically for piecewise linear functions using area formulas.

Properties of Definite Integrals

∫_a^a f dx=0; ∫_a^b f dx=−∫_b^a f dx; ∫_a^b cf dx=c∫_a^b f dx; ∫_a^b(f+g)dx=∫_a^b f dx+∫_a^b g dx; ∫_a^b f dx=∫_a^c f dx+∫_c^b f dx.

Common misconception: Students forget the sign flip when reversing limits: ∫_a^b=−∫_b^a.

How it can be tested: Apply integral properties to evaluate integrals from known values without computing antiderivatives.

Key Vocabulary
  • Riemann sum — Approximation of ∫f dx as sum of rectangle areas
  • left/right/midpoint sum — Three choices of where to sample f on each subinterval
  • net signed area — What the definite integral computes: area above minus area below x-axis
  • additivity of integrals — ∫_a^b f = ∫_a^c f + ∫_c^b f for any c
Objectives
  • Compute left and right Riemann sums from tables
  • Evaluate definite integrals geometrically
  • Apply integral properties to simplify expressions
How this can be tested
  • Riemann sums from tables appear in MC and FR
  • Geometric evaluation of integrals with triangles and semicircles
  • Integral properties tested in both MC and FR
Practice before moving on
  • Compute 4-rectangle left sum for f(x)=x² on [0,4]
  • Use a graph to evaluate ∫_0^4 f dx where f has triangular regions

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

Learn on this site
FTC Part 1

If F(x)=∫_a^x f(t)dt, then F'(x)=f(x). Differentiating an integral with variable upper limit returns the integrand evaluated at that limit.

Common misconception: Students differentiate F(x) by computing the integral first; FTC Part 1 says directly F'(x)=f(x).

How it can be tested: Apply FTC Part 1 with chain rule: d/dx[∫_a^{g(x)} f(t)dt]=f(g(x))·g'(x).

FTC Part 2

∫_a^b f(x)dx=F(b)−F(a) where F is any antiderivative of f. This converts definite integrals into evaluation of antiderivatives.

Common misconception: Students compute F(b)−F(a) as F(a)−F(b); always subtract F(a) from F(b).

How it can be tested: Find antiderivatives and evaluate them at the bounds to compute definite integrals.

Net Change Theorem

∫_a^b F'(x)dx=F(b)−F(a). The definite integral of a rate gives the net change in the original quantity.

Common misconception: Students confuse total change with total amount; ∫v dt gives displacement (net change), not total distance.

How it can be tested: Interpret ∫_a^b r(t)dt as the net change in the quantity whose rate is r(t).

Key Vocabulary
  • FTC Part 1 — d/dx[∫_a^x f(t)dt]=f(x)
  • FTC Part 2 — ∫_a^b f dx=F(b)−F(a)
  • antiderivative — F(x) such that F'(x)=f(x)
  • net change — ∫_a^b rate dt = quantity(b)−quantity(a)
Objectives
  • Apply FTC Part 1 directly and with chain rule
  • Evaluate definite integrals using FTC Part 2
  • Interpret definite integrals as net change in context
How this can be tested
  • FTC Part 1 with chain rule appears in almost every AP® exam
  • Net change interpretation is standard FR context
  • FTC Part 2 computation in both MC and FR
Practice before moving on
  • Differentiate G(x)=∫_0^{x²} sin(t)dt

Basic Antiderivative Rules and u-Substitution

Learn on this site
Power Rule for Integrals

∫xⁿdx=x^{n+1}/(n+1)+C for n≠−1; ∫1/x dx=ln|x|+C. Always add +C for indefinite integrals.

Common misconception: Students forget +C or try to apply the power rule to n=−1; the antiderivative of 1/x is ln|x|, not x⁰/0.

How it can be tested: Integrate polynomial expressions term by term; flag n=−1 and use ln|x|.

Trigonometric Antiderivatives

∫cos x dx=sin x+C; ∫sin x dx=−cos x+C; ∫sec²x dx=tan x+C; ∫sec x tan x dx=sec x+C.

Common misconception: Students write ∫sin x dx=cos x+C (wrong sign); the antiderivative of sin x is −cos x.

How it can be tested: Compute integrals of trig functions, checking signs carefully.

u-Substitution

If the integrand has f(g(x))·g'(x), let u=g(x), du=g'(x)dx. Replace all x-terms with u, integrate in u, then back-substitute.

Common misconception: Students forget to change limits for definite integrals with u-substitution, or forget to replace dx with du/g'(x).

How it can be tested: Perform u-substitution on indefinite and definite integrals; change limits for definite integrals.

Key Vocabulary
  • power rule (integration) — ∫xⁿdx=x^{n+1}/(n+1)+C
  • constant of integration — +C added to every indefinite integral
  • u-substitution — Reversing the chain rule; let u=inner function
  • changing limits — For definite integrals, substitute u(a) and u(b) as new bounds
Objectives
  • Apply power rule to integrate polynomials and rational functions
  • Integrate standard trig and exponential functions
  • Use u-substitution on composite integrands
How this can be tested
  • u-substitution is tested on both MC and FR sections
  • Power rule integration appears in every exam
  • Definite integral with u-substitution requires changing limits
Practice before moving on

Accumulation Functions and Area

Learn on this site
Accumulation Functions

A(x)=∫_a^x f(t)dt accumulates area under f from a to x. A'(x)=f(x) by FTC Part 1. A(x) inherits properties from f: A increases where f>0, has extrema where f=0.

Common misconception: Students interpret A(x) as a formula rather than a running total; A(x) changes as x increases and its rate of change is f(x).

How it can be tested: Analyze an accumulation function A(x) given a graph of f: find max/min of A, determine concavity.

Area Between Curves

∫_a^b [f(x)−g(x)]dx gives the area between f (upper) and g (lower) from a to b. Identify which function is on top on each subinterval.

Common misconception: Students integrate f−g over the full interval even when the curves cross; splitting at intersection points is required.

How it can be tested: Find intersection points, then integrate (upper−lower) on each subinterval.

Area with Respect to y

Sometimes it is easier to integrate with respect to y: ∫_c^d [right(y)−left(y)]dy. Useful when the region is bounded by curves that are easier to express as x=f(y).

Common misconception: Students always integrate with respect to x; sometimes the region is described more simply with horizontal rectangles (dy).

How it can be tested: Set up area integrals with respect to y for regions bounded by x=y² and x=y+2.

Key Vocabulary
  • accumulation function — A(x)=∫_a^x f(t)dt; its derivative is f(x)
  • area between curves — ∫_a^b[upper−lower]dx
  • intersection points — Where f(x)=g(x); needed to set up area between curves correctly
  • integrating with respect to y — ∫_c^d[right(y)−left(y)]dy for horizontally oriented regions
Objectives
  • Analyze accumulation functions from graphs of f
  • Set up and evaluate area between curves
  • Integrate with respect to y when appropriate
How this can be tested
  • Accumulation functions appear in nearly every AP® exam
  • Area between curves guaranteed in FR
  • Sometimes a 3-part FR question: find intersection, set up integral, evaluate
Practice before moving on
  • Given graph of f, analyze A(x)=∫_0^x f(t)dt for extrema and concavity
  • Find area between y=x² and y=x+2
