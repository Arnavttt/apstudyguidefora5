Sequences and Series Convergence

Learn on this site
Convergence of Sequences

A sequence {aₙ} converges if lim_{n→∞}aₙ = L (finite). Diverges if the limit does not exist or is ±∞.

Common misconception: Students confuse convergence of sequences with convergence of series; a convergent sequence means terms → L, while a convergent series means partial sums → S.

How it can be tested: Evaluate lim_{n→∞}aₙ for given sequences and classify as convergent or divergent.

Geometric and p-Series

Geometric series Σarⁿ converges if |r|<1 to a/(1−r). p-Series Σ1/nᵖ converges if p>1. These are the reference series for comparison tests.

Common misconception: Students use p-series test on non-p-series; ensure the series is exactly 1/nᵖ before applying.

How it can be tested: Identify geometric and p-series; state convergence condition and sum for geometric.

Divergence (nth Term) Test

If lim_{n→∞}aₙ ≠ 0, then Σaₙ diverges. If lim aₙ=0, the test is inconclusive — does not imply convergence.

Common misconception: Students conclude convergence when nth term limit = 0; the nth term test only proves divergence.

How it can be tested: Apply nth term test to quickly identify divergent series; recognize when it is inconclusive.

Key Vocabulary
  • convergent sequence — lim_{n→∞}aₙ=L for finite L
  • geometric series — Σarⁿ; converges to a/(1−r) when |r|<1
  • p-series — Σ1/nᵖ; converges iff p>1
  • nth term test — Diverges if lim aₙ ≠ 0; inconclusive if lim aₙ = 0
Objectives
  • Determine convergence of sequences by limits
  • Identify and evaluate geometric series
  • Apply nth term test correctly
How this can be tested
  • Geometric series tested in both MC and FR; must find sum
  • p-Series used in comparison tests
  • Nth term test a quick first check in MC
Practice before moving on
  • Classify Σ(0.5)ⁿ and Σ1/n as convergent or divergent
  • Apply nth term test to Σn/(n+1)
Key Formulas
  • Geometric series: \( \displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty ar^n=\dfrac{a}{1-r},\;|r|<1 \)
  • p-series: \( \displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\dfrac{1}{n^p}\;\text{converges iff }p>1 \)

Convergence Tests

Learn on this site
Ratio and Root Tests

Ratio Test: if lim|a_{n+1}/aₙ|=L<1, converges; L>1, diverges; L=1, inconclusive. Best for factorials and exponentials. Root Test: lim|aₙ|^{1/n}=L, same criteria.

Common misconception: Students apply ratio test to series with polynomials (where it gives L=1, inconclusive); ratio test is for series with factorials or exponentials.

How it can be tested: Apply ratio test to series with n! or aⁿ; recognize inconclusive result means switch tests.

Alternating Series Test

Σ(−1)ⁿbₙ converges if: (1) bₙ>0, (2) bₙ is decreasing, (3) lim bₙ=0. Error bounded by first omitted term.

Common misconception: Students apply AST without verifying bₙ is decreasing; all three conditions must be checked.

How it can be tested: Verify all three AST conditions; use error bound to estimate partial sum accuracy.

Integral and Comparison Tests

Integral Test: if f is continuous, positive, decreasing and aₙ=f(n), then Σaₙ and ∫f converge/diverge together. Direct Comparison: Σaₙ≤Σbₙ; if Σbₙ converges, so does Σaₙ.

Common misconception: Students apply integral test to series with non-monotone terms; verify the function is decreasing first.

How it can be tested: Use integral test for series like Σ1/(n ln n); use comparison to bound by known series.

Key Vocabulary
  • ratio test — lim|a_{n+1}/aₙ|; L<1 converges, L>1 diverges, L=1 inconclusive
  • alternating series test — Σ(−1)ⁿbₙ converges if bₙ decreasing to 0
  • AST error bound — |Error| ≤ b_{n+1} (first omitted term)
  • comparison test — Bound aₙ above/below by known convergent/divergent series
Objectives
  • Apply ratio test to factorial and exponential series
  • Verify all three AST conditions
  • Use comparison or integral test for series without factorials or alternating sign
How this can be tested
  • At least one convergence test question per exam
  • AST error bound is a distinct testable skill
  • Ratio test with n! in BC FR
Practice before moving on
  • Test Σn!/3ⁿ with ratio test
  • Test Σ(−1)ⁿ/n with alternating series test
Key Formulas
  • Ratio test: \( L=\lim_{n\to\infty}\!\left|\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|;\;L<1\Rightarrow\text{converges} \)
  • Alt. series test: \( \displaystyle\sum(-1)^n b_n\;\text{converges if }b_n\searrow 0 \)
  • Comparison test: \( 0\le a_n\le b_n;\;\sum b_n\text{ converges}\Rightarrow\sum a_n\text{ converges} \)

Power Series and Radius of Convergence

Learn on this site
Power Series

A power series Σcₙ(x−a)ⁿ converges for |x−a|

Common misconception: Students include the endpoints automatically; endpoints must be tested individually (they may converge at one, both, or neither).

How it can be tested: Find radius and interval of convergence using the ratio test.

Finding Radius of Convergence

Apply ratio test to power series: lim|c_{n+1}(x−a)^{n+1} / cₙ(x−a)ⁿ| = |x−a|·L < 1 gives R=1/L. If L=0, R=∞; L=∞, R=0.

Common misconception: Students solve incorrectly for R; the condition |x−a|·L<1 gives |x−a|<1/L=R.

How it can be tested: Solve for the radius R by setting the ratio test limit < 1.

Differentiating and Integrating Power Series

A power series can be differentiated or integrated term by term within its interval of convergence. The radius of convergence is preserved; check endpoints again for the new series.

Common misconception: Students change the radius of convergence when differentiating/integrating; the radius stays the same (endpoints may change).

How it can be tested: Differentiate or integrate a power series term by term and state the new interval of convergence.

Key Vocabulary
  • power series — Σcₙ(x−a)ⁿ; function defined by infinite polynomial centered at a
  • radius of convergence — R; series converges for |x−a|
  • interval of convergence — [a−R, a+R] with endpoint tests
  • term-by-term differentiation — Valid inside radius of convergence; radius unchanged
Objectives
  • Find radius and interval of convergence using ratio test
  • Differentiate and integrate power series term by term
  • Test endpoints separately to complete interval of convergence
How this can be tested
  • Power series radius/interval of convergence in BC FR
  • Term-by-term operations tested in MC
  • Endpoints require separate investigation
Practice before moving on
  • Find interval of convergence of Σxⁿ/n
  • Differentiate Σxⁿ/n! term by term
Key Formulas
  • Power series: \( \displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-a)^n \)
  • Radius R: \( R=\lim_{n\to\infty}\!\left|\dfrac{c_n}{c_{n+1}}\right| \)

Taylor and Maclaurin Series

Learn on this site
Taylor Series Formula

f(x)=Σ_{n=0}^∞ f^{(n)}(a)/n! · (x−a)ⁿ. Maclaurin series is Taylor at a=0. Memorize: eˣ, sin x, cos x, 1/(1−x).

Common misconception: Students try to derive all Taylor series from scratch on the exam; the four standard Maclaurin series must be memorized and recognized.

How it can be tested: Write and apply the four standard Maclaurin series; manipulate them to find series for related functions.

Lagrange Error Bound

If P_n(x) is the nth-degree Taylor polynomial, |error| ≤ M|x−a|^{n+1}/(n+1)! where M is a bound on |f^{(n+1)}| on the interval.

Common misconception: Students use the wrong M or wrong n; M must bound the (n+1)th derivative, not the nth.

How it can be tested: Apply Lagrange error bound to estimate the accuracy of a Taylor polynomial approximation.

Using Series to Evaluate Limits and Integrals

Replace f(x) with its Taylor series to evaluate limits (substitute 0/0 forms) or integrals of functions without elementary antiderivatives (like e^{−x²}).

Common misconception: Students apply L'Hôpital's rule when a series substitution would be faster and cleaner.

How it can be tested: Use Maclaurin series for sin x or eˣ to evaluate limits and definite integrals.

Key Vocabulary
  • Maclaurin for eˣ — Σxⁿ/n! = 1+x+x²/2!+x³/3!+...
  • Maclaurin for sin x — Σ(−1)ⁿx^{2n+1}/(2n+1)! = x−x³/6+x⁵/120−...
  • Maclaurin for cos x — Σ(−1)ⁿx^{2n}/(2n)! = 1−x²/2+x⁴/24−...
  • Lagrange error bound — |Rₙ(x)|≤M|x−a|^{n+1}/(n+1)!
Objectives
  • Write and recognize the four standard Maclaurin series
  • Apply Lagrange error bound
  • Use series to compute limits and integrals
How this can be tested
  • Taylor series is the largest BC FR topic
  • Lagrange error bound appears every year
  • Using series to evaluate limits/integrals tested in MC and FR
Practice before moving on
  • Write Maclaurin series for e^{2x} and sin(x²)
  • Use Lagrange bound for cos x approximation error
Key Formulas
  • Taylor series: \( \displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty\dfrac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n \)
  • e^x: \( e^x=\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty\dfrac{x^n}{n!} \)
  • sin x: \( \sin x=\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty\dfrac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} \)
  • cos x: \( \cos x=\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty\dfrac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!} \)
