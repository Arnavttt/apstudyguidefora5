Atomic Structure and the Mole

Learn on this site
Subatomic Particles and Isotopes

Atoms contain protons and neutrons in the nucleus and electrons in orbitals; isotopes are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons and thus different mass numbers.

Common misconception: Students confuse mass number and atomic mass; mass number is a whole-number count of protons + neutrons, while atomic mass is the weighted average of isotope masses.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask for mass number, atomic number, or isotope identity; FRQs use mass spectrometry data to calculate average atomic mass.

Mole Concept and Avogadro's Number

One mole equals 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number); molar mass (g/mol) allows conversion between grams, moles, and number of particles.

Common misconception: Students misapply molar mass; molar mass converts between grams and moles, not between moles and particles (use Avogadro's number for the latter).

How it can be tested: MCQs and FRQs require multi-step mole conversions; practice grams → moles → particles and vice versa.

Mass Spectrometry

A mass spectrometer separates ions by mass-to-charge ratio; the spectrum shows each isotope as a peak at its mass number with height proportional to relative abundance; average atomic mass = Σ(isotope mass × fractional abundance).

Common misconception: Students use percent abundance directly without converting to decimal fractions; divide percent by 100 before multiplying by isotope mass.

How it can be tested: FRQs provide a mass spectrum and ask students to calculate average atomic mass from the data.

Key Vocabulary
  • Atomic Number (Z) — The number of protons in an atom's nucleus; defines the element.
  • Mass Number (A) — The total number of protons plus neutrons in an atom's nucleus.
  • Isotope — Atoms of the same element (same Z) with different numbers of neutrons (different A).
  • Mole — The SI unit of amount; 1 mol = 6.022 × 10²³ particles.
  • Molar Mass — The mass in grams of one mole of a substance; numerically equal to the atomic or molecular weight in amu.
Objectives
  • Calculate average atomic mass from isotope abundances.
  • Convert between grams, moles, and number of particles.
  • Interpret a mass spectrum to identify isotopes and relative abundances.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs provide isotope abundance data and ask for average atomic mass calculations.
  • MCQs test mole conversions and the definition of isotope.
  • Questions ask students to identify peaks on a mass spectrum.
Practice before moving on
  • Calculate the average atomic mass of chlorine: 75.77% ³⁵Cl (34.97 amu) and 24.23% ³⁷Cl (36.97 amu).
  • Convert 5.00 g of NaCl (molar mass 58.44 g/mol) to moles and then to formula units.

Electron Configuration

Learn on this site
Quantum Numbers and Orbitals

Four quantum numbers describe each electron: n (principal, energy level), l (azimuthal, subshell shape: s=0, p=1, d=2, f=3), mₗ (magnetic, orbital orientation), and mₛ (spin, ±½).

Common misconception: Students confuse the subshell letter with its l value; s corresponds to l=0, p to l=1, d to l=2 — this matters when counting electrons or determining orbital shapes.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask for the quantum numbers of a specific electron; FRQs ask how many electrons can occupy a given subshell.

Aufbau, Pauli, and Hund's Rules

Electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy (Aufbau); no two electrons in an atom can have the same four quantum numbers (Pauli exclusion); electrons occupy degenerate orbitals singly before pairing (Hund's rule).

Common misconception: Students fill the 4s before 3d incorrectly when writing configurations for transition metals; Cr and Cu are exceptions where one electron moves from 4s to 3d for extra stability.

How it can be tested: MCQs give an element and ask for the correct electron configuration; FRQs ask about exceptions (Cr, Cu) and their explanation.

Photoelectron Spectroscopy

PES measures the energy required to remove electrons from each subshell; peaks at high binding energy correspond to core electrons (low n); peaks at low binding energy correspond to valence electrons; peak height reflects the number of electrons.

Common misconception: Students read PES spectra backward; higher binding energy (left in AP® convention) = inner/core electrons; lower binding energy = outer valence electrons.

How it can be tested: FRQs provide a PES spectrum and ask students to identify the element and assign peaks to subshells.

Key Vocabulary
  • Principal Quantum Number (n) — Indicates the energy level (shell) of an electron; n = 1, 2, 3, …; higher n = higher energy and larger orbital.
  • Subshell — A subdivision of an electron shell specified by the azimuthal quantum number l; s (l=0), p (l=1), d (l=2), f (l=3).
  • Hund's Rule — Electrons occupy all degenerate orbitals singly (with parallel spins) before any orbital is doubly occupied.
  • Pauli Exclusion Principle — No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers; each orbital holds at most 2 electrons with opposite spins.
  • Photoelectron Spectroscopy (PES) — A technique that measures the binding energy of electrons in each subshell; provides direct evidence for shell and subshell structure.
Objectives
  • Write the full and abbreviated electron configuration for any element.
  • Apply Aufbau, Pauli, and Hund's rules correctly.
  • Interpret a PES spectrum to identify an element and assign subshells.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs give a partial electron configuration and ask what element it represents.
  • FRQs provide a PES spectrum and ask for element identification and subshell assignment.
  • Questions test exceptions to Aufbau order (Cr, Cu) and their explanations.
Practice before moving on
  • Write ground-state electron configurations for Fe (Z=26), Cu (Z=29), and Cr (Z=24).
  • Interpret a PES spectrum showing 3 peaks; assign each to a subshell and identify the element.

Periodic Trends

Learn on this site
Atomic Radius

Atomic radius increases down a group (more electron shells) and decreases left to right across a period (increasing nuclear charge pulls electrons closer with same shielding).

Common misconception: Students think size always increases with atomic number; across a period, nuclear charge increases faster than shielding, shrinking the atom despite adding electrons.

How it can be tested: MCQs rank atoms by size; FRQs ask students to explain the trend using nuclear charge and shielding.

Ionization Energy and Electron Affinity

First ionization energy (IE₁) is the energy to remove the outermost electron; IE increases left to right across a period and up a group; small dips at Groups 3A and 6A reflect subshell stability.

Common misconception: Students miss the Group 3A and 6A IE dips; O (2p⁴) has lower IE than N (2p³) because removing one electron from a paired p orbital relieves electron-electron repulsion.

How it can be tested: FRQs explain the dip in IE from N to O using orbital diagrams.

Electronegativity and Ionic vs. Covalent Character

Electronegativity increases up and to the right on the periodic table; large electronegativity differences (ΔEN > 1.7) favor ionic bonds; small differences favor covalent bonds.

Common misconception: Students treat the ionic/covalent distinction as absolute; it is a spectrum — highly polar covalent bonds have some ionic character.

How it can be tested: MCQs classify bonds as ionic, polar covalent, or nonpolar covalent based on ΔEN.

Key Vocabulary
  • Atomic Radius — One-half the distance between two bonded atoms of the same element; increases down a group, decreases left to right across a period.
  • Ionization Energy (IE) — The energy required to remove an electron from a gaseous atom; IE₁ = first ionization energy; increases up and to the right on the periodic table.
  • Shielding (Screening) — The reduction of effective nuclear charge experienced by outer electrons due to inner electrons blocking some of the nucleus's attraction.
  • Electronegativity — A measure of an atom's ability to attract bonding electrons; increases up and to the right on the periodic table (F is highest).
  • Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff) — The net positive charge experienced by a valence electron after accounting for shielding by inner electrons.
Objectives
  • Explain atomic radius, ionization energy, and electronegativity trends using Zeff and shielding.
  • Explain the anomalous IE dip at Group 3A (e.g., B < Be) and Group 6A (O < N).
  • Classify bonds as ionic, polar covalent, or nonpolar covalent using ΔEN.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs rank elements by atomic radius or ionization energy.
  • FRQs ask students to explain a specific trend anomaly using orbital diagrams.
  • Questions classify bond type from electronegativity values.
Practice before moving on
  • Rank Na, Mg, Al, and P in order of increasing first ionization energy; explain the trend and any anomalies.
  • Explain why B has a lower first IE than Be despite B having more protons.

Coulomb's Law and Atomic Properties

Learn on this site
Coulomb's Law in Chemistry

Coulomb's law states that electrostatic force is proportional to the product of charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance; this explains trends in lattice energy, ionization energy, and atomic/ionic radius.

Common misconception: Students apply Coulomb's law only to physics; in chemistry it explains why smaller, higher-charged ions have greater lattice energies and why ion size affects many properties.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to use Coulomb's law reasoning to compare lattice energies or explain why one ion is larger than another.

Ion Formation and Sizes

Cations are smaller than their parent atoms (fewer electrons, same nuclear charge); anions are larger (more electrons, same nuclear charge); isoelectronic ions are ranked by nuclear charge.

Common misconception: Students think all ions are the same size; Na⁺ is much smaller than Na because removing an electron eliminates the outer shell, increasing Zeff on the remaining electrons.

How it can be tested: MCQs rank isoelectronic ions by size; FRQs compare ion sizes using Zeff arguments.

Electron Shielding and Valence Electrons

Core electrons shield valence electrons from the full nuclear charge; effective nuclear charge (Zeff) ≈ Z − shielding; higher Zeff → smaller radius, higher IE, higher electronegativity.

Common misconception: Students assume all electrons shield equally; inner electrons shield more effectively than electrons in the same subshell, which is why valence electrons feel a net positive charge much less than Z.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to estimate Zeff for an element using approximate shielding constants.

Key Vocabulary
  • Coulomb's Law — Electrostatic force ∝ q₁q₂/r²; in chemistry, stronger ion-ion attraction → higher lattice energy, higher melting point, shorter bond.
  • Cation — A positively charged ion formed by losing electrons; smaller than the parent atom.
  • Anion — A negatively charged ion formed by gaining electrons; larger than the parent atom.
  • Isoelectronic — Ions or atoms with the same number of electrons; among isoelectronic species, the one with the highest nuclear charge is smallest.
  • Lattice Energy — The energy released when gaseous ions form a crystalline ionic solid; larger magnitude for smaller, more highly charged ions.
Objectives
  • Apply Coulomb's law to explain differences in lattice energy between ionic compounds.
  • Compare ionic radii of isoelectronic species.
  • Explain how Zeff determines periodic trends.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs compare lattice energies of two ionic compounds and ask for the reasoning.
  • MCQs rank isoelectronic ions by radius.
  • Questions use Zeff to explain ionization energy or electronegativity differences.
Practice before moving on
  • Rank MgO, NaF, and CaO by lattice energy; justify using Coulomb's law.
  • Rank Na⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺ (all isoelectronic, 10 electrons) by ionic radius.
