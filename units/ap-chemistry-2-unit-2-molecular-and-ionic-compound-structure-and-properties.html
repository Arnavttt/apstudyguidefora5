Lewis Structures and Formal Charge

Learn on this site
Drawing Lewis Structures

Count total valence electrons; place atoms (least electronegative in center); satisfy octets with bonds and lone pairs; use multiple bonds if needed.

Common misconception: Students forget to check the octet for each atom; after drawing bonds, count electrons around each atom — 8 satisfies the octet (2 for H); use double or triple bonds to fix octets without adding electrons.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to draw Lewis structures for molecules with expanded octets, resonance, or formal charge optimization.

Formal Charge

Formal charge = (valence electrons) − (lone pair electrons) − ½(bonding electrons); the best Lewis structure minimizes formal charges and places negative formal charge on the more electronegative atom.

Common misconception: Students ignore formal charge; it determines which resonance structure better represents reality (e.g., in SO₃ or CO₂, the structure with lower formal charges is preferred).

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to calculate formal charges and use them to select the best Lewis structure from alternatives.

Resonance Structures

Resonance structures are used when multiple equivalent Lewis structures can be drawn; the actual molecule is a hybrid of all resonance structures — bonds are intermediate in length and order.

Common misconception: Students think resonance structures "flip" back and forth; the molecule does not oscillate — it exists as a single hybrid structure with intermediate bond properties.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask for the bond order in a resonance hybrid; FRQs draw resonance structures for ozone, nitrate, etc.

Key Vocabulary
  • Lewis Structure — A diagram showing all valence electrons as dots or bonds between atoms; lone pairs and bonding pairs are shown explicitly.
  • Formal Charge — The hypothetical charge assigned to an atom assuming equal sharing of bonding electrons; FC = valence e⁻ − lone pair e⁻ − ½(bonding e⁻).
  • Octet Rule — Atoms tend to form bonds until they are surrounded by 8 valence electrons; exceptions include H (2), Be (4), B (6), and Period 3+ elements (expanded octets).
  • Resonance — The delocalization of electrons in molecules where more than one valid Lewis structure can be drawn; actual structure is a hybrid.
  • Bond Order — The number of bonding pairs between two atoms; single = 1, double = 2, triple = 3; higher bond order → shorter, stronger bond.
Objectives
  • Draw correct Lewis structures for molecules and polyatomic ions.
  • Calculate formal charges and use them to select the best Lewis structure.
  • Draw resonance structures and describe the resonance hybrid.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask for Lewis structures of molecules with formal charge optimization.
  • MCQs ask about bond order in resonance hybrids.
  • Questions ask about expanded octets in Period 3+ elements.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw Lewis structures for SO₃, NO₃⁻, and PCl₅; identify any formal charges.
  • Compare two Lewis structures for CO₂ and explain which has better formal charges.

VSEPR Theory and Molecular Geometry

Learn on this site
Electron Domains and Geometry

VSEPR: electron domains (bonds + lone pairs) around a central atom arrange to minimize repulsion; electron geometry describes all domains, molecular geometry describes only atoms.

Common misconception: Students confuse electron geometry with molecular geometry; both describe the arrangement of electron domains, but molecular geometry ignores lone pairs.

How it can be tested: MCQs name molecular geometry given the number of bonding and lone pairs; FRQs predict the geometry from a Lewis structure.

Effect of Lone Pairs on Bond Angles

Lone pairs occupy more space than bonding pairs, compressing bond angles; H₂O has 104.5° (not 109.5°) and NH₃ has 107° because lone pairs repel bonding pairs more strongly.

Common misconception: Students assume all sp³ geometries have 109.5° angles; lone pair repulsion always reduces bond angles below the ideal tetrahedral angle.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to explain why the bond angle in H₂O is less than in CH₄.

Polarity of Molecules

A molecule is polar if it has polar bonds AND the bond dipoles do not cancel due to geometry; CO₂ is nonpolar (linear; dipoles cancel), H₂O is polar (bent; dipoles add).

Common misconception: Students think any molecule with polar bonds is polar; symmetrical molecules (CO₂, CCl₄) are nonpolar because dipoles cancel.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask whether a molecule is polar; FRQs ask students to predict polarity from geometry and bond dipoles.

Key Vocabulary
  • VSEPR — Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion theory; electron domains arrange to minimize repulsion, determining molecular geometry.
  • Electron Domain — A region of electron density around a central atom; includes single bonds, double bonds, triple bonds, and lone pairs (each counts as one domain).
  • Molecular Geometry — The 3D arrangement of atoms (not lone pairs) around a central atom; determined by electron domain geometry minus lone pairs.
  • Dipole Moment — A measure of bond polarity; arises when bonding electrons are unequally shared; net dipole moment indicates a polar molecule.
  • Bond Angle — The angle between two bonds to the same central atom; reduced below ideal by lone pair repulsion.
Objectives
  • Predict molecular geometry from Lewis structures using VSEPR.
  • Explain how lone pairs compress bond angles below ideal values.
  • Determine whether a molecule is polar based on geometry and bond polarity.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs present a Lewis structure and ask for molecular geometry.
  • FRQs ask students to predict geometry, bond angles, and polarity for a molecule.
  • Questions ask why CO₂ is nonpolar while H₂O is polar despite both having polar bonds.
Practice before moving on
  • Predict geometry, bond angles (approximate), and polarity for: CH₄, NH₃, H₂O, CO₂, BF₃.
  • Explain why CCl₄ is nonpolar even though each C–Cl bond is polar.

Hybridization and Bond Theory

Learn on this site
Hybridization of Atomic Orbitals

Hybridization explains molecular geometry: sp (linear, 180°), sp² (trigonal planar, 120°), sp³ (tetrahedral, 109.5°), sp³d (trigonal bipyramidal), sp³d² (octahedral); number of electron domains = hybridization type.

Common misconception: Students guess hybridization instead of counting electron domains; the hybridization of the central atom equals the total number of electron domains around it.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask for the hybridization of the central atom; FRQs ask students to relate hybridization to geometry and bond angles.

Sigma and Pi Bonds

A single bond is one sigma (σ) bond; a double bond is one σ + one pi (π) bond; a triple bond is one σ + two π bonds; σ bonds allow rotation, π bonds restrict rotation.

Common misconception: Students think double bonds have two sigma bonds; each bond has exactly one σ bond — additional bonds are π bonds formed by sideways p-orbital overlap.

How it can be tested: MCQs count the number of σ and π bonds in a molecule; FRQs ask why restricted rotation around a C=C double bond creates geometric isomers.

Ionic vs. Covalent Bonding

Ionic compounds form when electrons are fully transferred from a metal to a nonmetal; ionic solids have high melting points, conduct electricity when molten or dissolved, and have brittle crystalline structures.

Common misconception: Students think ionic bonds are always stronger than covalent; diamond (covalent network solid) has higher melting point than most ionic compounds because all bonds are strong covalent bonds throughout.

How it can be tested: MCQs compare properties of ionic and covalent compounds; FRQs ask students to explain ionic compound properties using lattice energy.

Key Vocabulary
  • sp³ Hybridization — The mixing of one s and three p orbitals to form four equivalent sp³ hybrid orbitals; tetrahedral electron geometry; 109.5° bond angles.
  • Sigma Bond (σ) — A bond formed by head-on orbital overlap along the internuclear axis; all single bonds are sigma bonds; allows free rotation.
  • Pi Bond (π) — A bond formed by sideways overlap of p orbitals above and below the internuclear axis; present in double (1π) and triple bonds (2π); restricts rotation.
  • Ionic Compound — A compound formed by electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions; forms a crystal lattice; high melting point, brittle, conducts when dissolved.
  • Network Covalent Solid — A solid where atoms are connected by a continuous network of covalent bonds (e.g., diamond, SiO₂); very high melting point; does not conduct electricity.
Objectives
  • Determine hybridization from electron domain count.
  • Count sigma and pi bonds in a molecule.
  • Compare the properties of ionic and covalent compounds.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask for hybridization of a central atom and number of σ/π bonds.
  • FRQs ask students to explain physical properties using bond theory.
  • Questions compare melting points of ionic, molecular, and network covalent solids.
Practice before moving on
  • Count all σ and π bonds in CH₃–CH=CH–CH₃; determine the hybridization of each carbon.
  • Explain why diamond has a higher melting point than NaCl despite both having only covalent or ionic bonds.

Ionic Compound Properties

Learn on this site
Lattice Energy and the Born-Haber Cycle

Lattice energy is the energy released when gaseous ions form a crystalline ionic solid; it can be calculated indirectly using the Born-Haber cycle (Hess's Law applied to ion formation).

Common misconception: Students think lattice energy is directly measurable; it is calculated indirectly because we cannot easily form ionic solids directly from gaseous ions in a controlled experiment.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to use lattice energy and periodic trends to compare the stability of ionic compounds.

Properties of Ionic Solids

Ionic solids are brittle (ion layers shift and like charges align, causing repulsion), have high melting points (strong lattice), and conduct electricity when dissolved or melted (ions free to move).

Common misconception: Students think ionic solids conduct electricity in solid form; they do not — ions are fixed in the lattice and cannot carry current; only when dissolved or melted do ions become mobile charge carriers.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask which state of an ionic compound conducts electricity; FRQs explain brittleness using Coulomb's law.

Comparing Compound Properties

Melting point order generally: metallic/ionic (strong, high MP) > network covalent > molecular (weak IMF, low MP); exceptions exist based on specific charges, sizes, and IMFs.

Common misconception: Students expect all ionic compounds to have higher melting points than all covalent compounds; diamond (network covalent) has a higher MP than most ionic compounds.

How it can be tested: MCQs rank compounds by melting point; FRQs justify the ranking using bond type and intermolecular or interionic forces.

Key Vocabulary
  • Born-Haber Cycle — A thermodynamic cycle using Hess's Law to calculate the lattice energy of an ionic compound from measurable enthalpy values.
  • Brittleness of Ionic Solids — When stress displaces ion layers, like charges align and repel, causing the crystal to fracture along cleavage planes.
  • Electrical Conductivity — Ionic compounds conduct electricity only when dissolved in water or melted, as ions become free to move; solid ionic compounds do not conduct.
  • Coordination Number — The number of ions of opposite charge immediately surrounding a given ion in an ionic crystal lattice.
  • Solubility — Ionic compounds dissolve in polar solvents (like water) when the energy of ion-dipole interactions with water (hydration energy) exceeds the lattice energy.
Objectives
  • Use the Born-Haber cycle concept to explain lattice energy.
  • Explain ionic solid brittleness and conductivity using ion arrangement.
  • Predict and rank melting points of ionic, molecular, and network covalent solids.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask about the conductivity of ionic compounds in different states.
  • FRQs compare lattice energies using Coulomb's law reasoning.
  • Questions rank melting points using bond type and intermolecular forces.
Practice before moving on
  • Explain why solid NaCl does not conduct electricity but aqueous NaCl does.
  • Rank NaF, MgO, and Al₂O₃ by lattice energy and melting point; justify the ranking.
