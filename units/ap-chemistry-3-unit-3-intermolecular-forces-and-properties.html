Unit 3 of AP® Chemistry

Intermolecular Forces and Properties

Intermolecular forces determine the physical properties of substances; the states of matter and phase changes are governed by IMFs.

Unit learning gateway

  • London dispersion, dipole-dipole, and hydrogen bonding are the three main IMFs.
  • Stronger IMFs → higher boiling point, higher viscosity, lower vapor pressure.
  • Vapor pressure and boiling point are related through the Clausius-Clapeyron equation.
  • Solubility follows "like dissolves like": polar dissolves polar, nonpolar dissolves nonpolar.

Types of Intermolecular Forces

Learn on this site
London Dispersion Forces

LDFs arise from temporary dipoles due to electron fluctuations; they exist in ALL molecules; strength increases with molecular size (more electrons, larger electron cloud, more polarizable).

Common misconception: Students think LDFs only exist in nonpolar molecules; they exist in ALL molecules (including polar ones) — they are just overshadowed by stronger IMFs in polar molecules.

How it can be tested: MCQs compare LDF strength based on molar mass; FRQs explain why larger nonpolar molecules have higher boiling points.

Dipole-Dipole and Hydrogen Bonding

Dipole-dipole forces occur between polar molecules (partial charge attraction); hydrogen bonding (a special strong dipole-dipole) occurs when H is bonded to N, O, or F and the H–N/O/F dipole interacts with a lone pair on another N, O, or F.

Common misconception: Students include H bonded to Cl or S in hydrogen bonding; only H bonded to N, O, or F creates hydrogen bonds — these atoms are small and highly electronegative.

How it can be tested: MCQs identify which molecules can hydrogen bond; FRQs explain the anomalously high boiling point of water.

Ion-Dipole Forces

Ion-dipole forces act between ions and polar molecules; they are the strongest intermolecular force and explain why ionic compounds dissolve in water (the hydration energy stabilizes the ions).

Common misconception: Students rank ion-dipole forces as weak because they are "intermolecular"; ion-dipole forces are actually the strongest intermolecular interaction and are crucial for understanding aqueous ionic solutions.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to explain why ionic compounds dissolve in water using ion-dipole interactions.

Key Vocabulary
  • London Dispersion Forces (LDF) — Weak intermolecular forces present in ALL molecules due to temporary induced dipoles; increase with molecular size and surface area.
  • Dipole-Dipole Force — Intermolecular attraction between the positive end of one polar molecule and the negative end of another.
  • Hydrogen Bond — A strong dipole-dipole interaction between an H atom bonded to N, O, or F and a lone pair on N, O, or F of another molecule.
  • Polarizability — The ease with which an electron cloud can be distorted to form a temporary dipole; increases with molecular size and number of electrons.
  • Ion-Dipole Force — The strongest intermolecular force; arises between an ion and a polar molecule; responsible for the dissolution of ionic compounds in water.
Objectives
  • Identify and rank IMFs present in given molecules.
  • Explain the criteria for hydrogen bonding.
  • Use IMF strength to predict relative boiling points.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask which IMF is strongest in a given molecule or between two molecules.
  • FRQs compare boiling points of molecules and ask students to use IMFs to justify the ranking.
  • Questions explain why water's properties (boiling point, surface tension) are anomalously high.
Practice before moving on
  • Rank CH₄, C₂H₆, NH₃, and H₂O by boiling point; justify using IMF types and strengths.
  • Explain why HF has a higher boiling point than HCl despite being lighter.

Properties of Liquids and Solids

Learn on this site
Vapor Pressure and Boiling Point

Vapor pressure is the pressure of gas above a liquid at equilibrium; it increases with temperature and decreases with stronger IMFs; a liquid boils when its vapor pressure equals atmospheric pressure.

Common misconception: Students confuse boiling point with evaporation; evaporation occurs at any temperature from the surface; boiling occurs throughout the liquid when VP = external pressure.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to compare vapor pressures; FRQs explain why stronger IMFs produce lower vapor pressure.

Surface Tension, Viscosity, and Capillary Action

Surface tension (energy needed to increase liquid surface area) and viscosity (resistance to flow) both increase with IMF strength; capillary action (liquid rising in a tube) reflects adhesion vs. cohesion.

Common misconception: Students think all properties increase with temperature; surface tension and viscosity both decrease with temperature because thermal energy disrupts IMFs.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask which liquid has higher surface tension or viscosity; FRQs explain capillary action using adhesion and cohesion.

Types of Solids

Solids are classified as ionic (high MP, brittle), metallic (conducts as solid, ductile), network covalent (highest MP, very hard), and molecular (low MP, soft, nonconducting).

Common misconception: Students confuse molecular solids with covalent network solids; ice (molecular solid) melts at 0°C because H-bonds between water molecules are broken, while quartz (network covalent) melts above 1700°C because covalent bonds throughout must break.

How it can be tested: MCQs identify the type of solid from its properties; FRQs justify melting point ranking using bond/IMF type.

Key Vocabulary
  • Vapor Pressure — The pressure exerted by the gas-phase molecules above a liquid at equilibrium; increases with temperature, decreases with stronger IMFs.
  • Normal Boiling Point — The temperature at which a liquid's vapor pressure equals 1 atm (standard atmospheric pressure).
  • Surface Tension — The energy required to increase a liquid's surface area; arises from unbalanced IMFs at the surface; higher for stronger IMFs.
  • Viscosity — Resistance of a liquid to flow; increases with stronger IMFs and decreases with increasing temperature.
  • Molecular Solid — A solid held together by intermolecular forces (not chemical bonds); low melting point; nonconducting; examples: ice, dry ice, wax.
Objectives
  • Relate IMF strength to vapor pressure and boiling point.
  • Explain surface tension and viscosity using IMF concepts.
  • Classify a solid type based on its properties.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs compare vapor pressures or boiling points of two substances.
  • FRQs ask students to rank solids by melting point using bond type.
  • Questions identify solid type from conductivity, hardness, and melting point data.
Practice before moving on
  • Compare the vapor pressures at 25°C of diethyl ether (LDFs only) and water (H-bonds); justify.
  • A solid conducts electricity, is malleable, and has a moderate melting point; what type of solid is it?

Solutions and Solubility

Learn on this site
"Like Dissolves Like" Principle

Polar and ionic solutes dissolve in polar solvents (like water) because ion-dipole or dipole-dipole interactions replace intramolecular/lattice forces; nonpolar solutes dissolve in nonpolar solvents due to LDF matching.

Common misconception: Students think all salts dissolve in water; some ionic compounds have such high lattice energies that hydration energy is insufficient — they are insoluble or slightly soluble.

How it can be tested: MCQs predict the solubility of a solute in a given solvent; FRQs explain the dissolution process using IMF interactions.

Colligative Properties

Colligative properties depend on the number of solute particles, not their identity: boiling point elevation (ΔTb = iKbm), freezing point depression (ΔTf = iKfm), and osmotic pressure (π = iMRT).

Common misconception: Students forget the van't Hoff factor i for electrolytes; NaCl dissociates into 2 ions (i=2), so it has twice the effect on colligative properties per mole compared to a nonelectrolyte.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to calculate the boiling point or freezing point of a solution; MCQs test how the van't Hoff factor affects colligative properties.

Concentration Units

Molarity (M = mol solute/L solution) is temperature-dependent; molality (m = mol solute/kg solvent) is temperature-independent and used for colligative properties; mole fraction and mass percent are also used.

Common misconception: Students confuse molarity and molality; molality is used in colligative property calculations because it does not change with temperature, making it more precise for these calculations.

How it can be tested: MCQs convert between concentration units; FRQs use molality in colligative property calculations.

Key Vocabulary
  • Solubility — The maximum amount of solute that dissolves in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature; governed by IMF compatibility ("like dissolves like").
  • Colligative Property — A property that depends only on the number of dissolved particles, not their identity; includes BP elevation, FP depression, vapor pressure lowering, and osmotic pressure.
  • Van't Hoff Factor (i) — The number of particles one formula unit produces when it dissolves; i=1 for nonelectrolytes, i=2 for NaCl, i=3 for CaCl₂.
  • Molality (m) — Moles of solute per kilogram of solvent; temperature-independent; used in colligative property calculations.
  • Osmosis — The net flow of solvent through a semipermeable membrane from a region of lower solute concentration to higher solute concentration.
Objectives
  • Apply "like dissolves like" to predict solubility.
  • Calculate boiling point elevation and freezing point depression.
  • Distinguish molarity from molality and use each correctly.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to calculate freezing point depression or boiling point elevation.
  • MCQs test the van't Hoff factor for electrolytes.
  • Questions ask which concentration unit is appropriate for colligative properties.
Practice before moving on
  • Calculate the freezing point of a solution made from 1.00 mol NaCl dissolved in 500 g water (Kf = 1.86°C/m).
  • Explain why ethanol (polar) mixes with water but hexane (nonpolar) does not.

Phase Diagrams and Phase Changes

Learn on this site
Phase Diagrams

A phase diagram shows the stable phase (solid, liquid, gas) at each combination of temperature and pressure; the triple point is where all three phases coexist; the critical point is where liquid and gas become indistinguishable.

Common misconception: Students misread phase diagrams; moving right along the temperature axis at constant pressure crosses phase boundaries; moving up along pressure at constant temperature also crosses boundaries.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask which phase is stable at a given T and P; FRQs ask students to trace a path on a phase diagram and identify phase changes.

Phase Change Thermodynamics

Endothermic phase changes (melting, vaporization, sublimation) require energy input; exothermic (freezing, condensation, deposition) release energy; the enthalpy of vaporization is always larger than enthalpy of fusion.

Common misconception: Students think all phase changes involve temperature changes; during a phase change at constant pressure, temperature is constant as energy goes into breaking IMFs, not raising kinetic energy.

How it can be tested: FRQs calculate the total energy needed to heat and vaporize a substance, including the plateau during the phase change.

Clausius-Clapeyron Equation

ln(P₂/P₁) = −ΔHvap/R × (1/T₂ − 1/T₁): relates the change in vapor pressure to temperature change; allows calculation of ΔHvap from two vapor pressure-temperature data points.

Common misconception: Students forget to use Kelvin temperatures; the Clausius-Clapeyron equation requires T in Kelvin — using Celsius gives wrong answers.

How it can be tested: FRQs provide two vapor pressure-temperature pairs and ask students to calculate ΔHvap.

Key Vocabulary
  • Phase Diagram — A graph showing which phase (solid, liquid, gas) is stable at each combination of temperature and pressure.
  • Triple Point — The unique temperature and pressure at which solid, liquid, and gas are all in equilibrium simultaneously.
  • Critical Point — The temperature and pressure above which the liquid and gas phases become indistinguishable (supercritical fluid).
  • Enthalpy of Vaporization (ΔHvap) — The heat required to vaporize 1 mol of a liquid at constant pressure; always positive (endothermic); larger for stronger IMFs.
  • Heating Curve — A graph showing temperature vs. heat added for a pure substance; shows flat regions (phase changes) and sloped regions (temperature increase within a phase).
Objectives
  • Interpret a phase diagram to identify stable phases and phase transitions.
  • Explain why temperature is constant during a phase change.
  • Apply the Clausius-Clapeyron equation to calculate ΔHvap.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask students to read a phase diagram and identify the phase at given T and P.
  • FRQs ask students to interpret heating curves and calculate total energy for a multi-step heating process.
  • Questions apply the Clausius-Clapeyron equation to real data.
Practice before moving on
  • Interpret a heating curve for water from -20°C to 120°C; identify each segment.
  • Use the Clausius-Clapeyron equation to find ΔHvap given two temperature-vapor pressure data points.
