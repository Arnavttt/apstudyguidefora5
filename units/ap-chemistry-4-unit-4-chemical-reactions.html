Balancing Equations and Reaction Types

Learn on this site
Balancing Chemical Equations

Equations must be balanced to satisfy conservation of mass and charge; coefficients (not subscripts) are adjusted; the sum of atoms of each element must be equal on both sides.

Common misconception: Students change subscripts to balance equations; only coefficients can be changed — altering subscripts changes the identity of the substance.

How it can be tested: MCQs give an unbalanced equation and ask for the coefficient of a specific compound; FRQs ask students to balance and use the equation for stoichiometry.

Types of Chemical Reactions

Major reaction types: combination (A + B → AB), decomposition (AB → A + B), single replacement (A + BC → AC + B), double replacement (AB + CD → AD + CB), and combustion (fuel + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O).

Common misconception: Students memorize only the five types without identifying them from equations; practice recognizing each type from the pattern of reactants and products.

How it can be tested: MCQs identify the reaction type; FRQs ask students to predict products of a given reaction type.

Oxidation-Reduction Overview

Redox reactions involve electron transfer; oxidation is loss of electrons (OIL), reduction is gain of electrons (RIG); oxidation number rules assign charges to track electron transfer.

Common misconception: Students confuse oxidizing and reducing agents; the oxidizing agent gains electrons (is reduced); the reducing agent loses electrons (is oxidized).

How it can be tested: MCQs ask for oxidation numbers; FRQs identify oxidizing and reducing agents in a given redox reaction.

Key Vocabulary
  • Law of Conservation of Mass — Matter is neither created nor destroyed; the total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products in a chemical reaction.
  • Coefficient — A number placed before a chemical formula in a balanced equation to indicate the relative number of moles of that substance.
  • Combustion Reaction — A reaction between a fuel and oxygen (O₂) producing CO₂ and H₂O; exothermic.
  • Oxidation State — A hypothetical charge assigned to an atom using oxidation number rules; used to identify redox reactions.
  • Redox Reaction — A reaction involving the transfer of electrons; oxidation (loss of e⁻) and reduction (gain of e⁻) always occur together.
Objectives
  • Balance chemical equations by adjusting coefficients.
  • Classify reactions as combination, decomposition, single replacement, double replacement, or combustion.
  • Assign oxidation states and identify oxidizing and reducing agents.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs give unbalanced equations and ask for the coefficient of a specific species.
  • FRQs ask students to balance and use equations for stoichiometry.
  • Questions assign oxidation numbers and identify the species oxidized and reduced.
Practice before moving on
  • Balance: __Fe + __O₂ → __Fe₂O₃; then balance: __C₃H₈ + __O₂ → __CO₂ + __H₂O.
  • Assign oxidation numbers to each element in KMnO₄ and MnO₂; identify which Mn is more oxidized.

Net Ionic Equations and Precipitation

Learn on this site
Writing Net Ionic Equations

Net ionic equations show only the species that actually change; spectator ions (unchanged, same form on both sides) are canceled out; strong electrolytes are written as dissociated ions.

Common misconception: Students include spectator ions in net ionic equations; only species that change form or participate in bonding/precipitation are included in the net ionic equation.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to write the molecular, complete ionic, and net ionic equations for a reaction.

Solubility Rules and Precipitation

Solubility rules predict which ionic compounds dissolve (soluble) or form precipitates (insoluble); key rules: NO₃⁻ always soluble; Cl⁻ soluble except with Ag⁺, Pb²⁺, Hg₂²⁺; SO₄²⁻ soluble except Ba²⁺, Pb²⁺, Ca²⁺.

Common misconception: Students memorize too many exceptions without understanding the pattern; focus on the most tested exceptions: AgCl↓, BaSO₄↓, PbI₂↓, and the fact that all nitrates and ammonium salts are soluble.

How it can be tested: MCQs predict whether a precipitate forms; FRQs write the net ionic equation for the precipitation reaction.

Acid-Base Reactions in Ionic Form

Strong acid + strong base neutralization produces water and a salt; net ionic equation is always: H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l); weak acids and bases are written as molecular formulas (not dissociated).

Common misconception: Students dissociate weak acids and bases when writing ionic equations; only strong electrolytes (strong acids, strong bases, soluble salts) are written in dissociated form.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to write the net ionic equation for weak acid-strong base reactions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Net Ionic Equation — An equation showing only the ions and molecules that actually participate in the reaction; spectator ions are removed.
  • Spectator Ion — An ion that is present in solution but does not change during the reaction; appears identically on both sides of the complete ionic equation.
  • Precipitate — An insoluble solid that forms when two aqueous solutions are mixed and an insoluble ionic compound results.
  • Strong Electrolyte — A substance that completely dissociates into ions in water; includes strong acids, strong bases, and soluble ionic compounds.
  • Solubility Rules — Empirical guidelines predicting which ionic compounds are soluble (dissociate) or insoluble (precipitate) in water.
Objectives
  • Write net ionic equations from complete ionic equations by canceling spectator ions.
  • Apply solubility rules to predict whether a precipitate forms when two solutions are mixed.
  • Write net ionic equations for strong acid-strong base and weak acid-strong base reactions.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask for the net ionic equation when two ionic solutions are mixed.
  • MCQs ask students to identify the spectator ions in a reaction.
  • Questions predict precipitate formation from pairs of ionic solutions.
Practice before moving on
  • Mix Na₂SO₄(aq) and BaCl₂(aq); predict whether a precipitate forms and write the net ionic equation.
  • Write the net ionic equation for the reaction of CH₃COOH (weak acid) with NaOH.

Stoichiometry and Limiting Reagents

Learn on this site
Mole-to-Mole Stoichiometry

The coefficients in a balanced equation give the mole ratios of reactants and products; use dimensional analysis (moles A × [mol B / mol A]) to convert between moles of different species.

Common misconception: Students use mass ratios instead of mole ratios; stoichiometry requires mole ratios from balanced equations, not mass ratios.

How it can be tested: FRQs set up a multi-step stoichiometry calculation; MCQs ask for the amount of a product formed from a given amount of reactant.

Limiting Reagent

The limiting reagent is the reactant that is fully consumed first; it determines the theoretical yield of product; the excess reagent remains after the reaction.

Common misconception: Students pick the reagent with the smallest mass as limiting; the limiting reagent is determined by dividing moles of each reactant by its stoichiometric coefficient and comparing.

How it can be tested: FRQs identify the limiting reagent and calculate theoretical yield; MCQs ask which reagent is limiting given amounts of both reactants.

Percent Yield and Percent Purity

Percent yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100%; less than 100% due to side reactions, incomplete reactions, or product loss; percent purity = (mass of pure substance / mass of sample) × 100%.

Common misconception: Students confuse theoretical yield with actual yield; theoretical yield is calculated from stoichiometry assuming 100% conversion; actual yield is the measured mass obtained.

How it can be tested: FRQs calculate theoretical yield from limiting reagent, then percent yield from actual yield.

Key Vocabulary
  • Mole Ratio — The ratio of coefficients of two substances in a balanced chemical equation; used to convert moles of one substance to moles of another.
  • Limiting Reagent — The reactant that is completely consumed first; limits the amount of product that can form.
  • Excess Reagent — The reactant that remains after the limiting reagent is fully consumed.
  • Theoretical Yield — The maximum amount of product calculated from stoichiometry assuming complete conversion of the limiting reagent.
  • Percent Yield — (Actual yield / Theoretical yield) × 100%; a measure of reaction efficiency.
Objectives
  • Set up and solve multi-step stoichiometry calculations using mole ratios.
  • Identify the limiting reagent and calculate theoretical yield.
  • Calculate percent yield from actual and theoretical yields.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to identify the limiting reagent and calculate theoretical and percent yield.
  • MCQs ask for the moles of product from given moles/grams of reactants.
  • Questions test percent yield as a diagnostic of reaction efficiency.
Practice before moving on
  • Given 5.00 g Fe and 4.00 g O₂ reacting (4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃), find the limiting reagent and theoretical yield of Fe₂O₃.
  • If 8.00 g of Fe₂O₃ is isolated in the above reaction, calculate the percent yield.

Solution Stoichiometry and Titration

Learn on this site
Molarity Calculations

Molarity (M) = moles of solute / liters of solution; M × V (L) = moles; this allows calculation of moles from volume and concentration, enabling solution stoichiometry.

Common misconception: Students confuse volume in mL and L; molarity uses liters — always convert mL to L by dividing by 1000 before using M × V = n.

How it can be tested: MCQs and FRQs use M × V = n as the bridge between solution volume and stoichiometry.

Titration

In an acid-base titration, a solution of known concentration (standard solution) is added until the equivalence point (stoichiometric neutralization); an indicator changes color at (near) the equivalence point.

Common misconception: Students confuse equivalence point and endpoint; the equivalence point is the theoretical stoichiometric point; the endpoint is where the indicator changes color (close but not identical to the equivalence point).

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to calculate the concentration of an unknown acid or base from titration data.

Gravimetric Analysis

Gravimetric analysis determines concentration by precipitating and weighing a known product; the mass of precipitate is used to back-calculate the amount of analyte in the original solution.

Common misconception: Students forget that stoichiometry must be used to relate precipitate to analyte; grams of precipitate → moles of precipitate → moles of analyte → concentration.

How it can be tested: FRQs provide mass of precipitate and ask for the concentration of the original solution.

Key Vocabulary
  • Molarity — Moles of solute per liter of solution (M = n/V); M × V = n gives moles from concentration and volume.
  • Equivalence Point — The point in a titration where stoichiometrically equal amounts of acid and base have been mixed; not necessarily at pH 7.
  • Endpoint — The point where the indicator changes color in a titration; a practical approximation of the equivalence point.
  • Standard Solution — A solution of precisely known concentration used in titration.
  • Gravimetric Analysis — An analytical technique that determines the amount of a substance by measuring the mass of a pure, stoichiometrically related product.
Objectives
  • Use M × V = n to calculate moles in solution stoichiometry.
  • Calculate the concentration of an unknown solution from titration data.
  • Solve gravimetric analysis problems using precipitate mass.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to calculate unknown acid/base concentration from titration.
  • MCQs use M × V = n in multi-step solution stoichiometry.
  • Questions apply gravimetric analysis to determine ion concentration in solution.
Practice before moving on
  • If 25.0 mL of 0.100 M NaOH neutralizes an unknown HCl solution, calculate the molarity of HCl if 30.0 mL of HCl was used.
  • A solution of AgNO₃ produces 0.717 g of AgCl precipitate from 50.0 mL of NaCl solution; find the concentration of NaCl.
