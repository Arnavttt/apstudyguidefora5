Unit 5 of AP® Chemistry

Kinetics

Chemical kinetics explains how fast reactions occur and identifies the factors that control reaction rates.

Unit learning gateway

  • Reaction rate is measured by the change in concentration of reactants or products over time.
  • Rate laws are experimentally determined; they cannot be written from balanced equations.
  • The rate constant k depends on temperature (Arrhenius equation).

Reaction Rates

Learn on this site
Defining and Measuring Reaction Rate

Reaction rate = change in concentration per unit time; expressed as −Δ[reactant]/Δt or +Δ[product]/Δt; rates related by stoichiometric coefficients (rate of A × 1/a = rate of B × 1/b).

Common misconception: Students apply the rate to any species without adjusting for stoichiometry; the rate of disappearance of each species must be divided by its stoichiometric coefficient to get the unique reaction rate.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to relate rates of different species; FRQs interpret concentration-time data to calculate average rate.

Factors Affecting Reaction Rate

Reaction rate increases with: higher concentration (more collisions), higher temperature (more energy), catalysts (lower activation energy), and greater surface area for heterogeneous reactions.

Common misconception: Students think higher pressure always increases rate; pressure effects apply only to gas-phase reactions where it effectively increases concentration.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to identify the factor causing a rate change; FRQs explain why a catalyst increases rate without being consumed.

Collision Theory and Activation Energy

Collisions must have sufficient energy (≥ Ea) and correct orientation to result in reaction; activation energy (Ea) is the minimum energy required; a catalyst provides an alternative pathway with lower Ea.

Common misconception: Students think all collisions cause reactions; only a small fraction of collisions have both sufficient energy and correct orientation — this explains why rates are slower than would be predicted from collision frequency alone.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to interpret an energy diagram showing Ea and explain how a catalyst changes it.

Key Vocabulary
  • Reaction Rate — The change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time; always positive.
  • Activation Energy (Ea) — The minimum energy required for a collision to result in a chemical reaction.
  • Catalyst — A substance that increases reaction rate by providing an alternative pathway with lower Ea; not consumed in the reaction.
  • Effective Collision — A collision with sufficient energy (≥ Ea) and correct orientation to result in bond breaking and product formation.
  • Transition State — The high-energy, unstable arrangement of atoms at the top of the energy barrier (activated complex); exists momentarily during the reaction.
Objectives
  • Calculate reaction rates from concentration-time data.
  • Identify factors that increase reaction rate and explain each using collision theory.
  • Interpret energy diagrams to identify Ea and the effect of a catalyst.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask which factor increases rate and why.
  • FRQs provide concentration-time data and ask students to calculate initial rate.
  • Questions explain how a catalyst increases rate without changing ΔH of the reaction.
Practice before moving on
  • Given [A] drops from 0.100 M to 0.060 M in 20 s, calculate the average rate of disappearance of A.
  • Draw an energy diagram for a reaction with and without a catalyst; label Ea, Ea(cat), and ΔH.

Rate Laws

Learn on this site
Writing Rate Laws and Reaction Orders

Rate law: rate = k[A]^m[B]^n; orders (m, n) must be determined experimentally — they cannot be deduced from stoichiometric coefficients; overall order = m + n.

Common misconception: Students write rate law orders from balanced equation coefficients; this is a classic AP® trap — rate law orders must come from experimental data.

How it can be tested: FRQs provide experimental data (trials with different initial concentrations and rates) and ask students to determine the order in each reactant and the rate constant k.

Integrated Rate Laws

Integrated rate laws relate concentration to time: zero-order: [A] = [A]₀ − kt; first-order: ln[A] = ln[A]₀ − kt; second-order: 1/[A] = 1/[A]₀ + kt; half-life of first-order: t₁/₂ = 0.693/k.

Common misconception: Students confuse which plot is linear for each order; zero-order: [A] vs. t is linear; first-order: ln[A] vs. t is linear; second-order: 1/[A] vs. t is linear.

How it can be tested: MCQs give a plot and ask for the reaction order; FRQs calculate half-life or k from concentration-time data.

Determining Rate Law from Experimental Data

Compare two trials where one concentration is constant: if doubling [A] doubles the rate → first order in A; if doubling [A] quadruples rate → second order; if rate is unchanged → zero order.

Common misconception: Students calculate orders by comparing trials where both concentrations change; always isolate one variable — compare trials where only one reactant's concentration changes.

How it can be tested: FRQs provide a data table with initial rates and concentrations; students must calculate order, k, and the rate at a different set of conditions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Rate Law — An equation relating reaction rate to the concentrations of reactants: rate = k[A]^m[B]^n; orders m and n are determined experimentally.
  • Rate Constant (k) — The proportionality constant in the rate law; units depend on overall reaction order; increases with temperature.
  • Reaction Order — The exponent to which a reactant concentration is raised in the rate law; determined experimentally, not from stoichiometry.
  • Half-Life (t₁/₂) — The time required for the concentration of a reactant to decrease to half its initial value; constant for first-order reactions (t₁/₂ = 0.693/k).
  • Integrated Rate Law — The rate law expressed in terms of concentration vs. time; allows determination of [A] at any time t given [A]₀ and k.
Objectives
  • Determine reaction orders from experimental data using the method of initial rates.
  • Use integrated rate laws to find concentration at a given time.
  • Calculate half-life from the rate constant for a first-order reaction.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs provide initial rate data and ask for rate law, rate constant, and prediction at new conditions.
  • MCQs ask which plot (linear) identifies the reaction order.
  • Questions test the first-order half-life equation.
Practice before moving on
  • From a data table with 3 trials, determine the rate law, then calculate k for a reaction A + B → products.
  • Use ln[A] = ln[A]₀ − kt to find [A] after 300 s if k = 0.0050 s⁻¹ and [A]₀ = 0.200 M.

The Arrhenius Equation and Temperature

Learn on this site
Arrhenius Equation

k = Ae^(−Ea/RT); where A is the frequency factor, Ea is activation energy, R is the gas constant, and T is temperature in Kelvin; as T increases, k increases exponentially.

Common misconception: Students use Celsius in the Arrhenius equation; absolute temperature (Kelvin) is required because the equation uses RT as an energy term.

How it can be tested: FRQs provide k at two temperatures and ask students to calculate Ea using the two-point Arrhenius: ln(k₂/k₁) = −Ea/R × (1/T₂ − 1/T₁).

Energy Diagrams and Mechanisms

An energy diagram shows potential energy vs. reaction progress; a multi-step mechanism has multiple peaks; the highest peak corresponds to the rate-determining step (slowest step).

Common misconception: Students confuse ΔH (thermodynamics) with Ea (kinetics); ΔH depends on reactants and products; Ea depends on the transition state and pathway — a catalyst changes Ea but not ΔH.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask which step is the rate-determining step from an energy diagram; FRQs interpret multi-step energy diagrams.

Catalysis: Homogeneous and Heterogeneous

Homogeneous catalysts are in the same phase as reactants; heterogeneous catalysts are in a different phase (usually solid with gas or liquid reactants); both lower Ea and appear in the mechanism but not the overall equation.

Common misconception: Students think catalysts are always consumed; they appear in an early step (as reactant) and are regenerated in a later step — the net amount of catalyst is unchanged.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to identify the catalyst in a multi-step mechanism and explain how it increases rate.

Key Vocabulary
  • Arrhenius Equation — k = Ae^(−Ea/RT); relates the rate constant k to activation energy Ea and temperature T; higher T or lower Ea increases k.
  • Frequency Factor (A) — A constant in the Arrhenius equation reflecting collision frequency and fraction of collisions with correct orientation.
  • Rate-Determining Step — The slowest elementary step in a reaction mechanism; it determines the overall reaction rate and the form of the rate law.
  • Heterogeneous Catalyst — A catalyst in a different phase from the reactants; typically a solid surface that adsorbs reactants and provides an alternative reaction pathway.
  • Reaction Intermediate — A species produced in one elementary step and consumed in a later step; appears in the mechanism but not the overall balanced equation.
Objectives
  • Use the Arrhenius equation to explain the temperature dependence of k.
  • Identify the rate-determining step from an energy diagram.
  • Distinguish homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysts.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to use two-point Arrhenius to calculate Ea from k values at two temperatures.
  • MCQs identify the rate-determining step from a multi-step energy diagram.
  • Questions ask about reaction intermediates and how to identify them in a mechanism.
Practice before moving on
  • Given k₁ = 2.0 × 10⁻³ s⁻¹ at 300 K and k₂ = 8.0 × 10⁻³ s⁻¹ at 320 K, calculate Ea.
  • Identify the catalyst and intermediates in the mechanism: NO₂ + F₂ → NO₂F + F (slow); F + NO₂ → NO₂F (fast).

Reaction Mechanisms

Learn on this site
Elementary Steps and Molecularity

A reaction mechanism is a series of elementary steps; each step has a molecularity (unimolecular, bimolecular, termolecular); the rate law for an elementary step is directly written from its stoichiometry (unlike overall reactions).

Common misconception: Students write rate laws from the overall balanced equation; this is only valid for elementary steps — the overall rate law must be determined experimentally or from the mechanism using the rate-determining step.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask for the rate law of a specific elementary step; FRQs ask students to verify that a proposed mechanism is consistent with the observed rate law.

Validating a Mechanism

A valid mechanism must: (1) have elementary steps that sum to the overall equation; (2) predict the experimentally observed rate law from the rate-determining step; any intermediates produced must be consistent with experimental observations.

Common misconception: Students think any mechanism that sums to the correct overall equation is valid; the mechanism must also predict the correct rate law from the rate-determining step.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to show that a proposed mechanism is consistent with the experimentally determined rate law.

Pre-equilibrium and Rate Laws

When a fast equilibrium precedes the rate-determining step, the concentration of the intermediate can be expressed in terms of reactant concentrations using the equilibrium constant, allowing derivation of the overall rate law.

Common misconception: Students struggle with pre-equilibrium mechanisms; write the K expression for the fast equilibrium step, solve for [intermediate], and substitute into the rate law for the slow step.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to derive the overall rate law for a two-step mechanism where step 1 is a fast equilibrium and step 2 is slow.

Key Vocabulary
  • Elementary Step — A single collision event in a reaction mechanism; its rate law is written directly from its stoichiometry.
  • Molecularity — The number of molecules colliding in an elementary step: unimolecular (1), bimolecular (2), termolecular (3, rare).
  • Mechanism — A proposed series of elementary steps that describes the pathway from reactants to products; must be consistent with the overall equation and experimental rate law.
  • Intermediate — A species formed in one elementary step and consumed in another; not found in the overall balanced equation.
  • Pre-equilibrium — A mechanism feature where a fast reversible step precedes the rate-determining step; the intermediate concentration is determined by the equilibrium expression.
Objectives
  • Write the rate law for an elementary step from its stoichiometry.
  • Verify that a proposed mechanism is consistent with the observed rate law.
  • Derive the rate law for a pre-equilibrium mechanism.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to show that a mechanism is consistent with the experimental rate law.
  • MCQs ask which proposed mechanism is consistent with the given rate law and overall equation.
  • Questions test the difference between rate laws for elementary steps and overall reactions.
Practice before moving on
  • Given mechanism: NO₂ + NO₂ → NO₃ + NO (slow); NO₃ + CO → NO₂ + CO₂ (fast); derive the overall equation, identify intermediates, and write the rate law.
  • Verify the mechanism step 1: A + B ⇌ C (fast); step 2: C + B → D + E (slow) is consistent with rate = k[A][B]².
