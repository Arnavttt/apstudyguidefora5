Enthalpy and Calorimetry

Learn on this site
Enthalpy Change

ΔH measures heat flow at constant pressure; negative ΔH = exothermic, positive ΔH = endothermic.

Common misconception: ΔH is not the same as ΔE (internal energy); they differ by PΔV work.

How it can be tested: Calculate ΔH from q = mcΔT in calorimetry problems.

Hess's Law

ΔH for a reaction equals the sum of ΔH values for any series of steps that add up to the overall reaction.

Common misconception: Reversing a reaction flips the sign of ΔH; multiplying by a factor scales ΔH.

How it can be tested: Add or reverse given reactions to derive target; manipulate ΔH accordingly.

Bond Enthalpy

ΔH ≈ Σ(bonds broken) − Σ(bonds formed); breaking bonds requires energy, forming bonds releases it.

Common misconception: Bond enthalpy values are averages and give approximate, not exact, ΔH.

How it can be tested: Use provided bond enthalpies to estimate ΔH for gas-phase reactions.

Key Vocabulary
  • enthalpy (ΔH) — Heat flow at constant pressure; negative for exothermic reactions.
  • calorimetry — Measurement of heat flow using q = mcΔT or q = CΔT.
  • Hess's law — ΔH of a reaction is the sum of ΔH values for any valid sequence of steps.
  • standard enthalpy of formation (ΔHf°) — Enthalpy change forming 1 mol of compound from elements in standard states.
  • bond enthalpy — Energy required to break one mole of a bond in the gas phase.
Objectives
  • Calculate ΔH using q = mcΔT from calorimetry data.
  • Apply Hess's law by combining reaction steps.
  • Estimate ΔH from bond enthalpies.
How this can be tested
  • Multi-step Hess's law problems combining 3+ reactions.
  • Calorimetry calculations with coffee-cup or bomb calorimeter context.
  • Comparing ΔH from formation enthalpies vs. bond enthalpies.

Entropy and the Second Law

Learn on this site
Entropy (ΔS)

Entropy measures dispersal of energy and matter; systems tend toward greater entropy (Second Law).

Common misconception: Entropy is not "disorder" in a colloquial sense but statistical dispersal of microstates.

How it can be tested: Predict sign of ΔS: more moles of gas, mixing, or phase changes to gas increase entropy.

Standard Entropy

S° values are absolute (not relative to elements) and increase with molecular complexity and phase.

Common misconception: Unlike ΔHf°, standard entropy of elements is NOT zero.

How it can be tested: Calculate ΔS°rxn = ΣS°(products) − ΣS°(reactants).

Third Law

Perfect crystalline solid at 0 K has S = 0; all real substances have positive S° at 298 K.

Common misconception: The Third Law sets the absolute scale; entropy can never be negative.

How it can be tested: Use Third Law to understand why S° for elements is nonzero.

Key Vocabulary
  • entropy (S) — Measure of energy dispersal and number of accessible microstates in a system.
  • Second Law of Thermodynamics — The entropy of the universe increases for any spontaneous process.
  • Third Law of Thermodynamics — Perfect crystal at 0 K has zero entropy.
  • microstates — Number of equivalent ways energy can be distributed among particles.
  • standard molar entropy (S°) — Absolute entropy of one mole of a substance at 298 K and 1 atm.
Objectives
  • Predict the sign of ΔS for a reaction from inspection.
  • Calculate ΔS°rxn from tabulated S° values.
  • Explain the Third Law and its significance.
How this can be tested
  • Predicting sign of ΔS from changes in moles of gas or phase.
  • Calculating ΔS° from provided S° data.
  • Relating entropy to number of microstates.

Gibbs Free Energy

Learn on this site
Gibbs Free Energy

ΔG = ΔH − TΔS; a reaction is spontaneous when ΔG < 0.

Common misconception: Spontaneous does not mean fast — kinetics and thermodynamics are independent.

How it can be tested: Determine spontaneity from signs of ΔH and ΔS at given temperature.

Temperature Dependence

When ΔH and ΔS have the same sign, spontaneity depends on temperature (T = ΔH/ΔS is the crossover point).

Common misconception: A reaction with ΔH > 0 and ΔS > 0 becomes spontaneous only above a threshold T.

How it can be tested: Use T = ΔH/ΔS to find where ΔG = 0.

ΔG° and Equilibrium

ΔG° = −RT ln K; a large K corresponds to a large negative ΔG°.

Common misconception: ΔG (not ΔG°) predicts spontaneity at non-standard conditions; they differ by RT ln Q.

How it can be tested: Use ΔG° = −RT ln K to interconvert between ΔG° and K.

Key Vocabulary
  • Gibbs free energy (ΔG) — Thermodynamic potential predicting spontaneity: ΔG = ΔH − TΔS.
  • spontaneous process — A process that occurs without continuous outside work; ΔG < 0.
  • standard Gibbs free energy (ΔG°) — ΔG at standard conditions (1 atm, 1 M, 298 K).
  • equilibrium constant (K) — Ratio of product to reactant activities at equilibrium; related to ΔG° by ΔG° = −RT ln K.
  • reaction quotient (Q) — Ratio of product to reactant concentrations at any point; compared to K to predict direction.
Objectives
  • Apply ΔG = ΔH − TΔS to predict spontaneity.
  • Identify temperature conditions for spontaneity when ΔH and ΔS have same sign.
  • Interconvert ΔG° and K using ΔG° = −RT ln K.
How this can be tested
  • Calculating ΔG from ΔH and ΔS at a given T.
  • Predicting sign of ΔG for all four ΔH/ΔS combinations.
  • Relating ΔG° to K magnitude and reaction favorability.

Thermodynamics Applications

Learn on this site
Coupled Reactions

Cells couple nonspontaneous reactions (ΔG > 0) to spontaneous ones (ATP hydrolysis) so the net ΔG is negative.

Common misconception: Coupling requires direct mechanistic connection, not just adding ΔG values.

How it can be tested: Add ΔG values for coupled reactions to determine overall spontaneity.

Electrochemistry Connection

ΔG° = −nFE°cell links thermodynamics to electrochemistry; positive E°cell means negative ΔG°.

Common misconception: Students confuse n (moles of electrons) with stoichiometric coefficients.

How it can be tested: Calculate ΔG° from E°cell and number of moles of electrons transferred.

Phase Transitions

At a phase transition (e.g., melting), ΔG = 0 and T = ΔH/ΔS; the two phases are in equilibrium.

Common misconception: ΔG = 0 at the transition temperature, not throughout the entire phase change.

How it can be tested: Calculate boiling/melting point from ΔH and ΔS of the phase transition.

Key Vocabulary
  • coupled reactions — Pairing a nonspontaneous reaction with a spontaneous one to drive it forward.
  • Faraday's constant (F) — 96,485 C/mol electrons; relates electrical charge to chemical change.
  • cell potential (E°cell) — Voltage produced by a galvanic cell under standard conditions.
  • phase transition — Change of state (solid↔liquid↔gas) occurring at constant T and P.
  • standard conditions — 298 K, 1 atm pressure, 1 M concentration for all species.
Objectives
  • Explain how coupled reactions can drive nonspontaneous processes.
  • Use ΔG° = −nFE°cell to relate electrochemistry and thermodynamics.
  • Determine phase transition temperature from ΔH and ΔS.
How this can be tested
  • Calculating ΔG° from cell potential in electrochemistry context.
  • Determining T at which a phase transition occurs using ΔG = 0.
  • Explaining biological energy coupling conceptually.
