Equilibrium Expressions

Learn on this site
Dynamic Equilibrium

At equilibrium, forward and reverse reaction rates are equal; concentrations remain constant but reactions continue.

Common misconception: Equilibrium does not mean equal concentrations of reactants and products.

How it can be tested: Recognize characteristics of equilibrium: constant concentrations, closed system, dynamic process.

Kc and Kp

Kc uses molar concentrations; Kp uses partial pressures. They are related by Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn where Δn = moles gas products − moles gas reactants.

Common misconception: Pure solids and liquids are omitted from K expressions because their activity is 1.

How it can be tested: Write Kc and Kp expressions for given reactions; relate them using Δn.

Magnitude of K

K >> 1: products favored at equilibrium. K << 1: reactants favored. K ≈ 1: significant amounts of both.

Common misconception: K magnitude says nothing about reaction rate.

How it can be tested: Interpret K values in terms of equilibrium position.

Key Vocabulary
  • equilibrium constant (Kc) — Ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations, each raised to stoichiometric power.
  • Kp — Equilibrium constant expressed in terms of partial pressures of gases.
  • Δn — Change in moles of gas (products − reactants) in a balanced equation.
  • dynamic equilibrium — State where forward and reverse rates are equal; concentrations remain constant.
  • activity — Effective concentration; pure solids/liquids have activity = 1 and are excluded from K.
Objectives
  • Write Kc expressions correctly excluding pure solids and liquids.
  • Interconvert Kc and Kp using Δn.
  • Interpret magnitude of K in terms of equilibrium position.
How this can be tested
  • Writing K expressions for heterogeneous equilibria.
  • Calculating Kp from Kc or vice versa.
  • Comparing K values to predict which direction equilibrium lies.

ICE Tables and Calculations

Learn on this site
ICE Table Method

ICE (Initial, Change, Equilibrium) tables organize concentration changes to solve for equilibrium values.

Common misconception: The change row must maintain stoichiometric ratios from the balanced equation.

How it can be tested: Set up ICE tables correctly and solve for x using the K expression.

Reaction Quotient Q

Q is calculated like K but at non-equilibrium conditions; comparing Q to K predicts reaction direction.

Common misconception: Q > K means too many products; reaction shifts left. Q < K means reaction shifts right.

How it can be tested: Calculate Q and compare to K to predict equilibrium direction.

Approximation Method

If K is very small (< 10⁻³), x is negligible compared to initial concentration; [A]eq ≈ [A]₀.

Common misconception: Always check that x < 5% of initial concentration to validate the approximation.

How it can be tested: Apply the 5% approximation rule to simplify equilibrium calculations.

Key Vocabulary
  • ICE table — Organized method to track Initial, Change, and Equilibrium concentrations.
  • reaction quotient (Q) — Same expression as K but calculated at any point, not just equilibrium.
  • 5% approximation — Simplification valid when K is small: assume x is negligible relative to initial concentration.
  • shift left — Equilibrium moves toward reactants (reverse reaction favored).
  • shift right — Equilibrium moves toward products (forward reaction favored).
Objectives
  • Construct ICE tables to find equilibrium concentrations.
  • Calculate Q and predict reaction direction by comparing to K.
  • Apply the 5% approximation where appropriate.
How this can be tested
  • Multi-step ICE table problems with Kc.
  • Determining direction of equilibrium shift from Q vs. K.
  • Validating the 5% approximation.

Le Chatelier's Principle

Learn on this site
Le Chatelier's Principle

When a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts to partially counteract the disturbance and re-establish equilibrium.

Common misconception: Le Chatelier's principle predicts direction but not the final equilibrium position or K value.

How it can be tested: Predict shifts for concentration, pressure, and temperature changes.

Concentration Changes

Adding a reactant shifts equilibrium right; removing a product also shifts right. Adding a product shifts left.

Common misconception: Adding an inert gas at constant volume does not affect equilibrium because partial pressures are unchanged.

How it can be tested: Predict equilibrium shift when specific concentrations are increased or decreased.

Temperature Effects

Increasing T shifts equilibrium in the endothermic direction; K changes with temperature.

Common misconception: Unlike concentration and pressure changes, temperature changes alter the value of K.

How it can be tested: Determine how K changes with temperature for exothermic vs. endothermic reactions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Le Chatelier's principle — System at equilibrium shifts to counteract a stress and re-establish equilibrium.
  • stress — Any disturbance that shifts a system from equilibrium (concentration, pressure, temperature change).
  • endothermic direction — Reaction direction that absorbs heat; favored by increasing temperature.
  • exothermic direction — Reaction direction that releases heat; favored by decreasing temperature.
  • inert gas — A gas that does not participate in the reaction; at constant volume, does not shift equilibrium.
Objectives
  • Apply Le Chatelier's principle to predict shifts from concentration changes.
  • Predict equilibrium shift from pressure changes by analyzing moles of gas.
  • Explain how temperature changes affect both equilibrium position and K.
How this can be tested
  • Multi-factor Le Chatelier problems (simultaneous changes).
  • Distinguishing between changes that affect K vs. those that only shift equilibrium.
  • Industrial applications (Haber process) connecting Le Chatelier to real chemistry.

Solubility Equilibria

Learn on this site
Ksp Expressions

Ksp (solubility product) is the equilibrium constant for dissolving an ionic solid; larger Ksp means greater solubility.

Common misconception: Ksp compares solubility only for salts of the same ion stoichiometry.

How it can be tested: Write Ksp expressions and calculate molar solubility from Ksp.

Common Ion Effect

Adding an ion already in solution decreases solubility of a sparingly soluble salt (Le Chatelier: shifts left).

Common misconception: The common ion effect is a specific application of Le Chatelier's principle to solubility.

How it can be tested: Calculate reduced solubility in a solution containing a common ion.

Precipitation

Precipitation occurs when ion product Q > Ksp; no precipitation if Q < Ksp; saturated solution if Q = Ksp.

Common misconception: Q for precipitation uses actual concentrations before mixing (account for dilution).

How it can be tested: Calculate Q after mixing solutions and predict whether precipitate forms.

Key Vocabulary
  • solubility product (Ksp) — Equilibrium constant for dissolution of sparingly soluble ionic compound.
  • molar solubility — Moles of solute dissolved per liter of saturated solution.
  • common ion effect — Reduction in solubility of a salt when a common ion is added to solution.
  • ion product (Q) — Product of ion concentrations at a given moment; compared to Ksp to predict precipitation.
  • saturated solution — Solution where dissolved and undissolved solute are in equilibrium; Q = Ksp.
Objectives
  • Write Ksp expressions and calculate molar solubility from Ksp.
  • Predict how the common ion effect reduces solubility.
  • Calculate ion product Q and predict precipitation.
How this can be tested
  • Calculating molar solubility with or without common ion.
  • Mixing two solutions and predicting precipitation using Q vs. Ksp.
  • Relating Ksp to molar solubility for different salt types.
