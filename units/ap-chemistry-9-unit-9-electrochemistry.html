Oxidation-Reduction Reactions

Learn on this site
Oxidation Numbers

Oxidation numbers track electron transfer: oxidation = increase in oxidation number; reduction = decrease.

Common misconception: Assigning oxidation numbers in polyatomic ions requires knowing common element oxidation states.

How it can be tested: Assign oxidation numbers and identify which species is oxidized/reduced.

Oxidizing and Reducing Agents

The oxidizing agent is reduced (gains electrons); the reducing agent is oxidized (loses electrons).

Common misconception: Students often confuse which agent is oxidized vs. reduced — the oxidizing agent undergoes reduction.

How it can be tested: Identify the oxidizing agent, reducing agent, and the changes in each for a given redox reaction.

Balancing Redox Reactions

Half-reaction method: split into oxidation and reduction half-reactions, balance atoms and charge, then combine.

Common misconception: In acidic solution, add H⁺ and H₂O to balance; in basic solution, add OH⁻ after balancing.

How it can be tested: Balance redox reactions using the half-reaction method in acidic and basic solutions.

Key Vocabulary
  • oxidation — Loss of electrons; increase in oxidation number.
  • reduction — Gain of electrons; decrease in oxidation number.
  • oxidizing agent — Species that accepts electrons and is itself reduced.
  • reducing agent — Species that donates electrons and is itself oxidized.
  • half-reaction — Equation showing only the oxidation or only the reduction process in a redox reaction.
Objectives
  • Assign oxidation numbers to all atoms in compounds and ions.
  • Identify oxidizing agent and reducing agent from a redox equation.
  • Balance redox reactions using the half-reaction method.
How this can be tested
  • Balancing complex redox reactions in acidic or basic solution.
  • Identifying which species is oxidized and which is reduced.
  • Recognizing common oxidizing agents (MnO₄⁻, Cr₂O₇²⁻, O₂).

Galvanic Cells

Learn on this site
Galvanic Cell Components

A galvanic cell converts chemical energy to electrical energy via spontaneous redox; oxidation occurs at the anode (−), reduction at the cathode (+).

Common misconception: Students confuse anode/cathode labels with sign: in galvanic cells, anode is negative.

How it can be tested: Describe galvanic cell components: anode, cathode, salt bridge, and electron flow.

Standard Cell Potential

E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode (using standard reduction potentials); positive E°cell means spontaneous reaction.

Common misconception: Standard reduction potentials are intensive properties — do not multiply by stoichiometric coefficients.

How it can be tested: Calculate E°cell from a table of standard reduction potentials.

Connecting to ΔG° and K

ΔG° = −nFE°cell; ΔG° = −RT ln K; therefore E°cell is directly related to K.

Common misconception: Larger positive E°cell corresponds to larger K and more negative ΔG°.

How it can be tested: Interconvert E°cell, ΔG°, and K using the relevant equations.

Key Vocabulary
  • galvanic (voltaic) cell — Electrochemical cell that converts spontaneous chemical energy to electrical energy.
  • anode — Electrode where oxidation occurs; negative in galvanic cells.
  • cathode — Electrode where reduction occurs; positive in galvanic cells.
  • standard reduction potential (E°red) — Tendency of a half-reaction to gain electrons under standard conditions.
  • salt bridge — Allows ion flow between half-cells to maintain charge balance without mixing solutions.
Objectives
  • Identify anode, cathode, and direction of electron flow in a galvanic cell.
  • Calculate E°cell from standard reduction potentials.
  • Use ΔG° = −nFE°cell to connect electrochemistry and thermodynamics.
How this can be tested
  • Drawing and labeling galvanic cells from a given redox reaction.
  • Calculating E°cell and predicting spontaneity.
  • Interconverting E°cell, ΔG°, and K.

Nernst Equation

Learn on this site
Nernst Equation

E = E° − (RT/nF)ln Q relates cell potential to concentration at non-standard conditions; at 25°C, E = E° − (0.0592/n)log Q.

Common misconception: E is zero at equilibrium (Q = K), consistent with ΔG = 0 at equilibrium.

How it can be tested: Use the Nernst equation to calculate cell potential at non-standard concentrations.

Concentration Cells

A concentration cell has identical electrodes but different ion concentrations; EMF is driven entirely by the concentration difference.

Common misconception: The spontaneous direction moves ions from high to low concentration (reduces Q toward K).

How it can be tested: Calculate E for a concentration cell using the Nernst equation.

Cell Potential and Equilibrium

When E = 0, Q = K; the battery is "dead." Using K = e^(nFE°/RT) to find K from E°cell.

Common misconception: The cell potential decreases as it approaches equilibrium and Q → K.

How it can be tested: Relate equilibrium constant to E°cell and interpret when a cell's potential reaches zero.

Key Vocabulary
  • Nernst equation — E = E° − (0.0592/n)log Q at 25°C; adjusts E for non-standard conditions.
  • concentration cell — Galvanic cell with identical half-reactions but different ion concentrations.
  • reaction quotient (Q) — Used in Nernst equation to account for current concentrations.
  • standard conditions — 1 M concentrations, 1 atm, 25°C; E = E° under these conditions.
  • dead battery — Cell at equilibrium (E = 0, Q = K); no more free energy available.
Objectives
  • Apply the Nernst equation to find E at non-standard concentrations.
  • Explain how concentration cells work.
  • Determine K from E°cell using the Nernst equation at equilibrium.
How this can be tested
  • Calculating E for cells with non-standard concentrations.
  • Predicting direction in concentration cells.
  • Finding K from E°cell.

Electrolysis and Faraday's Law

Learn on this site
Electrolysis

Electrolysis uses electrical energy to drive a nonspontaneous reaction; oxidation still at anode, reduction at cathode.

Common misconception: In electrolysis, the anode is connected to the positive terminal (opposite of galvanic cell labeling for current source).

How it can be tested: Identify products of electrolysis of molten salts and aqueous solutions.

Faraday's Law

Moles of electrons = (current × time) / F = (I × t) / 96485; links charge passed to moles deposited.

Common misconception: Students must include stoichiometry from half-reaction — 1 mol Cu²⁺ requires 2 mol e⁻.

How it can be tested: Calculate mass deposited or gas produced in electrolysis from current and time.

Electroplating and Applications

Electroplating deposits a thin metal layer on an object used as the cathode; plating metal dissolves from the anode.

Common misconception: The electrolyte must contain ions of the metal being plated for effective deposition.

How it can be tested: Describe the setup for electroplating and explain how metal is transferred.

Key Vocabulary
  • electrolysis — Use of electrical energy to drive a nonspontaneous redox reaction.
  • Faraday's constant (F) — 96,485 C/mol electrons; used to convert charge to moles of electrons.
  • coulomb (C) — Unit of electrical charge; 1 C = 1 A × 1 s.
  • electroplating — Electrolytic process depositing a thin metal coating on a surface.
  • ampere (A) — Unit of electrical current; 1 A = 1 C/s.
Objectives
  • Describe electrolysis and identify products at anode and cathode.
  • Apply Faraday's law to calculate mass deposited or moles of gas produced.
  • Explain the electroplating process.
How this can be tested
  • Calculating mass deposited using current, time, and Faraday's law.
  • Predicting products of electrolysis of water or molten NaCl.
  • Describing setup of an electroplating cell.
