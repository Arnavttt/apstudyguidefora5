Political Systems and Regime Types

Learn on this site
Democracies vs. Authoritarian Regimes

Liberal democracies guarantee competitive elections, civil liberties, and rule of law; authoritarian regimes concentrate power and restrict political competition.

Common misconception: Not all electoral systems are fully democratic — Iran holds elections but the Guardian Council vets candidates, making it a hybrid or authoritarian system.

How it can be tested: FRQ comparisons between AP® countries; MC identifying regime features.

Sources of Legitimacy

Weber identified three types: traditional (hereditary/custom), charismatic (personal appeal), and rational-legal (established law and rules).

Common misconception: Legitimacy is not the same as legality — a government can be technically legal yet lack popular legitimacy.

How it can be tested: MC examples of legitimacy crises; FRQ comparing UK and China legitimacy sources.

States, Nations, and Regimes

A state is a sovereign political unit; a nation is a cultural/ethnic community; a regime is the set of rules governing political competition.

Common misconception: "Country" and "state" are not identical — the UK is one state containing multiple nations (English, Scottish, Welsh, Northern Irish).

How it can be tested: Short-answer applying state/nation/regime distinctions to AP® countries.

Key Vocabulary

  • authoritarian regime
  • liberal democracy
  • rational-legal authority

Constitutions and Rule of Law

Learn on this site
Codified vs. Uncodified Constitutions

A codified constitution is written in a single document (e.g., Mexico 1917); the UK's uncodified constitution comprises statutes, conventions, and common law rather than one text.

Common misconception: Uncodified does not mean "unwritten" — the UK has many important written documents, just no single constitutional text.

How it can be tested: FRQ comparing constitutional structure of UK vs. Mexico; MC identifying codified countries.

Constitutional Principles

Key principles include separation of powers, judicial review, and rule of law; not all AP® states honor these equally in practice.

Common misconception: Russia has a written constitution guaranteeing rights, but the judiciary lacks independence to enforce them against the government.

How it can be tested: MC on which countries have meaningful judicial review; FRQ on rule-of-law gaps.

Constitutions in Authoritarian States

Authoritarian regimes may adopt constitutions that appear democratic on paper but are unenforced (e.g., China's 1982 constitution guarantees speech and assembly rights).

Common misconception: Having a constitution does not make a regime democratic — the independence of courts and enforcement mechanisms are what matter.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain how constitutions function differently in democratic vs. authoritarian contexts.

Key Vocabulary
  • codified constitution
  • rule of law
  • judicial review
  • separation of powers
  • parliamentary sovereignty
Objectives
  • Contrast codified and uncodified constitutional systems with AP® examples
  • Explain the role of judicial review in constraining executive power
  • Identify countries where constitutional rights are formally guaranteed but practically limited
How this can be tested
  • FRQ comparisons of constitutional design across AP® countries
  • MC on consequences of absent judicial review
  • Short-answer on the gap between formal rights and practical enforcement
Practice before moving on
  • Build a comparison table: AP® country, codified/not, meaningful judicial review, rule-of-law assessment
  • Explain why Russia's written constitution has not constrained presidential power

Federal and Unitary Systems

Learn on this site
Federal Systems

In federal states (Mexico, Nigeria, Russia), subnational governments hold constitutionally guaranteed powers; federalism can accommodate regional ethnic or cultural diversity.

Common misconception: Russia's federalism is largely nominal — Putin recentralized power by appointing regional governors (2004) and creating federal districts.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare federalism in Mexico and Nigeria; explain recentralization in Russia.

Unitary Systems

In unitary states (UK, China), the central government can create, modify, or abolish subnational units; devolution can grant regions limited autonomy but those powers remain revocable.

Common misconception: Devolution in the UK (to Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland) is not federalism — Westminster retains the power to override or revoke devolved authority.

How it can be tested: MC distinguishing devolution from federalism; FRQ on UK devolution.

Executive System Types

Parliamentary systems (UK) fuse executive and legislative power; presidential systems (Mexico, Nigeria) separate them; semi-presidential systems (Russia) feature both a powerful president and a prime minister.

Common misconception: In Russia, the PM is subordinate to the president, unlike France where cohabitation can occur; Russia's system concentrates power in the presidency.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare executive structure effects on policy-making in UK vs. Russia.

Key Vocabulary
  • federalism
  • devolution
  • unitary state
  • semi-presidential system
  • recentralization
Objectives
  • Contrast federal and unitary systems with AP® country examples
  • Explain devolution and its limits in the UK
  • Distinguish parliamentary, presidential, and semi-presidential executive structures
How this can be tested
  • FRQ comparing federal vs. unitary structures
  • MC on devolution and recentralization examples
  • Distinguish nominal from actual federalism
Practice before moving on
  • Which AP® countries are formally federal? How does practice differ from structure?
  • What powers did the UK devolve to Scotland, and could Parliament revoke them?

The Six AP® Countries: A Comparative Overview

Learn on this site
UK and China

The UK is a parliamentary democracy with a constitutional monarchy; China is a one-party state ruled by the Chinese Communist Party (CCP), with elected institutions subordinate to party structures.

Common misconception: The National People's Congress in China formally passes laws but in practice ratifies CCP decisions — it is not an independent legislature.

How it can be tested: FRQ comparing UK vs. China on legislative independence; MC on CCP institutional control.

Iran and Russia

Iran is a theocratic republic: elected institutions (president, Majles) coexist with unelected clerical bodies (Supreme Leader, Guardian Council) that hold ultimate authority. Russia is a competitive authoritarian state where elections occur but the opposition faces systematic disadvantage.

Common misconception: Iran's elections are real but constrained — the Guardian Council filters candidates, removing genuine competition.

How it can be tested: FRQ on how Iran's dual structure limits democracy; MC on Russian electoral manipulation.

Mexico and Nigeria

Mexico transitioned from PRI one-party dominance (ended 2000) to competitive multiparty democracy. Nigeria is a developing federal democracy marked by ethnic diversity, oil politics, and a history of military coups.

Common misconception: Mexico's transition was gradual through electoral reform — the PRI lost the presidency in 2000 after 71 years, not through a sudden revolution.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare democratic consolidation challenges in Mexico vs. Nigeria.

Key Vocabulary
  • competitive authoritarianism
  • Guardian Council
  • CCP (Chinese Communist Party)
  • PRI
  • democratic consolidation
Objectives
  • Characterize the political system of each AP® country
  • Explain how Iran combines elected and unelected institutions
  • Compare democratic development in Mexico and Nigeria
How this can be tested
  • FRQ requiring systematic comparison of two AP® country systems
  • MC on specific institutional features of each country
  • Short-answer identifying regime type and justifying the classification

Q1
Distinguish "state," "nation," and "regime," using one AP® country as an example.
  • A) Example: the UK is one state containing multiple nations; its regime is a parliamentary democracy.
  • B) China is a state with a Han majority nation but includes non-Han minorities the state struggles to integrate.
  • C) A state is a sovereign political entity with a defined territory; a nation is a community bound by shared identity (ethnic, cultural, linguistic).
  • D) A regime is the set of rules and institutions that govern how political power is obtained and exercised.
C is correct. A state is a sovereign political entity with a defined territory; a nation is a community bound by shared identity (ethnic, cultural, linguistic).
Q2
Compare how the UK and China derive political legitimacy.
  • A) China: legitimacy rests on CCP performance (economic development), nationalism, and the party's narrative of restoring China after the "century of humiliation."
  • B) UK citizens can remove governments through elections; Chinese citizens cannot remove the CCP.
  • C) Both states use nationalism as a supplemental legitimacy source, especially when performance-based legitimacy is threatened.
  • D) UK: legitimacy flows from competitive elections, parliamentary accountability, and rule of law.
D is correct. UK: legitimacy flows from competitive elections, parliamentary accountability, and rule of law.
Q3
What is judicial review, and in which AP® countries is it meaningful?
  • A) Mexico: the Supreme Court (SCJN) has exercised meaningful judicial review since democratic transition.
  • B) UK: parliamentary sovereignty limits judicial review of legislation, though courts review executive action.
  • C) China and Russia: courts lack independence; judicial review is not a genuine constraint on government.
  • D) Judicial review is the power of courts to invalidate laws or executive actions that violate the constitution.
D is correct. Judicial review is the power of courts to invalidate laws or executive actions that violate the constitution.
Q4
How does Iran's political system combine elected and unelected institutions?
  • A) Unelected: the Supreme Leader (rahbar) holds highest authority; the Guardian Council vets candidates and can block legislation.

  • C) Unelected bodies hold veto power, making Iran a theocratic republic rather than a liberal democracy.
  • D) Elected: the President and the Majles (parliament) are chosen by popular vote.
D is correct. Elected: the President and the Majles (parliament) are chosen by popular vote.
