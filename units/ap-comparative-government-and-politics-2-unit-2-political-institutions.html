Legislatures: Structure and Power

Learn on this site
Unicameral vs. Bicameral Legislatures

Most AP® countries have bicameral legislatures (two chambers); China's National People's Congress is technically unicameral but exercises minimal independent authority.

Common misconception: Having two legislative chambers does not automatically mean the legislature is powerful — Russia's Federation Council mostly rubber-stamps presidential decisions.

How it can be tested: MC on bicameral vs. unicameral structures; FRQ on legislative power in authoritarian vs. democratic states.

Legislative Power in AP® Countries

The UK Parliament (especially the Commons) is genuinely powerful — it can remove the PM through a vote of no confidence. Russia's State Duma and Iran's Majles have limited independence from executive dominance.

Common misconception: The UK's House of Lords can delay legislation but cannot permanently block it (Parliament Acts 1911, 1949).

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare legislative independence in the UK vs. Russia.

Legislative-Executive Relations

In parliamentary systems (UK), the government must maintain legislative confidence; in presidential systems (Mexico, Nigeria), the president and legislature are elected separately and may be from different parties.

Common misconception: Divided government (when different parties control executive and legislature) is possible in presidential systems but not in parliamentary systems.

How it can be tested: MC on consequences of divided government; FRQ on how UK parliamentary fusion differs from Mexican separation.

Executive Structures

Learn on this site
Parliamentary Executives

In the UK, the Prime Minister is the leader of the majority party; the PM and Cabinet are collectively responsible to the Commons and must resign if they lose a confidence vote.

Common misconception: The UK monarch is head of state but has no real governing power — the PM and Cabinet hold executive authority.

How it can be tested: MC on parliamentary accountability; FRQ on how collective ministerial responsibility works.

Presidential Executives

In Mexico and Nigeria, a directly elected president serves a fixed term and is not dependent on legislative confidence; this provides executive stability but can lead to gridlock.

Common misconception: Mexico's president serves one six-year term (sexenio) and cannot be re-elected — this was designed to prevent the kind of indefinite rule seen under the PRI.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare executive accountability in UK vs. Mexico; MC on presidential term limits.

Semi-Presidential and Theocratic Executives

Russia's semi-presidential system pairs a powerful president with a PM (appointed by the president); France is the textbook semi-presidential system. Iran's executive includes a directly elected president but the Supreme Leader holds ultimate authority over all branches.

Common misconception: Russia's president is far more powerful than France's — the PM's role is essentially administrative rather than political.

How it can be tested: FRQ comparing executive power in Russia's semi-presidential system vs. UK's parliamentary system.

Key Vocabulary
  • collective ministerial responsibility
  • fixed-term presidency
  • semi-presidential system
  • Supreme Leader (Iran)
  • sexenio
Objectives
  • Explain parliamentary executive accountability through collective responsibility
  • Contrast presidential and parliamentary executives on accountability and flexibility
  • Explain the role of Iran's Supreme Leader in the executive structure

  • Short-answer on how the Supreme Leader constrains Iran's elected president

Judiciaries and Rule of Law

Learn on this site
Independent vs. Controlled Judiciaries

Independent judiciaries (UK, Mexico post-2000) can check executive and legislative power; controlled judiciaries (China, Russia) serve government interests.

Common misconception: Mexico's Supreme Court (SCJN) has grown more independent since 2000, but concerns about judicial reform under recent governments remain.

How it can be tested: FRQ comparing judicial independence in two AP® countries; MC on consequences of controlled judiciaries.

Judicial Review Across AP® Countries

The UK's Supreme Court can review executive action but not strike down Acts of Parliament (parliamentary sovereignty). Mexico and Nigeria have stronger formal judicial review powers.

Common misconception: Russia has a Constitutional Court, but it has consistently upheld Putin's policies rather than constraining them.

How it can be tested: MC on which countries have meaningful constitutional review; short-answer on UK parliamentary sovereignty limits.

Informal Judicial Controls

In authoritarian states, judges face pressure through appointment, salary control, tenure insecurity, and implicit threats — creating self-censorship even without direct orders.

Common misconception: Informal pressure can produce the same results as formal judicial subordination — judges in Russia anticipate government preferences.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain how informal mechanisms limit judicial independence in Russia.

Key Vocabulary
  • judicial independence
  • constitutional court
  • parliamentary sovereignty
  • SCJN (Mexico)
  • rule of law
Objectives
  • Contrast judicial independence in democratic vs. authoritarian AP® countries
  • Explain how parliamentary sovereignty limits UK judicial review
  • Describe informal mechanisms that constrain judiciaries in authoritarian states
How this can be tested

  • MC on consequences of courts lacking independence
  • Short-answer on how informal controls work in practice

Bureaucracies and State Capacity

Learn on this site
Role of Bureaucracy

Bureaucracies implement laws, deliver public services, and regulate the economy; their effectiveness varies dramatically across AP® countries from the UK's professionalized civil service to Nigeria's corruption-prone bureaucracy.

Common misconception: Bureaucratic power (the "administrative state") can persist across different elected governments — civil servants outlast politicians.

How it can be tested: MC on examples of bureaucratic capacity; FRQ on how weak state capacity affects governance in Nigeria.

Meritocracy vs. Patronage

The UK uses a merit-based civil service selected through competitive exams; China's state also values meritocracy within the CCP framework; Nigeria and Mexico have historically relied more on patronage appointments.

Common misconception: China's meritocracy operates within party loyalty constraints — advancement requires CCP membership and political reliability, not just competence.

How it can be tested: FRQ comparing civil service recruitment in UK and China; MC on consequences of patronage-based bureaucracy.

State Capacity and Development

State capacity — the government's ability to effectively implement policy — is crucial for development. Nigeria's oil wealth coexists with low state capacity, corruption, and weak service delivery.

Common misconception: "Resource curse" theory argues that oil-dependent states develop extractive institutions rather than building broad state capacity.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain how Nigeria's oil wealth affects state capacity and political incentives.

Key Vocabulary

  • state capacity

  • patronage
  • resource curse
