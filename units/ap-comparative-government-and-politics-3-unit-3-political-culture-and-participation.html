Political Culture

Learn on this site
Defining Political Culture

Political culture refers to shared values, beliefs, and orientations toward government and politics; it includes attitudes toward authority, participation, and legitimacy.

Common misconception: Political culture is not static — it evolves over time through historical experience, education, and political socialization.

How it can be tested: FRQ: how does political culture explain different levels of citizen trust in government across AP® countries.

Political Cultures of AP® Countries

UK: civic culture with high institutional trust; Russia: more deferential, shaped by centuries of autocratic rule; China: emphasizes collective harmony over individual rights; Iran: traditional Islamic values intersect with modernizing trends; Mexico/Nigeria: cultures shaped by colonial history and post-independence politics.

Common misconception: Citizens in authoritarian states may privately dissent while publicly conforming — political culture surveys undercount actual dissent.

How it can be tested: MC comparing political culture features; FRQ on how Chinese Confucian traditions support CCP authority claims.

Political Socialization

Political socialization is the process by which individuals learn political values — through family, education, media, and religion.

Common misconception: Authoritarian states actively control political socialization: China's education system promotes patriotism and CCP narratives; Iran's curriculum emphasizes Islamic law and revolution.

How it can be tested: FRQ: how does China's state-controlled education shape political culture and support regime legitimacy?

Key Vocabulary
  • political culture
  • civic culture
  • political socialization
  • deference
  • collective identity
Objectives

Civil Society and Interest Groups

Learn on this site
Civil Society in Democracies

Civil society includes NGOs, unions, religious groups, professional associations, and advocacy organizations that operate between the state and individual; in democracies, these groups freely organize and lobby government.

Common misconception: Strong civil society is associated with democratic consolidation — Tocqueville observed that Americans' habit of associating underpinned democratic governance.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare the role of civil society in UK vs. Nigeria.

Civil Society in Authoritarian States

Authoritarian states control or co-opt civil society: China requires NGOs to register with the state and restricts foreign funding; Russia declared many NGOs "foreign agents" after 2012.

Common misconception: China's mass organizations (ACFTU, ACWF) appear to be civil society but are CCP-controlled transmission belts rather than independent advocacy groups.

How it can be tested: MC on how authoritarian states control civil society; FRQ on consequences for political participation.

Religious Organizations as Civil Society

Religious institutions can be powerful civil society actors: Catholic Church in Mexico advocated for democratic transition; Iranian clerical networks supported the 1979 revolution; Nigerian religious organizations provide social services.

Common misconception: In Iran, the clergy are not independent civil society — they are embedded in the state structure through the Council of Guardians and other bodies.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare the political role of religious organizations in Mexico and Nigeria.

Key Vocabulary
  • civil society
  • NGO
  • interest group
  • corporatism
  • foreign agents law (Russia)
Objectives
  • Explain the role of civil society in democratic consolidation
  • Compare civil society freedom in the UK vs. China
  • Describe how authoritarian states manage or co-opt civil society
How this can be tested
  • FRQ comparing civil society across democratic and authoritarian AP® countries
  • MC on specific civil society restrictions in Russia or China
  • Short-answer on religious organizations as political actors
Practice before moving on
  • Which AP® countries have restricted NGOs as "foreign agents"? What is the justification?
  • How does China's official trade union (ACFTU) differ from trade unions in the UK?

Electoral Systems and Voting Behavior

Learn on this site
Plurality vs. Proportional Systems

The UK uses a first-past-the-post (plurality) system for Commons elections — tends to produce two dominant parties and strong majorities. Proportional representation (used in many European systems and for some UK elections) produces more parties and coalition governments.

Common misconception: The UK's FPTP system often produces parliamentary majorities from minority vote shares — the winning party may get less than 50% of votes.

How it can be tested: MC on electoral system effects; FRQ on how FPTP shapes the UK party system.

Elections in Authoritarian States

Russia, Iran, and China hold elections, but with restrictions: Iran vets candidates (Guardian Council), Russia uses administrative pressure and fraud, China's national-level elections are indirect with no competitive multiparty choice.

Common misconception: Having elections is not sufficient for democracy — competitive, free, and fair elections are the requirement; many authoritarian states hold "elections" for legitimacy without genuine competition.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain how elections function differently in Russia vs. the UK.

Voter Turnout and Political Participation

Turnout varies by country: UK typically 60-70%; Mexico around 60-65%; Nigeria often below 40% with significant regional variation; authoritarian states may report high turnout as a legitimacy signal.

Common misconception: High reported turnout in authoritarian states may be manufactured — Chinese "elections" for local people's congresses report near-universal participation but with no real choice.

How it can be tested: MC on factors affecting turnout; short-answer on why turnout differs in democratic vs. authoritarian contexts.

Key Vocabulary
  • first-past-the-post (FPTP)
  • proportional representation
  • electoral manipulation
  • competitive election
  • voter turnout
Objectives
  • Explain how the UK's FPTP electoral system shapes party politics
  • Distinguish competitive elections from elections in authoritarian states
  • Compare voter turnout patterns and their meanings across AP® countries
How this can be tested
  • FRQ comparing electoral systems in UK vs. one other AP® country
  • MC on how electoral rules affect representation
  • Short-answer on why elections in Russia or Iran are not fully democratic
Practice before moving on
  • How would UK politics change if proportional representation replaced FPTP?
  • Explain three ways Russia's 2024 election differed from a genuine competitive election.

Social Movements and Political Change

Learn on this site
Social Movements in Democracies

Democratic systems provide legal channels for protest and advocacy; social movements have driven major policy changes (UK suffragettes, Mexican Zapatistas, South African anti-apartheid movement influenced Nigeria).

Common misconception: Social movements succeed when they build broad coalitions, apply sustained pressure, and align with political opportunities — not simply by having righteous causes.

How it can be tested: FRQ: how have social movements influenced political change in Mexico?

Social Movements Under Authoritarianism

Authoritarian states face collective action problems from citizens: China's 1989 Tiananmen protests were violently suppressed; Iran's Green Movement (2009) and the 2022 protests ("Woman, Life, Freedom") challenged but did not topple the regime.

Common misconception: Authoritarian states often pre-emptively demobilize social movements through selective repression, co-optation, and channeling grievances into state-controlled organizations.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare the fate of social movements in China vs. Iran; MC on authoritarian repression strategies.

Revolution and Political Change

Iran's 1979 Islamic Revolution replaced a secular monarchy (Shah) with a theocratic republic — one of history's most dramatic political transformations. Mexico's 1910 revolution created the political system that produced the PRI's long dominance.

Common misconception: Revolutions are exceptional events — most political change happens through gradual reform, electoral shifts, or negotiated transitions, not sudden revolution.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare causes and consequences of Iran's 1979 revolution and Mexico's 2000 democratic transition.

Key Vocabulary
  • social movement
  • collective action
  • political opportunity structure
  • Islamic Revolution (1979)
  • democratic transition
Objectives
  • Explain how social movements create political change in democratic contexts
  • Describe authoritarian strategies for controlling social movements
  • Compare Iran's 1979 revolution and Mexico's 2000 democratic transition
How this can be tested
  • FRQ on social movement success factors in democratic vs. authoritarian contexts
  • MC on Iran's 1979 revolution and its political consequences
  • Short-answer comparing authoritarian repression strategies in China and Iran
Practice before moving on
  • What factors allowed Iran's Islamic Revolution to succeed in 1979?
