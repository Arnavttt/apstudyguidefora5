Party Systems Across AP® Countries

Learn on this site
One-Party vs. Multiparty Systems

One-party systems (China: CCP; Iran: effectively limited to approved parties) prohibit genuine opposition. Dominant-party systems (Russia: United Russia; historically Mexico: PRI) allow nominal competition but structurally favor one party. The UK, Mexico (post-2000), and Nigeria operate competitive multiparty systems.

Common misconception: Russia's United Russia is not ideologically driven like communist parties — it is a "party of power" built around presidential authority rather than a doctrine.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare the role of political parties in UK vs. China.

UK Party System

The UK has two dominant parties (Labour and Conservative) due to FPTP; smaller parties (Lib Dems, SNP, Green) win seats but rarely govern alone. Party discipline is strong — MPs generally vote along party lines.

Common misconception: The 2019 UK election produced an 80-seat Conservative majority from 43% of the vote — illustrating how FPTP can amplify seat shares.

How it can be tested: MC on UK party system features; FRQ on how party discipline affects parliamentary government.

Parties in Authoritarian States

In China, the CCP is the state — party membership is required for advancement; the eight minor parties that exist are subordinate "democratic parties" that support CCP leadership. In Iran, parties exist but must comply with Islamic law and Supreme Leader authority; they are often banned after elections.

Common misconception: China's minor parties are not opposition parties — they function as consultative bodies within the system, not as genuine alternatives to CCP rule.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain how the CCP uses party structures to maintain control in China.

Electoral Rules and Their Effects

Learn on this site
Electoral System Design

Electoral systems translate votes into seats or offices; key designs include single-member plurality (FPTP), proportional representation (PR), and mixed systems. Each design has consequences for party competition and representation.

Common misconception: No electoral system is "neutral" — all choices advantage some parties or interests over others.

How it can be tested: FRQ on how UK FPTP vs. a hypothetical PR system would affect representation and government formation.

Elections in Hybrid and Authoritarian States

Russia uses proportional representation for Duma elections (with a 5% threshold that benefits United Russia) combined with single-member districts; the system is designed to maintain incumbent advantage. Iran uses a two-round majority system for Majles elections with Guardian Council candidate filtering.

Common misconception: In Russia, the 5% PR threshold means votes for parties below the threshold are effectively wasted — this concentrates seats with United Russia and prevents small opposition parties from gaining representation.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare how electoral rules in Russia and the UK produce different outcomes despite both holding regular elections.

Campaign Finance and Access

Democratic states regulate campaign finance to limit undue influence: UK has spending caps per constituency; Mexico's INE provides public campaign funding and restricts private donations.

Common misconception: In Russia and China, campaign finance rules effectively favor incumbents — state media access, state resources, and bureaucratic support substitute for private donations.

How it can be tested: MC on campaign finance reforms; short-answer on how media access advantages incumbents in authoritarian states.

Key Vocabulary
  • proportional representation
  • single-member plurality
  • electoral threshold
  • campaign finance
  • INE (Mexico)
Objectives
  • Explain how different electoral rules produce different political outcomes
  • Describe how Russia's electoral system advantages United Russia
  • Compare campaign finance regulation in the UK and Mexico
How this can be tested

  • Short-answer on incumbency advantages in authoritarian elections

Interest Groups and Policy-Making

Learn on this site
Pluralism vs. Corporatism

Pluralist systems (UK, US) feature many competing interest groups that lobby government freely; corporatist systems organize key interests (business, labor) into official bodies that have formal input into policy-making.

Common misconception: The UK's TUC (Trades Union Congress) and CBI (Confederation of British Industry) operate in a pluralist environment, lobbying rather than having formal policy-making roles.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare interest group access to government in the UK vs. Mexico during the PRI era.

State Corporatism in Authoritarian Contexts

Mexico under the PRI used state corporatism — labor unions (CTM), peasant organizations (CNC), and business groups were formally incorporated into the PRI structure and controlled by the state.

Common misconception: This corporatist arrangement gave the PRI control over organized labor and prevented independent union organizing, a key mechanism of authoritarian persistence.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain how PRI's corporatist structure maintained one-party dominance.

Business, Labor, and Government

In China, business interests interact with the CCP through party membership and formal channels; private entrepreneurs increasingly join the CCP. Labor interests are represented only through the state-controlled ACFTU.

Common misconception: In the UK, business groups (CBI) and trade unions (TUC) openly compete for policy influence; neither has formal veto power over legislation.

How it can be tested: Compare business-state relations in China vs. UK in a FRQ context.

Key Vocabulary
  • pluralism
  • corporatism
  • CTM (Mexico)
  • lobbying
  • state corporatism
Objectives
  • Distinguish pluralist and corporatist systems of interest representation
  • Explain how Mexico's PRI used state corporatism to maintain power
  • Compare business-state relations in China and the UK
How this can be tested
  • FRQ comparing interest group systems in pluralist vs. corporatist AP® countries
  • MC on PRI's corporatist structure and its role in authoritarian persistence
  • Short-answer on labor union independence in China vs. UK

Media, Communication, and Political Participation

Learn on this site
Free vs. State-Controlled Media

Democratic states (UK, Mexico) guarantee press freedom; the UK has the BBC (public but editorially independent) and a competitive private press. Authoritarian states (China, Russia) control media through state ownership, licensing restrictions, and intimidation of journalists.

Common misconception: Russia's state media (RT, VGTRK) actively promotes government narratives internationally and domestically; independent outlets like Novaya Gazeta have faced closure or extreme pressure.

How it can be tested: FRQ: compare media freedom and its political effects in UK vs. Russia.

Internet, Social Media, and Censorship

China's Great Firewall blocks foreign social media (Google, Facebook, Twitter) and censors domestic content; citizens use WeChat and Weibo under CCP surveillance. Iran blocks many foreign platforms and slows internet during protests.

Common misconception: Social media has enabled protest coordination in both authoritarian and democratic states — but authoritarian states have developed sophisticated countermeasures including "fifty-cent army" (paid pro-government commenters in China).

How it can be tested: FRQ: how does China's internet censorship affect political participation?

Media in Transitional Democracies

Mexico has a more free press than during PRI era but journalists face violence (Mexico is one of the most dangerous countries for journalists). Nigeria has active private media but faces government intimidation and corruption.

Common misconception: A free press is a necessary but not sufficient condition for democracy — Nigeria has private media but also has elections undermined by patronage and violence.

How it can be tested: Short-answer on the role of media in Mexico's democratic transition; FRQ on comparing press freedom across AP® countries.
