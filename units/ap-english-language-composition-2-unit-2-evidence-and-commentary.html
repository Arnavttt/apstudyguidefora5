Types of Evidence

Learn on this site
Facts, Data, and Statistics

Quantitative evidence (data, statistics, research findings) adds precision and credibility; writers must present statistics accurately and in context to avoid misleading readers.

Common misconception: Statistics can mislead without lying: citing a percentage without its denominator, cherry-picking a favorable time period, or confusing correlation with causation are common errors.

How it can be tested: AP® essay: evaluate a passage's statistical evidence for relevance, accuracy, and how well it supports the claim.

Examples and Anecdotes

Specific examples (including case studies and anecdotes) make arguments concrete and vivid; they are especially effective for illustrating abstract claims.

Common misconception: A single example or anecdote is not sufficient evidence for a broad claim — examples illustrate, but data generalizes.

How it can be tested: AP® synthesis essay: use examples to illustrate claims but pair them with broader evidence for support.

Expert Opinion and Testimony

Expert testimony adds credibility — citing authorities who have relevant expertise strengthens an argument. Personal testimony (of non-experts) can add emotional power and authenticity.

Common misconception: Expert opinion is not infallible — credentials must be relevant to the claim (a physicist's opinion on economics is not automatically authoritative).

How it can be tested: Evaluate sources: is this expert's credentials relevant to this claim? Is there consensus among experts or does the author cherry-pick a minority view?

Key Vocabulary

  • statistics
  • anecdote
  • expert testimony
  • credibility
Objectives
  • Distinguish among types of evidence and explain their different functions

  • Assess expert testimony for relevance and credibility

  • Essay: select the most effective evidence from provided sources

Writing Effective Commentary

Learn on this site
What Is Commentary?

Commentary is the writer's explanation of how evidence supports the claim — it is the "so what?" after evidence is presented. Without commentary, evidence sits inert in an essay.

Common misconception: A common error is to assume evidence "speaks for itself" — it never does; the writer must connect evidence to the claim explicitly.

How it can be tested: AP® rubric: evidence and commentary rows reward students who explain how evidence functions in the argument, not just what it shows.

Levels of Commentary

Surface commentary explains what evidence shows; deeper commentary explains why the evidence is significant, how it connects to broader context, or what it reveals about the argument's assumptions.

Common misconception: AP® scoring distinguishes "adequate" commentary (explains connection to claim) from "sophisticated" commentary (explores complexity, considers alternative interpretations, or acknowledges limitations).

How it can be tested: Practice: write the same piece of evidence with surface commentary, then rewrite with deeper commentary.

Avoiding the "Quote Dump"

A "quote dump" is a series of quotations with little or no commentary — it shows evidence but does not demonstrate analysis. The writer's voice and reasoning must dominate.

Common misconception: The proportion of quotation to commentary should heavily favor commentary; a useful heuristic is that for every line of quotation, there should be at least two lines of commentary.

How it can be tested: AP® essays: write brief quotations (specific phrases, not long blocks) followed by substantial commentary connecting them to your argument.

Key Vocabulary
  • commentary
  • warrant
  • quote dump
  • analysis
  • textual evidence
Objectives
  • Explain the role of commentary in connecting evidence to claims
  • Write commentary at multiple depths: surface and deep
  • Avoid quote dumping by making commentary dominate
How this can be tested
  • Essay rubric: distinguish evidence-only responses from evidence-with-commentary responses
  • Practice: take a quote and write three levels of commentary for it
  • Peer review: identify quote dumps in a draft and add commentary
Practice before moving on
  • Take three quotations from a passage and write a full paragraph for each with thorough commentary
  • Revise a "quote dump" paragraph — add commentary after each quotation that explains its function

Integrating Evidence Smoothly

Learn on this site
Signal Phrases

Signal phrases introduce quotations and attribute them to sources: "As Smith argues..."; "In her 2019 report, Jones contends..."; they prevent dropped quotations and provide context.

Common misconception: Dropped quotations (quotations with no introduction) are a common error — they disrupt reading flow and fail to establish who is speaking.

How it can be tested: Practice: rewrite dropped quotations using appropriate signal phrases.

Paraphrase vs. Direct Quotation

Paraphrase restates evidence in the writer's own words; it integrates more smoothly than quotation and avoids overloading the essay with others' words; it requires accurate representation of the source.

Common misconception: Paraphrase is not the same as changing a few words — it requires genuine restatement of meaning in the writer's own phrasing and sentence structure.

How it can be tested: AP®: use direct quotation for specific language that matters to the analysis; use paraphrase for data, ideas, or arguments where exact words are not crucial.

Avoiding Plagiarism Through Attribution

Every borrowed idea or exact language must be attributed to its source; even paraphrase requires attribution in academic writing.

Common misconception: In AP® synthesis essays, sources should be cited as "(Source A)" using the provided designations; failing to attribute to sources weakens credibility.

How it can be tested: Practice: integrate evidence from three sources into one paragraph using signal phrases and attribution.

Key Vocabulary
  • signal phrase
  • paraphrase
  • attribution
  • dropped quotation
  • citation
Objectives
  • Use signal phrases to introduce quotations and paraphrases
  • Distinguish between appropriate paraphrase and mere word substitution
  • Attribute all borrowed evidence correctly in AP® synthesis essays
How this can be tested
  • Essay: identify dropped quotations and rewrite with signal phrases
  • Practice distinguishing accurate paraphrase from problematic paraphrase
  • AP® synthesis practice: cite sources using provided designation system
Practice before moving on
  • Find three dropped quotations in a student essay; rewrite each with a signal phrase
  • Practice paraphrasing a complex paragraph accurately without looking at the original

Evaluating Evidence Quality

Learn on this site
Relevance and Sufficiency

Evidence must be relevant (directly connected to the claim) and sufficient (enough to support the claim; one example is rarely enough for a broad assertion).

Common misconception: Relevant evidence can still be insufficient — a single study supporting a sweeping claim does not provide adequate support.

How it can be tested: AP®: evaluate sources for relevance before including them in synthesis essays; irrelevant evidence weakens arguments.

Credibility and Bias

Credible sources have expertise, appropriate methodology, and transparency about funding and methods; biased sources may present accurate information selectively or frame it misleadingly.

Common misconception: Bias does not automatically disqualify a source — all sources have some perspective; what matters is whether the perspective distorts or suppresses important information.

How it can be tested: AP® synthesis: consider the source's credibility and potential biases when deciding how much weight to give its evidence.

Logical Fallacies

Common logical fallacies include ad hominem (attacking the person, not the argument), straw man (misrepresenting the opposing view), hasty generalization (generalizing from insufficient evidence), and appeal to authority (treating any authority as definitive).

Common misconception: Identifying a fallacy in an opponent's argument is a valid rhetorical move, but the writer must then provide a valid alternative argument rather than simply attacking the fallacy.

How it can be tested: AP®: identify logical fallacies in provided texts and explain how they weaken the argument.

Key Vocabulary
  • relevance
  • sufficiency
  • credibility
  • bias
  • logical fallacy
Objectives
  • Evaluate evidence for relevance and sufficiency
  • Assess source credibility and identify potential bias
  • Identify common logical fallacies and explain how they weaken arguments
How this can be tested
  • MC on identifying evidence quality issues in passages
  • FRQ: evaluate specific evidence used in a passage for relevance and credibility
  • Practice identifying logical fallacies in provided arguments
Practice before moving on
  • Evaluate three sources provided for a synthesis prompt: rank them by credibility and explain your reasoning
  • Find an example of a straw man or hasty generalization in a real editorial
