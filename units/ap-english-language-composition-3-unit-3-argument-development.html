Structuring an Argument

Learn on this site
Classical Argument Structure

Classical argument includes: introduction/thesis, background, confirmation (evidence for the claim), refutation (addressing counterarguments), and conclusion. This structure signals rhetorical awareness and thoroughness.

Common misconception: Classical argument is not the only valid structure — AP® essays may use alternative organizational patterns, but must still address opposing views.

How it can be tested: Practice: outline an argument using classical structure before drafting; identify which section each paragraph belongs to.

Paragraph Development: Topic Sentences

Each body paragraph should begin with a topic sentence that makes a claim (not just announces a topic); topic sentences should connect to the thesis and advance the argument.

Common misconception: A topic sentence that merely announces a topic ("The next thing to discuss is...") is weaker than one that makes a claim ("The regulation of social media is necessary because algorithms amplify harmful content").

How it can be tested: AP® essay: every body paragraph's topic sentence should make a sub-claim that supports the thesis.

Transitions and Coherence

Transitions connect ideas within and between paragraphs; they signal relationships (addition, contrast, causation, concession) and guide the reader through the argument's logic.

Common misconception: Transitions are not just transitional words — they include conceptual connections that show how one idea leads to another.

How it can be tested: AP® scoring: coherent structure is rewarded; disorganized essays with weak transitions score lower on the sophistication dimension.

Key Vocabulary

  • topic sentence
  • transition
  • coherence
  • refutation
Objectives
  • Outline an argument using classical argument structure
  • Write topic sentences that make sub-claims rather than merely announcing topics
  • Use transitions to signal logical relationships between ideas
How this can be tested

  • Peer review: identify weak transitions and suggest conceptual connections

Logical Reasoning and Warrants

Learn on this site
The Toulmin Model

Toulmin's model of argument includes claim (what you assert), grounds (evidence), warrant (the logical assumption connecting evidence to claim), backing (support for the warrant), qualifier (limits on the claim), and rebuttal (exceptions).

Common misconception: The warrant is often the most vulnerable part of an argument — it is the unstated assumption that links evidence to conclusion; skilled analyzers find and evaluate warrants.

How it can be tested: FRQ: identify the warrant in a passage's argument and evaluate whether it is sound.

Inductive vs. Deductive Reasoning

Inductive reasoning moves from specific examples to general conclusions; deductive reasoning applies a general principle to a specific case. Both have appropriate uses and both can fail.

Common misconception: Induction can fail if examples are unrepresentative (hasty generalization); deduction can fail if the general principle is wrong or if the case doesn't fit the category.

How it can be tested: AP®: identify whether an argument uses inductive or deductive reasoning and evaluate its logic.

Logical Fallacies Review

Beyond those previously studied, additional fallacies include: false dichotomy (presenting only two options when more exist), slippery slope (assuming one event will inevitably lead to extreme consequences), bandwagon (something is right because many people believe it), and appeal to tradition (something is right because it's always been done that way).

Common misconception: Fallacies are rhetorical tricks, not logical arguments — a sophisticated reader identifies fallacies and dismisses the reasoning (though the conclusion might still be correct for other reasons).

How it can be tested: Practice: identify fallacies in advertisements and political rhetoric.

Key Vocabulary
  • Toulmin model
  • warrant
  • inductive reasoning
  • deductive reasoning
  • false dichotomy
Objectives
  • Apply the Toulmin model to identify claims, grounds, and warrants in arguments
  • Distinguish inductive and deductive reasoning and identify when each fails
  • Identify logical fallacies beyond ad hominem and straw man
How this can be tested

  • MC: identify logical fallacies in provided arguments
  • Practice applying Toulmin model to student-generated arguments
Practice before moving on

  • Identify two logical fallacies in a given editorial and explain how they weaken the argument

Counterarguments and Concession

Learn on this site
Why Address Counterarguments?

Addressing counterarguments demonstrates intellectual honesty, builds credibility, and preemptively defuses objections; ignoring obvious counterarguments signals weak argumentation.

Common misconception: Addressing a counterargument does not mean agreeing with it — it means engaging with it seriously and explaining why your position still stands or why the counterargument is outweighed.

How it can be tested: AP® argument essay: the sophistication point often goes to students who genuinely engage with complexity rather than dismissing opposition.

Concession and Rebuttal

Concession acknowledges the validity of part of a counterargument ("While it is true that X..."); rebuttal then explains why your position still holds ("...this does not outweigh Y because...").

Common misconception: Effective concession-rebuttal shows nuanced thinking; a weak concession either concedes too much (undermining the thesis) or is merely rhetorical ("some people might think X, but they are wrong").

How it can be tested: AP®: practice the concession-rebuttal move: acknowledge the strongest version of the opposing argument, then refute it with evidence and reasoning.

Qualifying Claims

Qualifiers (most, many, often, in certain contexts) limit the scope of a claim to make it more defensible; overconfident claims ("always," "never") are often indefensible and invite easy refutation.

Common misconception: Qualifying is not hedging — it is precision. A qualified claim ("Social media increases anxiety in adolescents in high-use contexts") is more defensible and accurate than an absolute one.

How it can be tested: AP®: revise absolute claims to include appropriate qualifiers; explain how qualifiers strengthen rather than weaken the argument.

Key Vocabulary

  • qualifier
  • nuance
Objectives

  • Evaluate: does a given essay engage seriously with counterarguments?

  • Take an absolute claim and add appropriate qualifiers; explain why the qualified version is stronger

The Argument Essay (Free Response)

Learn on this site
AP® Argument Essay Overview

The AP® Language argument FRQ provides a broad claim or statement and asks students to develop their own argument, using evidence from their own knowledge, reading, and experience.

Common misconception: Unlike the synthesis essay, the argument essay does not provide sources — students must supply their own evidence; this tests whether students can draw on their own reading and knowledge.

How it can be tested: AP® exam strategy: spend 5 minutes planning, 35 minutes drafting, 5 minutes reviewing; ensure you have a clear thesis, at least two pieces of specific evidence, and genuine commentary.

Planning and Prewriting

Effective prewriting includes: understanding the prompt, generating a defensible position, brainstorming evidence, and outlining the argument before drafting.

Common misconception: Students who skip prewriting often produce disorganized essays that can't earn the organization and development scores.

How it can be tested: Practice: given a prompt, spend exactly 5 minutes producing a thesis, three pieces of evidence, and a brief counterargument before writing.

Scoring: Thesis, Evidence, Commentary, Sophistication

The argument essay is scored on a 1–6 scale (with a separate sophistication point): 1 point for thesis, 4 points for evidence and commentary (0-4), 1 point for sophistication.

Common misconception: The evidence and commentary score rewards both the quality of evidence and the quality of commentary; a response with three well-analyzed pieces of evidence outscores one with six poorly explained pieces.

How it can be tested: Study rubric examples: read released scored essays and identify what earned each point — especially what separates a 3 from a 4 on evidence/commentary.

Key Vocabulary
  • argument essay
  • prewriting
  • AP® rubric
  • thesis point
  • sophistication point
Objectives
  • Explain the structure and expectations of the AP® Language argument FRQ
  • Practice efficient prewriting that produces a usable plan in 5 minutes
  • Identify what each point on the AP® argument rubric requires
How this can be tested
  • Timed practice: 40-minute argument essay from a released AP® prompt
  • Rubric review: score a released student essay and compare to official score
  • Prewriting drill: 5-minute planning for three different prompts
Practice before moving on
  • Score your own timed essay using the AP® rubric; identify which point you missed and why
  • Compare two released essays (one 4, one 5 on evidence/commentary) and identify the specific differences
