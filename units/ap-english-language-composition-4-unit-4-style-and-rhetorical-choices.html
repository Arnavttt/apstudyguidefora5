Diction and Connotation

Learn on this site
Denotation vs. Connotation

Denotation is a word's literal dictionary meaning; connotation is the emotional associations and cultural meanings attached to a word. Writers choose words for both.

Common misconception: Synonyms are rarely identical in effect — "slim," "slender," "thin," and "scrawny" all denote similar body types but have very different connotations.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain how specific word choices contribute to the writer's argument or characterization of the subject.

Formal vs. Informal Diction

Formal diction uses technical vocabulary, Latinate words, and avoids contractions; informal diction uses everyday language, contractions, and colloquialisms. Each signals the writer's relationship with the audience and the occasion's formality.

Common misconception: Formal diction is not always more effective — informal diction can create intimacy, humor, or authenticity that formal language cannot.

How it can be tested: AP® analysis: identify specific diction choices and explain how they create effects appropriate (or inappropriate) to the rhetorical situation.

Loaded Language and Euphemism

Loaded language uses words with strong emotional connotations to provoke a response; euphemism replaces harsh or negative words with softer alternatives.

Common misconception: Recognizing loaded language and euphemism is essential for critical reading — both can manipulate audiences by bypassing careful reasoning.

How it can be tested: AP®: identify examples of loaded language or euphemism in a passage and explain how they serve the writer's argument.

Key Vocabulary
  • denotation
  • connotation
  • diction
  • loaded language
  • euphemism
Objectives
  • Distinguish denotation from connotation and explain why connotation matters in argument
  • Identify formal and informal diction and explain the effects of each
  • Recognize loaded language and euphemism as rhetorical tools
How this can be tested
  • FRQ: analyze specific diction choices and their effects on the argument
  • MC on identifying connotation differences between near-synonyms
  • Essay: explain how a passage's diction serves or undermines its argument
Practice before moving on
  • Find five pairs of synonyms with different connotations and explain the difference in each pair
  • Find examples of euphemism in news reports about military actions or corporate failures

Syntax and Sentence Structure

Learn on this site
Sentence Types and Effects

Simple sentences (one independent clause) create clarity and emphasis; compound sentences connect equal ideas; complex sentences show subordination of ideas; cumulative sentences build detail; periodic sentences withhold the main clause for emphasis.

Common misconception: Short sentences after long ones create emphasis through contrast — the sudden brevity commands attention.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain how sentence structure choices create pacing, emphasis, and emotional effect.

Parallel Structure

Parallel structure (parallelism) uses the same grammatical form for connected ideas; it creates rhythm, balance, and emphasis ("government of the people, by the people, for the people").

Common misconception: Faulty parallelism disrupts readability: "She likes hiking, to swim, and photography" should be "She likes hiking, swimming, and photography."

How it can be tested: AP®: identify parallel structures in passages and explain how they contribute to the argument's persuasive effect.

Anaphora and Epistrophe

Anaphora repeats words or phrases at the beginning of successive clauses or sentences for emphasis and rhythm; epistrophe repeats at the end. Both are powerful in persuasive and ceremonial rhetoric.

Common misconception: "We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields..." — Churchill's anaphora creates determination and rhythm.

How it can be tested: AP®: identify anaphora or epistrophe and explain the effect (emphasis, emotion, unity) they create.

Key Vocabulary
  • syntax
  • periodic sentence
  • cumulative sentence
  • parallelism
  • anaphora
Objectives
  • Explain how different sentence types create different effects
  • Identify parallel structure and explain its rhetorical effects
  • Analyze anaphora and epistrophe in political and ceremonial rhetoric
How this can be tested
  • FRQ: analyze syntax choices and their effects on pace, emphasis, and emotion
  • MC on identifying sentence types and structural devices
  • Practice writing cumulative and periodic sentences on the same topic
Practice before moving on
  • Rewrite three sentences as periodic sentences; explain how the effect changes
  • Find an example of anaphora in a famous speech; explain why it is effective there

Ethos, Pathos, and Logos

Learn on this site
Ethos: Credibility Appeals

Ethos appeals to the audience's sense of the speaker's credibility, expertise, and character; writers establish ethos through credentials, citation of credible sources, fair treatment of opposing views, and appropriate tone.

Common misconception: Ethos is not just about credentials — a speaker can establish ethos through demonstrated expertise, values alignment with the audience, and honest engagement with complexity.

How it can be tested: AP® FRQ: analyze how a writer establishes ethos and whether it is effective for the intended audience.

Pathos: Emotional Appeals

Pathos appeals to the audience's emotions — fear, hope, sympathy, anger, joy — to move them toward the writer's position; effective pathos connects emotionally relevant content to the argument's claims.

Common misconception: Pathos is not inherently manipulative — connecting emotional truth to logical arguments is legitimate rhetoric; the problem is pathos that bypasses reason entirely.

How it can be tested: AP®: identify pathos appeals and explain whether they are legitimate (supporting the argument's logic) or manipulative (substituting for logic).

Logos: Logical Appeals

Logos appeals to reason through statistics, evidence, logical reasoning, and well-structured arguments; it is the appeal that relies on the audience's capacity for rational evaluation.

Common misconception: Logos alone is often insufficient — audiences are not purely rational; effective arguments combine logos with appropriate ethos and pathos.

How it can be tested: AP®: identify logos appeals and evaluate the quality of the reasoning and evidence.

Key Vocabulary
  • ethos
  • pathos
  • logos
  • credibility
  • emotional appeal
Objectives
  • Identify and explain ethos, pathos, and logos appeals in a given text
  • Evaluate whether pathos appeals are legitimate or manipulative in context
  • Analyze how a writer balances the three appeals to suit the rhetorical situation
How this can be tested
  • FRQ: analyze how a passage uses two or more rhetorical appeals effectively
  • MC on identifying specific appeals and their effects
  • Essay: evaluate which appeal is most dominant and most effective in a given text
Practice before moving on
  • Analyze an advertisement: identify which appeals dominate and evaluate their effectiveness
  • Compare a scientific paper (logos-dominant) and a fundraising letter (pathos-dominant) on the same topic

Tone and Its Effects

Learn on this site
Identifying Tone

Tone is the writer's attitude toward the subject and audience, expressed through diction, syntax, evidence choices, and rhetorical appeals. Tone can be formal, ironic, satirical, earnest, angry, mournful, celebratory, and many others.

Common misconception: Tone is distinct from mood (which describes the reader's emotional response); tone is an authorial choice, mood is an audience effect.

How it can be tested: FRQ: identify the tone of a passage and explain how specific choices create it.

Irony and Satire

Irony occurs when the literal meaning differs from the intended meaning; satire uses irony, exaggeration, and humor to critique social or political subjects.

Common misconception: Verbal irony says the opposite of what is meant; situational irony is when events turn out opposite to expectations; dramatic irony is when the audience knows something characters don't.

How it can be tested: AP®: identify irony or satire and explain how they serve the writer's rhetorical purpose.

Tone Shifts

A deliberate tone shift within a text signals a turning point — from anger to hope, from satire to sincerity — and often marks the most rhetorically important moment.

Common misconception: Tone shifts can be subtle and require careful attention to diction and syntax changes.

How it can be tested: FRQ: identify a tone shift and explain its rhetorical effect — why did the writer change tone at this moment?

Key Vocabulary
  • tone
  • irony
  • satire
  • tone shift
  • mood
Objectives
  • Identify tone in a text and explain how specific choices create it
  • Distinguish among types of irony and explain how each functions rhetorically
  • Identify and analyze tone shifts as rhetorical turning points
How this can be tested
  • FRQ: identify the tone of a passage and explain how diction and syntax create it
  • MC on distinguishing tone from mood; identifying irony types
  • Essay: analyze how a tone shift serves the argument at a pivotal moment
Practice before moving on
  • Find a satirical piece (The Onion, Colbert) and identify the target and method of satire
  • Identify a tone shift in a letter or speech; explain what happens before and after the shift and why it matters
