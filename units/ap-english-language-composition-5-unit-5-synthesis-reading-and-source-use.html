Reading Multiple Sources

Learn on this site
Reading Across Sources

When reading multiple sources on a topic, readers should identify each source's main claim, evidence, and perspective, then look for patterns: agreement, disagreement, complementary information, and gaps.

Common misconception: Don't read each source in isolation — after reading all sources, identify how they speak to each other, what they agree and disagree on, and what each contributes uniquely.

How it can be tested: AP® synthesis prompt strategy: read all sources first, annotating each with its main claim and perspective, before deciding on your thesis.

Identifying Perspective and Purpose

Each source in an AP® synthesis prompt has a perspective (the viewpoint it represents) and a purpose (what it is trying to do — inform, persuade, advocate, document).

Common misconception: A government report and an activist organization's report may present identical statistics with very different emphases and omissions.

How it can be tested: AP®: consider the source's context when evaluating its evidence — a medical journal article and a pharmaceutical company press release should be weighted differently.

Synthesizing Information

Synthesis is combining information from multiple sources into a new, coherent argument — not just summarizing each source separately but connecting them to develop a position.

Common misconception: Synthesis goes beyond "Source A says X; Source B says Y" — it identifies relationships: "While Source A and B agree that X, they differ on Y, which suggests Z."

How it can be tested: AP® rubric: synthesis essays that develop an argument using multiple sources are rewarded over essays that summarize sources one at a time.

Key Vocabulary
  • synthesis
  • perspective
  • source evaluation
  • annotation
  • cross-source reading
Objectives
  • Read multiple sources to identify main claims, evidence, and perspective
  • Distinguish between sources' perspectives and evaluate their credibility in context
  • Synthesize information from multiple sources into a coherent argument
How this can be tested
  • AP® synthesis FRQ: read all provided sources and annotate before writing
  • Practice identifying source perspectives and explaining how they affect evidence presentation
  • Compare synthesis essays that connect sources vs. those that summarize each separately
Practice before moving on
  • Read two sources on the same topic from different perspectives; identify three points of agreement and two disagreements
  • Practice writing one paragraph that synthesizes three sources around a single claim

The AP® Synthesis Essay

Learn on this site
Synthesis Essay Format

The AP® Language synthesis FRQ provides 6-7 sources (text and visual) on a topic and asks students to write an argument that synthesizes at least three sources.

Common misconception: The essay must have a clear thesis, use sources as evidence (not just acknowledge them), and develop a coherent argument — it is not a summary of sources.

How it can be tested: AP® strategy: don't just cite sources; integrate them. Each source used should be explained in terms of how it supports your specific argument.

Citing Sources in AP® Essays

AP® synthesis essays cite sources by their provided designation: "(Source A)," "(Source B)," etc. Students must reference at least three sources to score in the upper range.

Common misconception: A source is "used" when it is cited and integrated into the argument — not just mentioned or listed.

How it can be tested: AP® rubric: evidence and commentary scores require that sources are not just cited but genuinely used to support the line of reasoning.

Avoiding "Source Shopping"

"Source shopping" is selecting only sources that confirm a predetermined thesis while ignoring contradictory sources — it produces weaker essays that miss complexity.

Common misconception: The strongest synthesis essays engage with the most challenging source and explain how their argument accounts for it.

How it can be tested: AP® sophistication point: essays that acknowledge the complexity of the issue — including what sources reveal about limits or counterarguments — are positioned to earn the sophistication point.

Key Vocabulary
  • synthesis essay
  • source citation
  • source integration
  • AP® FRQ format
  • source shopping
Objectives
  • Explain the format and expectations of the AP® synthesis essay
  • Cite sources correctly using AP® designation format
  • Avoid source shopping by engaging with challenging or contradictory sources
How this can be tested
  • Timed synthesis essay practice: 40 minutes on a released AP® prompt with provided sources
  • Rubric review: identify how many sources are cited and whether they are integrated or merely mentioned
  • Peer review: check for source shopping — which sources were ignored and why?
Practice before moving on
  • Write a synthesis essay plan: thesis + three sources + counterargument source
  • Revise a synthesis essay that mentions sources without integrating them — add commentary that connects each source to the thesis

Developing Your Own Voice in Source-Based Writing

Learn on this site
Your Argument, Not the Sources'

In synthesis writing, sources serve the writer's argument — the writer is not serving the sources. The thesis is the writer's own position; sources provide evidence for it.

Common misconception: A common error is writing an essay that has no thesis of its own and merely reports what each source says — this earns a low score because it is not an argument.

How it can be tested: Practice: write a thesis first, then select sources that support it, rather than reading sources and then summarizing them.

Integrating Sources Without Losing Voice

Source material should be introduced with context, quoted or paraphrased accurately, and followed by commentary that connects it to the thesis.

Common misconception: When sources dominate the essay and the writer's voice disappears, the essay fails as an argument — the writer's analysis is what makes it an essay.

How it can be tested: AP®: maintain your own analytical voice throughout; sources should illuminate your argument, not replace it.

Acknowledging Alternative Perspectives

Effective synthesis acknowledges that sources disagree and explains why the writer's position accounts for this disagreement.

Common misconception: A synthesis essay that pretends all sources agree (when they don't) or ignores inconvenient sources misrepresents the complexity of the issue.

How it can be tested: AP® sophistication: the most sophisticated essays explain how they have weighed sources that conflict and why their position accounts for the strongest opposing evidence.

Key Vocabulary
  • writer's voice
  • source integration
  • alternative perspective
  • attribution
  • rhetorical synthesis
Objectives
  • Explain how sources serve the writer's argument rather than replacing it
  • Integrate sources smoothly while maintaining the writer's analytical voice
  • Acknowledge alternative perspectives from sources while maintaining a coherent argument
How this can be tested
  • Essay: write a synthesis that uses three sources but where the student's voice is clearly dominant
  • Peer review: identify whose voice dominates — writer or sources — and suggest corrections
  • Practice acknowledging a contradicting source and explaining why it doesn't undermine your position
Practice before moving on
  • Write a paragraph using three sources where the analytical commentary is twice as long as the quoted material
  • Find a source that contradicts your position and write a concession-rebuttal paragraph addressing it

Visual and Quantitative Sources

Learn on this site
Reading Visual Sources

AP® synthesis prompts often include visual sources: charts, graphs, photographs, cartoons, and infographics. Each visual source has a creator, audience, purpose, and context that shapes how it should be interpreted.

Common misconception: Visual sources are not "objective" — chart design choices (axes, scale, color) can emphasize or obscure information.

How it can be tested: FRQ: describe what the visual source shows and explain how it supports (or complicates) the argument.

Interpreting Charts and Graphs

Quantitative visuals (bar graphs, line charts, pie charts) must be read carefully: note the axes, units, time period, data source, and any anomalies.

Common misconception: Common misreadings include: confusing correlation with causation in a graph, ignoring the scale of the y-axis, or not noting when data is from a biased source.

How it can be tested: AP®: accurately describe what the data shows; explain how it is relevant to the argument; note any limitations of the data.

Integrating Visual Evidence

Visual sources should be integrated just like textual sources: described accurately, cited by designation, and connected to the argument through commentary.

Common misconception: Avoid merely describing a visual source ("Source C shows a graph") — explain what the graph reveals and how it supports the thesis.

How it can be tested: AP® practice: write a paragraph using a visual source as evidence, with full commentary connecting it to the argument.

Key Vocabulary
  • visual source
  • quantitative data
  • infographic
  • data interpretation
  • chart analysis
Objectives
  • Accurately describe and interpret visual sources in an AP® synthesis context
  • Read quantitative graphics critically, noting scale, source, and potential misrepresentations
  • Integrate visual evidence with commentary that connects it to the thesis
How this can be tested
  • FRQ: analyze how a provided chart or photograph supports the synthesis argument
  • MC on interpreting specific data from a graph
  • Practice writing a paragraph that integrates a visual source with commentary
Practice before moving on
  • Find three news articles that use misleading graphs; explain what makes each graph misleading
  • Write a synthesis paragraph that uses both a textual source and a visual source as complementary evidence

What is synthesis and how does it differ from summary?
  • A) Summary restates what individual sources say — often one at a time, sequentially.
  • B) Synthesis develops an argument by combining information from multiple sources, showing how they relate to each other and to the writer's own position.
  • C) The key move in synthesis: instead of "Source A says X; Source B says Y," write "While Source A argues X, Source B's evidence of Y complicates this claim, suggesting that Z is more nuanced than it appears."
  • D) AP® rubric: synthesis essays earn higher scores when sources are integrated into a coherent argument rather than summarized sequentially.
A is correct. Summary restates what individual sources say — often one at a time, sequentially.

  • B) Sources are cited by designation: "(Source A)," "(Source B)."
  • C) Rubric points: thesis (1), evidence and commentary using sources (4), sophistication (1).
  • D) Students receive 6-7 sources on a topic (including visual sources) and write an argument that incorporates at least three sources.

  • C) Read all sources before deciding on a thesis — your position should respond to the full range of evidence available.

  • C) It produces a shallow argument that misrepresents the issue by presenting only evidence that supports one view.
  • D) It also misses the sophistication point — the highest-scoring essays engage with the complexity the sources present, not just the supporting evidence.
