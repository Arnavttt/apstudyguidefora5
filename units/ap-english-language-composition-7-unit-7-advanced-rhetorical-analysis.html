The Rhetorical Analysis Essay

Learn on this site
Overview of the AP® Rhetorical Analysis FRQ

The AP® Language rhetorical analysis essay provides a prose passage and asks students to analyze how rhetorical choices contribute to the writer's argument or purpose.

Common misconception: The essay is not about agreeing or disagreeing with the argument — it is about how the argument works: what strategies the writer uses and how those strategies create effects on the audience.

How it can be tested: AP® strategy: 15 minutes reading and annotating the passage, 25 minutes drafting, 5 minutes reviewing.

The "How" Focus

The critical shift in rhetorical analysis is from "what does the text say" (summary) to "how does the text achieve its effect" (analysis). Every claim in the essay should be about how a specific choice creates a specific effect.

Common misconception: "The author uses statistics" is summary. "The author opens with statistics on childhood obesity rates to establish urgency and situate the reader as a stakeholder rather than a detached observer" is analysis.

How it can be tested: Practice: rewrite three summary statements as analytical statements about how choices create effects.

Thesis for Rhetorical Analysis

A rhetorical analysis thesis makes a claim about how the text's choices achieve its rhetorical purpose. It goes beyond naming devices to explaining the relationship between choices and effects.

Common misconception: "The author uses ethos, pathos, and logos" is a weak thesis (just naming appeals). "By establishing credibility through expert citations and then shifting to emotional narrative, the author moves the audience from skepticism to moral urgency" is a strong thesis.

How it can be tested: AP® rubric thesis point: the thesis must make a claim about the argument's choices and their effects, not just summarize or identify devices.

Key Vocabulary
  • rhetorical analysis
  • how focus
  • analytical thesis
  • choices and effects
  • purpose
Objectives
  • Explain the goal and format of the AP® rhetorical analysis FRQ
  • Shift from summary to analysis by focusing on "how" choices create effects
  • Write a thesis that claims a relationship between choices and effects
How this can be tested
  • Timed practice: 40-minute rhetorical analysis from a released AP® prompt
  • Revision: rewrite summary-based responses as analytical responses
  • Rubric practice: score student essays on the thesis criterion
Practice before moving on
  • Annotate a passage with "how" notes: what effect does each choice create?
  • Write three thesis statements for the same passage: bad, acceptable, strong — compare them

Structure as a Rhetorical Choice

Learn on this site
Organizational Patterns

Organization is a rhetorical choice: chronological, problem-solution, compare-contrast, cause-effect, and general-to-specific all create different effects on how an audience processes an argument.

Common misconception: Analyzing structure means explaining why the writer chose this particular order — what effect does beginning with statistics (vs. a story) create? What does ending with a call to action accomplish?

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain how the passage's structure contributes to its rhetorical purpose.

Openings and Closings

How a text opens establishes tone, positions the reader, and signals what the argument will do; how a text closes determines what the reader carries away.

Common misconception: A text that opens with statistics creates one relationship with the audience; one that opens with a personal story creates another. Neither is inherently better — the question is which is appropriate for the audience and purpose.

How it can be tested: AP®: analyze the opening and closing as rhetorical choices: what effect do they create and why might the writer have chosen them?

Juxtaposition and Contrast

Juxtaposition places contrasting ideas, examples, or images next to each other to highlight differences and create emphasis.

Common misconception: Juxtaposition is not just a stylistic device — it is an argumentative strategy: placing the wealthy and the poor, the before and the after, the ideal and the reality together makes the contrast impossible to ignore.

How it can be tested: AP®: identify juxtaposition and explain the argumentative purpose it serves.

Key Vocabulary
  • organizational structure
  • juxtaposition
  • opening/closing
  • structural choice
  • transition
Objectives
  • Analyze organizational patterns as rhetorical choices
  • Explain how openings and closings serve the writer's rhetorical purpose
  • Identify and analyze juxtaposition as an argumentative strategy
How this can be tested
  • FRQ: analyze how a passage's structure contributes to its argument
  • MC on identifying organizational patterns and their effects
  • Practice: explain why a specific passage opens the way it does
Practice before moving on
  • Reorganize a passage's structure and explain how the different organization changes the argument's effect
  • Find two passages on the same topic with different organizational patterns; explain how structure shapes each argument

Implicit Arguments and Assumptions

Learn on this site
What Is Left Unsaid

Much of an argument's persuasive work happens through implicit assumptions — things the writer does not state but the audience is expected to accept.

Common misconception: Identifying implicit assumptions requires asking: "What must the audience already believe for this evidence to count as support for this claim?"

How it can be tested: AP® advanced analysis: state the implicit assumption in an argument and evaluate whether it is valid.

Ideology and Unexamined Premises

Ideological assumptions are deep, often unexamined beliefs about how the world works (e.g., that markets distribute resources efficiently, or that individuals are responsible for their own circumstances).

Common misconception: These assumptions are rarely argued for explicitly because the writer assumes they are shared by the audience; identifying them reveals the argument's dependence on a particular worldview.

How it can be tested: FRQ: analyze what ideological assumptions underlie a political or economic argument in a passage.

Rhetorical Questions as Hidden Claims

Rhetorical questions assert claims without stating them directly: "If we can't trust the government with our phone data, should we trust them with our medical records?" assumes distrust of government as a shared premise.

Common misconception: Rhetorical questions avoid direct argumentation while asserting claims — they are powerful precisely because they do not invite the audience to examine the assumption.

How it can be tested: AP®: identify rhetorical questions and state the implied claim explicitly; evaluate whether the implied claim is supportable.

Key Vocabulary
  • implicit assumption
  • ideology
  • rhetorical question
  • unexamined premise
  • critical reading
Objectives
  • Identify implicit assumptions in arguments and evaluate their validity
  • Recognize ideological premises and explain how they shape argument
  • Analyze rhetorical questions by stating their implied claims explicitly
How this can be tested
  • FRQ advanced: analyze the implicit assumptions that underlie a passage's argument
  • Practice: rewrite rhetorical questions as explicit claims and evaluate them
  • Essay: identify an ideological premise in an argument and analyze how it shapes the argument's conclusions
Practice before moving on
  • Find three implicit assumptions in an op-ed; state each explicitly
  • Convert five rhetorical questions to explicit claims; evaluate whether each is defensible

Tone Analysis at the Advanced Level

Learn on this site
Multiple Tones Coexisting

Sophisticated texts may hold multiple tones simultaneously: elegiac yet hopeful, ironic yet sincere, outraged yet controlled; identifying the interplay of tones reveals the argument's full complexity.

Common misconception: Don't stop at one tone — ask whether any other emotional register coexists with the primary tone and what the combination accomplishes.

How it can be tested: FRQ: analyze how a passage's tonal complexity contributes to its rhetorical effectiveness.

Irony and Sarcasm in Non-Fiction

Irony and sarcasm in argumentative prose create critical distance and signal that the writer does not endorse what they appear to be saying; they can make a critique more biting than direct statement.

Common misconception: The risk of irony: if the audience doesn't recognize it, they take the literal meaning at face value — irony requires shared context to work.

How it can be tested: AP®: identify irony in a passage, explain how it creates critical distance, and analyze what the writer really means versus what they literally say.

Voice and Persona

The voice of a text (the way the speaker presents themselves) may be different from the actual author; analyzing voice means analyzing the persona the author constructs and what effect that persona creates.

Common misconception: A scientist writing as a dispassionate expert creates one effect; the same scientist writing as a concerned parent creates another — both may be authentic, but the persona is a rhetorical choice.

How it can be tested: AP®: analyze how the speaker's voice or persona shapes the audience's reception of the argument.

Key Vocabulary
  • tonal complexity
  • irony in non-fiction
  • voice
  • persona
  • critical distance
Objectives
  • Identify and analyze multiple tones coexisting in a sophisticated text
  • Explain how irony creates critical distance in argumentative prose
  • Analyze voice and persona as rhetorical choices
How this can be tested
  • FRQ: analyze tonal complexity in a passage and explain how multiple tones serve the purpose
  • Practice: identify irony in a contemporary essay and explain the contrast between literal and intended meaning
  • Essay: analyze how a speaker's constructed persona shapes the audience's response
Practice before moving on
  • Find a passage with tonal complexity (angry + controlled, ironic + sincere); analyze how both tones work together
  • Read a passage where the speaker constructs a persona; explain what the persona is and why it was chosen
