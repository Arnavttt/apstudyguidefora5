Narrative Structure and Plot

Learn on this site
Story vs. Plot

Story is the chronological sequence of events; plot is the writer's arrangement of those events for effect — including flashbacks, in medias res openings, and non-linear structures.

Common misconception: Not all fiction is chronological — analyzing narrative structure requires identifying how the writer's choices about order, pacing, and emphasis shape meaning.

How it can be tested: AP® FRQ: explain how a specific structural choice (flashback, frame narrative) contributes to the story's meaning or effect.

Conflict and Tension

Conflict drives narrative: character vs. character, character vs. society, character vs. nature, character vs. self. Internal conflict is often the most significant in literary fiction.

Common misconception: The most important conflict in literary fiction is usually internal (psychological) rather than external — the protagonist's struggle with their own beliefs, desires, or identity.

How it can be tested: MC: identify the central conflict type; FRQ: explain how conflict develops and what it reveals about character.

Epiphany and Resolution

An epiphany is a moment of sudden realization or insight by a character; in short fiction (especially Chekhov and Joyce), the ending often withholds resolution, leaving readers with irresolution or ambiguity.

Common misconception: Not all short fiction ends with resolution — literary fiction often ends with ambiguity that reflects the complexity of life; this is a deliberate choice, not a flaw.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain what the ending reveals about the story's central idea and whether resolution is achieved.

Key Vocabulary
  • plot
  • conflict
  • epiphany
  • in medias res
  • resolution
Objectives
  • Distinguish story from plot and explain how structural choices create meaning
  • Identify types of conflict and explain how they develop character
  • Analyze epiphanies and ambiguous endings as literary choices
How this can be tested
  • AP® FRQ: explain how a structural choice contributes to meaning
  • MC on plot structure and conflict types
  • Essay: analyze how an ambiguous ending creates or denies resolution
Practice before moving on
  • Diagram the plot structure of a short story; identify which structural choices are most significant
  • Find an epiphany in a story; explain what the character realizes and what it reveals about theme

Characterization

Learn on this site
Direct and Indirect Characterization

Direct characterization explicitly states what a character is like; indirect characterization reveals character through actions, dialogue, thoughts, and others' reactions.

Common misconception: Literary fiction relies primarily on indirect characterization — readers infer character from behavior, which is more artistically complex and reader-involving than telling.

How it can be tested: AP® FRQ: analyze how an author's characterization methods reveal character psychology or develop theme.

Character Change and Static Characters

Dynamic characters undergo significant change; static characters remain essentially the same. The protagonist is often dynamic; foils (contrasting characters) and static characters help define the protagonist by contrast.

Common misconception: Not all protagonists are dynamic — sometimes the protagonist's failure to change is the story's central statement about theme.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain how a character's change (or refusal to change) develops the story's central idea.

Motivation and Psychology

Understanding why characters act as they do requires analyzing their desires, fears, and contradictions; complex characters have motivations that are sometimes contradictory or not fully conscious.

Common misconception: Characters in literary fiction are rarely purely motivated by obvious self-interest — complex motivation (guilt, repressed desire, social pressure) is what distinguishes literary from genre fiction.

How it can be tested: AP®: analyze a character's motivation and explain how it reveals something about the work's central idea.

Key Vocabulary
  • characterization
  • dynamic character
  • static character
  • foil
  • motivation
Objectives
  • Distinguish direct and indirect characterization with textual evidence
  • Explain the significance of character change or stasis in the context of theme
  • Analyze complex character motivation and its relationship to the work's central idea
How this can be tested
  • AP® FRQ: analyze how characterization methods reveal psychology or theme
  • MC on identifying character type and motivation
  • Essay: analyze how a foil illuminates the protagonist's character
Practice before moving on
  • Find examples of both direct and indirect characterization for the protagonist of a story; compare their effects
  • Identify a static character; explain why their stasis is significant to the story's meaning

Setting and Atmosphere

Learn on this site
Setting as More Than Background

Setting (time, place, social environment) is not merely background — it shapes character, creates atmosphere, and develops theme. In Gothic fiction, Southern Gothic, and magical realism, setting is central to meaning.

Common misconception: Setting is not just "where the story takes place" — it includes social context, historical moment, and the emotional environment established by descriptions.

How it can be tested: AP® FRQ: analyze how setting contributes to the story's atmosphere and meaning.

Atmosphere and Mood

Atmosphere is the emotional environment created by setting, diction, and imagery; it primes the reader's emotional response and shapes how events are interpreted.

Common misconception: Atmosphere is created through multiple channels simultaneously — word choice, sentence rhythm, imagery, and color all contribute; no single element creates atmosphere alone.

How it can be tested: FRQ: explain how specific descriptive choices create the story's atmosphere and contribute to its meaning.

Setting as Symbol

Settings often function symbolically — a decaying house signals moral decay; a wild natural landscape signals freedom or danger; a confined room symbolizes psychological entrapment.

Common misconception: When analyzing setting symbolically, ground the interpretation in specific textual details — the interpretation must be justified by evidence, not imposed from outside the text.

How it can be tested: AP®: identify a setting that functions symbolically and explain how it deepens the story's meaning.

Key Vocabulary
  • setting
  • atmosphere
  • mood
  • pathetic fallacy
  • symbolic setting
Objectives
  • Analyze setting as a meaningful element of fiction, not just background
  • Explain how atmosphere is created through specific descriptive choices
  • Identify symbolic functions of setting and ground them in textual evidence
How this can be tested
  • AP® FRQ: analyze how setting contributes to atmosphere and meaning
  • MC on identifying setting choices and their effects
  • Essay: analyze a symbolic setting and explain its thematic significance
Practice before moving on
  • Map the setting details of a story; identify which are most atmospherically significant
  • Find a setting that functions symbolically; explain what it represents with textual evidence

Point of View and Narrator

Learn on this site
Types of Narration

First-person narration (I) offers intimacy and limited perspective; third-person limited (he/she/they) focuses on one character's perception; third-person omniscient has access to all characters' minds; second-person (you) is rare and creates direct reader implication.

Common misconception: First-person narrators are not necessarily reliable — an unreliable narrator has limited knowledge, distorted perception, or conscious/unconscious deception that the reader must recognize.

How it can be tested: AP® FRQ: analyze how the point of view shapes what the reader knows and how they interpret events.

Unreliable Narration

An unreliable narrator presents a distorted account that the reader can recognize as inaccurate through gaps, contradictions, and what other characters reveal. Examples: Stevens in "The Remains of the Day," Holden Caulfield in "The Catcher in the Rye."

Common misconception: Recognizing unreliable narration is an advanced reading skill — the unreliable narrator's distortions are as meaningful as what they report, often revealing psychological truths the narrator doesn't consciously acknowledge.

How it can be tested: FRQ: analyze how an unreliable narrator's distortions reveal meaning that the narrator doesn't consciously intend.

Narrative Distance

Narrative distance is the degree of emotional or psychological closeness between the narrator and the story's events; close narration creates intensity; distant narration creates irony or objectivity.

Common misconception: Narrative distance can shift within a story — a narrator who is emotionally close to events in the climax but distant in the denouement creates a specific effect.

How it can be tested: AP®: analyze shifts in narrative distance and explain what effect they create.

Key Vocabulary
  • point of view
  • unreliable narrator
  • narrative distance
  • first-person
  • omniscient narrator
Objectives
  • Distinguish narrative points of view and explain how each shapes reader knowledge
  • Identify unreliable narration and explain how distortions create meaning
  • Analyze shifts in narrative distance and their effects
How this can be tested

  • MC on identifying narrative perspective and its effects
  • Essay: analyze an unreliable narrator's distortions and their thematic significance
