The Speaker and the Poem's Situation

Learn on this site
Speaker vs. Poet

The speaker of a poem is the voice that speaks the poem — a persona created by the poet that may or may not represent the poet's actual views. Analyzing a poem's speaker is the foundation of literary analysis.

Common misconception: Never assume the speaker is the poet — even in confessional poetry, the speaker is a crafted persona. The question is what the speaker's situation and voice reveal.

How it can be tested: AP® FRQ: analyze how the speaker's situation, relationship to the subject, and tone contribute to the poem's meaning.

The Poem's Situation

Every poem exists in a situation: who is speaking, to whom, about what, and under what circumstances? Identifying the situation is the first step in interpretation.

Common misconception: The situation may not be immediately obvious — some poems require several readings to determine who is speaking to whom and why.

How it can be tested: AP®: before analyzing a poem's language, establish the situation clearly; then analyze how language choices serve the speaker's purpose.

Tone in Poetry

Tone is the speaker's attitude toward the subject and audience; in poetry, tone is created through diction, imagery, and form. Tone can be ironic, elegiac, celebratory, defiant, resigned, or any number of complex combinations.

Common misconception: Tone in poetry is often more complex than a single word — a poem may be simultaneously sorrowful and celebratory, which is part of its meaning.

How it can be tested: FRQ: identify the tone of a poem and explain how specific choices create it.

Key Vocabulary
  • speaker
  • persona
  • situation
  • tone
  • dramatic monologue
Objectives
  • Distinguish the speaker from the poet and analyze the speaker's situation
  • Identify the poem's dramatic situation as the foundation for interpretation
  • Analyze tone in poetry through specific diction and imagery choices
How this can be tested
  • AP® FRQ: analyze how the speaker's situation and tone contribute to the poem's meaning
  • MC on identifying speaker and situation
  • Essay: analyze tonal complexity in a poem
Practice before moving on

Imagery and Sensory Language

Learn on this site
Types of Imagery

Imagery is language that creates sensory experience: visual, auditory, tactile, olfactory, gustatory, and kinesthetic. Literary analysis requires identifying which senses are engaged and why.

Common misconception: Imagery is not decoration — it creates specific emotional and intellectual effects that must be analyzed, not just identified.

How it can be tested: AP® FRQ: analyze how specific images contribute to the poem's meaning or the speaker's emotional state.

Patterns of Imagery

Extended or repeated imagery (imagery patterns) creates thematic emphasis; a poem consistently using images of cold and darkness is making a statement through that accumulation.

Common misconception: Identifying a pattern is more analytically powerful than identifying a single image — the pattern suggests intentionality and thematic significance.

How it can be tested: AP®: identify dominant imagery patterns and explain what they collectively reveal about the poem's meaning.

Concrete vs. Abstract

Literary poetry grounds abstract ideas (love, mortality, justice) in concrete, sensory images — this is what makes poetry more powerful than philosophical statement.

Common misconception: "My love is like a red, red rose" (concrete image) communicates differently than "My love is beautiful and pure" (abstract statement).

How it can be tested: AP®: analyze how a poem uses concrete imagery to explore abstract themes.

Key Vocabulary
  • imagery
  • imagery pattern
  • concrete
  • abstract
  • sensory detail
Objectives
  • Identify types of imagery and explain their sensory and emotional effects
  • Recognize imagery patterns and analyze their thematic significance
  • Explain how concrete imagery explores abstract ideas
How this can be tested
  • AP® FRQ: analyze how imagery creates meaning in a poem
  • MC on identifying image types and effects
  • Essay: trace an imagery pattern through a poem and explain its thematic significance
Practice before moving on
  • Annotate a poem by type of image (visual, auditory, tactile, etc.); identify which senses dominate and explain why
  • Map the imagery pattern in a poem; write a paragraph explaining what the pattern reveals about theme

Figurative Language

Learn on this site
Metaphor and Simile

Metaphor states a comparison directly ("Life is a journey"); simile uses "like" or "as" ("Life is like a journey"). Both create meaning through comparison, but metaphors carry more rhetorical force.

Common misconception: Extended metaphors (conceits) develop a comparison across many lines or through an entire poem; analyzing the full implications of the comparison is central to AP® Literary analysis.

How it can be tested: FRQ: analyze how a metaphor or extended conceit creates meaning in a poem.

Personification and Apostrophe

Personification attributes human qualities to non-human things; apostrophe addresses an absent, dead, or non-human entity directly ("O Death, where is thy sting?").

Common misconception: Personification and apostrophe are not just decorative — they are argumentative: personifying nature suggests that nature has intentionality; apostrophe creates a relationship between speaker and subject.

How it can be tested: AP®: analyze what personification or apostrophe reveals about the speaker's relationship to the subject.

Symbol vs. Allegory

A symbol is an object, person, or event that carries meaning beyond its literal existence; allegory is a sustained narrative where every element has a corresponding abstract meaning.

Common misconception: Not every concrete element is a symbol — a symbol must carry consistent additional meaning supported by textual evidence.

How it can be tested: AP®: identify a symbol in a poem, ground it in evidence, and explain what it represents and why that representation matters to the poem's meaning.

Key Vocabulary
  • metaphor
  • simile
  • extended metaphor
  • personification
  • symbol
Objectives
  • Distinguish metaphor from simile and explain how each creates meaning
  • Analyze personification and apostrophe as rhetorical choices
  • Distinguish symbols from non-symbolic concrete details and ground symbolic interpretations in evidence
How this can be tested
  • AP® FRQ: analyze how figurative language creates meaning
  • MC on identifying figurative language types and effects
  • Essay: trace an extended metaphor through a poem
Practice before moving on
  • Find an extended metaphor in a poem; trace all the ways the vehicle illuminates the tenor
  • Find a symbol in a poem; list five textual details that support the symbolic interpretation

Poetic Form and Sound

Learn on this site
Line and Stanza

The poetic line (not the sentence) is the basic unit of poetry; line breaks create emphasis and meaning. Stanzas group lines into meaningful units; stanza length and structure are formal choices.

Common misconception: Where a line breaks controls what the reader pauses on — the last word of a line receives emphasis; enjambment (continuing across a line break) creates different rhythmic and semantic effects than end-stopped lines.

How it can be tested: AP®: analyze how line breaks and stanza structure create meaning or emphasis.

Meter and Rhythm

Meter is the regular pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables; iambic pentameter (10 syllables, da-DUM pattern, 5 repetitions) is the dominant English meter. Free verse has no regular meter.

Common misconception: Metrical variation (departing from the established pattern) creates emphasis — a sudden spondee (two stressed syllables) in an iambic poem calls attention to itself.

How it can be tested: AP®: identify the dominant meter and explain how metrical variations create emphasis.

Sound Devices

Alliteration (repeated initial consonants), assonance (repeated vowel sounds), consonance (repeated consonant sounds), and onomatopoeia (words that sound like what they mean) create sonic effects that reinforce meaning.

Common misconception: Sound devices are not ornamental — they create semantic effects: sibilant sounds (s, sh) create hissing or soothing effects; plosive consonants (p, b, t) create explosive effects.

How it can be tested: AP®: identify specific sound devices and explain how they contribute to the poem's meaning or emotional effect.

Key Vocabulary
  • line break
  • enjambment
  • end-stopped
  • meter
  • alliteration
Objectives
  • Analyze how line breaks and stanza structure create meaning
  • Identify meter and explain the effect of metrical variations
  • Identify sound devices and explain their semantic and emotional effects
How this can be tested
  • AP® FRQ: analyze how formal choices (line, stanza, sound) contribute to meaning
  • MC on identifying metrical patterns and sound devices
  • Essay: analyze how enjambment functions in a specific poem
Practice before moving on
  • Mark stressed and unstressed syllables in a stanza; identify the dominant meter and any variations
  • Annotate a poem for sound devices; explain the effect of the dominant sound pattern
