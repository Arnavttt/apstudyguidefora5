Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services

Learn on this site
Types of Biodiversity

Biodiversity encompasses genetic, species, and ecosystem diversity; high diversity increases ecosystem resilience.

Common misconception: Biodiversity is more than just species count — genetic and ecosystem diversity matter equally.

How it can be tested: Distinguish genetic, species, and ecosystem diversity and explain each's contribution to stability.

Ecosystem Services

Provisioning (food, water), regulating (climate, flood control), cultural (recreation), and supporting services (nutrient cycling) all depend on biodiversity.

Common misconception: Ecosystem services are often undervalued until lost; economic valuation helps highlight their importance.

How it can be tested: Categorize ecosystem services and connect them to biodiversity.

Biodiversity Hotspots

Hotspots are regions with exceptional species richness and endemism that face significant habitat loss.

Common misconception: Hotspots cover only ~2.5% of Earth's surface but contain over 50% of the world's plant species.

How it can be tested: Identify characteristics of biodiversity hotspots and explain why they are conservation priorities.

Key Vocabulary
  • genetic diversity — Variety of genes within a species; enables adaptation to change.
  • species richness — Total number of different species in an area.
  • ecosystem services — Benefits that humans derive from healthy ecosystems.
  • endemic species — Species found only in a specific geographic area and nowhere else.
  • biodiversity hotspot — Region with high species richness and endemism that is under threat.
Objectives
  • Distinguish three types of biodiversity.
  • Categorize ecosystem services by type.
  • Explain why hotspots are conservation priorities.
How this can be tested
  • Identifying ecosystem services from examples.
  • Connecting biodiversity loss to loss of ecosystem services.
  • Explaining why hotspot conservation is cost-effective.

Threats to Biodiversity

Learn on this site
HIPPO Threats

The main threats to biodiversity are Habitat loss, Invasive species, Pollution, Population growth, and Overharvesting (HIPPO); habitat loss is the leading cause.

Common misconception: Climate change is increasingly recognized as a sixth major threat, sometimes added to form HIPPO+C.

How it can be tested: Rank and explain the HIPPO threats; identify the most significant cause of biodiversity loss.

Habitat Fragmentation

Fragmentation breaks continuous habitat into patches, isolating populations and reducing genetic diversity (edge effects increase, interior habitat decreases).

Common misconception: Small, isolated fragments support fewer species than one large equivalent area.

How it can be tested: Explain edge effects and how corridors mitigate fragmentation.

Invasive Species

Invasive species outcompete natives by lacking predators, parasites, and competitors in their new environment.

Common misconception: Not all introduced species become invasive; invasiveness depends on traits and available niches.

How it can be tested: Describe how invasive species affect native communities and identify examples.

Key Vocabulary
  • habitat fragmentation — Breaking large habitat into smaller, isolated patches.
  • edge effect — Altered conditions at the boundary of two habitat types; often unfavorable for interior species.
  • invasive species — Non-native organism that spreads rapidly and harms native species or ecosystems.
  • wildlife corridor — Strip of habitat connecting isolated fragments; allows gene flow and migration.
  • overharvesting — Removing individuals from a population faster than they can reproduce.
Objectives
  • Identify and rank HIPPO threats to biodiversity.
  • Explain how habitat fragmentation reduces biodiversity.
  • Describe mechanisms by which invasive species harm native ecosystems.
How this can be tested
  • Applying HIPPO framework to a case study.
  • Explaining why wildlife corridors help fragmented populations.
  • Evaluating management strategies for invasive species.

Island Biogeography

Learn on this site
Species-Area Relationship

Larger islands support more species because they have more habitat and can sustain larger populations; S ∝ A^z (z ≈ 0.25).

Common misconception: The species-area relationship applies to habitat "islands" like forest fragments, not just oceanic islands.

How it can be tested: Predict species number from island area using the species-area relationship.

Immigration and Extinction Rates

Species richness equilibrium occurs where immigration rate equals extinction rate; closer/larger islands reach higher equilibria.

Common misconception: Equilibrium is dynamic — species composition changes even when richness stays constant.

How it can be tested: Use the immigration-extinction graph to predict equilibrium species number.

Application to Conservation

Island biogeography theory predicts that larger and less-isolated nature reserves support more species; design should maximize size and connectivity.

Common misconception: One large reserve is generally better than several small reserves of equal total area (though debate continues).

How it can be tested: Apply island biogeography principles to reserve design decisions.

Key Vocabulary
  • island biogeography — Theory predicting species richness on islands based on area and isolation.
  • species-area relationship — Larger areas support more species; S ∝ A^z.
  • immigration rate — Rate at which new species colonize an island; higher for closer islands.
  • extinction rate — Rate at which species disappear from an island; lower on larger islands.
  • equilibrium species number — Stable number of species where immigration = extinction rates.
Objectives
  • Apply the species-area relationship to predict species richness.
  • Interpret immigration-extinction equilibrium diagrams.
  • Apply island biogeography to conservation reserve design.
How this can be tested
  • Comparing two nature reserves using island biogeography principles.
  • Predicting effect of increasing isolation on extinction rate.
  • Explaining the SLOSS debate (Single Large Or Several Small reserves).

Ecological Succession

Learn on this site
Primary Succession

Primary succession begins on bare rock with no soil; pioneer species (lichens, mosses) break down rock and build soil over centuries.

Common misconception: Primary succession does not start from existing soil — this is the key distinction from secondary succession.

How it can be tested: Describe the sequence of primary succession from bare rock to climax community.

Secondary Succession

Secondary succession occurs after disturbance on land that retains soil; it is faster than primary succession because soil and seed banks remain.

Common misconception: Secondary succession does not restore the exact original community — disturbance history matters.

How it can be tested: Explain why secondary succession is faster and predict the successional stages after abandonment.

Climax Communities and Disturbance

Climax community is the stable end-state of succession for a given climate; disturbance resets succession.

Common misconception: The climax community concept is idealized — most ecosystems exist in shifting mosaics maintained by disturbances.

How it can be tested: Explain the role of fire and other disturbances in maintaining ecosystem diversity.

Key Vocabulary
  • primary succession — Succession beginning on bare substrate with no existing soil.
  • secondary succession — Succession on disturbed land where soil remains intact.
  • pioneer species — First colonizers of bare or disturbed habitat; often hardy generalists.
  • climax community — Theoretically stable, self-sustaining community at the end of succession.
  • disturbance — Event that disrupts an ecosystem and resets or redirects succession.
Objectives
  • Distinguish primary and secondary succession with examples.
  • Identify pioneer species and explain their role.
  • Explain how fire maintains certain ecosystems.
How this can be tested
  • Sequencing successional stages after a volcanic eruption or farm abandonment.
  • Comparing timescales of primary vs. secondary succession.
  • Analyzing intermediate disturbance hypothesis.
