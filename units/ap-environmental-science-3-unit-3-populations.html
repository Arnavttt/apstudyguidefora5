Population Growth Models

Learn on this site
Exponential Growth

Populations grow exponentially when resources are unlimited; J-shaped curve. Growth rate: dN/dt = rN.

Common misconception: Exponential growth is unsustainable in nature because resources are finite.

How it can be tested: Calculate population size after a given time using exponential growth; identify conditions where it occurs.

Logistic Growth

Logistic growth slows as population approaches carrying capacity (K); S-shaped curve. dN/dt = rN(K−N)/K.

Common misconception: Populations don't always reach a smooth K — they can overshoot and oscillate around K.

How it can be tested: Predict population trajectory near K; identify the inflection point where growth rate is maximum.

Carrying Capacity

K is the maximum population size a habitat can sustain indefinitely based on resource availability; environmental factors determine K.

Common misconception: K is not fixed — it changes if resources improve or degrade.

How it can be tested: Explain what limits K and predict how resource changes shift K.

Key Vocabulary
  • exponential growth — Population growth at a constant rate; produces a J-shaped curve.
  • logistic growth — Growth that slows as population approaches carrying capacity; S-shaped curve.
  • carrying capacity (K) — Maximum population size sustainable by available resources.
  • intrinsic rate of increase (r) — Maximum growth rate per capita under ideal conditions.
  • overshoot — Temporary population size exceeding carrying capacity, followed by die-off.
Objectives
  • Distinguish exponential and logistic growth with graphs.
  • Calculate maximum growth rate in logistic model.
  • Explain what determines carrying capacity.
How this can be tested
  • Interpreting population growth graphs (J vs. S curve).
  • Identifying where on the logistic curve growth rate is maximum (at N = K/2).
  • Predicting overshoot and crash scenarios.

Life History Strategies

Learn on this site
r-selected Species

r-selected species: short lifespan, rapid reproduction, many offspring with little parental care, early maturity (weeds, insects, small mammals).

Common misconception: r-selected species thrive in unstable, unpredictable environments where rapid reproduction is advantageous.

How it can be tested: Identify r-selected traits and explain when this strategy is advantageous.

K-selected Species

K-selected species: long lifespan, slow reproduction, few offspring with high parental investment, late maturity (elephants, whales, humans).

Common misconception: K-selected species are more vulnerable to extinction because they reproduce slowly.

How it can be tested: Identify K-selected traits and explain vulnerability to overharvesting.

Survivorship Curves

Type I (humans, elephants): most die old. Type II (birds, squirrels): constant mortality. Type III (fish, insects): most die young.

Common misconception: Survivorship curves describe population-level patterns, not individual lifespan.

How it can be tested: Classify species by survivorship curve type and relate to r/K strategy.

Key Vocabulary
  • r-selected species — Adapted to unstable environments; produce many offspring with little parental care.
  • K-selected species — Adapted to stable environments; produce few offspring with high parental investment.
  • Type I survivorship — Most individuals survive to old age; late mortality (large mammals).
  • Type II survivorship — Constant mortality rate throughout life (many birds).
  • Type III survivorship — Most die young; few survive to adulthood (fish, insects, plants).
Objectives
  • Distinguish r- and K-selected life history strategies.
  • Classify species by survivorship curve type.
  • Explain why K-selected species are more vulnerable to extinction.
How this can be tested
  • Predicting which strategy is advantageous in stable vs. disturbed environments.
  • Matching species to survivorship curve types.
  • Connecting K-selection to vulnerability in overharvesting scenarios.

Human Population Growth

Learn on this site
Demographic Transition Model

The DTM shows four stages: pre-industrial (high birth/death), transitional (declining death, high birth), industrial (declining birth), and post-industrial (low birth/death; stable or declining population).

Common misconception: Countries can stall in Stage 2, experiencing rapid population growth as death rates fall before birth rates do.

How it can be tested: Identify a country's DTM stage from birth/death rate data and predict population trajectory.

Age-Structure Diagrams

Age-structure pyramids show population by age and sex; wide base = rapid growth, uniform = stable, narrow base = declining.

Common misconception: Age-structure diagrams show future trends, not current growth rate.

How it can be tested: Interpret age-structure diagrams to predict future population growth.

Total Fertility Rate and Population Momentum

Replacement-level TFR ≈ 2.1 in developed countries; population momentum means growth continues even after TFR drops to replacement due to a large young cohort.

Common misconception: Even if TFR reaches replacement, population keeps growing for decades — this is momentum.

How it can be tested: Explain how population momentum and age structure affect future population size.

Key Vocabulary
  • demographic transition model (DTM) — Model showing stages of population growth as societies develop economically.
  • total fertility rate (TFR) — Average number of children a woman has in her lifetime.
  • replacement-level fertility — TFR at which population size remains stable (~2.1 in developed countries).
  • population momentum — Continued population growth after TFR drops to replacement, due to large young cohort.
  • age-structure diagram — Bar graph showing population distribution by age and sex; predicts future growth.
Objectives
  • Identify DTM stages and predict population change.
  • Interpret age-structure diagrams for growth trend.
  • Explain population momentum.
How this can be tested
  • Classifying countries by DTM stage from data.
  • Predicting growth from age-structure diagrams.
  • Explaining why global population keeps growing despite falling TFR in many countries.

Factors Limiting Population Growth

Learn on this site
Density-Dependent Factors

Density-dependent limiting factors (disease, predation, competition) become more intense as population density increases.

Common misconception: Not all disease outbreaks are density-dependent — some spread regardless of density.

How it can be tested: Identify density-dependent factors and predict how they change with population density.

Density-Independent Factors

Density-independent factors (frost, drought, fire) affect populations regardless of their size.

Common misconception: Natural disasters are the classic density-independent factor — they kill the same proportion at any density.

How it can be tested: Distinguish density-dependent from density-independent factors and predict population impact.

Reproductive Strategies and Population Management

Maximum sustainable yield (MSY) for harvested populations = harvest at K/2 where logistic growth rate is highest.

Common misconception: Harvesting beyond MSY drives population toward extinction; below MSY wastes potential.

How it can be tested: Calculate MSY and explain the rationale for harvesting at population level K/2.

Key Vocabulary
  • density-dependent factor — Limiting factor whose effect increases with population density (disease, competition, predation).
  • density-independent factor — Limiting factor whose effect is the same regardless of population density (drought, frost, fire).
  • maximum sustainable yield (MSY) — Largest harvest that can be taken indefinitely; occurs at N = K/2.
  • intraspecific competition — Competition between members of the same species for the same resources.
  • predator-prey cycle — Oscillating relationship between predator and prey populations (e.g., lynx-hare).
Objectives
  • Distinguish density-dependent and density-independent limiting factors.
  • Explain the concept of maximum sustainable yield.
  • Analyze predator-prey cycle dynamics.
How this can be tested
  • Classifying limiting factors as density-dependent or independent.
  • Applying MSY to fisheries management scenarios.
  • Interpreting predator-prey cycle graphs.
