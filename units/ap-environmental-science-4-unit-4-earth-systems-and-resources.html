Earth's Geologic Systems

Learn on this site
Plate Tectonics

Tectonic plate movement causes earthquakes, volcanoes, and mountain building; driven by convection in the mantle.

Common misconception: Plate tectonics operates over millions of years but causes sudden events (earthquakes) at plate boundaries.

How it can be tested: Match tectonic boundary type (convergent, divergent, transform) to geological features.

Volcanoes and Environmental Effects

Volcanic eruptions release CO₂, SO₂, and ash; large eruptions can cause short-term global cooling by blocking sunlight.

Common misconception: Volcanic eruptions add CO₂ to the atmosphere, but the total amount is small compared to human emissions.

How it can be tested: Describe how volcanic eruptions affect climate and soils.

Earthquakes and Tsunamis

Earthquakes along fault lines release energy; tsunamis are generated by underwater earthquakes displacing water.

Common misconception: Tsunamis are not tidal waves — they are generated by seismic activity, not tides.

How it can be tested: Explain how earthquakes and tsunamis pose environmental and human risks.

Key Vocabulary
  • plate tectonics — Theory that Earth's lithosphere is divided into moving plates driven by mantle convection.
  • convergent boundary — Where two plates move toward each other; produces subduction zones, mountains, or volcanoes.
  • divergent boundary — Where two plates move apart; produces mid-ocean ridges or rift valleys.
  • transform boundary — Where two plates slide past each other; produces earthquakes (e.g., San Andreas Fault).
  • subduction — Process where one tectonic plate slides beneath another into the mantle.
Objectives
  • Match plate boundary types to geological features.
  • Explain volcanic environmental effects (cooling, soil enrichment).
  • Describe how tsunamis form and their environmental impacts.
How this can be tested
  • Identifying boundary types from descriptions of geological features.
  • Explaining why volcanic soils are fertile.
  • Comparing environmental risks at different plate boundaries.

Soil Formation and Properties

Learn on this site
Soil Formation (Pedogenesis)

Soil forms from parent material through weathering + organic matter addition over centuries; influenced by climate, organisms, topography, and time.

Common misconception: Soil formation takes hundreds to thousands of years; it is a nonrenewable resource on human timescales.

How it can be tested: Identify factors that affect rate of soil formation and explain why soil is considered nonrenewable.

Soil Horizons

Soil profile includes O (organic litter), A (topsoil, humus-rich), E (eluviated), B (subsoil, accumulated minerals), C (parent material), and R (bedrock) horizons.

Common misconception: Topsoil (A horizon) is most important for agriculture; erosion removes the most fertile layer.

How it can be tested: Identify soil horizons and explain how erosion threatens agricultural productivity.

Soil Properties

Soil texture (sand, silt, clay proportions) determines drainage and nutrient retention; loam (balanced texture) is ideal for agriculture. Organic matter and pH also affect fertility.

Common misconception: Clay soils hold more nutrients but drain poorly; sandy soils drain quickly but hold few nutrients.

How it can be tested: Predict soil drainage and fertility from texture; explain how pH affects nutrient availability.

Key Vocabulary
  • pedogenesis — Process of soil formation from parent material over time.
  • humus — Decomposed organic matter in soil; improves water retention and nutrient availability.
  • soil horizon — Distinct layer in a soil profile (O, A, E, B, C, R horizons).
  • loam — Balanced mixture of sand, silt, and clay; ideal agricultural soil texture.
  • soil erosion — Removal of topsoil by wind or water; major threat to agricultural productivity.
Objectives
  • Describe factors controlling soil formation rate.
  • Identify soil horizons and their importance.
  • Relate soil texture to drainage and fertility.
How this can be tested
  • Identifying threats to soil quality (erosion, compaction, salinization).
  • Explaining why loam is ideal for farming.
  • Calculating erosion consequences for agricultural productivity.

The Hydrologic Cycle

Learn on this site
Hydrologic Cycle Pathways

Water cycles through evaporation, transpiration, condensation, precipitation, runoff, infiltration, and groundwater flow; solar energy and gravity drive the cycle.

Common misconception: Transpiration (water released by plants) is often underappreciated but can exceed evaporation in forested areas.

How it can be tested: Trace water through all hydrologic cycle pathways and identify the driving forces.

Groundwater and Aquifers

Aquifers store groundwater in porous rock; recharge depends on infiltration. Overdraft (pumping faster than recharge) depletes aquifers and can cause land subsidence.

Common misconception: Once an aquifer is depleted, recharge can take thousands of years.

How it can be tested: Explain aquifer recharge, overdraft, and consequences of groundwater depletion.

Freshwater Availability

Only ~2.5% of Earth's water is fresh; most is locked in glaciers. Surface water and groundwater are the usable sources. Agriculture accounts for ~70% of global freshwater withdrawals.

Common misconception: Despite abundant total water, freshwater is unevenly distributed and often over-exploited.

How it can be tested: Identify major human uses of freshwater and the consequences of over-extraction.

Key Vocabulary
  • evapotranspiration — Combined water loss from evaporation and plant transpiration.
  • aquifer — Underground layer of porous rock or sediment storing groundwater.
  • infiltration — Water moving from the surface down through soil into the water table.
  • water table — Upper boundary of the saturated zone in soil or rock.
  • overdraft — Pumping groundwater faster than it is naturally recharged.
Objectives
  • Trace water through the hydrologic cycle.
  • Explain aquifer recharge and consequences of overdraft.
  • Identify major freshwater uses and threats to availability.
How this can be tested
  • Explaining how deforestation affects the hydrologic cycle.
  • Predicting consequences of aquifer overdraft.
  • Calculating freshwater use from data.

The Atmosphere

Learn on this site
Atmospheric Layers

The troposphere (weather), stratosphere (ozone layer), mesosphere, and thermosphere each have distinct temperature profiles and roles.

Common misconception: Weather occurs in the troposphere; ozone protection occurs in the stratosphere — students often confuse these layers.

How it can be tested: Identify atmospheric layers and their key characteristics.

Coriolis Effect and Wind Patterns

Earth's rotation deflects moving air and water rightward in the Northern Hemisphere (leftward in Southern), creating trade winds, westerlies, and polar easterlies.

Common misconception: The Coriolis effect does NOT determine the direction of drain whirlpools — the effect is too weak at that scale.

How it can be tested: Explain how the Coriolis effect creates global wind patterns and influences weather.

Ocean Currents and Climate

Surface ocean currents are driven by wind; thermohaline circulation (deep-water) is driven by temperature and salinity differences. Both affect regional climate significantly.

Common misconception: Ocean currents warm or cool adjacent land areas; disrupting them (e.g., AMOC slowdown) could dramatically alter climates.

How it can be tested: Explain how ocean currents like the Gulf Stream moderate regional climates.

Key Vocabulary
  • troposphere — Lowest atmospheric layer, 0–12 km; contains weather and most water vapor.
  • stratosphere — Layer 12–50 km; contains ozone layer that absorbs UV radiation.
  • Coriolis effect — Deflection of moving air/water caused by Earth's rotation.
  • thermohaline circulation — Deep ocean circulation driven by density differences from temperature and salinity.
  • trade winds — Persistent winds near the equator flowing from subtropical high-pressure zones toward the equator.
Objectives
  • Identify atmospheric layers and their functions.
  • Explain the Coriolis effect and resulting wind patterns.
  • Describe how ocean currents affect regional climates.
How this can be tested
  • Predicting wind direction from Coriolis effect.
  • Explaining how thermohaline circulation is affected by freshwater input (glacial melt).
  • Connecting ocean currents to the climate of Western Europe.
