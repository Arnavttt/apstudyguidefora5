Agriculture and Soil Management

Learn on this site
Industrial Agriculture

Industrial agriculture uses monocultures, synthetic fertilizers, pesticides, and mechanization to maximize yield; it increases productivity but reduces biodiversity and degrades soil.

Common misconception: Monocultures increase efficiency but are highly vulnerable to pest outbreaks and disease.

How it can be tested: Evaluate trade-offs of industrial agriculture vs. sustainable alternatives.

Soil Degradation

Salinization (irrigated soil salt buildup), erosion (removal of topsoil), and desertification (degradation of dryland soil) reduce agricultural productivity.

Common misconception: Salinization is caused by evaporation of irrigation water leaving salt behind, not by the salt in crops.

How it can be tested: Describe causes and prevention of salinization, erosion, and desertification.

Sustainable Agriculture

Contour plowing, terracing, cover crops, crop rotation, no-till farming, and integrated pest management (IPM) reduce erosion, restore nutrients, and cut chemical use.

Common misconception: Sustainable practices often reduce short-term yield while improving long-term productivity and ecosystem health.

How it can be tested: Identify and explain sustainable soil and pest management practices.

Key Vocabulary
  • monoculture — Growing a single crop over a large area; efficient but vulnerable to pests and disease.
  • salinization — Buildup of salts in soil from evaporation of irrigation water; reduces fertility.
  • contour plowing — Plowing across slopes (along contour lines) to reduce water erosion.
  • integrated pest management (IPM) — Combining biological, cultural, and minimal chemical controls to manage pests.
  • no-till farming — Planting without plowing; reduces erosion and preserves soil structure.
Objectives
  • Identify environmental impacts of industrial agriculture.
  • Explain how salinization and erosion reduce soil productivity.
  • Describe sustainable agricultural practices and their benefits.
How this can be tested
  • Comparing conventional vs. sustainable farming methods.
  • Explaining why contour plowing and terracing reduce erosion.
  • Evaluating IPM as a pest control strategy.

Forestry and Fisheries Management

Learn on this site
Logging Methods

Clear-cutting removes all trees and causes erosion, habitat loss, and stream sedimentation; selective cutting and shelterwood cutting are more sustainable.

Common misconception: Clear-cutting is efficient and economically profitable but has severe ecological impacts.

How it can be tested: Compare logging methods by ecological impact.

Sustainable Forestry

Sustainable forestry maintains biodiversity, old-growth characteristics, and watershed function; certification (FSC) helps consumers identify sustainably harvested wood.

Common misconception: Tree plantations are NOT sustainable forests — they lack biodiversity and ecosystem services of native forests.

How it can be tested: Distinguish tree plantations from sustainable forests; explain FSC certification.

Fisheries Management

Overfishing depletes fish stocks; management tools include catch limits, size limits, gear restrictions, marine protected areas (MPAs), and aquaculture.

Common misconception: Aquaculture (fish farming) can reduce pressure on wild stocks but introduces its own environmental problems (disease, waste, escape of non-native species).

How it can be tested: Evaluate fisheries management strategies and trade-offs of aquaculture.

Key Vocabulary
  • clear-cutting — Removing all trees from an area; maximizes timber yield but causes severe ecological damage.
  • selective cutting — Removing only specific trees while leaving the forest structure mostly intact.
  • marine protected area (MPA) — Designated ocean area where fishing and other activities are restricted.
  • aquaculture — Farming of fish, shellfish, or aquatic plants; can reduce wild-catch pressure.
  • bycatch — Non-target species accidentally caught in fishing gear; major source of unintended mortality.
Objectives
  • Compare logging methods by ecological impact.
  • Explain strategies for sustainable fisheries management.
  • Evaluate aquaculture as a solution to overfishing.
How this can be tested
  • Recommending a logging method for a specific context.
  • Explaining why MPAs help fish populations recover.
  • Weighing pros and cons of aquaculture.

Mining and Urban Land Use

Learn on this site
Mining Impacts

Surface mining (strip mining, open-pit) destroys habitats and creates acid mine drainage; underground mining causes subsidence and releases pollutants. Reclamation can partially restore mined land.

Common misconception: Acid mine drainage forms when sulfide minerals in exposed rock react with air and water, producing sulfuric acid.

How it can be tested: Describe environmental impacts of surface and underground mining and reclamation strategies.

Urban Sprawl

Urban sprawl converts farmland and natural habitat to suburbs; increases impervious surfaces, stormwater runoff, air pollution, and car dependence.

Common misconception: Urban sprawl is often considered environmentally worse than compact urban development per capita.

How it can be tested: Explain how urban sprawl affects land use, water quality, and energy consumption.

Smart Growth and Land Use Planning

Smart growth principles include compact development, transit-oriented design, mixed land use, and green space preservation; they reduce sprawl impacts.

Common misconception: Smart growth benefits both environment and human health, but faces political and economic resistance.

How it can be tested: Identify smart growth strategies and explain their environmental benefits.

Key Vocabulary
  • acid mine drainage — Acidic water formed when sulfide minerals from mining react with air and water; toxic to aquatic life.
  • surface mining — Removing soil and rock to extract near-surface mineral deposits; includes strip and open-pit mining.
  • urban sprawl — Low-density spread of cities into surrounding natural or agricultural land.
  • impervious surface — Hard surfaces (pavement, rooftops) that prevent water infiltration and increase runoff.
  • smart growth — Urban planning approach emphasizing compact, transit-connected, mixed-use development.
Objectives
  • Describe environmental impacts of surface and underground mining.
  • Explain how urban sprawl degrades land and water.
  • Identify smart growth strategies and their benefits.
How this can be tested
  • Comparing surface and underground mining environmental impacts.
  • Explaining why impervious surfaces worsen stormwater problems.
  • Evaluating smart growth policies.

Irrigation and Water Management

Learn on this site
Irrigation Methods

Flood irrigation is cheap but wastes water; drip irrigation is most efficient (90%+ delivery to roots); sprinkler irrigation is intermediate. Irrigation accounts for ~70% of freshwater use.

Common misconception: Drip irrigation saves water but is expensive to install, limiting its use in developing countries.

How it can be tested: Compare irrigation methods by water efficiency and environmental impact.

Water Diversion and Dams

Dams provide hydroelectric power, flood control, and irrigation water but fragment river habitat, block fish migration, trap sediment, and displace communities.

Common misconception: Dams may seem "green" (hydropower) but have major ecological costs downstream and upstream.

How it can be tested: Explain benefits and costs of large dams and water diversion projects.

Water Conservation

Water conservation strategies include pricing reform (true-cost pricing), demand management, water recycling, xeriscaping, and drought-resistant crop breeding.

Common misconception: True-cost pricing can reduce waste but disproportionately affects low-income users if not accompanied by equity measures.

How it can be tested: Identify water conservation strategies and analyze trade-offs.

Key Vocabulary
  • drip irrigation — Delivers water directly to plant roots via tubes; most water-efficient method.
  • flood irrigation — Flooding fields with water; least efficient method with high evaporation loss.
  • dam — Structure that impounds a river; provides benefits but fragments aquatic habitat.
  • xeriscaping — Landscaping using drought-tolerant plants to reduce irrigation needs.
  • true-cost pricing — Charging the full environmental and resource cost of water to encourage conservation.
Objectives
  • Compare irrigation methods by efficiency.
  • Evaluate costs and benefits of large dams.
  • Identify water conservation strategies.
How this can be tested
  • Recommending irrigation methods for different contexts.
  • Analyzing environmental trade-offs of dam construction.
  • Applying conservation strategies to a water scarcity scenario.
