Fossil Fuels

Learn on this site
Coal, Oil, and Natural Gas

Fossil fuels are ancient organic matter compressed over millions of years; combustion releases CO₂, SO₂, NOₓ, and particulates, driving climate change and air pollution.

Common misconception: Natural gas is the cleanest-burning fossil fuel but still emits CO₂ and can leak methane (a more potent greenhouse gas).

How it can be tested: Compare coal, oil, and natural gas by environmental impact and uses.

Environmental Impacts of Extraction

Coal mining destroys habitat (mountaintop removal), oil spills harm marine ecosystems, hydraulic fracturing (fracking) risks groundwater contamination and induces earthquakes.

Common misconception: Fracking itself does not contaminate groundwater in most cases — leaks from poorly cased wells are the main risk.

How it can be tested: Identify environmental risks of each fossil fuel extraction method.

Energy Return on Investment (EROI)

EROI = energy out / energy in; high EROI means more net energy. Fossil fuels historically have high EROI, but it declines as easy reserves are depleted.

Common misconception: EROI for tar sands and shale oil is much lower than for conventional oil.

How it can be tested: Calculate EROI and use it to compare energy sources.

Key Vocabulary
  • fossil fuel — Non-renewable energy formed from ancient organic matter; includes coal, oil, natural gas.
  • combustion — Burning of fuels in oxygen to produce CO₂, H₂O, and energy.
  • fracking (hydraulic fracturing) — Injecting high-pressure fluid into rock to release natural gas or oil.
  • mountaintop removal — Surface mining that blasts off mountain summits to expose coal seams.
  • EROI (energy return on investment) — Ratio of energy produced to energy invested in extraction and processing.
Objectives
  • Compare environmental impacts of coal, oil, and natural gas.
  • Identify extraction risks for each fossil fuel.
  • Calculate and interpret EROI.
How this can be tested
  • Evaluating trade-offs between fossil fuel types.
  • Explaining why fracking is controversial.
  • Using EROI to compare energy sources.

Nuclear Energy

Learn on this site
Nuclear Fission

Nuclear fission splits uranium-235 or plutonium-239 atoms, releasing enormous energy; chain reactions are controlled in a reactor.

Common misconception: Nuclear power produces no CO₂ during operation, but uranium mining and waste storage have environmental costs.

How it can be tested: Explain how nuclear fission produces electricity and identify its advantages and risks.

Nuclear Waste

Spent nuclear fuel is highly radioactive and remains dangerous for thousands of years; no permanent storage solution exists in the U.S.

Common misconception: Low-level waste (gloves, tools) is much less hazardous than high-level waste (spent fuel rods).

How it can be tested: Describe the challenge of nuclear waste management.

Nuclear Accidents

Meltdowns (Chernobyl 1986, Fukushima 2011) can release radioactive material over large areas; risk is low probability but high consequence.

Common misconception: Modern reactor designs are much safer than Chernobyl-era designs.

How it can be tested: Evaluate nuclear energy in terms of safety, waste, and climate benefits.

Key Vocabulary
  • nuclear fission — Splitting of a heavy nucleus (U-235) releasing large amounts of energy.
  • chain reaction — Self-sustaining series of fission events; controlled in reactors, uncontrolled in bombs.
  • radioactive waste — Byproduct of nuclear reactions that emits radiation for thousands of years.
  • meltdown — Overheating of reactor core due to cooling failure; can release radioactive material.
  • half-life — Time for half of a radioactive substance to decay; determines waste storage duration.
Objectives
  • Explain how nuclear fission generates electricity.
  • Describe the nuclear waste challenge.
  • Evaluate nuclear energy trade-offs vs. fossil fuels and renewables.
How this can be tested
  • Comparing nuclear energy to fossil fuels on CO₂ and safety.
  • Explaining why nuclear waste is difficult to manage.
  • Analyzing trade-offs of nuclear expansion.

Renewable Energy Sources

Learn on this site
Solar and Wind

Solar PV converts sunlight to electricity; wind turbines convert kinetic energy. Both are intermittent (dependent on sun/wind availability) and require storage or backup.

Common misconception: Solar and wind produce no emissions during operation but require energy and materials to manufacture.

How it can be tested: Compare solar and wind energy by advantages, limitations, and environmental impacts.

Hydroelectric, Geothermal, and Biomass

Hydroelectric is reliable but requires dams with ecological costs; geothermal taps Earth's heat (limited to geologically active areas); biomass burns organic matter and can be carbon-neutral if managed sustainably.

Common misconception: Biomass can be worse than coal if forests are cleared for fuel crops; sustainability depends on source.

How it can be tested: Evaluate hydroelectric, geothermal, and biomass energy sources.

Energy Efficiency

Energy efficiency reduces demand at the source; more effective per dollar than building new generation. Conservation (behavioral) differs from efficiency (technological).

Common misconception: Efficiency improvements can rebound (Jevons paradox): using less per unit may increase total use.

How it can be tested: Distinguish efficiency from conservation; explain the rebound effect.

Key Vocabulary
  • solar photovoltaic (PV) — Converts sunlight directly to electricity using semiconductor cells.
  • wind turbine — Converts wind kinetic energy to electricity; most cost-effective in high-wind areas.
  • intermittency — Variable output of solar and wind energy depending on weather conditions.
  • geothermal energy — Heat from Earth's interior used for electricity generation or direct heating.
  • energy efficiency — Ratio of useful energy output to total energy input; reducing waste energy.
Objectives
  • Compare renewable sources by reliability, cost, and environmental impact.
  • Explain why energy efficiency is often the most cost-effective strategy.
  • Describe the rebound effect.
How this can be tested
  • Evaluating renewable energy trade-offs for a given location.
  • Calculating efficiency improvements and their effects.
  • Applying Jevons paradox to energy policy.

Energy Policy and the Future

Learn on this site
Energy Mix and Transition

A diversified energy portfolio balances reliability, cost, and environmental impact; transitioning from fossil fuels requires grid modernization, storage, and policy support.

Common misconception: No single renewable source can replace fossil fuels alone — an energy mix is needed.

How it can be tested: Evaluate energy transition strategies and barriers.

Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)

CCS captures CO₂ from power plant emissions and stores it underground; still expensive and unproven at large scale, but could reduce emissions from fossil fuel use.

Common misconception: CCS does not prevent all fossil fuel emissions and is not a substitute for renewable deployment.

How it can be tested: Explain CCS technology and its limitations.

Energy Poverty and Equity

Energy poverty affects ~800 million people lacking electricity; renewable microgrids can provide access without fossil fuel infrastructure.

Common misconception: Energy poverty is primarily a developing-world issue but also affects low-income communities in wealthy nations.

How it can be tested: Discuss equity dimensions of energy access and transition.

Key Vocabulary
  • energy portfolio — Combination of energy sources used to meet electricity demand.
  • carbon capture and storage (CCS) — Technology that captures CO₂ from combustion and stores it underground.
  • smart grid — Electricity network with digital controls to balance supply and demand, integrate renewables.
  • energy poverty — Lack of access to reliable, affordable, modern energy services.
  • feed-in tariff — Policy that pays renewable energy producers a guaranteed price for electricity fed to the grid.
Objectives
  • Explain why a diverse energy mix is needed for a reliable grid.
  • Describe CCS technology and its limitations.
  • Analyze equity issues in energy transitions.
How this can be tested
  • Designing an energy mix for a given region.
  • Evaluating CCS as a climate strategy.
  • Explaining barriers to renewable adoption in developing countries.
