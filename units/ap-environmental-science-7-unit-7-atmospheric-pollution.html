Air Pollutants and Smog

Learn on this site
Primary vs. Secondary Pollutants

Primary pollutants are emitted directly (CO, SO₂, NOₓ, particulates); secondary pollutants form from reactions in the atmosphere (ozone, sulfuric acid, nitric acid).

Common misconception: Tropospheric ozone is a harmful secondary pollutant, unlike stratospheric ozone which protects us.

How it can be tested: Distinguish primary and secondary pollutants and give examples of each.

Photochemical Smog

Photochemical smog forms when NOₓ and VOCs react in sunlight to produce ozone (O₃) and other secondary pollutants; worst on hot, sunny, still days in cities with traffic.

Common misconception: Ground-level ozone and stratospheric ozone are opposite: ground-level harms health; stratospheric ozone protects it.

How it can be tested: Describe conditions that produce photochemical smog and its health effects.

Industrial Smog

Industrial (gray/London) smog forms from SO₂ and particulates in cold, humid conditions; primarily from coal burning.

Common misconception: Industrial smog is associated with older industrial cities; photochemical smog with modern car-dependent cities.

How it can be tested: Compare photochemical and industrial smog by source and conditions.

Key Vocabulary
  • primary pollutant — Pollutant emitted directly from a source into the atmosphere.
  • secondary pollutant — Pollutant formed by chemical reactions among primary pollutants in the atmosphere.
  • photochemical smog — Brown haze formed when NOₓ and VOCs react in sunlight to produce ground-level ozone.
  • volatile organic compound (VOC) — Organic chemicals that evaporate easily and contribute to smog formation.
  • particulate matter (PM) — Tiny solid or liquid particles suspended in air; PM2.5 most harmful to health.
Objectives
  • Distinguish primary and secondary air pollutants.
  • Explain photochemical smog formation and health effects.
  • Compare industrial and photochemical smog.
How this can be tested
  • Identifying pollutants as primary or secondary.
  • Explaining why ground-level ozone is harmful despite being the same molecule as protective stratospheric ozone.
  • Analyzing conditions that worsen smog.

Acid Deposition

Learn on this site
Formation of Acid Rain

SO₂ and NOₓ from fossil fuel combustion react with water vapor to form H₂SO₄ and HNO₃, which fall as acid precipitation (pH < 5.6).

Common misconception: Normal rain is already slightly acidic (pH ~5.6) due to dissolved CO₂; acid rain is significantly more acidic.

How it can be tested: Trace the chemical pathway from SO₂/NOₓ emissions to acid rain formation.

Ecological Effects

Acid rain acidifies lakes (killing acid-sensitive organisms), damages forests, and leaches calcium and other nutrients from soil.

Common misconception: Acidification affects entire ecosystems, not just the most acid-sensitive species.

How it can be tested: Describe ecological impacts of acid deposition on aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems.

Control and Solutions

Scrubbers remove SO₂ from power plant emissions; catalytic converters reduce NOₓ from vehicles; switching to low-sulfur coal or renewables reduces acid rain at the source.

Common misconception: Liming lakes treats symptoms (low pH) but does not address the root cause (emissions).

How it can be tested: Evaluate acid rain control strategies by addressing source vs. symptom.

Key Vocabulary
  • acid deposition — Wet (rain, snow) or dry deposition of acids formed from SO₂ and NOₓ emissions.
  • scrubber — Industrial device that removes SO₂ from power plant emissions before release.
  • catalytic converter — Device in vehicles that converts NOₓ, CO, and VOCs to less harmful gases.
  • buffering capacity — Ability of soil or water to neutralize acid inputs; granite areas have low buffering capacity.
  • liming — Adding lime (CaCO₃) to acidified lakes to raise pH; treats symptoms, not causes.
Objectives
  • Trace acid rain formation from SO₂/NOₓ to deposition.
  • Describe impacts of acid rain on lakes and forests.
  • Evaluate acid rain control technologies.
How this can be tested
  • Explaining the chemistry of acid rain formation.
  • Comparing scrubbers vs. fuel switching as solutions.
  • Analyzing why granite-bedrock areas are more vulnerable to acidification.

Stratospheric Ozone Depletion

Learn on this site
Ozone Layer Function

Stratospheric ozone (O₃) absorbs UV-B radiation, protecting life from DNA damage, skin cancer, and cataracts.

Common misconception: Stratospheric ozone and ground-level ozone are the same molecule but have opposite effects on human health.

How it can be tested: Explain the importance of the stratospheric ozone layer.

CFCs and Ozone Depletion

Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) release chlorine in the stratosphere; one Cl atom can destroy 100,000 ozone molecules. The ozone hole forms over Antarctica in spring.

Common misconception: The ozone hole forms due to ice crystal surfaces in polar stratospheric clouds that accelerate CFC reactions — it's not just from direct CFC emissions.

How it can be tested: Describe the mechanism of CFC-caused ozone depletion.

Montreal Protocol

The Montreal Protocol (1987) phased out CFCs globally; ozone layer is slowly recovering, demonstrating effective international environmental policy.

Common misconception: Ozone recovery is slow because CFCs persist in the stratosphere for decades.

How it can be tested: Explain the success of the Montreal Protocol as a model for global environmental agreements.

Key Vocabulary
  • stratospheric ozone — O₃ layer 15–35 km altitude that absorbs harmful UV-B radiation.
  • chlorofluorocarbon (CFC) — Industrial compound once used in refrigerants and aerosols; destroys ozone in stratosphere.
  • ozone hole — Seasonal area of severe ozone depletion over Antarctica, caused by CFCs.
  • UV-B radiation — Ultraviolet radiation that damages DNA; absorbed by stratospheric ozone.
  • Montreal Protocol — 1987 international treaty that phased out CFCs; considered the most successful environmental treaty.
Objectives
  • Explain how CFCs destroy stratospheric ozone.
  • Describe the Antarctic ozone hole.
  • Evaluate the Montreal Protocol as a policy success.
How this can be tested
  • Explaining the catalytic chain reaction of ozone destruction.
  • Comparing ozone depletion to climate change as international policy challenges.
  • Predicting ozone recovery timeline.

Indoor Air Pollution

Learn on this site
Radon and Carbon Monoxide

Radon (radioactive gas from uranium decay in soil) seeps into buildings and causes lung cancer; CO from incomplete combustion is colorless and odorless, causing poisoning.

Common misconception: Radon is a significant cancer risk even in well-sealed homes; testing and ventilation are the solutions.

How it can be tested: Identify radon and CO as indoor air pollutants and describe their sources and health effects.

VOCs and Sick Building Syndrome

VOCs from paints, furnishings, and cleaning products cause health problems indoors; sick building syndrome describes non-specific symptoms from poor indoor air quality.

Common misconception: Indoor air is often more polluted than outdoor air in developed countries due to synthetic materials and poor ventilation.

How it can be tested: Describe indoor VOC sources and sick building syndrome.

Indoor Pollution in Developing Countries

In developing countries, burning biomass (wood, dung, charcoal) for cooking indoors causes severe air pollution; a leading cause of respiratory disease globally.

Common misconception: Indoor biomass burning kills more people annually than outdoor air pollution in many developing regions.

How it can be tested: Explain indoor biomass combustion as a major global health issue.

Key Vocabulary
  • radon — Radioactive gas produced from uranium decay in soil; seeps into buildings and causes lung cancer.
  • carbon monoxide (CO) — Odorless, colorless gas from incomplete combustion; prevents oxygen transport in blood.
  • sick building syndrome — Health condition caused by poor indoor air quality from chemicals, mold, or poor ventilation.
  • biomass burning — Combustion of wood, dung, or agricultural residues for cooking; major indoor pollutant in developing countries.
  • ventilation — Air exchange that dilutes indoor pollutants; key strategy for improving indoor air quality.
Objectives
  • Identify major indoor air pollutants and their health effects.
  • Explain sick building syndrome.
  • Describe the global health impact of indoor biomass burning.
How this can be tested
  • Comparing indoor vs. outdoor air quality in developed countries.
  • Identifying solutions to indoor air pollution.
  • Analyzing equity dimensions of indoor air pollution globally.
