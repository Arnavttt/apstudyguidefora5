The Greenhouse Effect and Climate

Learn on this site
Natural Greenhouse Effect

Greenhouse gases (H₂O, CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, O₃) trap outgoing infrared radiation, warming Earth to a habitable 15°C average; without it, Earth would be −18°C.

Common misconception: The greenhouse effect itself is natural and essential for life; the problem is human enhancement of it.

How it can be tested: Explain the natural greenhouse effect and distinguish it from the enhanced (human-caused) greenhouse effect.

Enhanced Greenhouse Effect

Human activities (fossil fuel combustion, deforestation, agriculture, cement production) have increased atmospheric CO₂ from 280 ppm (pre-industrial) to over 420 ppm today.

Common misconception: CO₂ is not the most potent greenhouse gas (methane is ~80× stronger per molecule), but it is emitted in the largest quantities.

How it can be tested: Identify human sources of major greenhouse gases and explain why CO₂ is the primary driver of current warming.

Carbon Cycle and Feedback Loops

Positive feedbacks (ice-albedo, permafrost methane release, water vapor) amplify warming; negative feedbacks (increased plant growth, ocean absorption) partially offset it.

Common misconception: Positive feedback does not mean beneficial — it means self-amplifying; ice-albedo feedback is a major amplifier of warming.

How it can be tested: Describe positive and negative climate feedback loops.

Key Vocabulary
  • greenhouse effect — Warming of Earth's surface by greenhouse gases trapping outgoing infrared radiation.
  • greenhouse gas — Gas that absorbs and re-emits infrared radiation (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, H₂O, O₃).
  • albedo — Reflectivity of a surface; ice has high albedo, ocean and forests have low albedo.
  • positive feedback — Self-amplifying process that increases the original change (e.g., ice melts → lower albedo → more warming → more ice melts).
  • carbon sink — Reservoir that absorbs more carbon than it releases (forests, oceans).
Objectives
  • Explain the greenhouse effect and the role of each GHG.
  • Identify human sources of CO₂, CH₄, and N₂O.
  • Describe climate feedback loops.
How this can be tested
  • Distinguishing natural vs. enhanced greenhouse effect.
  • Explaining the ice-albedo feedback loop.
  • Identifying which human activities produce which greenhouse gases.

Evidence and Consequences of Climate Change

Learn on this site
Evidence for Climate Change

Lines of evidence: direct temperature measurements, ice core records, sea level rise, shrinking glaciers/Arctic ice, species range shifts, and phenological changes all confirm warming.

Common misconception: Ice cores provide temperature records going back 800,000 years — long before human thermometers.

How it can be tested: Summarize multiple lines of evidence for current climate change.

Climate Change Impacts

Consequences include: sea level rise (thermal expansion + ice melt), increased extreme weather, ocean acidification (CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃), coral bleaching, biome shifts, and increased disease vectors.

Common misconception: Ocean acidification is separate from temperature rise — it is caused directly by CO₂ dissolving in water, lowering pH.

How it can be tested: Predict consequences of climate change for ecosystems, coastlines, and human society.

Ocean Acidification

Oceans absorb ~25% of human CO₂ emissions; CO₂ dissolves to form carbonic acid, lowering ocean pH (from 8.2 to 8.1 since industrial revolution — a 26% increase in H⁺).

Common misconception: A decrease of 0.1 pH units represents a 26% increase in acidity because pH is logarithmic.

How it can be tested: Explain ocean acidification chemistry and impacts on marine organisms.

Key Vocabulary
  • ice core — Cylindrical sample from polar ice containing trapped air bubbles with ancient atmospheric CO₂ records.
  • sea level rise — Increase in global mean sea level from thermal expansion of water and melting glaciers/ice sheets.
  • ocean acidification — Decrease in ocean pH caused by absorption of atmospheric CO₂.
  • coral bleaching — Expulsion of symbiotic algae from coral when water temperature rises; leads to coral death if prolonged.
  • phenological change — Shift in timing of biological events (flowering, migration) in response to climate change.
Objectives
  • List multiple lines of evidence for climate change.
  • Predict impacts of climate change on ecosystems and coastlines.
  • Explain the chemistry of ocean acidification.
How this can be tested
  • Interpreting CO₂ and temperature graphs from ice cores.
  • Explaining how ocean acidification affects shell-forming organisms.
  • Connecting sea level rise to coastal displacement of human populations.

Mitigation Strategies

Learn on this site
Reducing Emissions

Mitigation reduces GHG emissions by switching to renewables, improving energy efficiency, electrifying transportation, and sustainable land use.

Common misconception: Mitigation does not mean ending climate change — some warming is already locked in; it reduces future harm.

How it can be tested: Identify and evaluate major mitigation strategies for climate change.

Carbon Sequestration

Reforestation, soil carbon storage, ocean fertilization, and direct air capture store CO₂; these supplement (but cannot replace) emission reductions.

Common misconception: Planting trees absorbs CO₂ but takes decades and may not offset emissions if trees burn or die.

How it can be tested: Evaluate carbon sequestration methods and their limitations.

International Climate Policy

Paris Agreement (2015) sets voluntary targets to limit warming to 1.5–2°C above pre-industrial levels; challenges include enforcement, equity between nations, and ambition gap.

Common misconception: The Paris Agreement is voluntary and non-binding — its effectiveness depends on national commitment.

How it can be tested: Explain the Paris Agreement framework and challenges for international climate policy.

Key Vocabulary
  • mitigation — Actions that reduce greenhouse gas emissions or enhance carbon sinks to slow climate change.
  • carbon sequestration — Capturing and storing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere in biological or geological reservoirs.
  • reforestation — Planting trees in deforested areas; removes CO₂ from atmosphere over decades.
  • Paris Agreement — 2015 international accord aiming to limit global warming to 1.5–2°C above pre-industrial levels.
  • net-zero emissions — State where GHG emissions equal removals; the global target to stop climate change.
Objectives
  • Identify major GHG mitigation strategies.
  • Evaluate carbon sequestration methods and limitations.
  • Explain the Paris Agreement and challenges for climate policy.
How this can be tested
  • Comparing mitigation strategies by cost and effectiveness.
  • Evaluating reforestation vs. direct air capture.
  • Analyzing equity issues in the Paris Agreement.

Adaptation and Sustainability

Learn on this site
Climate Adaptation

Adaptation adjusts human systems to cope with actual or expected climate impacts: sea walls, drought-resistant crops, early warning systems, and managed retreat from flood zones.

Common misconception: Adaptation does not stop climate change — it reduces harm from changes that are already occurring or locked in.

How it can be tested: Distinguish mitigation from adaptation and give examples of each.

Sustainable Development

Sustainable development meets present needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs (Brundtland definition); balances economic, social, and environmental goals.

Common misconception: Sustainability is not just environmental — social equity and economic viability are equally important pillars.

How it can be tested: Apply the three pillars of sustainability to evaluate development decisions.

Environmental Justice

Environmental justice recognizes that environmental burdens (pollution, climate impacts) fall disproportionately on low-income and minority communities; solutions must address equity alongside ecology.

Common misconception: Climate change is an environmental justice issue: those least responsible for emissions (poor nations, poor communities) are most vulnerable.

How it can be tested: Explain environmental justice and apply it to climate and pollution case studies.

Key Vocabulary
  • adaptation — Adjusting human systems to minimize harm from climate change impacts that are already occurring.
  • sustainable development — Development that meets present needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet theirs.
  • three pillars of sustainability — Environmental, economic, and social dimensions that must all be balanced.
  • managed retreat — Planned relocation of communities away from high-risk coastal or flood areas.
  • environmental justice — Equitable distribution of environmental benefits and burdens across all communities.
Objectives
  • Distinguish mitigation from adaptation with examples.
  • Apply the Brundtland definition of sustainable development.
  • Explain environmental justice and its relevance to climate change.
How this can be tested
  • Evaluating adaptation strategies for a coastal city.
  • Applying three pillars of sustainability to a development scenario.
  • Analyzing who bears climate change burdens and why.
