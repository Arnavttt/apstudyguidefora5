Louis XIV and French Absolutism

Learn on this site
Theory of Divine Right Monarchy

Absolutist theory held that monarchs derived authority directly from God and were accountable to God alone; Bossuet articulated divine right theory in France.

Common misconception: Absolute monarchs were not unlimited — they were constrained by law, nobility, and practical limits; "absolute" meant supreme, not unlimited.

How it can be tested: Explain divine right theory and the theoretical foundations of absolutism.

Louis XIV's Methods of Control

Louis XIV (r. 1643–1715) centralized power by domesticating the nobility at Versailles, creating a professional bureaucracy, building a large army, and using intendants to control provinces.

Common misconception: The Palace of Versailles was a political instrument for controlling the nobility, not just a luxury project.

How it can be tested: Analyze Louis XIV's methods for centralizing royal authority.

Cultural Power and Wars

Louis XIV used art, architecture, and cultural patronage to project royal glory; his wars expanded France's borders but bankrupted the treasury; he revoked the Edict of Nantes (1685), persecuting Huguenots.

Common misconception: Louis XIV's revocation of the Edict of Nantes was a political and religious decision that drove 200,000 skilled Huguenots to flee France.

How it can be tested: Evaluate the costs and benefits of Louis XIV's foreign policy and religious persecution.

Key Vocabulary
  • absolutism — System of government where a monarch holds supreme, uncontested authority.
  • divine right of kings — Theory that monarchs derive authority directly from God and are answerable only to God.
  • Versailles — Louis XIV's palace outside Paris; center of royal government and cultural display.
  • intendants — Royal officials appointed by Louis XIV to administer provinces, bypassing local nobles.
  • Revocation of the Edict of Nantes (1685) — Louis XIV's cancellation of Huguenot religious rights; drove 200,000 Protestants from France.
Objectives
  • Explain divine right theory and its limits.
  • Analyze Louis XIV's specific methods of centralizing power.
  • Evaluate the consequences of Louis XIV's religious policy.
How this can be tested
  • Long-essay on Louis XIV as the model absolute monarch.
  • Document-based question on divine right theory.
  • Short-answer on Versailles as a political instrument.

Eastern European Absolutism

Learn on this site
Prussia and the Hohenzollerns

Frederick William I built a powerful military state in Prussia; Frederick the Great (r. 1740–1786) was an "enlightened despot" who promoted culture and religious tolerance while maintaining autocratic power.

Common misconception: Enlightened despotism meant applying Enlightenment ideas to governance, not limiting royal power — Frederick the Great was still absolutist.

How it can be tested: Describe Prussian militarism and Frederick the Great's enlightened despotism.

Austria and the Habsburgs

Austria faced challenges from diverse nationalities; Maria Theresa (r. 1740–1780) modernized the state and fought Frederick the Great; her son Joseph II was the most radical enlightened despot, emancipating serfs and tolerating Jews.

Common misconception: Maria Theresa opposed many Enlightenment ideas personally but modernized Austria pragmatically; she was motivated by state power, not ideology.

How it can be tested: Compare Maria Theresa and Joseph II as Habsburg rulers.

Russia under Peter and Catherine

Peter the Great (r. 1682–1725) westernized Russia — built St. Petersburg, modernized the army, and forced nobles to adopt Western customs. Catherine the Great (r. 1762–1796) expanded Russia and patronized Enlightenment culture but strengthened serfdom.

Common misconception: Peter the Great's westernization was selective — he adopted Western technology and administration but not Western liberalism.

How it can be tested: Evaluate Peter and Catherine's westernization programs and their limits.

Key Vocabulary
  • enlightened despotism — Form of absolutism where rulers used Enlightenment ideas to reform government while retaining absolute power.
  • Frederick the Great — Prussian king (r. 1740–1786); model enlightened despot; military genius; tolerated religious diversity.
  • Maria Theresa — Habsburg ruler (r. 1740–1780); modernized Austria; rival of Frederick the Great.
  • Peter the Great — Russian tsar (r. 1682–1725); forced westernization of Russia; built St. Petersburg as a "window to the West."
  • Catherine the Great — Russian empress (r. 1762–1796); expanded Russia; enlightened in culture but strengthened serfdom after Pugachev Revolt.
Objectives
  • Define enlightened despotism and identify its key rulers.
  • Compare Peter the Great and Catherine the Great's westernization programs.
  • Analyze the contradiction between Enlightenment ideals and continued serfdom in Russia.
How this can be tested
  • Comparing enlightened despotism in Prussia, Austria, and Russia.
  • Analyzing why Joseph II's radical reforms mostly failed.
  • Explaining the limits of Peter the Great's westernization.

English Constitutionalism

Learn on this site
English Civil War

Charles I's attempts to rule without Parliament and impose religious uniformity led to civil war (1642–1651); Parliament (Cavaliers vs. Roundheads) and Cromwell's New Model Army defeated the king; Charles I was executed in 1649.

Common misconception: Charles I was executed for treason against his own people — a radical concept that the king was accountable to law.

How it can be tested: Explain the causes of the English Civil War and the significance of Charles I's execution.

The Interregnum and Restoration

Cromwell ruled as Lord Protector (military dictatorship); after his death, Parliament restored the monarchy (Charles II, 1660); Charles II and James II again challenged Parliament's authority.

Common misconception: The Restoration was not a return to pre-war absolutism — Parliament had established its authority and would not fully surrender it.

How it can be tested: Trace the political instability between the Civil War and the Glorious Revolution.

Glorious Revolution and Constitutional Monarchy

Parliament invited William of Orange to replace James II (1688) without bloodshed; the Bill of Rights (1689) established constitutional monarchy, limited royal power, guaranteed parliamentary authority, and protected individual rights.

Common misconception: The Glorious Revolution was not primarily about religion — it was about limiting royal authority and establishing parliamentary supremacy.

How it can be tested: Explain the Glorious Revolution and the principles of the Bill of Rights.

Key Vocabulary
  • English Civil War (1642–1651) — Conflict between King Charles I and Parliament; Parliament won; Charles I executed in 1649.
  • Oliver Cromwell — Parliamentary general and Lord Protector (1653–1658); military dictator after the Civil War.
  • Glorious Revolution (1688) — Bloodless replacement of James II by William of Orange; Parliament asserted supremacy over the monarchy.
  • Bill of Rights (1689) — English law establishing constitutional monarchy, limiting royal power, and protecting parliamentary rights.
  • constitutional monarchy — System where the monarch's power is limited by a constitution and/or parliament.
Objectives
  • Explain the causes of the English Civil War.
  • Analyze the significance of Charles I's execution.
  • Explain the Glorious Revolution and Bill of Rights.
How this can be tested
  • Long-essay comparing English constitutional development to French absolutism.
  • Analyzing the Bill of Rights as a limit on royal power.
  • Tracing the development of parliamentary power 1640–1689.

The Dutch Republic

Learn on this site
Dutch Republic as an Alternative Model

The Dutch Republic (1581–1795) was a federal state governed by merchant oligarchs (regents); it demonstrated that a republic could be stable, prosperous, and tolerant without a strong king.

Common misconception: The Dutch Republic was not democratic; it was governed by an oligarchy of wealthy merchants, not elected representatives.

How it can be tested: Explain the Dutch political model and its significance as an alternative to absolutism.

Dutch Commercial Dominance

The Dutch East India Company (VOC, 1602) was the world's first publicly traded company; Dutch innovations in finance (stock exchange, banking) made Amsterdam the commercial center of Europe in the 17th century.

Common misconception: The Dutch Golden Age was based on commerce and finance, not on overseas territorial empire — trade colonies were enough.

How it can be tested: Explain Dutch commercial and financial innovations.

Religious Toleration in the Dutch Republic

The Dutch Republic practiced relative religious tolerance — not from idealism but from commercial pragmatism; Spinoza, Descartes, and other thinkers found refuge there.

Common misconception: Dutch toleration was practical, not principled — it attracted skilled workers and merchants regardless of religion.

How it can be tested: Analyze Dutch religious tolerance and its economic roots.

Key Vocabulary
  • Dutch Republic — Federal republic (1581–1795) of seven northern provinces; governed by merchant oligarchs (regents).
  • Dutch East India Company (VOC) — World's first publicly traded joint-stock company (1602); built Dutch commercial empire in Asia.
  • regents — Wealthy merchant oligarchs who governed Dutch cities and provinces.
  • Dutch Golden Age — 17th-century period of Dutch cultural, commercial, and artistic achievement.
  • joint-stock company — Business owned by shareholders; spreads risk and raises capital; pioneered by the Dutch VOC.
Objectives
  • Explain the Dutch Republic as an alternative political model.
  • Describe Dutch commercial and financial innovations.
  • Analyze the roots and nature of Dutch religious tolerance.
How this can be tested
  • Comparing the Dutch Republic to French absolutism.
  • Explaining why joint-stock companies enabled global trade.
  • Analyzing Dutch toleration as pragmatic rather than principled.
