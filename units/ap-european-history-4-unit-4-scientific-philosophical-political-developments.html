The Scientific Revolution

Learn on this site
Heliocentric Model

Copernicus (heliocentric theory), Brahe (observational data), Kepler (elliptical orbits), Galileo (telescopic observations), and Newton (laws of gravity) together replaced the Ptolemaic geocentric model.

Common misconception: The Scientific Revolution did not happen overnight — it took over 150 years from Copernicus (1543) to Newton (1687).

How it can be tested: Trace the development of the heliocentric model and identify each scientist's contribution.

Scientific Method

Francis Bacon promoted empiricism and inductive reasoning; René Descartes promoted deductive reasoning and rationalism; together they shaped the modern scientific method of hypothesis, observation, and experimentation.

Common misconception: Bacon and Descartes had opposite approaches — empiricism (observation first) vs. rationalism (reason first) — but both rejected medieval scholastic authority.

How it can be tested: Explain empiricism and rationalism as alternative approaches to scientific knowledge.

Social and Religious Impact

The Scientific Revolution challenged Church authority by demonstrating that natural phenomena followed laws discoverable by reason; it contributed to secularization without immediately being atheist.

Common misconception: Most early scientists (Newton, Galileo) remained religious — the Scientific Revolution did not produce atheism but rather a God who operated through natural laws.

How it can be tested: Analyze the relationship between the Scientific Revolution and religious authority.

Key Vocabulary
  • heliocentrism — Model placing the Sun at the center of the solar system; proposed by Copernicus (1543).
  • empiricism — Philosophical approach holding that knowledge comes from sensory experience and observation.
  • rationalism — Philosophical approach holding that reason, not sense experience, is the primary source of knowledge.
  • scientific method — Systematic procedure of hypothesis, observation, experimentation, and peer review.
  • Newton's laws — Laws of motion and universal gravitation explaining the movement of objects and planets.
Objectives
  • Trace the development of the heliocentric model.
  • Distinguish empiricism from rationalism.
  • Analyze how the Scientific Revolution affected religious authority.
How this can be tested
  • Analyzing a scientific text for empiricist or rationalist reasoning.
  • Explaining how Newton's laws unified terrestrial and celestial mechanics.
  • Connecting the Scientific Revolution to later Enlightenment thought.

The Enlightenment

Learn on this site
Core Enlightenment Ideas

The Enlightenment applied scientific reason to human society, government, and religion; key ideas included reason, natural rights, progress, toleration, and skepticism of traditional authority.

Common misconception: The Enlightenment was not purely secular — many philosophes were deists, not atheists; they believed in a rational God who didn't intervene.

How it can be tested: Identify core Enlightenment ideas and their critique of traditional society.

Key Enlightenment Thinkers

Locke (natural rights, consent of governed), Montesquieu (separation of powers), Voltaire (religious tolerance, free speech), Rousseau (general will, popular sovereignty), Wollstonecraft (women's rights).

Common misconception: Rousseau's general will could justify tyranny of the majority — not purely libertarian; his thought influenced both democracy and totalitarianism.

How it can be tested: Identify each philosophe's key contributions and their political implications.

Encyclopedia and Public Sphere

Diderot's Encyclopedia (1751–1772) systematized Enlightenment knowledge; salons (led by women) and coffeehouses created a public sphere for debate outside Church and state control.

Common misconception: Salons gave educated women influence in the public sphere despite their exclusion from formal political life.

How it can be tested: Explain how the Encyclopedia and salons spread Enlightenment ideas.

Key Vocabulary
  • philosophes — French term for Enlightenment intellectuals who applied reason to criticize society and government.
  • natural rights — Locke's idea that humans possess inherent rights to life, liberty, and property that governments cannot remove.
  • separation of powers — Montesquieu's idea that government should be divided into legislative, executive, and judicial branches.
  • general will — Rousseau's concept of the collective interest of society as a whole; basis for popular sovereignty.
  • deism — Belief in a rational God who created the universe but does not intervene; common among Enlightenment thinkers.
Objectives
  • Identify core Enlightenment ideas.
  • Match each philosophe to their key contribution.
  • Explain how salons and the Encyclopedia spread Enlightenment ideas.
How this can be tested
  • Document-based questions comparing Locke and Rousseau on liberty.
  • Analyzing how Enlightenment ideas influenced the American and French Revolutions.
  • Explaining the role of salons in the public sphere.

The French Revolution

Learn on this site
Causes of the French Revolution

Long-term causes: Enlightenment ideas, social inequality (Estates system), financial crisis from wars. Short-term cause: Louis XVI's bankruptcy and inability to reform taxation.

Common misconception: The French Revolution was not caused by Enlightenment ideas alone — social inequality and financial crisis were equally important triggers.

How it can be tested: Distinguish long-term and short-term causes of the French Revolution.

Phases of the Revolution

Constitutional monarchy (1789–1792) → Republic and Reign of Terror (1793–1794, Robespierre's radical phase) → Directory → Napoleon's coup (1799). Each phase represented radicalization then reaction.

Common misconception: The Reign of Terror was not a perversion of revolutionary ideals but an extreme application of them — defending the Republic through violence.

How it can be tested: Trace the phases of the French Revolution and explain the radicalization process.

Declaration of the Rights of Man

The Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen (1789) proclaimed natural rights, popular sovereignty, and legal equality; inspired by Enlightenment philosophy.

Common misconception: The Declaration was not universally applied — it excluded women and eventually justified the Terror.

How it can be tested: Analyze the Declaration of the Rights of Man as an Enlightenment document and its contradictions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Estates — French social divisions: First (clergy), Second (nobility), Third (commoners); basis of pre-revolutionary society.
  • Declaration of the Rights of Man (1789) — French revolutionary document proclaiming natural rights, popular sovereignty, and legal equality.
  • Reign of Terror (1793–1794) — Radical phase of the French Revolution; Robespierre and Committee of Public Safety executed thousands.
  • Robespierre — Leader of the Committee of Public Safety; orchestrated the Reign of Terror; executed in Thermidorian Reaction.
  • Napoleon Bonaparte — Military general who seized power in 1799 coup; ended the Revolution; became Emperor in 1804.
Objectives
  • Identify causes of the French Revolution by category.
  • Trace the phases of the Revolution.
  • Analyze the Declaration of the Rights of Man.
How this can be tested
  • Long-essay on causes of the French Revolution.
  • Analyzing Robespierre's speech on virtue and terror.
  • Tracing how Enlightenment ideals were applied and distorted in the Revolution.

Napoleon and the Revolutionary Legacy

Learn on this site
Napoleon's Reforms

Napoleon's Napoleonic Code (1804) spread revolutionary principles — legal equality, property rights, religious toleration — across conquered Europe; he also centralized administration and created the lycée system.

Common misconception: Napoleon preserved some revolutionary ideals (legal equality) while abandoning others (democracy) — he was not purely a revolutionary or purely a reactionary.

How it can be tested: Evaluate Napoleon's reforms as continuation and betrayal of revolutionary ideals.

Napoleon's Empire and Downfall

Napoleon conquered most of Europe but overextended; the Continental System failed; the Russian campaign (1812) decimated his army; he was defeated at Waterloo (1815) and exiled to St. Helena.

Common misconception: Napoleon's Russian campaign failed because of guerrilla resistance and the brutal winter — not just French military weakness.

How it can be tested: Explain Napoleon's conquests and the factors leading to his downfall.

Legacy and Congress of Vienna

The Congress of Vienna (1815) sought to restore conservative order; Metternich's principle of legitimacy and balance of power aimed to prevent future revolution; nationalism and liberalism remained suppressed but persistent.

Common misconception: The Congress of Vienna was not simply reactionary — it created a relatively stable European order that prevented major wars for nearly a century.

How it can be tested: Evaluate the Congress of Vienna's principles and long-term effectiveness.

Key Vocabulary
  • Napoleonic Code — Legal code (1804) establishing equal civil rights, property rights, and religious tolerance; spread across Napoleon's empire.
  • Continental System — Napoleon's economic blockade of Britain; failed because it hurt French allies more than Britain.
  • Congress of Vienna (1815) — International conference restoring conservative order after Napoleon; established balance of power.
  • Metternich — Austrian statesman who dominated the Congress of Vienna; champion of conservatism and opponent of liberalism and nationalism.
  • legitimacy — Congress of Vienna principle restoring pre-revolutionary dynasties to their thrones.
Objectives
  • Evaluate Napoleon as both a revolutionary and a conservative figure.
  • Explain the factors leading to Napoleon's defeat.
  • Analyze the Congress of Vienna's principles and legacy.
How this can be tested
  • Long-essay on Napoleon as revolutionary hero or tyrant.
  • Analyzing the Congress of Vienna as conservative reaction.
  • Connecting Napoleonic legacy to 19th-century nationalism.
