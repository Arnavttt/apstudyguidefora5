Mercantilism and Colonial Rivalry

Learn on this site
Mercantilist Theory

Mercantilism held that national wealth depended on accumulating gold and silver through a favorable balance of trade; colonies provided raw materials and captive markets.

Common misconception: Mercantilism was a zero-sum theory — one nation's gain was another's loss, encouraging colonial competition.

How it can be tested: Explain mercantilist theory and its role in driving European colonial competition.

Anglo-French Rivalry

Britain and France competed for colonies in North America (French and Indian War / Seven Years' War, 1756–1763), India, and the Caribbean; Britain emerged dominant.

Common misconception: The Seven Years' War was a world war with fronts in Europe, North America, India, and the Caribbean — often called the first global war.

How it can be tested: Trace Anglo-French colonial rivalry and the significance of the Seven Years' War.

Impact of Colonial Competition

British victory in the Seven Years' War left France bankrupt; British debt led to taxation of American colonists; French humiliation contributed to support for the American Revolution and to France's own revolution.

Common misconception: The Seven Years' War set up both the American and French Revolutions by creating fiscal crises.

How it can be tested: Connect the Seven Years' War to both the American and French Revolutions.

Key Vocabulary
  • mercantilism — Economic theory that national wealth depends on favorable trade balance and accumulation of gold; colonies were essential.
  • Seven Years' War (1756–1763) — Global conflict between Britain and France; Britain gained Canada and dominance in India.
  • balance of trade — Difference between value of exports and imports; mercantilists believed exports should exceed imports.
  • French and Indian War — North American theater of the Seven Years' War (1754–1763); Britain defeated France for Canada.
  • Navigation Acts — English laws requiring colonial trade to be conducted in English ships and through English ports.
Objectives
  • Explain mercantilist theory.
  • Explain the Seven Years' War and its outcomes.
  • Connect colonial competition to revolutionary crises.
How this can be tested
  • Analyzing how mercantilism caused Anglo-French rivalry.
  • Explaining the fiscal consequences of the Seven Years' War for France and Britain.
  • Connecting colonial taxation to the American Revolution.

Social Structures and Revolution

Learn on this site
Old Regime Social Structure

The Old Regime (Ancien Régime) divided French society into Three Estates; the privileged First (Church) and Second (nobility) avoided most taxation; the Third Estate (97% of population) bore the tax burden.

Common misconception: The Old Regime was not uniformly oppressive — some commoners were wealthy, and some nobles were poor; the injustice was structural.

How it can be tested: Analyze the social and fiscal injustices of the Old Regime.

Enlightenment Challenges to Hierarchy

Enlightenment ideas questioning natural hierarchy and divine right — applied to social structure — undermined the ideological justification for the Estates system.

Common misconception: Enlightenment philosophy was both a cause and effect of social tension — ideas and material conditions interacted.

How it can be tested: Analyze how Enlightenment ideas challenged social hierarchy.

Peasant and Urban Conditions

Urban workers (sans-culottes) faced bread shortages; peasants faced feudal obligations on top of taxes; crop failures (1788) created immediate crisis.

Common misconception: The Revolution was not just an intellectual event — hunger, poverty, and fiscal crisis were equally important triggers.

How it can be tested: Connect material conditions (food crisis, debt, taxation) to revolutionary action.

Key Vocabulary
  • Old Regime (Ancien Régime) — Pre-revolutionary French social and political system based on the Three Estates.
  • First Estate — The clergy; exempt from most taxation; about 1% of population.
  • Second Estate — The nobility; largely tax-exempt; about 2% of population.
  • Third Estate — Commoners (97% of population); bore most tax burden; included bourgeoisie, urban workers, and peasants.
  • sans-culottes — Urban working-class radicals in the French Revolution; wore trousers rather than aristocratic breeches.
Objectives
  • Describe the social injustices of the Old Regime.
  • Explain how Enlightenment ideas undermined the Estates system ideologically.
  • Connect material conditions to revolutionary action.
How this can be tested
  • Analyzing primary sources on Old Regime taxation.
  • Explaining the role of the bourgeoisie in the Revolution.
  • Connecting crop failure to bread riots to revolution.

The American Revolution and Its European Impact

Learn on this site
American Revolutionary Ideas

The American Declaration of Independence (1776) applied Locke's natural rights theory to justify revolution; it became a model for European reformers.

Common misconception: The American Revolution was more conservative than the French — it sought to restore rights believed to be violated, not overturn all of society.

How it can be tested: Analyze the Declaration of Independence as an application of Enlightenment ideas.

Impact on Europe

The American Revolution demonstrated that Enlightenment ideals could be applied in practice; inspired French reformers and philosophes; exposed France's military weakness and deepened its financial crisis.

Common misconception: French involvement in the American Revolution was motivated by anti-British strategy, not revolutionary ideology — but it inadvertently helped fuel France's own revolution.

How it can be tested: Explain how the American Revolution influenced European political thinking.

Haitian Revolution

The Haitian Revolution (1791–1804) was the only successful slave revolt in history; enslaved Haitians applied universal rights proclaimed in the Declaration of Rights to demand freedom, challenging the racial limits of the revolution.

Common misconception: The French Declaration of Rights applied to all humans in theory but excluded enslaved people in practice; Haiti's revolutionaries exposed this contradiction.

How it can be tested: Explain the Haitian Revolution as a radical application and critique of French revolutionary ideals.

Key Vocabulary
  • Declaration of Independence (1776) — American document applying Locke's natural rights; justified revolution against Britain.
  • natural rights — Locke's concept of inherent rights to life, liberty, and property; foundational to both American and French revolutions.
  • Haitian Revolution (1791–1804) — Slave revolt in French Saint-Domingue leading to the first Black republic; only successful slave revolution in history.
  • Toussaint L'Ouverture — Leader of the Haitian Revolution; formerly enslaved; became a general who negotiated with France.
  • universal rights — Concept that human rights apply to all people regardless of race, class, or nationality.
Objectives
  • Analyze the Declaration of Independence as an Enlightenment document.
  • Explain how the American Revolution influenced European reformers.
  • Describe the Haitian Revolution and its challenge to European revolutionary ideals.
How this can be tested
  • Comparing the American and French Revolutions as Enlightenment applications.
  • Analyzing the Haitian Revolution's challenge to the universality of rights.
  • Explaining the financial consequences of French support for America.

Women, Gender, and Revolution

Learn on this site
Women in the Revolution

Women participated actively in the French Revolution (Women's March on Versailles, 1789) but were excluded from the Declaration of Rights; Olympe de Gouges's Declaration of the Rights of Woman (1791) challenged this exclusion.

Common misconception: The French Revolution did not improve women's legal status; in some respects the Napoleonic Code restricted women's rights compared to the Old Regime.

How it can be tested: Analyze women's roles in the Revolution and the limits of revolutionary gender equality.

Wollstonecraft's Feminism

Mary Wollstonecraft's A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792) argued that women's apparent inferiority resulted from lack of education, not nature; applied Enlightenment logic to gender equality.

Common misconception: Wollstonecraft did not call for voting rights — her focus was on education and rational equality, not political participation.

How it can be tested: Explain Wollstonecraft's argument for women's education and rational equality.

Limits of Revolutionary Equality

Despite universal rhetoric, both the American and French Revolutions preserved inequality — slavery, women's exclusion, and property requirements for political participation.

Common misconception: The gap between revolutionary ideals and practice was a defining tension that fueled later social movements in the 19th century.

How it can be tested: Analyze the gap between revolutionary ideals of equality and the persistence of inequality.

Key Vocabulary
  • Olympe de Gouges — French feminist who wrote Declaration of Rights of Woman (1791); executed during the Terror.
  • Declaration of the Rights of Woman (1791) — Document by Olympe de Gouges demanding equal rights for women; rejected by the Revolutionary government.
  • Mary Wollstonecraft — English feminist author of A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792); argued women's inferiority was due to lack of education.
  • Women's March on Versailles (1789) — Women's protest march to Versailles demanding bread and the king's return to Paris; key revolutionary event.
  • Napoleonic Code and women — Napoleon's legal code gave husbands legal authority over wives; restricted women's rights relative to Old Regime.
Objectives
  • Analyze women's participation and exclusion in the French Revolution.
  • Explain Wollstonecraft's argument.
  • Evaluate the limits of revolutionary equality for marginalized groups.
How this can be tested
  • Comparing Olympe de Gouges and Wollstonecraft as feminist thinkers.
  • Analyzing the Napoleonic Code's impact on women's rights.
  • Connecting revolutionary inequality to 19th-century social movements.
