New Imperialism

Learn on this site
Causes of New Imperialism

New Imperialism (1870–1914) was driven by: economic (markets, raw materials), political (national prestige, strategic advantage), and ideological (civilizing mission) motives.

Common misconception: Imperialism was not driven by economic factors alone — national pride and Social Darwinism were equally important motivations.

How it can be tested: Identify economic, political, and ideological causes of New Imperialism.

Scramble for Africa

European powers partitioned Africa at the Berlin Conference (1884–1885) without African input; by 1914, only Ethiopia and Liberia remained independent.

Common misconception: The Berlin Conference did not begin colonial conquest of Africa — Europeans had been trading there for centuries; it formalized the rapid interior partition.

How it can be tested: Explain the Berlin Conference and the consequences of African partition.

Imperialism in Asia

Britain dominated India (formal control after 1857 Sepoy Mutiny), while China was subjected to unequal treaties, spheres of influence, and the Boxer Rebellion (1900). Japan industrialized and became an imperial power itself.

Common misconception: China was not formally colonized — it was subjected to informal empire through unequal treaties, extraterritoriality, and economic dominance.

How it can be tested: Compare formal and informal imperialism in Asia using India and China as cases.

Key Vocabulary
  • New Imperialism — European colonial expansion in Africa and Asia c. 1870–1914; driven by industrial capitalism and nationalism.
  • Berlin Conference (1884–1885) — Meeting of European powers to divide Africa; no African representatives; formalized the "Scramble for Africa."
  • civilizing mission — Ideological justification for imperialism claiming Europeans were bringing civilization and progress to "inferior" peoples.
  • Sepoy Mutiny (1857) — Indian uprising against British East India Company; led to direct British Crown rule of India.
  • informal empire — Control through economic and political pressure rather than direct territorial annexation; used in China.
Objectives
  • Identify causes of New Imperialism.
  • Explain the Berlin Conference and African partition.
  • Compare formal and informal imperialism.
How this can be tested
  • Document-based question on justifications for imperialism.
  • Analyzing the Berlin Conference as an example of European power dynamics.
  • Comparing British rule in India with informal imperialism in China.

Social Darwinism and Racism

Learn on this site
Social Darwinism

Herbert Spencer applied Darwin's evolutionary ideas to human society — "survival of the fittest" justified inequality, imperialism, and laissez-faire capitalism as natural outcomes of competition.

Common misconception: Darwin did not support Social Darwinism — Spencer misapplied biological concepts to social policy.

How it can be tested: Define Social Darwinism and explain how it was used to justify imperialism and inequality.

Scientific Racism

Europeans used pseudo-scientific theories of racial hierarchy to justify colonialism; racial classifications were used to rank peoples and cultures from "civilized" to "primitive."

Common misconception: Scientific racism had no valid scientific basis — it was ideology dressed as science, now completely discredited.

How it can be tested: Explain how racial pseudo-science justified imperialism and identify its flaws.

Anti-Imperialism

Some Europeans (J.A. Hobson, Lenin) criticized imperialism as exploitative and economically harmful to colonized peoples; colonized peoples organized resistance movements.

Common misconception: Anti-imperial criticism came from both European intellectuals and colonized peoples — the voices of the colonized are often underrepresented in traditional narratives.

How it can be tested: Identify European critics of imperialism and indigenous resistance movements.

Key Vocabulary
  • Social Darwinism — Misapplication of Darwin's evolution to human society; used to justify inequality and imperialism.
  • Herbert Spencer — British philosopher who coined "survival of the fittest"; applied Darwinian ideas to social competition.
  • scientific racism — Pseudo-scientific racial hierarchy used to justify colonialism and racial inequality.
  • J.A. Hobson — British economist who criticized imperialism as economically harmful in Imperialism: A Study (1902).
  • Social Darwinist argument — Claim that stronger nations "naturally" dominate weaker ones; used to justify colonial conquest.
Objectives
  • Define Social Darwinism and its misapplication of evolution.
  • Explain scientific racism and its use in justifying imperialism.
  • Identify critics of imperialism.
How this can be tested
  • Analyzing a Social Darwinist text for flawed reasoning.
  • Connecting scientific racism to imperialist policy.
  • Evaluating anti-imperial critiques from European and colonial perspectives.

19th-Century Political Movements

Learn on this site
Women's Suffrage

The suffrage movement demanded voting rights for women; British suffragettes (WSPU, Emmeline Pankhurst) used militant tactics; women first won voting rights after World War I.

Common misconception: Suffrage was not the only feminist cause — education, property rights, and legal equality were also central demands.

How it can be tested: Describe the women's suffrage movement and its tactics and achievements.

Labor Movement and Socialism

Trade unions organized workers for better wages and conditions; socialist parties (SPD in Germany, Labour in Britain) entered electoral politics; anarchism challenged all state authority.

Common misconception: The labor movement was more focused on practical improvements (wages, hours) than on Marxist revolution in most cases.

How it can be tested: Trace the development of the labor movement from trade unions to socialist political parties.

Revolutions of 1848

The Revolutions of 1848 spread across Europe as liberal and nationalist movements challenged conservative order; most were suppressed, but they accelerated constitutional reforms and strengthened nationalism.

Common misconception: 1848 revolutions mostly failed in the short term but had long-term impact — they demonstrated the power of nationalist and liberal ideas.

How it can be tested: Explain causes and outcomes of the 1848 revolutions.

Key Vocabulary
  • suffragette — Woman who campaigned militantly for voting rights; especially in Britain with the WSPU.
  • Emmeline Pankhurst — Founder of the Women's Social and Political Union (WSPU); led militant British suffrage campaign.
  • trade union — Worker organization bargaining collectively for wages, hours, and conditions.
  • Social Democratic Party (SPD) — German socialist political party; largest in Reichstag by 1912; pursued reform over revolution.
  • Revolutions of 1848 — Wave of liberal and nationalist revolutions across Europe in 1848; mostly suppressed but influential.
Objectives
  • Describe the women's suffrage movement and its tactics.
  • Trace the development of the labor movement.
  • Explain causes and outcomes of the 1848 revolutions.
How this can be tested
  • Comparing militant and moderate suffrage tactics.
  • Explaining why the SPD pursued reform over revolution.
  • Analyzing why the 1848 revolutions mostly failed.

Culture and Thought in the 19th Century

Learn on this site
Romanticism

Romanticism (c. 1780–1850) reacted against Enlightenment rationalism by emphasizing emotion, nature, nationalism, and individual genius; major figures include Wordsworth, Byron, Delacroix, Beethoven.

Common misconception: Romanticism was not anti-intellectual — it valued different kinds of knowledge, particularly intuition and imagination over pure reason.

How it can be tested: Define Romanticism and contrast it with Enlightenment rationalism.

Realism and Naturalism

Realism (c. 1850–1900) depicted everyday life and social problems honestly; Dickens exposed industrial poverty; Zola's naturalism applied scientific observation to human behavior.

Common misconception: Realist literature was not pessimistic for its own sake — it aimed to expose social problems and drive reform.

How it can be tested: Explain Realism as a response to industrialization.

Darwin and Religion

Darwin's On the Origin of Species (1859) threatened religious accounts of creation; some Christians rejected evolution; others (liberal theology) accommodated it; it accelerated secularization of European thought.

Common misconception: Darwin's theory was controversial in its time but was rapidly accepted by most scientists within a decade.

How it can be tested: Analyze the impact of Darwinian evolution on European religious and intellectual life.

Key Vocabulary
  • Romanticism — Cultural movement (c. 1780–1850) emphasizing emotion, nature, and individual genius over reason.
  • Realism — Artistic movement depicting everyday life and social conditions honestly.
  • Charles Dickens — English novelist who exposed industrial poverty and social inequality; exemplar of social realism.
  • Émile Zola — French novelist; developed naturalism — applying scientific observation to human behavior.
  • secularization — Long-term decline in the public role of religion in European intellectual and political life.
Objectives
  • Define Romanticism and contrast it with Enlightenment values.
  • Explain Realism as a literary response to industrialization.
  • Analyze Darwin's impact on European religious thought.
How this can be tested
  • Comparing Romantic and Realist cultural movements.
  • Analyzing a Dickens passage for social criticism.
  • Explaining how Darwin accelerated secularization.
