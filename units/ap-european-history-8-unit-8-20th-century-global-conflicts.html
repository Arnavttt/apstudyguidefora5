World War I

Learn on this site
Causes of WWI (MAIN)

MAIN causes: Militarism (arms race), Alliance system (Triple Alliance vs. Triple Entente), Imperialism (colonial competition), Nationalism (especially in the Balkans). Immediate trigger: assassination of Franz Ferdinand (June 1914).

Common misconception: The assassination of Franz Ferdinand was the spark, not the underlying cause — the powder keg of militarism, alliances, and Balkan nationalism was already built.

How it can be tested: Identify MAIN causes and explain the assassination as trigger vs. underlying cause.

Total War

WWI was a total war involving mass mobilization, industrial-scale killing (machine guns, artillery, poison gas), home front involvement, and propaganda; trench warfare characterized the Western Front.

Common misconception: Trench warfare was a result of the stalemate between offensive and defensive military technology — no one planned for static trench warfare.

How it can be tested: Explain why WWI became a total war and describe trench warfare on the Western Front.

Consequences of WWI

The Treaty of Versailles (1919) imposed war guilt, reparations, territorial losses, and military restrictions on Germany; it humiliated Germany without destroying its capacity for revenge.

Common misconception: The "stab in the back" myth — that Germany was betrayed from within — was false but politically powerful in Weimar Germany.

How it can be tested: Analyze the Treaty of Versailles and explain how it contributed to German resentment.

Key Vocabulary
  • MAIN — Acronym for WWI causes: Militarism, Alliance system, Imperialism, Nationalism.
  • Triple Alliance — Pre-WWI alliance of Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy.
  • Triple Entente — Pre-WWI alliance of France, Russia, and Britain.
  • Treaty of Versailles (1919) — WWI peace treaty imposing harsh terms on Germany; source of interwar resentment.
  • total war — War involving entire society — military, economy, and civilian population — not just armies.
Objectives
  • Explain MAIN causes of WWI.
  • Describe total war and trench warfare.
  • Analyze the Treaty of Versailles.
How this can be tested
  • Long-essay on causes of WWI.
  • Analyzing Versailles terms as causes of WWII.
  • Explaining the home front as part of total war.

The Russian Revolution and Soviet State

Learn on this site
February and October Revolutions

February 1917: Tsar Nicholas II fell after bread riots and military mutinies; October 1917: Lenin's Bolsheviks seized power with the slogan "Peace, Land, Bread."

Common misconception: The October Revolution was a coup by a small Bolshevik minority, not a popular mass uprising — Lenin exploited chaos and Provisional Government weakness.

How it can be tested: Distinguish the February and October Revolutions in terms of causes, actors, and outcomes.

Lenin and Bolshevik State

Lenin established a one-party state, signed Treaty of Brest-Litovsk (1918) leaving WWI, won the Civil War, and introduced NEP (New Economic Policy) to stabilize the economy.

Common misconception: The NEP was a pragmatic retreat from communism, not an ideological shift — Lenin called it "one step backward to take two steps forward."

How it can be tested: Describe Lenin's policies and how he consolidated Bolshevik power.

Stalinism

Stalin collectivized agriculture (causing famine killing millions), industrialized rapidly through Five-Year Plans, and purged rivals through show trials and the Gulag system.

Common misconception: Stalin's purges eliminated both real enemies and loyal communists — fear, not just ideology, drove the Terror.

How it can be tested: Evaluate Stalin's methods and the human cost of forced modernization.

Key Vocabulary
  • Bolsheviks — Lenin's faction of Russian Marxists; seized power in October 1917; became Communist Party.
  • Soviet — Workers' and soldiers' council; became the basic unit of the Soviet state.
  • Treaty of Brest-Litovsk (1918) — Peace treaty with Germany; Russia gave up vast territory to exit WWI.
  • collectivization — Stalin's forced merging of private farms into collective farms; caused famine killing millions.
  • Gulag — Soviet forced labor camp system; millions imprisoned or died under Stalin.
Objectives
  • Distinguish the February and October Revolutions.
  • Describe Lenin's consolidation of Bolshevik power.
  • Evaluate the costs of Stalinist modernization.
How this can be tested
  • Comparing February and October Revolutions.
  • Analyzing Lenin's NEP as pragmatic retreat.
  • Evaluating Stalin's Five-Year Plans in terms of human cost.

Fascism and World War II

Learn on this site
Rise of Fascism

Fascism rose in Italy (Mussolini, 1922) and Germany (Hitler, 1933) from conditions of economic crisis, national humiliation, weak democracy, and fear of communism; fascism was ultranationalist, anti-communist, and totalitarian.

Common misconception: Fascism was not simply socialism — it rejected both Marxist class struggle and liberal democracy in favor of ultranationalist authoritarian rule.

How it can be tested: Explain the conditions enabling fascism and compare Italian and German fascism.

Nazi Germany

Hitler's NSDAP® used propaganda, legal and violent means to seize power; the Nuremberg Laws (1935) stripped Jews of citizenship; Kristallnacht (1938) accelerated persecution toward genocide.

Common misconception: Nazism did not become genocidal overnight — it escalated from discrimination to segregation to mass murder over a decade.

How it can be tested: Trace the escalation of Nazi persecution from 1933 to the Holocaust.

World War II and the Holocaust

WWII began with German invasion of Poland (1939); Holocaust killed 6 million Jews and 5–6 million others; war ended with atomic bombs on Japan (1945) and Soviet victory in the East.

Common misconception: The Holocaust was not a secret — it required widespread participation and knowledge across occupied Europe.

How it can be tested: Explain the Holocaust as an intentional genocide enabled by Nazi ideology and state power.

Key Vocabulary
  • fascism — Ultranationalist, authoritarian ideology rejecting liberalism and communism; promoted in Italy by Mussolini.
  • Mussolini — Italian fascist dictator (1922–1943); first fascist leader to gain power in Europe.
  • Nuremberg Laws (1935) — German laws stripping Jews of citizenship and prohibiting marriage with non-Jews.
  • Holocaust — Systematic Nazi genocide of 6 million Jews and millions of others (Roma, disabled, political opponents).
  • Weimar Republic — German democratic government (1919–1933); undermined by economic crisis, political extremism, and weak institutions.
Objectives
  • Explain the conditions enabling the rise of fascism.
  • Trace Nazi persecution from 1933 to genocide.
  • Analyze the Holocaust as organized state genocide.
How this can be tested
  • Long-essay on why Weimar Germany fell to Hitler.
  • Analyzing the Nuremberg Laws as a step toward genocide.
  • Comparing Italian and German fascism.

The Interwar Period

Learn on this site
Great Depression

The Great Depression (1929–1939) caused mass unemployment across Europe; governments responded with austerity (worsening it) or Keynesian stimulus; it destabilized democracies and boosted extremist parties.

Common misconception: The Great Depression was global, not just American — European banks and industries were tightly connected to U.S. markets.

How it can be tested: Explain how the Great Depression affected European politics.

Appeasement

Britain and France appeased Hitler at Munich (1938), giving him the Sudetenland; this failed to prevent war; Chamberlain's "peace in our time" was discredited by Germany's invasion of the rest of Czechoslovakia (1939).

Common misconception: Appeasement was not cowardice — it reflected genuine desire to avoid another catastrophic war and belief that Hitler's demands were limited.

How it can be tested: Evaluate appeasement as a policy and explain why it failed.

Soviet-Nazi Pact

The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact (1939) between the USSR and Germany shocked the world; it included secret protocols dividing Eastern Europe; it enabled Germany to invade Poland without Soviet interference.

Common misconception: The Soviet-Nazi Pact was a pragmatic, not ideological, agreement — both Stalin and Hitler planned to eventually betray each other.

How it can be tested: Explain the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact and its consequences.

Key Vocabulary
  • Great Depression — Global economic crisis beginning 1929; caused mass unemployment and political instability.
  • appeasement — British/French policy of making concessions to Hitler to avoid war; failed at Munich (1938).
  • Munich Agreement (1938) — Britain and France granted Hitler the Sudetenland to preserve peace; discredited by Hitler's subsequent aggression.
  • Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact (1939) — Soviet-Nazi non-aggression pact with secret protocols dividing Eastern Europe.
  • Keynesian economics — Economic theory advocating government spending to stimulate economy during depression.
Objectives
  • Explain the Great Depression's impact on European politics.
  • Evaluate appeasement as a rational policy choice.
  • Explain the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact and its consequences.
How this can be tested
  • Analyzing Munich Agreement as rational or naive.
  • Explaining how the Great Depression boosted fascism.
  • Predicting consequences of the Soviet-Nazi Pact.
