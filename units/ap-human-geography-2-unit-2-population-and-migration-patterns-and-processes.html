Population Distribution and Density

Learn on this site
Arithmetic vs. Physiological Density

Arithmetic (crude) density = total population ÷ total land area. Physiological density = total population ÷ arable (farmable) land — a better measure of agricultural pressure. Egypt has low arithmetic density but extremely high physiological density because only the Nile Valley is habitable.

Common misconception: Students confuse arithmetic density (total land) with physiological density (arable land). Physiological density reveals more about food security pressure.

How it can be tested: MCQ presents population and land data and asks which density measure best reveals agricultural stress on a country.

Factors Affecting Population Distribution

Populations cluster in areas with favorable physical geography: flat terrain, mild climate, fertile soil, fresh water access — these are ecumene (permanently inhabited areas). Extreme environments — deserts, high mountains, polar regions — are sparsely populated non-ecumene.

Common misconception: Physical geography limits but does not fully determine distribution — Tokyo exists in an earthquake zone; Amsterdam is below sea level. Human factors (economic opportunity, governance) also drive distribution.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to explain why a specific region is densely or sparsely populated using both physical and human factors.

Population Pyramids

A population pyramid graphs the age-sex structure of a population. A wide base and narrow top indicate high birth and death rates (Stage 2 DTM). A column-shaped pyramid indicates low birth and death rates (Stage 4). An inverted pyramid (narrow base) indicates below-replacement fertility (Stage 5).

Common misconception: Students read pyramids incorrectly — the LEFT side is always MALE; the RIGHT is FEMALE. A wide cohort does not mean a high proportion of elderly; it means many people in that age group.

How it can be tested: MCQ shows a pyramid and asks you to identify the DTM stage, likely growth trajectory, or a policy challenge the country faces.

Key Vocabulary
  • arithmetic density — Total population divided by total land area
  • physiological density — Total population divided by arable land — measures agricultural pressure
  • ecumene — Permanently inhabited areas of Earth's surface
  • population pyramid — Bar graph showing age and sex distribution of a population
  • dependency ratio — Ratio of non-working (young + elderly) to working-age population
Objectives
  • Distinguish arithmetic from physiological density and explain when physiological density is more informative
  • Identify physical and human factors that explain population clustering
  • Interpret a population pyramid to identify demographic stage and policy challenges
How this can be tested
  • Calculate physiological density from given data and explain what a high value means
  • Describe what a wide base vs. a narrow base on a population pyramid indicates
  • Explain why Egypt has low arithmetic density but high physiological density
Practice before moving on
  • Compare population pyramids of two countries at different DTM stages and list three differences
  • Look up physiological density data for three countries and explain what the variation reveals

The Demographic Transition Model

Learn on this site
The DTM and Its Five Stages

The Demographic Transition Model (DTM) describes how populations shift from high birth and death rates to low birth and death rates as countries industrialize. Stage 1: high CBR, high CDR, stable population. Stage 2: CDR drops (medical advances), CBR stays high — population EXPLODES. Stage 3: CBR drops as urbanization, education, and women's rights increase. Stage 4: low CBR and CDR, stable. Stage 5: below-replacement fertility, population decline.

Common misconception: Students confuse which rate drops first — the DEATH rate drops first in Stage 2 (medical/sanitation advances), not the birth rate. Birth rate drops later in Stage 3.

How it can be tested: MCQ presents a country's vital rates and asks which DTM stage it is in; FRQ asks you to explain the cause of rate changes.

Natural Increase Rate and Doubling Time

Natural Increase Rate (NIR) = CBR − CDR (expressed as a %). Population doubles in approximately 70 ÷ NIR years (Rule of 70). A country with NIR of 2% doubles in ~35 years. NIR peaks in Stage 2 when CDR falls sharply but CBR remains high.

Common misconception: NIR does not account for migration — it only measures births minus deaths. Total population change also includes net migration.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to calculate NIR from CBR and CDR data, or apply the Rule of 70 to estimate doubling time.

Epidemiological Transition Model

The Epidemiological Transition Model (ETM) complements the DTM by tracking how causes of death change as countries develop. Stage 2: infectious/parasitic diseases dominate. Stages 3–4: chronic diseases (heart disease, cancer) dominate as people live longer. Stage 5: diseases of affluence and re-emerging diseases (antibiotic resistance).

Common misconception: Students forget the ETM is about CAUSES OF DEATH, not about birth rates. It explains why death rates fall in DTM Stage 2 (infectious disease control).

How it can be tested: FRQ may ask you to link the ETM to the DTM, explaining how controlling infectious disease drives the CDR decline in Stage 2.

Key Vocabulary
  • crude birth rate — Number of births per 1,000 people per year
  • crude death rate — Number of deaths per 1,000 people per year
  • natural increase rate — CBR minus CDR — the rate of population growth excluding migration
  • total fertility rate — Average number of children born to a woman over her lifetime
  • replacement-level fertility — TFR of ~2.1 needed to maintain a stable population without migration
Objectives
  • Describe the five stages of the DTM and the vital rate changes at each stage
  • Calculate NIR using CBR and CDR data and apply the Rule of 70
  • Explain how the Epidemiological Transition Model complements the DTM
How this can be tested
  • Identify a country's DTM stage from given CBR, CDR, and NIR data
  • Explain why CDR falls before CBR in the transition from Stage 1 to Stage 2
  • Describe the policy challenges a Stage 4 country faces due to an aging population
Practice before moving on
  • Plot CBR, CDR, and NIR for three countries at different DTM stages on the same graph
  • Research Japan's TFR and identify its DTM stage; explain one consequence for that country

Types and Causes of Migration

Learn on this site
Push and Pull Factors

Migration is driven by push factors (forces driving people away from origin — poverty, conflict, environmental disaster) and pull factors (forces attracting people to destination — jobs, safety, family). Ravenstein's Laws (1885) describe migration patterns: most migrants move short distances, step-migrate through urban hierarchy, and are young adults.

Common misconception: Students confuse push and pull by listing vague factors. Always ask: does this force REPEL from origin (push) or ATTRACT to destination (pull)?

How it can be tested: MCQ presents a migration scenario and asks you to classify the factors as push, pull, or intervening obstacles.

Types of Migration

Voluntary migration: individuals choose to move — economic migrants, lifestyle migration. Forced migration: people are compelled to move — refugees (cross international borders), internally displaced persons (IDPs, move within home country), asylum seekers. Internal migration: within a country — rural-to-urban. International migration: across national borders.

Common misconception: Refugees cross INTERNATIONAL borders — IDPs are displaced within their own country. Students often confuse these two.

How it can be tested: FRQ may ask you to distinguish refugees from IDPs and explain the legal protections (or lack thereof) each group receives.

Intervening Obstacles and Opportunities

Intervening obstacles are barriers that discourage migration: physical (mountains, oceans), cultural (language, discrimination), economic (cost of moving), political (border controls). Intervening opportunities are attractive alternatives encountered en route — they can stop a migrant before reaching the original destination.

Common misconception: Intervening opportunities explain why migrants often stop short of their intended destination when they find a suitable place along the way.

How it can be tested: MCQ may ask why migration from rural Mexico to Chicago stops in Texas cities — Texas provides intervening opportunities.

Key Vocabulary
  • push factor — A condition at the origin that drives people to leave — poverty, conflict, natural disaster
  • pull factor — A condition at the destination that attracts migrants — employment, safety, family networks
  • refugee — Person forced to cross an international border due to persecution, conflict, or disaster
  • IDP — Internally Displaced Person — forced from home but remains within home country borders
  • intervening obstacle — A barrier that discourages or prevents migration between origin and destination
Objectives
  • Classify migration factors as push, pull, or intervening obstacles
  • Distinguish refugees from IDPs and explain the difference in legal status
  • Apply Ravenstein's Laws to predict migration patterns from a given scenario
How this can be tested
  • Identify push and pull factors from a described migration case study
  • Explain why most international migrants move to neighboring countries
  • Describe how chain migration sustains a migration flow over time
Practice before moving on
  • Map the push and pull factors for Syrian refugee migration since 2011
  • Explain how Ravenstein's Laws apply to rural-to-urban migration in India

Effects of Migration on Origin and Destination

Learn on this site
Brain Drain and Remittances

Brain drain: emigration of highly educated and skilled workers from developing countries to developed ones, reducing human capital at origin. Remittances: money sent by migrants back to their home country — for many developing nations, remittances exceed foreign direct investment and foreign aid.

Common misconception: Brain drain is not always permanent — brain circulation describes return migration of skilled workers who bring back capital and skills.

How it can be tested: FRQ may ask you to evaluate whether emigration overall helps or hurts a developing country given both brain drain and remittances.

Cultural Effects of Migration

Migrants bring cultural traits — language, food, religion, music — to destination countries, creating cultural diffusion and diaspora communities. At origin, out-migration can reduce cultural vitality. Destination countries experience acculturation (cultural change through contact), assimilation (adopting host culture), and sometimes nativist backlash.

Common misconception: Assimilation and acculturation are not the same: acculturation = change through contact (retain home identity); assimilation = full adoption of host culture (lose home identity).

How it can be tested: MCQ may distinguish between cultural diffusion types: relocation diffusion (migrants carry culture physically) vs. expansion diffusion (ideas spread through networks).

Demographic Effects of Migration

Immigration typically increases fertility rates at destination (immigrants often have higher TFR than native population, at least initially). Emigration of young adults reduces birth rates and workforce at origin. Immigration is increasingly used by Stage 4/5 countries to offset aging populations and labor shortages.

Common misconception: Students assume immigration always increases population at destination proportionally. Immigration also changes age structure — working-age immigrants shift the dependency ratio.

How it can be tested: AP® FRQ asks how immigration affects demographic indicators in both origin and destination countries.

Key Vocabulary
  • brain drain — Emigration of highly skilled or educated workers from a country, reducing its human capital
  • remittances — Money sent by migrants back to their home country — a major income source for developing nations
  • diaspora — Community of people living outside their homeland who maintain cultural connection to it
  • acculturation — Cultural change resulting from contact between two cultures — partial adoption of new traits
  • assimilation — Process by which an immigrant group fully adopts the culture of the host society
Objectives
  • Explain the concept of brain drain and identify a country severely affected by it
  • Describe how remittances benefit origin countries and their limitations
  • Distinguish between acculturation and assimilation with examples
How this can be tested
  • Evaluate whether emigration is net positive or net negative for a developing country
  • Explain how immigration affects the age structure and dependency ratio of a destination country
  • Describe cultural effects of migration on both origin and destination regions
Practice before moving on
  • Research the top remittance-receiving countries and explain what this reveals about migration flows
  • Find an example of cultural diffusion through migration in your own community
