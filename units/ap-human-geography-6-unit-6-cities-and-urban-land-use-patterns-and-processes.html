Urban Origins and World Cities

Learn on this site
Origins of Urbanization

Cities first emerged in river valley civilizations (Mesopotamia, Nile, Indus, Huang He) due to agricultural surplus, trade, and administrative needs; urbanization accelerated with industrialization.

Common misconception: Early cities were not primarily centers of manufacturing—they were administrative, religious, and trade centers.

How it can be tested: FRQ may ask you to explain conditions that gave rise to early cities using specific hearths as evidence.

World Cities and Global Urban Hierarchy

World cities (global cities) like New York, London, and Tokyo serve as command-and-control centers of the global economy, concentrating finance, media, and corporate headquarters.

Common misconception: A world city is defined by its global economic function, not simply its population size—Shanghai is large but debates persist about its world city status.

How it can be tested: MCQ may ask you to identify characteristics of world cities or rank cities in a global hierarchy.

Primate Cities and Urban Primacy

A primate city is at least twice the size of the next largest city in a country and dominates the national economy, culture, and politics (e.g., Bangkok in Thailand, Paris in France).

Common misconception: Not all large capital cities are primate cities—the U.S. has no primate city because no single metro dominates as decisively as Bangkok dominates Thailand.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to explain consequences of urban primacy for national development.

Key Vocabulary
  • World city
  • Primate city
  • Urban primacy
  • Global city hierarchy
  • Urbanization
Objectives
  • Explain geographic conditions that supported early city formation.
  • Describe characteristics and functions of world/global cities.
  • Analyze consequences of urban primacy for national development.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests definitions and examples of world cities and primate cities.
  • FRQ uses specific cities to ask about global economic roles.
  • Short-answer connects primate city dominance to regional inequality.
Practice before moving on
  • List 5 world cities and justify each using economic criteria.
  • Research Bangkok as a primate city—what share of Thailand's GDP does it produce?

Urban Land Use Models

Learn on this site
Concentric Zone Model (Burgess)

The Concentric Zone Model shows a city organized in rings from the CBD outward: transition zone (industry/poor housing), working-class homes, middle-class homes, and commuter zone.

Common misconception: The model was based on Chicago in the early 20th century—it fits older industrial cities poorly and fails for newer polycentric cities.

How it can be tested: MCQ shows a concentric ring diagram and asks you to identify zones or match the model to a city type.

Sector Model (Hoyt) and Multiple Nuclei Model (Harris and Ullman)

The Sector Model shows pie-slice shaped sectors of land use radiating from the CBD along transportation routes; the Multiple Nuclei Model recognizes multiple growth centers rather than one CBD.

Common misconception: The Multiple Nuclei Model does not assume that all cities began from a single CBD—it acknowledges that cities can develop from several nuclei simultaneously.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to match a city's layout to the appropriate model and justify your choice with evidence.

Latin American, Southeast Asian, and African City Models

Alternative urban models reflect different development paths: the Latin American city model shows a spine of commercial development and elite residential areas extending from the CBD; Southeast Asian cities lack a clear CBD and have colonial port zones.

Common misconception: Western land use models do NOT apply universally—using Burgess or Hoyt to describe cities in the Global South is a common error.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to compare city structures across world regions using the appropriate model for each.

Key Vocabulary
  • Concentric Zone Model
  • Sector Model
  • Multiple Nuclei Model
  • CBD
  • Latin American city model
Objectives
  • Describe each urban model and identify the city type it best represents.
  • Explain why Western models do not apply to cities in the Global South.
  • Apply the correct model to a described city and justify the choice.
How this can be tested
  • Diagram interpretation of city models is heavily tested in MCQ.
  • FRQ asks you to apply or critique a model using a real city.
  • Comparative questions match models to city descriptions.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw all three Western models side-by-side with labeled zones.
  • Research São Paulo and identify which model best fits its structure.

Suburbanization, Edge Cities, and Urban Sprawl

Learn on this site
Suburbanization and White Flight

Suburbanization accelerated in the U.S. after WWII due to federal highway construction, FHA mortgage subsidies, and preference for single-family homes; white flight describes the migration of white middle-class residents from cities to suburbs as minority populations grew.

Common misconception: White flight was enabled by government policy (redlining, discriminatory FHA loans)—it was not simply individual preference.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to explain structural causes of suburbanization beyond personal preference.

Edge Cities and Boomburbs

Edge cities are suburban nodes with significant office, retail, and employment (e.g., Tysons Corner, VA); boomburbs are rapidly growing suburban cities that have crossed 100,000 population while maintaining suburban character.

Common misconception: Edge cities are not simply large malls—they function as secondary CBDs with significant employment concentration.

How it can be tested: MCQ and FRQ test definitions of edge cities and their economic functions.

Urban Sprawl and Smart Growth

Urban sprawl is low-density, automobile-dependent development spreading outward from a city core; smart growth policies counter sprawl with mixed-use zoning, transit-oriented development, and infill construction.

Common misconception: Smart growth is not anti-growth—it seeks to concentrate growth in existing urban areas rather than eliminating development entirely.

How it can be tested: FRQ evaluates pros and cons of sprawl and asks for smart growth solutions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Suburbanization
  • White flight
  • Redlining
  • Edge city
  • Smart growth
Objectives
  • Explain government policies that enabled suburbanization in the U.S.
  • Distinguish edge cities from traditional suburbs.
  • Evaluate smart growth as a response to urban sprawl.
How this can be tested
  • FRQ asks for explanation of suburbanization causes beyond preference.
  • MCQ tests edge city definitions and examples.
  • Policy evaluation questions assess smart growth strategies.
Practice before moving on
  • Research one federal housing policy (FHA, GI Bill, Interstate Highway Act) and its spatial impact.
  • Identify two edge cities near a major metropolitan area using a map.

Urban Challenges: Inequality, Gentrification, and Sustainability

Learn on this site
Gentrification

Gentrification occurs when higher-income residents move into low-income urban neighborhoods, renovating housing and attracting businesses, but displacing existing lower-income residents.

Common misconception: Gentrification is not simply neighborhood improvement—displacement of long-term residents is a defining and contested consequence.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to evaluate both benefits (investment, reduced crime) and harms (displacement) of gentrification.

Segregation and Inequality

Residential segregation by race and income persists in U.S. cities through discriminatory housing policies (redlining), exclusionary zoning, and income inequality; hyper-segregation concentrates poverty and limits social mobility.

Common misconception: Segregation is not just historical—contemporary zoning and lending practices continue to reinforce spatial inequality.

How it can be tested: FRQ may ask you to explain the geographic consequences of residential segregation on access to schools, jobs, and services.

Sustainable Urban Development

Sustainable cities reduce carbon emissions through transit investment, green building, urban green space, compact development, and renewable energy; the UN Sustainable Development Goals include targets for inclusive, safe, and resilient cities.

Common misconception: Sustainable urban development is not exclusively about environment—social equity (affordable housing, access to services) is equally central.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to evaluate urban sustainability initiatives and their effectiveness in reducing inequality and environmental impact.

Key Vocabulary
  • Gentrification
  • Displacement
  • Redlining
  • Exclusionary zoning
  • Sustainable city
Objectives
  • Explain geographic causes and consequences of gentrification.
  • Analyze how past policies created persistent residential segregation.
  • Evaluate strategies cities use to achieve environmental and social sustainability.
How this can be tested
  • FRQ evaluates gentrification with benefit/harm framework.
  • MCQ tests definitions of redlining and exclusionary zoning.
  • Policy questions assess sustainable urban development strategies.
Practice before moving on
  • Research one gentrifying neighborhood and document evidence of change.
  • Identify one city recognized for sustainability and list three specific policies it uses.
