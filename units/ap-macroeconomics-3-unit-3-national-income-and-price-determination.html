Aggregate Demand

Learn on this site
The AD Curve and Its Slope

Aggregate demand (AD) is the total spending on domestic output at various price levels: AD = C + I + G + NX. The AD curve slopes downward because a higher price level reduces real wealth (wealth effect), raises interest rates reducing investment (interest rate effect), and makes exports more expensive (exchange rate effect).

Common misconception: The AD curve slopes down for different reasons than a regular demand curve — it is NOT because people buy less at higher prices. The three effects explain why a higher price level reduces total spending.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks which effect explains the downward slope of AD; FRQ asks you to explain a shift in AD.

Shifts of the Aggregate Demand Curve

AD shifts right when C, I, G, or NX increase: higher consumer confidence, business optimism, government spending increases, lower taxes, or weaker currency. AD shifts left when these decrease. Government spending and tax changes are the tools of fiscal policy.

Common misconception: A change in the price level causes movement ALONG the AD curve; a change in spending components (C, I, G, NX) shifts the AD curve.

How it can be tested: FRQ frequently asks you to draw and shift the AD/AS diagram.

The Multiplier Effect

An initial change in spending (ΔG or ΔC) produces a larger change in GDP through the spending multiplier. Spending multiplier = 1 / MPS = 1 / (1 − MPC). A $100 increase in government spending multiplies through the economy as each recipient spends a fraction (MPC) of their income.

Common misconception: The multiplier applies to ALL autonomous spending changes (G, I, C, NX) — students sometimes think only government spending has a multiplier.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to calculate the multiplier, the change in GDP, or the change in spending needed to close a gap.

Key Vocabulary
  • aggregate demand (AD) — Total spending on domestic output (C+I+G+NX) at various price levels
  • wealth effect — Higher price level reduces real wealth, causing households to spend less
  • spending multiplier — 1/MPS — the ratio by which an initial spending change is magnified in total GDP
  • MPC — Marginal propensity to consume — the fraction of additional income spent on consumption
  • MPS — Marginal propensity to save — the fraction of additional income saved; MPC + MPS = 1
Objectives
  • Explain the three reasons the AD curve slopes downward.
  • Identify what shifts the AD curve left or right.
  • Calculate the spending multiplier and the resulting change in GDP.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ identifies which AD effect explains the downward slope.
  • FRQ draws and shifts the AD/AS model.
  • FRQ calculates multiplier, change in GDP, or spending needed to close gap.
Practice before moving on
  • If MPC = 0.8, calculate the spending multiplier and the GDP impact of a $50 billion increase in G.
  • List three factors that shift AD rightward.

Aggregate Supply

Learn on this site
Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)

The SRAS curve is upward sloping — at higher price levels, firms are willing to produce more because input costs (wages, resource prices) are sticky in the short run. SRAS shifts when input costs (wages, raw material prices) change, productivity changes, or government regulations change.

Common misconception: SRAS is upward sloping because input prices (especially wages) do NOT immediately adjust with the price level in the short run. If input prices adjusted instantly, the SRAS would be vertical.

How it can be tested: MCQ and FRQ ask you to identify what shifts SRAS and in which direction.

Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)

The LRAS curve is vertical at the economy's potential output (full employment GDP). In the long run, wages and prices are fully flexible — output returns to potential regardless of the price level. The LRAS shifts only when productive capacity changes (technology, resources, institutions).

Common misconception: LRAS is vertical because in the long run, all prices — including wages — are flexible. The economy always returns to potential GDP; only the price level changes.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to shift LRAS in response to long-run growth factors.

Short-Run vs. Long-Run Equilibrium

Short-run macroeconomic equilibrium is where AD intersects SRAS. Long-run equilibrium is where AD, SRAS, and LRAS all intersect (at potential GDP). If the economy is in short-run equilibrium below LRAS, wages will eventually fall, shifting SRAS right until long-run equilibrium is restored.

Common misconception: Self-correction through wage adjustment is slow — this is why Keynes famously said "in the long run we are all dead," arguing for active fiscal policy rather than waiting for self-correction.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to show short-run and long-run equilibrium on an AD/AS graph.

Key Vocabulary
  • SRAS — Short-run aggregate supply curve; upward sloping because input prices are sticky
  • LRAS — Long-run aggregate supply curve; vertical at potential GDP where all prices are flexible
  • wage stickiness — Wages adjust slowly downward in recessions, keeping SRAS from shifting quickly
  • potential GDP — The output level at full employment; where LRAS is located
  • self-correction — The long-run process by which wages fall during a recession, shifting SRAS right to restore potential GDP
Objectives
  • Explain why the SRAS curve is upward sloping.
  • Explain why the LRAS curve is vertical.
  • Describe the self-correction mechanism from a recessionary gap.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ identifies SRAS and LRAS shifters.
  • FRQ draws and shifts AD/AS; labels SR and LR equilibrium.
  • FRQ traces the self-correction process through wage adjustment.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw an AD/AS diagram showing a recessionary gap and trace the self-correction mechanism.
  • List three events that shift SRAS left (supply shock).

Fiscal Policy

Learn on this site
Expansionary Fiscal Policy

Expansionary fiscal policy (used during recessions) involves increasing government spending (G) and/or decreasing taxes (T) to shift AD right and close a recessionary gap. It increases real GDP and the price level in the short run.

Common misconception: Expansionary fiscal policy shifts AD right — the SRAS does NOT shift in response to fiscal policy. Students sometimes incorrectly shift both curves.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to draw expansionary fiscal policy on an AD/AS graph.

Contractionary Fiscal Policy

Contractionary fiscal policy (used to reduce inflation) involves decreasing G and/or increasing T to shift AD left and close an inflationary gap. It reduces the price level (or slows inflation) but also reduces output.

Common misconception: Contractionary fiscal policy is politically unpopular because it reduces spending or raises taxes — governments rarely implement it willingly.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to identify the correct fiscal policy tool for a given economic condition.

Automatic Stabilizers and Crowding Out

Automatic stabilizers are fiscal mechanisms that respond to the business cycle without legislation: unemployment insurance rises in recessions (expansionary) and falls in expansions (contractionary). Crowding out occurs when government borrowing raises interest rates, reducing private investment.

Common misconception: Automatic stabilizers reduce the multiplier's effect — they dampen both expansions and recessions. They reduce cyclical swings but cannot eliminate them.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to explain crowding out and why it reduces the effectiveness of fiscal policy.

Key Vocabulary
  • expansionary fiscal policy — Increasing G or decreasing T to shift AD right; used to close a recessionary gap
  • contractionary fiscal policy — Decreasing G or increasing T to shift AD left; used to close an inflationary gap
  • automatic stabilizers — Fiscal mechanisms (unemployment insurance, progressive taxes) that stabilize the economy without legislation
  • crowding out — Government borrowing raises interest rates, reducing private investment and offsetting fiscal stimulus
  • budget deficit — Government spending exceeds tax revenue in a given year
Objectives
  • Describe the tools of expansionary and contractionary fiscal policy.
  • Explain automatic stabilizers and give two examples.
  • Explain the crowding out effect and why it limits fiscal policy effectiveness.
How this can be tested
  • FRQ draws fiscal policy on an AD/AS diagram.
  • MCQ selects the correct fiscal policy for a given output gap.
  • FRQ explains crowding out mechanism.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw AD/AS showing a recessionary gap; apply expansionary fiscal policy; label the new equilibrium.
  • Explain why progressive income taxes act as automatic stabilizers.

The Multiplier in Detail

Learn on this site
Spending Multiplier Calculation

Spending multiplier (k) = 1 / MPS = 1 / (1 − MPC). If MPC = 0.8, k = 5; a $20B increase in G leads to a $100B increase in GDP. This works because each dollar spent becomes income for someone else who spends a fraction (MPC) of it, and so on.

Common misconception: The multiplier assumes no leakages beyond saving — in reality taxes, imports, and crowding out reduce the real-world multiplier below 1/(1−MPC).

How it can be tested: FRQ requires you to calculate multiplier, ΔGDP, or the required ΔG to close an output gap.

Tax Multiplier

The tax multiplier = −MPC / MPS (negative because a tax INCREASE reduces spending). It is smaller in absolute value than the spending multiplier because some of a tax cut is saved, not spent. A $1 increase in G raises GDP by 1/MPS; a $1 tax cut raises GDP by MPC/MPS.

Common misconception: A tax cut's impact is smaller than an equal spending increase because part of the tax cut leaks out as saving — the spending multiplier equals 1 + |tax multiplier|.

How it can be tested: FRQ compares spending vs. tax policy multiplier effects.

Balanced Budget Multiplier

If G and T both increase by the same amount, GDP rises by exactly that amount (balanced budget multiplier = 1). Increasing G raises GDP by 1/MPS; increasing T reduces GDP by MPC/MPS; the net effect is 1/MPS − MPC/MPS = 1.

Common misconception: The balanced budget multiplier equals exactly 1 — simultaneous equal increases in G and T are expansionary, not neutral. This surprises students who assume the effects cancel.

How it can be tested: FRQ may ask you to calculate the net effect of simultaneous equal changes in G and T.

Key Vocabulary
  • spending multiplier — 1/MPS — the factor by which an autonomous spending change multiplies into total GDP change
  • tax multiplier — −MPC/MPS — negative because a tax increase reduces GDP
  • MPC — Marginal propensity to consume; fraction of additional income spent: ΔC/ΔY
  • MPS — Marginal propensity to save; fraction of additional income saved: MPC + MPS = 1
  • balanced budget multiplier — When G and T increase equally, GDP rises by exactly the amount of the increase (= 1)
Objectives
  • Calculate the spending multiplier and GDP change from a given MPC.
  • Explain why the tax multiplier is smaller than the spending multiplier.
  • Calculate the effect of simultaneous equal changes in G and T.
How this can be tested
  • FRQ calculates multiplier, ΔGDP, and required ΔG to close a gap.
  • MCQ compares spending and tax policy effects.
  • FRQ applies balanced budget multiplier.
Practice before moving on
  • If MPC = 0.6, calculate: spending multiplier, tax multiplier, and GDP impact of $50B increase in G.
  • Calculate how much G must increase to close a $200B recessionary gap if MPC = 0.75.
