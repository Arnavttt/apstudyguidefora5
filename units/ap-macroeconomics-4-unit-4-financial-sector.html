Money and Banking

Learn on this site
Functions and Measures of Money

Money serves three functions: medium of exchange (avoids barter), store of value (holds purchasing power over time), and unit of account (standard for measuring prices). M1 includes currency and demand deposits (checking accounts); M2 adds savings deposits and money market accounts.

Common misconception: Credit cards are NOT money — they are a way to access borrowed money. Debit cards access existing money (demand deposits, which are M1).

How it can be tested: MCQ identifies which assets are M1, M2, or neither.

Fractional Reserve Banking

Banks hold a fraction of deposits as reserves and lend out the rest, creating money. Required reserve ratio (RRR) is set by the Fed; excess reserves can be lent. If RRR = 10%, a bank with $100 deposit holds $10 reserves and lends $90.

Common misconception: Banks cannot lend out more than their excess reserves. Students sometimes think banks can lend an unlimited multiple of deposits simultaneously.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to calculate required reserves, excess reserves, and the maximum amount a single bank can lend.

Money Multiplier

Money multiplier = 1 / RRR. If RRR = 10% (0.10), multiplier = 10. An initial $100 deposit can ultimately create up to $1,000 in total money supply. This requires all excess reserves to be lent and redeposited — the actual multiplier is smaller.

Common misconception: The money multiplier is a MAXIMUM — it assumes all excess reserves are lent and all loans are redeposited. In practice, cash leakage and excess reserves reduce the actual multiplier.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to calculate the money multiplier and the maximum change in money supply from an initial deposit.

Key Vocabulary
  • M1 — Currency in circulation plus demand deposits (checking accounts)
  • M2 — M1 plus savings deposits, money market accounts, and small time deposits
  • required reserve ratio (RRR) — The fraction of deposits banks must hold as reserves, set by the Federal Reserve
  • excess reserves — Reserves held above the required minimum; can be lent out to create money
  • money multiplier — 1/RRR — the maximum ratio by which an initial deposit expands the money supply
Objectives
  • Identify M1 and M2 components and classify financial assets.
  • Calculate required reserves, excess reserves, and the amount a single bank can lend.
  • Calculate the money multiplier and maximum money supply change.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ classifies assets as M1, M2, or neither.
  • FRQ calculates required reserves, excess reserves, and single-bank lending capacity.
  • FRQ calculates money multiplier and maximum deposit expansion.
Practice before moving on
  • If a bank receives a $500 deposit and RRR = 20%, calculate required reserves, excess reserves, and how much the bank can lend.
  • If RRR = 5%, what is the money multiplier?

The Money Market

Learn on this site
Money Supply and Demand

The money market shows the supply and demand for money. Money supply (MS) is vertical (set by the Fed). Money demand (MD) is downward sloping — at lower nominal interest rates, people hold more money (opportunity cost of holding cash is lower). Equilibrium determines the nominal interest rate.

Common misconception: The money market determines the NOMINAL interest rate, not the real interest rate. The real interest rate = nominal interest rate − inflation.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to draw the money market and show the effect of monetary policy actions.

Shifts in Money Demand

Money demand shifts right when nominal GDP increases (people need more money for transactions) or when price level rises. Money demand shifts left when nominal GDP falls or technology (electronic payments) reduces transaction money needs.

Common misconception: Money demand is NOT the demand for income or wealth — it is specifically the demand to hold money rather than bonds or other assets.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks what shifts the money demand curve.

Monetary Policy and the Money Market

When the Fed increases the money supply (MS shifts right), the nominal interest rate falls. When the Fed decreases the money supply (MS shifts left), the nominal interest rate rises. Lower interest rates stimulate investment and AD; higher rates reduce investment and AD.

Common misconception: Expansionary monetary policy → MS right → nominal interest rate falls → investment increases → AD shifts right. Each step in this transmission chain may appear in FRQ.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to trace the full transmission mechanism from monetary policy to GDP.

Key Vocabulary
  • money market — Model showing equilibrium nominal interest rate from money supply and money demand
  • nominal interest rate — The stated interest rate not adjusted for inflation; determined in the money market
  • money demand — Inversely related to interest rate — lower interest rates increase desire to hold money
  • money supply — Vertical in the money market diagram; set by the Federal Reserve
  • real interest rate — Nominal interest rate minus inflation; relevant for investment decisions
Objectives
  • Draw the money market and label MS, MD, and equilibrium interest rate.
  • Explain what shifts the money demand curve.
  • Trace the effect of an increase in money supply through to AD.
How this can be tested
  • FRQ draws money market and traces monetary policy transmission.
  • MCQ identifies effects of MS or MD shifts on nominal interest rate.
  • FRQ connects money market interest rate change to investment and AD.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw the money market before and after an increase in money supply; label the new equilibrium.
  • Trace: Fed buys bonds → ? → ? → investment → AD.

The Federal Reserve and Monetary Policy Tools

Learn on this site
Structure and Goals of the Fed

The Federal Reserve (Fed) is the U.S. central bank, established in 1913. Its dual mandate is price stability (2% inflation target) and maximum employment. The Fed is independent from political pressure, though it is ultimately accountable to Congress.

Common misconception: The Fed is independent but not unaccountable — Congress created it and can change its mandate. "Independent" means monetary policy decisions are insulated from day-to-day political pressure.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about the Fed's mandate and organizational structure.

Monetary Policy Tools

The Fed has three main tools: (1) Open market operations — buying or selling Treasury bonds to change bank reserves and money supply; (2) Discount rate — the interest rate the Fed charges banks for loans; (3) Reserve requirement — the minimum fraction of deposits banks must hold. Open market operations are the primary tool.

Common misconception: When the Fed BUYS bonds, it injects reserves into the banking system (expansionary). When it SELLS bonds, it removes reserves (contractionary). Students often confuse the direction.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to identify the correct tool and direction for a given policy goal.

Expansionary vs. Contractionary Monetary Policy

Expansionary monetary policy (for recessions): buy bonds / lower discount rate / lower RRR → increases MS → lowers interest rate → increases I → shifts AD right. Contractionary (for inflation): sell bonds / raise discount rate / raise RRR → decreases MS → raises interest rate → decreases I → shifts AD left.

Common misconception: All three tools work in the same direction but through the same mechanism: changing bank reserves and thus the money supply and interest rates.

How it can be tested: FRQ draws the money market + AD/AS for a full monetary policy question.

Key Vocabulary
  • Federal Reserve (Fed) — The U.S. central bank; controls monetary policy to pursue price stability and maximum employment
  • open market operations — The Fed buying or selling Treasury bonds to change bank reserves and money supply
  • discount rate — The interest rate the Fed charges commercial banks that borrow from it
  • federal funds rate — The interest rate banks charge each other for overnight loans; the Fed's primary target
  • reserve requirement — Minimum fraction of deposits banks must hold; lowering it is expansionary
Objectives
  • List and explain the Fed's three monetary policy tools.
  • Identify whether a given Fed action is expansionary or contractionary.
  • Trace the full effect of contractionary monetary policy on the economy.
How this can be tested
  • FRQ identifies correct monetary policy tool for an output gap.
  • MCQ identifies whether a Fed action is expansionary or contractionary.
  • FRQ draws money market and AD/AS for full monetary policy question.
Practice before moving on
  • Complete the transmission chain: Fed sells bonds → ? → ? → ? → AD shifts.
  • Compare the effect of all three Fed tools on money supply and interest rates.

Loanable Funds Market

Learn on this site
Supply and Demand for Loanable Funds

The loanable funds market shows the real interest rate determined by the supply of savings (households saving) and demand for loans (firms borrowing for investment). Supply slopes upward (higher real rates incentivize more saving); demand slopes downward (lower real rates make investment cheaper).

Common misconception: The loanable funds market determines the REAL interest rate; the money market determines the NOMINAL interest rate. They are connected but distinct models.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to draw the loanable funds market and show shifts.

Shifts in Loanable Funds

Supply shifts right when savings increase (government surplus, foreign capital inflows, lower taxes increasing private saving). Demand shifts right when investment increases (business optimism, tax incentives). A government deficit shifts demand right (crowding out) and raises the real interest rate.

Common misconception: Government borrowing to cover a deficit is demand in the loanable funds market — it competes with private borrowers and raises real interest rates, reducing investment (crowding out).

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to show crowding out in the loanable funds market.

Connection to Monetary Policy

Expansionary monetary policy lowers nominal interest rates (money market) and may lower real interest rates if it doesn't generate inflation expectations. Lower real rates in the loanable funds market increase business investment, shifting AD right.

Common misconception: The money market and loanable funds market both involve interest rates but model different things — students often confuse nominal rates (money market) with real rates (loanable funds).

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to identify which market and which interest rate is relevant for a given question.

Key Vocabulary
  • loanable funds market — Model showing real interest rate from supply of saving and demand for borrowing/investment
  • real interest rate — Nominal interest rate minus inflation; relevant for saving and investment decisions
  • crowding out — Government deficit borrowing raises real interest rate in loanable funds market, reducing private investment
  • foreign capital inflow — When foreign investors purchase domestic assets, shifting loanable funds supply right and lowering real rates
  • private saving — Household and business saving available for lending in the loanable funds market
Objectives
  • Draw the loanable funds market and label supply, demand, and equilibrium real interest rate.
  • Explain how a government deficit causes crowding out in the loanable funds market.
  • Distinguish the loanable funds market from the money market.
How this can be tested
  • FRQ draws loanable funds market and shows crowding out.
  • MCQ distinguishes money market from loanable funds market.
  • FRQ traces connection from government deficit to reduced investment.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw the loanable funds market before and after a government deficit; trace the crowding out effect.
  • Explain how a foreign capital inflow affects real interest rates and investment.
