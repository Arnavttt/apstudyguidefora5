The Phillips Curve

Learn on this site
Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC)

The short-run Phillips curve (SRPC) shows an inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment — when unemployment falls (expansion), inflation rises; when unemployment rises (recession), inflation falls. It is the mirror image of the AD/AS model.

Common misconception: The SRPC reflects a trade-off, not a fixed relationship — it shifts when inflation expectations or supply shocks change.

How it can be tested: MCQ and FRQ ask you to use the SRPC to predict inflation-unemployment combinations.

Long-Run Phillips Curve (LRPC)

The long-run Phillips curve (LRPC) is vertical at the natural rate of unemployment (NRU). In the long run, there is no trade-off between inflation and unemployment — any attempt to keep unemployment below the NRU only results in higher inflation.

Common misconception: The LRPC being vertical means monetary and fiscal policy cannot permanently lower unemployment below the NRU — they can only change the price level.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to show the SRPC and LRPC together and trace a policy action.

Shifts of the SRPC

The SRPC shifts right (stagflation) when supply shocks increase inflation at every unemployment rate (e.g., oil price spike) or when inflation expectations rise. The SRPC shifts left when inflation expectations fall or supply improves.

Common misconception: A supply shock (SRAS shifts left in AD/AS) is equivalent to the SRPC shifting right — both show higher inflation and higher unemployment simultaneously.

How it can be tested: MCQ connects SRPC shifts to AD/AS supply shocks.

Key Vocabulary
  • Phillips curve — Shows the inverse short-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment
  • SRPC — Short-run Phillips curve; downward sloping; shows inflation-unemployment trade-off
  • LRPC — Long-run Phillips curve; vertical at the NRU; no long-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment
  • stagflation — Rising inflation combined with rising unemployment; shifts SRPC right
  • natural rate of unemployment (NRU) — The unemployment rate in the long run; where LRPC is located
Objectives
  • Explain the short-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment.
  • Explain why the LRPC is vertical at the NRU.
  • Identify what causes the SRPC to shift.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ reads SRPC to predict inflation or unemployment outcomes.
  • FRQ draws SRPC and LRPC and traces policy effects.
  • MCQ connects supply shocks to SRPC shifts.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw SRPC and LRPC; label NRU; show a supply shock shifting SRPC right.
  • Explain why expansionary policy cannot permanently reduce unemployment below the NRU.

Long-Run Neutrality of Money

Learn on this site
Long-Run Neutrality

In the long run, increases in the money supply only raise the price level proportionally — they do not increase real output. This is called the neutrality of money: monetary policy affects nominal variables (price level, nominal GDP) but not real variables (real GDP, employment) in the long run.

Common misconception: Money is neutral in the long run because workers and firms eventually adjust wages and prices to reflect higher money supply — real purchasing power and output return to their natural levels.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to distinguish short-run vs. long-run effects of monetary expansion.

Quantity Theory of Money

The quantity theory states: MV = PQ, where M = money supply, V = velocity (how often money turns over), P = price level, Q = real output. If V and Q are fixed, doubling M doubles P — inflation is a monetary phenomenon.

Common misconception: The quantity theory assumes V is stable — if velocity changes unpredictably, the relationship between money growth and inflation breaks down. This is why the Fed focuses on interest rates rather than money supply targets.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to apply the quantity equation MV = PQ.

Inflationary Expectations

If workers expect higher future inflation, they demand higher wages now, shifting SRAS left and increasing the price level. Inflation expectations can become self-fulfilling: expected inflation causes wage demands that cause actual inflation.

Common misconception: This is why central bank credibility matters — if people believe the Fed will control inflation, expectations remain anchored and inflation is easier to control.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to show how a change in inflation expectations shifts the SRPC.

Key Vocabulary
  • neutrality of money — In the long run, money supply changes affect only the price level, not real output
  • quantity theory of money — MV = PQ; links money supply growth to price level if velocity and real output are stable
  • velocity of money (V) — The average number of times a unit of money is spent in a year
  • inflation expectations — What workers and firms expect future inflation to be; affects wage demands and pricing
  • self-fulfilling inflation — When expected inflation causes wage and price increases that realize the expectation
Objectives
  • Explain the long-run neutrality of money.
  • Apply the quantity theory equation MV = PQ to a given scenario.
  • Explain how inflation expectations can become self-fulfilling.
How this can be tested
  • FRQ distinguishes short-run from long-run monetary policy effects.
  • MCQ applies MV = PQ.
  • FRQ shows how rising inflation expectations shift the SRPC.
Practice before moving on
  • If M doubles and V and Q are constant, what happens to P?
  • Explain why central bank credibility helps keep inflation expectations anchored.

Fiscal Policy in the Long Run

Learn on this site
Debt and Deficits

Budget deficit = G > T in a given year. National debt = accumulated deficits over time. High national debt can crowd out private investment (raising real interest rates), reduce fiscal flexibility, and require higher future taxes. However, deficits during recessions are often justified by the multiplier effect.

Common misconception: Budget deficit and national debt are different: deficit is a FLOW (one year's imbalance); debt is a STOCK (accumulated over all years). Students frequently confuse the two.

How it can be tested: MCQ distinguishes deficit from debt; FRQ asks about effects of persistent deficits.

Ricardian Equivalence

Ricardian equivalence argues that tax cuts financed by deficits don't stimulate the economy because rational consumers save the tax cut to pay anticipated future taxes. In practice, evidence for Ricardian equivalence is mixed — many consumers do not behave this way.

Common misconception: The AP® exam uses Ricardian equivalence as a concept to evaluate but doesn't require you to prove or disprove it — just explain its logic and limitations.

How it can be tested: FRQ may ask you to explain Ricardian equivalence as a limitation of fiscal policy.

Supply-Side Economics

Supply-side economics argues that tax cuts (especially on businesses and high-income earners) and deregulation increase incentives to invest and work, shifting SRAS and LRAS rightward to increase long-run potential output. Critics argue benefits accrue to the wealthy without sufficient trickle-down.

Common misconception: Supply-side policies shift SRAS and LRAS — they are fundamentally different from demand-side (Keynesian) policies that shift AD.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to show supply-side policy on an AD/AS diagram.

Key Vocabulary
  • budget deficit — Annual government spending exceeds tax revenue; a flow variable
  • national debt — Accumulated sum of all past budget deficits; a stock variable
  • Ricardian equivalence — Theory that deficit-financed tax cuts don't stimulate the economy because consumers save to pay future taxes
  • supply-side economics — Policy approach using tax cuts and deregulation to shift SRAS/LRAS right and boost potential output
  • crowding out — Higher government borrowing raises real interest rates, reducing private investment
Objectives
  • Distinguish budget deficit from national debt.
  • Explain Ricardian equivalence and why it limits fiscal policy.
  • Compare supply-side and demand-side policy effects using AD/AS.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ distinguishes deficit from debt.
  • FRQ explains Ricardian equivalence as a fiscal policy limitation.
  • FRQ draws supply-side policy on AD/AS diagram.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw an AD/AS graph showing the effect of a supply-side tax cut on SRAS and LRAS.
  • Explain why Ricardian equivalence would make a tax cut ineffective as stimulus.

Policy Trade-offs and the Ideal Policy Mix

Learn on this site
Fiscal vs. Monetary Policy Trade-offs

Fiscal policy (changes in G and T) has larger initial multiplier effects but suffers from time lags (recognition, legislative, implementation). Monetary policy (Fed tools) acts faster but may be less effective at the zero lower bound (when nominal rates are near zero). Both shift AD.

Common misconception: Monetary policy is generally considered more flexible and faster — the Fed meets regularly and acts quickly. Fiscal policy requires Congressional action, creating long lags.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to compare fiscal and monetary policy on speed, flexibility, and side effects.

The Zero Lower Bound Problem

When nominal interest rates are near zero, conventional expansionary monetary policy loses effectiveness — the Fed cannot lower rates further. This is the "liquidity trap" (Keynes). At the zero lower bound, quantitative easing (QE) — buying long-term bonds to lower long-term rates — may be used.

Common misconception: The zero lower bound became relevant after the 2008 financial crisis when the Fed lowered rates to near zero and held them there for years.

How it can be tested: FRQ may ask you to explain why monetary policy is limited at the zero lower bound.

Policy Coordination

Fiscal and monetary policy can work together (coordination) or against each other (conflict). Expansionary fiscal policy (raising G) and expansionary monetary policy (buying bonds) both shift AD right — they reinforce each other. Contractionary monetary policy paired with expansionary fiscal policy creates mixed signals.

Common misconception: Fiscal-monetary coordination is complicated by the institutional independence of the Fed — the government cannot compel the Fed to accommodate its fiscal policy.

How it can be tested: FRQ may ask you to evaluate whether a given policy mix is appropriate for current economic conditions.

Key Vocabulary
  • recognition lag — Time between when an economic problem begins and policymakers recognize it
  • implementation lag — Time between recognizing a problem and implementing a policy response
  • zero lower bound — When nominal interest rates approach zero, limiting conventional monetary policy effectiveness
  • quantitative easing (QE) — Fed purchase of long-term assets to lower long-term interest rates when the zero lower bound is reached
  • policy coordination — Fiscal and monetary policies working in the same direction to stabilize the economy
Objectives
  • Compare fiscal and monetary policy on speed and flexibility.
  • Explain the zero lower bound problem and how QE addresses it.
  • Explain why fiscal and monetary policy can sometimes conflict.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ compares fiscal vs. monetary policy characteristics.
  • FRQ explains zero lower bound limitation.
  • FRQ evaluates policy coordination given an economic scenario.
Practice before moving on
  • Compare fiscal and monetary policy on: who controls it, speed, side effects, and effectiveness at zero lower bound.
  • Explain why expansionary fiscal policy might be more effective than monetary policy in a deep recession.
