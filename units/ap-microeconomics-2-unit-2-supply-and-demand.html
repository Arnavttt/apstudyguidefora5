Supply and Demand

The supply and demand model determines market prices and quantities; price controls create surpluses and shortages.

Demand and Its Determinants

Learn on this site
Law of Demand

As price decreases, quantity demanded increases (ceteris paribus) because of the substitution effect and income effect.

Common misconception: Students confuse a change in quantity demanded (movement along the curve due to price change) with a change in demand (shift of the whole curve).

How it can be tested: MCQs present a price change and ask whether demand or quantity demanded changed, and in which direction.

Demand Curve Shifts

Demand shifts right when income rises (for normal goods), the price of complements falls, the price of substitutes rises, tastes improve, expectations rise, or the number of buyers increases.

Common misconception: Students think any factor that "makes people buy more" shifts demand right; a price decrease only increases quantity demanded, not demand itself.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to draw a demand shift and explain which determinant caused it.

Consumer Surplus

Consumer surplus is the difference between the maximum price consumers are willing to pay and the actual price paid, represented by the area below the demand curve and above the price.

Common misconception: Students confuse consumer surplus with total consumer spending; spending is P×Q while surplus is the triangle above price and below demand.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to calculate consumer surplus from a linear demand curve and a given market price.

Key Vocabulary
  • Law of Demand — As price rises, quantity demanded falls; as price falls, quantity demanded rises, ceteris paribus.
  • Substitute Goods — Goods that can replace each other; a rise in the price of one increases demand for the other.
  • Complement Goods — Goods used together; a rise in the price of one decreases demand for the other.
  • Normal Good — A good for which demand increases when income increases.
  • Consumer Surplus — The benefit consumers receive beyond what they pay, shown as the area below demand and above price.
Objectives
  • State the law of demand and explain the substitution and income effects.
  • List and explain the five non-price determinants of demand.
  • Calculate consumer surplus from a demand curve and market price.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs frequently ask students to identify whether demand or quantity demanded changes given a scenario.
  • FRQs ask students to draw a demand shift, label it correctly, and explain the cause.
  • Questions test consumer surplus calculation from linear demand diagrams.
Practice before moving on
  • List 5 goods; for each, identify one event that would shift demand right and one that would shift it left.
  • Draw a demand curve for coffee and show the effect of a rise in the price of tea (a substitute).

Supply and Its Determinants

Learn on this site
Law of Supply

As price increases, quantity supplied increases because higher prices make production more profitable, encouraging more output.

Common misconception: Students assume supply always increases with price for all time horizons; in the very short run (fixed supply), the supply curve can be vertical.

How it can be tested: MCQs present a price change and ask whether supply or quantity supplied changed.

Supply Curve Shifts

Supply shifts right (increases) when input costs fall, technology improves, number of sellers increases, subsidies are granted, or favorable expectations arise; supply shifts left when taxes rise or inputs become scarce.

Common misconception: Students confuse a price change (movement) with a shift; only non-price factors shift the supply curve.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to draw a supply shift and identify the determinant.

Producer Surplus

Producer surplus is the difference between the price received by producers and the minimum price they would accept, shown as the area above the supply curve and below the price.

Common misconception: Students confuse producer surplus with profit; PS is the area above supply and below price, while profit subtracts all fixed costs too.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to calculate or compare producer surplus before and after a price change.

Key Vocabulary
  • Law of Supply — As price rises, quantity supplied rises; as price falls, quantity supplied falls, ceteris paribus.
  • Input Costs — The costs of resources used in production; a rise in input costs decreases supply.
  • Technology — Improvements in production methods that lower costs and increase supply at every price.
  • Producer Surplus — The benefit producers receive beyond their minimum acceptable price, shown above supply and below price.
  • Subsidy — A government payment to producers that lowers their costs and increases supply.
Objectives
  • State the law of supply and explain why the supply curve slopes upward.
  • List and explain the non-price determinants of supply.
  • Calculate producer surplus from a supply curve and market price.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask whether a scenario causes a shift of supply or a change in quantity supplied.
  • FRQs require drawing supply shifts and identifying the determinant.
  • Questions test producer surplus calculation from supply diagrams.
Practice before moving on
  • List 5 goods; for each, identify an event that shifts supply right and one that shifts it left.
  • Draw a supply curve for wheat and show the effect of a new harvesting technology.

Market Equilibrium

Learn on this site
Finding Equilibrium

Market equilibrium occurs where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied; the equilibrium price "clears" the market with no surplus or shortage.

Common misconception: Students think equilibrium is a "fair" price; it is simply the price at which the market clears, regardless of equity.

How it can be tested: FRQs give supply and demand equations and ask students to solve for equilibrium price and quantity algebraically.

Surplus and Shortage

A surplus (excess supply) occurs when price is above equilibrium, causing downward pressure; a shortage (excess demand) occurs when price is below equilibrium, causing upward pressure.

Common misconception: Students confuse surplus with supply; a surplus is a temporary market condition at a specific price, while supply is the whole relationship between price and quantity.

How it can be tested: MCQs give a price and ask whether a surplus or shortage exists and predict the direction of price adjustment.

Shifts in Equilibrium

When demand or supply shifts, both equilibrium price and quantity change; double shifts can make price or quantity indeterminate depending on the relative magnitudes.

Common misconception: Students assume both price and quantity always change in the same direction; when supply and demand shift in opposite directions, one outcome (P or Q) is indeterminate.

How it can be tested: FRQs present two simultaneous shifts and ask students to determine the new equilibrium and explain when a variable is indeterminate.

Key Vocabulary
  • Market Equilibrium — The price and quantity at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied and the market clears.
  • Surplus — Excess supply; occurs when price is above equilibrium, causing downward pressure on price.
  • Shortage — Excess demand; occurs when price is below equilibrium, causing upward pressure on price.
  • Market-Clearing Price — Another term for equilibrium price; the price at which there is no unsatisfied buyer or seller.
  • Indeterminate — When the direction of change for price or quantity cannot be determined without knowing relative magnitudes of shifts.
Objectives
  • Find equilibrium price and quantity algebraically given supply and demand equations.
  • Determine whether a surplus or shortage exists at a given price.
  • Predict the new equilibrium after one or two shifts in supply and/or demand.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs frequently ask students to solve for equilibrium or show a new equilibrium graphically.
  • MCQs ask the direction of price and quantity change after a shift or pair of shifts.
  • Questions test when a resulting variable is indeterminate after two simultaneous shifts.
Practice before moving on
  • Given QD = 100 - 2P and QS = 20 + 3P, solve for equilibrium P and Q.
  • Draw four separate diagrams: (1) D↑, (2) S↑, (3) D↑+S↑, (4) D↑+S↓, and predict outcomes.

Price Controls and Elasticity

Learn on this site
Price Ceilings and Floors

A price ceiling set below equilibrium creates a shortage; a price floor set above equilibrium creates a surplus; controls set at or above/below equilibrium have no effect.

Common misconception: Students think any price ceiling causes a shortage; only a binding ceiling (below equilibrium) causes one.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to draw a price control, label the surplus/shortage, and identify winners and losers.

Price Elasticity of Demand

PED measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a price change; elastic demand (|PED| > 1) means revenue falls when price rises; inelastic (|PED| < 1) means revenue rises.

Common misconception: Students confuse slope with elasticity; a steeper demand curve is more inelastic, but slope and elasticity are not the same concept.

How it can be tested: MCQs present a price change and revenue change and ask whether demand is elastic or inelastic.

Other Elasticities

Price elasticity of supply measures producer responsiveness; cross-price elasticity determines substitutes (positive) vs. complements (negative); income elasticity distinguishes normal (positive) from inferior goods (negative).

Common misconception: Students think income elasticity just measures how much demand changes with income without distinguishing normal from inferior goods.

How it can be tested: MCQs test the sign and magnitude of cross-price and income elasticity to classify goods.

Key Vocabulary
  • Price Ceiling — A legal maximum price; if set below equilibrium, creates a shortage.
  • Price Floor — A legal minimum price; if set above equilibrium, creates a surplus.
  • Price Elasticity of Demand — The percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price; measures responsiveness.
  • Elastic Demand — Demand where |PED| > 1; consumers are very responsive to price changes.
  • Income Elasticity — Measures how quantity demanded changes as income changes; positive for normal goods, negative for inferior goods.
Objectives
  • Identify binding vs. non-binding price controls and their effects.
  • Calculate PED and determine whether demand is elastic or inelastic.
  • Use cross-price and income elasticity to classify good relationships.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask the effect of a price ceiling or floor on quantity and whether it is binding.
  • FRQs ask students to calculate elasticity and predict the revenue effect of a price change.
  • Questions use elasticity signs to classify goods as substitutes, complements, normal, or inferior.
Practice before moving on
  • Calculate PED for a price rise from $10 to $12 that reduces quantity from 100 to 80 units.
  • Draw a binding price floor in the labor market (minimum wage) and identify the surplus.
