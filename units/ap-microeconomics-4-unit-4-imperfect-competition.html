Unit 4 of AP® Microeconomics

Imperfect Competition

Monopoly and other imperfect competitors are price makers that produce less and charge more than competitive markets.

Monopoly

Learn on this site
Monopoly Characteristics and Sources

A monopoly has a single seller with no close substitutes; barriers to entry include patents, economies of scale (natural monopoly), government licenses, and control over key resources.

Common misconception: Students think monopolies always earn positive profit; a monopolist maximizes profit but can still earn zero or negative economic profit if ATC > P.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to identify sources of monopoly power and distinguish monopoly from perfect competition.

Monopoly Pricing: MR = MC

The monopolist produces where MR = MC to find profit-maximizing quantity, then sets price on the demand curve above that quantity; P > MR = MC.

Common misconception: Students set price at MR = MC; the monopolist sets quantity at MR = MC but reads price from the demand curve, not the MR curve.

How it can be tested: FRQs give a monopoly diagram and ask students to identify profit-maximizing Q, price, and the area of economic profit or deadweight loss.

Deadweight Loss and Inefficiency

Monopoly produces less than the socially optimal quantity (where P = MC), creating deadweight loss and a transfer of consumer surplus to the monopolist as producer surplus.

Common misconception: Students think higher price just transfers welfare from consumers to producers; part of consumer surplus is also destroyed (deadweight loss) because output falls below the efficient level.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to draw and shade deadweight loss and explain why monopoly is allocatively inefficient.

Key Vocabulary
  • Monopoly — A market with a single seller producing a good with no close substitutes.
  • Barriers to Entry — Factors that prevent new firms from entering a market, enabling monopoly power.
  • Price Maker — A firm with market power that can set price above marginal cost by restricting output.
  • Deadweight Loss — The loss of total surplus due to monopoly underproduction; the welfare cost of market power.
  • Natural Monopoly — A market where one firm can serve the entire market at lower cost than multiple firms (LRATC declines throughout).
Objectives
  • Identify the sources of monopoly power and barriers to entry.
  • Find the profit-maximizing output and price for a monopolist.
  • Identify and explain deadweight loss in a monopoly diagram.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs show a monopoly diagram and ask for profit-maximizing Q, price, profit rectangle, and DWL triangle.
  • MCQs ask the difference between monopoly and competitive equilibrium in terms of price and quantity.
  • Questions ask students to classify a firm as having monopoly power based on market structure features.
Practice before moving on
  • Given a monopolist with demand P = 50 - Q and MC = 10, find Q*, P*, and calculate DWL.
  • Draw a natural monopoly diagram; explain why average cost pricing is preferred to unregulated monopoly.

Price Discrimination

Learn on this site
Types of Price Discrimination

First-degree discrimination charges each consumer their maximum willingness to pay (eliminates consumer surplus); third-degree charges different groups different prices based on elasticity.

Common misconception: Students think price discrimination is illegal; it is legal and common (student discounts, airline pricing), and it increases the firm's profit by capturing consumer surplus.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to identify examples of price discrimination and its effects on consumer surplus.

Conditions for Price Discrimination

Price discrimination requires: (1) market power, (2) ability to segment markets, (3) inability of consumers to resell (arbitrage).

Common misconception: Students think any price difference is discrimination; only price differences not based on cost differences constitute price discrimination.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to explain why a firm can charge different prices in two markets and calculate profit-maximizing price in each.

Effects of Price Discrimination

Price discrimination increases the monopolist's profit, reduces deadweight loss (more output is sold), but reduces consumer surplus; perfect price discrimination eliminates DWL entirely.

Common misconception: Students think price discrimination always harms consumers; while individual consumer surplus may fall, total output increases and some consumers who would have been excluded gain access.

How it can be tested: MCQs compare the welfare effects of price discrimination versus single-price monopoly.

Key Vocabulary
  • Price Discrimination — Charging different prices to different consumers for the same good, not based on cost differences.
  • First-Degree Discrimination — Charging each consumer their maximum willingness to pay, extracting all consumer surplus.
  • Third-Degree Discrimination — Charging different groups different prices based on their price elasticity of demand.
  • Arbitrage — Buying a good at a lower price and reselling at a higher price; prevents price discrimination.
  • Market Segmentation — Dividing consumers into identifiable groups with different demand elasticities to enable price discrimination.
Objectives
  • Identify and explain the three types of price discrimination.
  • List the three conditions necessary for price discrimination to work.
  • Compare welfare effects of price discrimination vs. single-price monopoly.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs present a scenario (student discount, airline seat pricing) and ask which type of price discrimination applies.
  • FRQs ask students to find profit-maximizing prices in two separate markets with different elasticities.
  • Questions ask students to explain changes in consumer surplus, producer surplus, and DWL.
Practice before moving on
  • Explain why airlines can price discriminate between business and leisure travelers.
  • Calculate profit for a third-degree price discriminating monopolist given demand in two markets.

Oligopoly and Game Theory

Learn on this site
Oligopoly Characteristics

Oligopoly has few large sellers with mutual interdependence; each firm must consider rivals' reactions when making price and output decisions; products may be homogeneous or differentiated.

Common misconception: Students think oligopolies always collude; in practice, collusion is illegal in most countries and is often unstable due to incentives to cheat.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to identify oligopoly characteristics and distinguish them from other market structures.

Prisoner's Dilemma and Nash Equilibrium

Game theory models strategic interaction; the Nash equilibrium is a strategy combination where no player benefits from unilaterally changing strategy — in the prisoner's dilemma, the dominant strategy leads to a worse outcome for both players.

Common misconception: Students think Nash equilibrium is always the best joint outcome; it is the stable equilibrium but can be inferior (as in the prisoner's dilemma where both firms compete when cooperation would be better).

How it can be tested: FRQs present a payoff matrix and ask students to find dominant strategies and the Nash equilibrium.

Collusion and Cartels

Collusion occurs when oligopolists cooperate to restrict output and raise price, acting like a monopoly; cartels (like OPEC) are formal collusive agreements, which are illegal in the US.

Common misconception: Students think cartels are always stable; they are inherently unstable because each member has an incentive to cheat by producing more than the agreed quota.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students why cartels tend to break down over time.

Key Vocabulary
  • Oligopoly — A market with a few large sellers whose pricing and output decisions are mutually interdependent.
  • Mutual Interdependence — The condition in oligopoly where each firm's decisions affect and are affected by rivals' decisions.
  • Nash Equilibrium — A set of strategies where no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing their own strategy.
  • Dominant Strategy — A strategy that yields the best outcome regardless of what the other player does.
  • Cartel — A formal agreement among oligopolists to restrict output and fix prices, acting as a collective monopoly.
Objectives
  • List the main characteristics of oligopoly.
  • Find the dominant strategy and Nash equilibrium from a payoff matrix.
  • Explain why cartels are unstable.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs provide a 2×2 payoff matrix and ask students to identify dominant strategies, Nash equilibrium, and whether players cooperate.
  • MCQs ask students to classify a market structure and identify the features of oligopoly.
  • Questions ask why collusion is difficult to sustain over time.
Practice before moving on
  • Build a payoff matrix for two airlines choosing to collude or compete on ticket prices.
  • Identify the Nash equilibrium and explain whether it is also the best joint outcome.

Monopolistic Competition

Learn on this site
Characteristics of Monopolistic Competition

Monopolistic competition has many firms, differentiated products, some market power (downward-sloping demand), and free entry/exit; examples include restaurants, clothing, and consumer goods.

Common misconception: Students confuse monopolistic competition with perfect competition; the key difference is product differentiation, which gives each firm a downward-sloping demand curve and some price-setting ability.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to identify characteristics and real-world examples of monopolistic competition.

Short-Run and Long-Run Equilibrium

In the short run, monopolistically competitive firms can earn positive, zero, or negative economic profit; in the long run, free entry eliminates profit until P = ATC (not P = minimum ATC).

Common misconception: Students expect long-run tangency at minimum ATC as in perfect competition; in monopolistic competition, the tangency is on the downward-sloping part of ATC, so firms produce at excess capacity.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to draw both short-run and long-run equilibrium for a monopolistically competitive firm and explain the adjustment process.

Excess Capacity and Product Variety

Monopolistic competition leads to excess capacity (firms produce less than minimum efficient scale) and price above MC, creating inefficiency; however, product variety and non-price competition provide benefits.

Common misconception: Students think excess capacity is always wasteful; it is the cost of product variety — consumers pay more and firms produce less than efficiently, but they get more diverse product choices.

How it can be tested: MCQs compare monopolistic competition to perfect competition in terms of price, output, and efficiency.

Key Vocabulary
  • Monopolistic Competition — A market with many firms selling differentiated products with free entry and exit.
  • Product Differentiation — Real or perceived differences in products that give firms some market power and a downward-sloping demand curve.
  • Excess Capacity — The gap between actual output and minimum efficient scale output in monopolistically competitive long-run equilibrium.
  • Non-Price Competition — Competition based on product quality, advertising, location, and service rather than price.
  • Long-Run Tangency — In monopolistic competition's long run, the demand curve is tangent to ATC above minimum ATC, giving zero economic profit.
Objectives
  • List the four characteristics of monopolistic competition.
  • Draw and explain long-run equilibrium for a monopolistically competitive firm.
  • Explain excess capacity and the trade-off between efficiency and product variety.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs ask students to draw and compare short-run profit and long-run zero-profit equilibrium.
  • MCQs ask how monopolistic competition differs from perfect competition and monopoly.
  • Questions ask students to identify excess capacity on a diagram and explain its welfare implications.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw a monopolistically competitive firm in short-run profit and then in long-run equilibrium.
  • Explain why P > minimum ATC in monopolistic competition but P = minimum ATC in perfect competition.
