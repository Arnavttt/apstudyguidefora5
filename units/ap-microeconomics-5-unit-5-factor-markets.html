Factor Markets

Firms hire inputs up to the point where the marginal revenue product equals the input price; wages are determined in labor markets.

Unit learning gateway

  • Factor demand is derived from the demand for the product the factor helps produce.
  • MRP = MRC is the input hiring rule (analogous to MR = MC in product markets).

Derived Demand and Marginal Revenue Product

Learn on this site
Derived Demand

The demand for factors of production (labor, capital, land) is derived from the demand for the output those factors produce; if demand for the product falls, demand for the factor also falls.

Common misconception: Students think factor demand is separate from product demand; they are directly linked — a rise in product demand shifts the MRP curve right, raising wages and employment.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to trace the effect of a product demand change through to the factor market.

Marginal Revenue Product

MRP = MP × MR (or MP × P for competitive firms); it is the additional revenue generated by hiring one more unit of a factor and represents the firm's demand for that factor.

Common misconception: Students confuse MRP with marginal product; MRP includes the revenue value of the product, not just physical output.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to calculate MRP from a production table and determine the optimal number of workers to hire.

Hiring Rule: MRP = MRC

A firm maximizes profit by hiring labor until MRP = MRC (marginal resource cost); in competitive labor markets, MRC = wage rate.

Common misconception: Students apply the product-market rule (MR = MC) to factor markets without adapting it; the correct parallel is MRP = MRC in factor markets.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to use a labor demand table to find the profit-maximizing employment level at a given wage.

Key Vocabulary
  • Derived Demand — The demand for a factor of production that comes from the demand for the output it helps produce.
  • Marginal Revenue Product — The additional revenue a firm earns by hiring one more unit of a factor; MRP = MP × MR.
  • Marginal Resource Cost — The additional cost of hiring one more unit of a factor; equals the wage in competitive labor markets.
  • Labor Demand Curve — The MRP curve; downward sloping because of diminishing marginal product.
  • Hiring Rule — Hire factors until MRP = MRC; this maximizes profit in factor markets.
Objectives
  • Explain why factor demand is a derived demand.
  • Calculate MRP using marginal product and output price.
  • Apply the MRP = MRC rule to find the profit-maximizing employment level.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs provide a production table and wage rate; students must calculate MRP and find optimal labor input.
  • MCQs ask how a change in product price or worker productivity shifts the MRP curve.
  • Questions link MRP = MRC hiring rule to the MR = MC product market rule.
Practice before moving on
  • Given P = $5 per unit and the 4th worker adds 20 units, calculate MRP for the 4th worker.
  • If wage = $80 and MRP for 5th worker = $90, should the firm hire the 5th worker? Explain.

Wage Determination in Competitive Labor Markets

Learn on this site
Labor Supply and Demand

In competitive labor markets, the market wage is determined by the intersection of labor demand (sum of MRP curves) and labor supply (individual decisions to work); each firm is a wage taker.

Common misconception: Students think firms set wages; in competitive markets, wages are determined by supply and demand, and individual firms accept the market wage.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask what happens to wages and employment when labor supply or demand shifts.

Wage Differentials

Wages differ across occupations because of differences in MRP (skills and education), compensating differentials (unpleasant conditions), and labor market imperfections (discrimination, geographic immobility).

Common misconception: Students think wage differences always reflect discrimination; many differences reflect legitimate productivity and compensating differential effects.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to explain why wages differ between two occupations and identify the economic rationale.

Minimum Wage as a Price Floor

A minimum wage set above the equilibrium wage creates a labor surplus (unemployment) equal to QS - QD; it raises wages for employed workers but reduces employment.

Common misconception: Students think minimum wages always cause mass unemployment; the effect depends on how far above equilibrium the floor is set and the elasticities of labor supply and demand.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to draw a minimum wage, identify the surplus, and calculate unemployment.

Key Vocabulary
  • Competitive Labor Market — A market with many employers and workers where the wage equals MRP and both sides are wage takers.
  • Compensating Differential — A wage premium paid to workers in dangerous, unpleasant, or unattractive jobs to attract sufficient labor supply.
  • Wage Discrimination — Paying workers with equal MRP different wages based on characteristics like gender or race.
  • Labor Surplus — Excess supply of labor (unemployment) that results when wage is above equilibrium.
  • Marginal Factor Cost — The additional cost to the firm of hiring one more worker; equals the wage in competitive markets.
Objectives
  • Explain how wages are determined in a competitive labor market.
  • Identify three reasons why wages differ across occupations.
  • Draw and analyze the effects of a minimum wage on a competitive labor market.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask what shifts labor demand or supply and how wages change as a result.
  • FRQs ask students to draw the minimum wage as a price floor and calculate the resulting unemployment.
  • Questions ask students to identify compensating differentials vs. wage discrimination.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw a competitive labor market; add a minimum wage above equilibrium; label surplus.
  • Explain why coal miners earn more than equally educated retail workers.

Capital and Land Markets

Learn on this site
Capital Market and Investment Decisions

Firms invest in capital until the expected rate of return equals the real interest rate; the demand for loanable funds slopes downward because more investment projects are profitable at lower interest rates.

Common misconception: Students confuse capital (physical goods) with financial capital (money); in factor market analysis, capital refers to physical machinery and equipment.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask how a change in the interest rate or expected rate of return affects the optimal level of investment.

Land Market and Economic Rent

Land is perfectly inelastic in supply; its price (rent) is determined entirely by demand — changes in demand for land change rent but not quantity; all earnings of a fixed-supply factor above opportunity cost are economic rent.

Common misconception: Students confuse rent (payment for land) with economic rent (any payment above opportunity cost); economic rent applies to any factor with inelastic supply, not just land.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to draw a perfectly inelastic supply of land and show how rent is determined by demand.

Human Capital

Human capital is the accumulated education, training, and skills of workers; investment in human capital raises MRP and therefore wages.

Common misconception: Students think any schooling is always profitable investment in human capital; the return depends on the wage premium earned minus the cost of education, which varies by field and institution.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask how investment in human capital affects labor demand, wages, and MRP.

Key Vocabulary
  • Physical Capital — Human-made inputs used in production such as machinery, equipment, and buildings.
  • Rate of Return — The expected annual profit from an investment as a percentage of its cost; firms invest when rate of return > real interest rate.
  • Economic Rent — Payment to a factor above its opportunity cost; all earnings of a perfectly inelastic factor are economic rent.
  • Human Capital — The knowledge, skills, and education embodied in workers that raise their marginal product and wages.
  • Loanable Funds Market — The market where investment demand (borrowers) meets saving supply (lenders), determining the real interest rate.
Objectives
  • Explain how firms decide how much capital to invest using expected rate of return vs. interest rate.
  • Draw a land market diagram and explain why all land rent is economic rent.
  • Describe how human capital investment affects wages.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask how interest rate changes affect investment and capital demand.
  • FRQs ask students to draw a perfectly inelastic supply curve for land and identify the economic rent.
  • Questions connect human capital investment to higher MRP and wages.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw the land market with perfectly inelastic supply; show how a rise in demand raises rent but not quantity.
  • If the interest rate rises from 3% to 6%, explain how a firm's investment decision changes.

Monopsony in Labor Markets

Learn on this site
Monopsony Characteristics

A monopsony is a market with a single buyer of labor; the monopsonist faces an upward-sloping labor supply curve and must raise wages to attract more workers, making MRC > wage for all units beyond the first.

Common misconception: Students think monopsony means one employer in a geographic area; any firm with significant power to set wages below competitive levels exhibits monopsony power.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to identify monopsony characteristics and contrast them with competitive labor markets.

Monopsony Equilibrium

A monopsonist hires where MRP = MRC (not where MRP = wage); since MRC > wage, the monopsonist hires fewer workers and pays a lower wage than in a competitive labor market.

Common misconception: Students confuse where the monopsonist sets MRC = MRP with where they read the wage; employment is set at MRC = MRP, then wage is read from the supply curve at that employment level.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to draw a monopsony diagram and identify the profit-maximizing employment level, wage, and deadweight loss.

Minimum Wage Under Monopsony

In a monopsony labor market, a minimum wage set between the competitive and monopsonistic wage can actually increase both wages and employment, unlike in a competitive market.

Common misconception: Students think minimum wages always reduce employment; under monopsony, a minimum wage can correct the market failure and increase employment.

How it can be tested: MCQs test the counterintuitive result that minimum wages can increase employment in a monopsony.

Key Vocabulary
  • Monopsony — A labor market with a single dominant buyer (employer) that has the power to set wages below the competitive level.
  • Marginal Resource Cost (Monopsony) — For a monopsonist, MRC > wage because hiring more workers requires paying all workers a higher wage.
  • Wage Exploitation — The gap between MRP and the monopsonistic wage; workers are paid less than the value of their marginal product.
  • Bilateral Monopoly — A labor market where a monopsony employer faces a monopoly union; wage outcome depends on bargaining power.
  • Monopsony Deadweight Loss — The loss of surplus from monopsonistic under-hiring compared to competitive equilibrium.
Objectives
  • Explain why MRC > wage for a monopsonist.
  • Find the profit-maximizing employment level and wage in a monopsony.
  • Explain the paradox that a minimum wage can increase employment in a monopsony.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs provide a monopsony diagram (supply, MRC, MRP) and ask for employment, wage, and DWL.
  • MCQs ask students to compare monopsony outcomes to competitive outcomes.
  • Questions ask the effect of a minimum wage on employment under monopsony.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw a monopsony diagram with upward-sloping supply and MRC above supply; identify Q*, W*, and DWL.
  • Explain why a hospital in a small town may have monopsony power over nurses.
