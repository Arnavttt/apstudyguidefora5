Unit 6 of AP® Microeconomics

Market Failure and the Role of Government

Externalities, public goods, and information failures cause markets to allocate resources inefficiently, creating a role for government correction.

Unit learning gateway

  • Negative externalities lead to overproduction; positive externalities lead to underproduction.
  • Pigouvian taxes and subsidies can internalize externalities.

Externalities

Learn on this site
Negative Externalities

A negative externality occurs when a third party bears costs from a market transaction; the social cost (MSC) exceeds private cost (MPC), leading to overproduction and deadweight loss.

Common misconception: Students think externalities are just "side effects"; negative externalities cause market overproduction because firms ignore the full social cost.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to draw a negative externality, identify the market and socially optimal output, and show deadweight loss.

Positive Externalities

A positive externality benefits third parties not in the transaction; social benefit (MSB) exceeds private benefit (MPB), leading to underproduction and deadweight loss.

Common misconception: Students assume externalities are always negative; education, vaccination, and R&D create positive externalities causing market underproduction.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to draw a positive externality, identify underproduction, and recommend a government subsidy.

Government Corrections: Taxes and Subsidies

A Pigouvian tax on a negative externality equal to the marginal external cost shifts MPC up to MSC, achieving the socially optimal output; a subsidy equal to marginal external benefit shifts demand up to MSB.

Common misconception: Students confuse the direction of the shift for tax vs. subsidy; a tax increases costs (shifts supply left), while a subsidy increases demand or decreases costs.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask what type of government intervention corrects a given externality and in which direction it shifts the curve.

Key Vocabulary
  • Externality — A cost or benefit from a transaction that falls on parties outside the transaction (third parties).
  • Negative Externality — A cost imposed on third parties; causes the market to overproduce relative to the social optimum.
  • Positive Externality — A benefit received by third parties; causes the market to underproduce relative to the social optimum.
  • Pigouvian Tax — A tax equal to the marginal external cost that corrects negative externality overproduction.
  • Marginal Social Cost — The total cost to society of producing one more unit; MSC = MPC + marginal external cost.
Objectives
  • Distinguish between private and social costs/benefits.
  • Identify overproduction (negative externality) and underproduction (positive externality) on a diagram.
  • Recommend the correct government policy to correct each type of externality.
How this can be tested
  • FRQs show an externality diagram and ask students to identify the socially optimal output and DWL.
  • MCQs ask the type of externality present and the correct corrective policy.
  • Questions ask students to calculate the optimal Pigouvian tax given MPC and MSC schedules.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw a negative externality for a coal plant; identify market output, socially optimal output, and DWL.
  • Draw a positive externality for vaccination; identify underproduction and recommend a subsidy.

Public Goods and Common Resources

Learn on this site
Characteristics of Public Goods

Public goods are non-rival (one person's use doesn't reduce availability) and non-excludable (cannot prevent non-payers from consuming); these features create the free-rider problem and market underprovision.

Common misconception: Students confuse public goods with government-provided goods; a good is "public" based on its economic characteristics (non-rival + non-excludable), not who provides it.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to classify goods as public, private, club, or common resource based on rivalry and excludability.

Free-Rider Problem

Because public goods are non-excludable, people can consume them without paying, leading to underprovision by private markets; government provision funded by taxes solves the free-rider problem.

Common misconception: Students think free-rider means someone who rides for free; in economics, it means people who benefit without paying, causing the good to be underprovided.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to explain why a specific good is underprovided by private markets and recommend government provision.

Common Resources and the Tragedy of the Commons

Common resources are rival but non-excludable; each user imposes a negative externality on others, leading to overuse — the tragedy of the commons.

Common misconception: Students confuse common resources with public goods; both are non-excludable, but common resources are rival (one person's use reduces availability for others), leading to depletion.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to identify common resources and explain the tragedy of the commons.

Key Vocabulary
  • Public Good — A good that is both non-rival and non-excludable; undersupplied by private markets due to the free-rider problem.
  • Non-Rival — One person's consumption does not reduce the amount available for others (e.g., national defense).
  • Non-Excludable — It is impossible to prevent people from consuming the good once it is provided.
  • Free-Rider Problem — The tendency to benefit from a public good without paying for it, leading to market underprovision.
  • Tragedy of the Commons — The overuse of common resources because individual users do not bear the full social cost of their consumption.
Objectives
  • Classify goods using the rival/excludable matrix.
  • Explain the free-rider problem and why it causes market failure.
  • Describe the tragedy of the commons and a policy to prevent overuse.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask students to classify national defense, fish in the ocean, cable TV, and pizza by good type.
  • FRQs ask students to explain why private markets underprovide public goods.
  • Questions ask for policy solutions to the tragedy of the commons.
Practice before moving on
  • Classify 8 goods (national defense, cable TV, a hotdog, fish in the ocean, etc.) in the rival/excludable matrix.
  • Explain why the free-rider problem prevents private firms from profitably providing a new city park.

Asymmetric Information

Learn on this site
Adverse Selection

Adverse selection occurs before a transaction when one party has better information; in insurance markets, high-risk individuals are more likely to buy insurance, raising costs for everyone.

Common misconception: Students think adverse selection means "bad selection"; it means the market selects the "wrong" (high-risk) participants due to information asymmetry before the contract.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to identify adverse selection scenarios and explain why they lead to market failure.

Moral Hazard

Moral hazard occurs after a transaction when the insured party takes more risk because they bear less of the cost; e.g., insured drivers may drive more carelessly.

Common misconception: Students confuse adverse selection (pre-transaction information problem) with moral hazard (post-transaction behavior change).

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to distinguish adverse selection from moral hazard and provide examples of each.

Remedies for Asymmetric Information

Government remedies include mandatory disclosure (product safety requirements), licensing (medical and legal credentials), and insurance mandates; private remedies include signaling (degrees, warranties) and screening.

Common misconception: Students think only government can solve information asymmetry; private markets also develop signaling and screening mechanisms.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to match a specific remedy (warranty, licensing, mandatory insurance) to the information problem it addresses.

Key Vocabulary
  • Asymmetric Information — A situation where one party in a transaction has more or better information than the other.
  • Adverse Selection — A pre-transaction problem where the party with more information self-selects in a way that is harmful (e.g., sicker people buying health insurance).
  • Moral Hazard — A post-transaction problem where the insured party takes more risk because they are protected from the full consequences.
  • Signaling — Actions taken by informed parties to credibly communicate their quality to uninformed parties (e.g., education as a signal of ability).
  • Screening — Actions taken by uninformed parties to learn the private information of the informed party (e.g., insurance medical exams).
Objectives
  • Define and distinguish adverse selection from moral hazard.
  • Provide real-world examples of each type of information problem.
  • Explain both private and public remedies for information asymmetry.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs present a scenario and ask whether it illustrates adverse selection or moral hazard.
  • FRQs ask students to explain why a specific market fails due to information asymmetry.
  • Questions ask students to identify the remedy and whether it is a private or public solution.
Practice before moving on
  • Explain how adverse selection affects the market for used cars (the "lemons" problem).
  • Give an example of moral hazard in health insurance and suggest one policy remedy.

Income Distribution and Government Policy

Learn on this site
Measuring Income Inequality

The Lorenz curve shows the cumulative share of income received by the bottom x% of households; the Gini coefficient measures overall inequality (0 = perfect equality, 1 = maximum inequality).

Common misconception: Students read the Lorenz curve incorrectly; the further the curve bows below the line of perfect equality, the greater the inequality.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to interpret Lorenz curves and explain what a higher Gini coefficient implies.

Causes of Income Inequality

Income inequality arises from differences in education and human capital, inherited wealth, discrimination, market power (monopsony), and luck; both market forces and institutional factors contribute.

Common misconception: Students attribute inequality entirely to one cause (e.g., discrimination); multiple factors contribute and the relative importance varies by country and time period.

How it can be tested: FRQs ask students to identify and explain specific causes of income inequality in a given scenario.

Government Redistribution Policies

Government reduces inequality through progressive taxation, transfer payments (welfare, food stamps), provision of public goods, and anti-discrimination laws; each policy involves efficiency-equity trade-offs.

Common misconception: Students think redistribution is always "free"; taxes can distort incentives and reduce efficiency, creating a trade-off between equity and allocative efficiency.

How it can be tested: MCQs ask students to identify redistribution policies and their efficiency trade-offs.

Key Vocabulary
  • Lorenz Curve — A graph showing the cumulative share of income received by cumulative percentages of the population; further from the diagonal means greater inequality.
  • Gini Coefficient — A measure of income inequality equal to the area between the Lorenz curve and the line of perfect equality divided by the total area under the line; ranges from 0 to 1.
  • Progressive Tax — A tax system where the average tax rate rises with income, reducing income inequality.
  • Transfer Payments — Government payments to households (welfare, Social Security, Medicaid) that redistribute income.
  • Equity-Efficiency Trade-off — The tension between redistributing income fairly (equity) and maintaining incentives for productive activity (efficiency).
Objectives
  • Read and interpret a Lorenz curve and Gini coefficient.
  • Identify at least four causes of income inequality.
  • Evaluate redistribution policies using the equity-efficiency trade-off.
How this can be tested
  • MCQs ask students to compare two Lorenz curves and identify which shows greater inequality.
  • FRQs ask students to explain a government policy to reduce inequality and identify one efficiency cost.
  • Questions connect income inequality to factor market imperfections like monopsony and discrimination.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw two Lorenz curves (one more unequal); label the Gini coefficients and explain the difference.
  • Evaluate a progressive income tax: how does it reduce inequality and what efficiency cost does it impose?
