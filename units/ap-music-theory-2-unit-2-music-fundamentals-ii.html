Intervals

Learn on this site
Measuring Intervals

An interval is the distance between two pitches. Count the number of letter names (including both notes) for the generic name; then count semitones to determine quality (perfect, major, minor, etc.).

Common misconception: Students count only the space between notes, forgetting to include both endpoints — C to E is a third, not a second.

How it can be tested: Identify intervals by name and quality from notation or letter names.

Interval Quality

Perfect intervals (unison, 4th, 5th, octave) can be diminished or augmented. Major/minor intervals (2nd, 3rd, 6th, 7th) can be major, minor, diminished, or augmented. A diminished interval is one half step smaller than perfect/minor; augmented is one half step larger than perfect/major.

Common misconception: Students confuse the rules for perfect vs. major/minor intervals; perfect intervals cannot be "major" or "minor."

How it can be tested: Classify an interval as P, M, m, A, or d; invert intervals correctly (P stays P, M↔m, A↔d).

Key Vocabulary
  • interval — The distance in pitch between two notes
  • perfect interval — Intervals of unison, 4th, 5th, and octave; associated with a stable, open sound
  • major interval — Interval that is a half step larger than a minor interval of the same number
  • tritone — The augmented 4th or diminished 5th — exactly 6 half steps; highly dissonant
Objectives
  • Name any interval on sight
  • Construct a requested interval above or below a given pitch
  • Invert intervals correctly
How this can be tested
  • Invert a major 3rd
  • Build a perfect 5th above D
  • Identify a diminished 7th
Practice before moving on
  • Fill an interval identification worksheet covering all diatonic and chromatic intervals

Triads and Seventh Chords

Learn on this site
Triad Quality

Triads are three-note chords built in thirds. Quality is determined by the intervals: major (M3+m3), minor (m3+M3), augmented (M3+M3), diminished (m3+m3).

Common misconception: Students rely only on "major sounds happy, minor sounds sad" — on the exam, they must build and identify triads by interval content, not by listening.

How it can be tested: Build and identify major, minor, augmented, and diminished triads from any root.

Seventh Chords

A seventh chord adds a 7th above the root to a triad. Types: major-major (MM7, e.g., C-E-G-B), dominant (Mm7, e.g., G-B-D-F), minor-minor (mm7), half-diminished (dm7 or ø7), fully-diminished (dd7 or °7).

Common misconception: Students confuse the half-diminished (dm7) and fully-diminished (°7) seventh chords; the former has a diminished triad plus a minor 7th, the latter has a diminished triad plus a diminished 7th.

How it can be tested: Identify seventh chord quality from notation; build any requested seventh chord.

Key Vocabulary
  • major triad — Root + major third + perfect fifth (M3 + m3)
  • minor triad — Root + minor third + perfect fifth (m3 + M3)
  • diminished triad — Root + minor third + diminished fifth (m3 + m3)
  • dominant seventh — Major triad + minor seventh (Mm7); the chord most strongly implying resolution to tonic
  • leading tone — Scale degree 7 — a half step below the tonic, creates strong pull toward resolution
Objectives
  • Build major, minor, diminished, and augmented triads
  • Identify seventh chord types
  • Explain why the dominant seventh chord creates tension
How this can be tested
  • Identify chord quality from a score excerpt
  • Build a Mm7 chord above D
  • Spell a fully-diminished seventh chord on B
Practice before moving on
  • Analyze chord quality for all triads built on each scale degree of C major
