Roman Numeral Analysis and Chord Inversions

Learn on this site
Roman Numerals in Major and Minor

Roman numerals label chords by scale degree: I, II, III, IV, V, VI, VII. Uppercase = major; lowercase = minor; ° = diminished; + = augmented. In minor keys, common chords use raised 7th (harmonic minor).

Common misconception: Students use uppercase for all chords in minor; the chords built on each scale degree in minor must be derived from the actual scale in use.

How it can be tested: Analyze a short chord progression with Roman numerals in both major and minor.

Chord Inversions and Figured Bass

Root position has the root in the bass. First inversion (6) has the third in the bass. Second inversion (6/4) has the fifth in the bass. Figured bass numbers indicate intervals above the bass note.

Common misconception: Students confuse the figure "6" with a sixth interval in the chord; the 6 indicates a first inversion, specifying what interval appears above the bass.

How it can be tested: Read figured bass notation and write the correct chord voicing; identify inversions from notation.

Key Vocabulary
  • root position — Chord with the root in the bass voice
  • first inversion — Chord with the third in the bass; figured bass symbol "6"
  • second inversion — Chord with the fifth in the bass; figured bass symbol "6/4"; used in specific contexts (cadential, passing, pedal)
  • figured bass — Numbers below a bass line indicating which intervals to build above it
Objectives
  • Label any chord with Roman numeral and inversion symbol
  • Write a chord from figured bass notation
  • Explain when to use second inversion chords
How this can be tested
  • Analyze a 4-measure Bach chorale excerpt with Roman numerals
  • Write I6 and V6/4 chords in D major
Practice before moving on
  • Analyze the chords in C major: C-E-G, F-A-C, G-B-D, C-E-G using Roman numerals
  • Identify whether E-G-C in C major is I6 or IV in root position

SATB Voice Leading Fundamentals

Learn on this site
Range and Spacing in SATB

Each voice has a standard range: Soprano C4–G5, Alto G3–C5, Tenor C3–G4, Bass E2–C4. Voices should stay within these ranges and avoid large leaps (especially augmented/diminished intervals).

Common misconception: Students write soprano notes in the tenor range or create chord spellings with wide gaps; SATB spacing follows specific guidelines for consonance.

How it can be tested: Check voice ranges and spacing in a given SATB setting; rewrite with corrections.

Parallel Motion and Voice Independence

Parallel perfect octaves and fifths between any two voices are prohibited because they destroy the independence of those voices, making them sound like one. Contrary motion is preferred to maintain independence.

Common misconception: Students confuse "parallel" with "similar" motion; parallel fifths means both voices move in the same direction by a perfect fifth each time, not just any fifth.

How it can be tested: Identify parallel fifths and octaves in a score; rewrite to avoid them.

Key Vocabulary
  • SATB — Four-part chorale texture: Soprano, Alto, Tenor, Bass
  • parallel fifths — When two voices both move by a perfect fifth in the same direction — prohibited in common practice
  • contrary motion — Two voices moving in opposite directions — preferred for voice independence
  • doubling — In SATB, which pitch of a chord is duplicated across voices; typically the root is doubled in root-position chords
Objectives
  • Write smooth SATB voice leading from I to V to I
  • Identify and correct parallel octaves or fifths
  • Apply proper doubling to triads
How this can be tested
  • Write a 4-chord I-IV-V-I progression in C major in SATB
  • Find and fix all voice-leading errors in a given chorale
Practice before moving on
  • Identify the voice leading error in this progression and explain how to fix it
