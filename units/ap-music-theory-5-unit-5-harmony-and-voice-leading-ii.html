Secondary Dominants

Learn on this site
Secondary Dominant Function

A secondary dominant chord (V/x) temporarily tonicizes a non-tonic chord. It contains the leading tone of the target chord. Example: V/V in C major is D major (D-F#-A), which strongly pulls to G.

Common misconception: Students confuse secondary dominants with borrowed chords; secondary dominants tonicize a chord within the key, while borrowed chords come from the parallel mode.

How it can be tested: Identify and label secondary dominants; write the correct resolution.

Resolution of Secondary Dominants

A secondary dominant must resolve to the chord it tonicizes: V/IV → IV; V/V → V; V/ii → ii. The secondary leading tone resolves upward by half step; the chordal seventh resolves downward by step.

Common misconception: Students write the secondary dominant but fail to resolve it correctly, especially when the seventh is involved.

How it can be tested: Write a secondary dominant with correct resolution in a given key.

Key Vocabulary
  • secondary dominant — A major triad or dominant seventh chord built on the fifth of a non-tonic chord, temporarily tonicizing that chord
  • tonicization — Briefly treating a chord other than the tonic as a temporary tonal center, usually for one or two chords
  • borrowed chord — A chord taken from the parallel major or minor key (mode mixture), not to be confused with a secondary dominant
  • applied chord — Another term for secondary dominant — a chord "applied" to make the next chord sound like a temporary tonic
Objectives
  • Identify secondary dominants from a score
  • Build V/V, V/IV, and V/ii in any key
  • Resolve secondary dominants correctly in SATB
How this can be tested
  • Analyze a Bach chorale excerpt for secondary dominants
  • Write a 4-measure progression that includes V7/V → V → I
Practice before moving on
  • Identify the secondary dominant in the progression I-V7/IV-IV-I in C major

Non-Chord Tones

Learn on this site
Types of Non-Chord Tones

Non-chord tones (NCTs) are notes that do not belong to the prevailing chord. Passing tone (PT): stepwise between chord tones. Neighbor tone (NT): step away and back. Suspension (SUS): held from previous chord, resolves down by step. Appoggiatura: approached by leap, resolved by step. Escape tone: step then leap away.

Common misconception: Students label all non-chord tones as "passing tones"; each type has specific approach and resolution requirements.

How it can be tested: Identify NCT type from its approach and resolution pattern; label correctly in a score.

Suspensions by Number

Suspensions are labeled by the intervals of preparation, suspension, and resolution: 4-3 (suspension of 4th resolving to 3rd), 7-6, 9-8, 2-3 (bass suspension). The suspension must be prepared (same pitch in previous chord) and resolved downward by step.

Common misconception: Students confuse 4-3 and 9-8 suspensions; the numbers describe the interval above the bass at each stage.

How it can be tested: Prepare and resolve 4-3 and 7-6 suspensions correctly; label them in a score.

Key Vocabulary
  • passing tone — NCT approached and left by step in the same direction
  • neighbor tone — NCT that steps away from a chord tone then returns to it
  • suspension — NCT held over from the previous chord, creating dissonance that resolves downward by step
  • appoggiatura — NCT approached by leap and left by step in the opposite direction
  • anticipation — NCT that arrives early — the note belongs to the next chord
Objectives
  • Identify NCT type from notation
  • Write a 4-3 suspension correctly
  • Distinguish accented from unaccented NCTs
How this can be tested
  • Label all NCTs in a short chorale excerpt
  • Add a 7-6 suspension to a V-I cadence
Practice before moving on
  • Identify the type of NCT in measure 2 of the given excerpt
