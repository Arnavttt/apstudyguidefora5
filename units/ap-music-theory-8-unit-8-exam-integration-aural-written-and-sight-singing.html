Sight Singing and Melodic Dictation

Learn on this site
Sight Singing Strategies

Establish the key and beat, then sing using solfège (Do-Re-Mi) or scale degree numbers. Look ahead, maintain steady tempo, and connect melodic intervals to familiar songs for challenging leaps.

Common misconception: Students rush or stop when they see a difficult interval; preparation — identifying the key, clef, and first pitch — prevents most errors.

How it can be tested: Sing prepared melodies; sight sing unfamiliar diatonic melodies; identify errors in a given melody.

Melodic Dictation Process

Listen for: (1) the starting pitch's scale degree, (2) melodic contour, (3) rhythmic patterns, (4) specific intervals. Write what you hear in fragments, filling gaps on subsequent hearings.

Common misconception: Students try to transcribe everything in one listening; the multi-pass approach (rhythm first, then pitches) is more reliable.

How it can be tested: Transcribe a short melody played 3 times; notate rhythm and pitches correctly.

Key Vocabulary
  • solfège — A system of syllables (Do, Re, Mi, Fa, Sol, La, Ti) assigned to scale degrees for sight singing
  • dictation — Writing down music as it is played — can be melodic, rhythmic, or harmonic
  • error detection — Identifying discrepancies between a performed melody and the printed score
  • tonic sol-fa — A moveable-do system where "Do" is always the tonic regardless of key
Objectives
  • Sing diatonic melodies at sight
  • Transcribe a short melody from dictation
  • Detect errors in a melody performed differently from the score
How this can be tested
  • Practice sight singing 8-measure melodies in major and minor keys
  • Do 3 rounds of 4-measure melodic dictation exercises
Practice before moving on

Harmonic Dictation and Part Writing

Learn on this site
Harmonic Dictation Strategies

Listen for: (1) bass line movement, (2) chord quality (major, minor, diminished), (3) inversions, (4) non-chord tones. Work from the bass up. Identify cadences first (ends of phrases), then fill in interior chords.

Common misconception: Students try to identify every chord on first hearing; identifying the bass line, cadence types, and phrase endings first makes chord identification easier.

How it can be tested: Complete a harmonic dictation by identifying chord quality, root, and inversion from recorded examples.

Free Response Part Writing

For part writing questions: check soprano for range/leaps, space bass-tenor 1.5 octave max, double the root in root-position triads, resolve the leading tone (7̂→1̂) and the chordal seventh (down by step), avoid parallel 5ths/8ths. Write I-V7-I with correct voice leading as a warm-up pattern.

Common misconception: Students forget to check all four voices for errors; a systematic check (parallel fifths, leading tone resolution, spacing, range) after writing prevents lost points.

How it can be tested: Complete a short part-writing question with figured bass or Roman numerals; check self for all common errors.

Key Vocabulary
  • harmonic dictation — Writing down the chords of a progression as heard — identifying root, quality, and inversion
  • part writing — Writing all four SATB voices to realize a given harmonic progression or figured bass
  • resolution — The movement of a dissonant or unstable tone to a consonant or stable one
  • voice crossing — When a lower voice moves above a higher voice or vice versa — a voice-leading error to avoid
Objectives
  • Identify chords from a recorded 4-chord progression
  • Complete an SATB part-writing exercise from Roman numerals
  • Check own part writing for all common errors
How this can be tested
  • Do 5 harmonic dictation exercises using I, IV, V, ii, vi
  • Complete a part-writing realization of a Bach chorale opening
Practice before moving on
  • Complete the following part-writing exercise: given Bass E, F, G, C; add the upper voices for I6-IV-V-I in C major
