Conditions for Simple Harmonic Motion

Learn on this site
Restoring Force

SHM occurs when the net force on an object is proportional to displacement and directed toward equilibrium: F = -kx.

Common misconception: Larger amplitude does not change the period of SHM.

How it can be tested: Free-response derivation of restoring force or period.

Hooke's Law

A spring exerts a restoring force F = -kx; the spring constant k (N/m) measures stiffness.

Common misconception: The minus sign means restoring, not that force is always negative.

How it can be tested: Calculate spring force given k and displacement.

Equilibrium and Displacement

At equilibrium the net force is zero; displacement x is measured from that point, and SHM oscillates symmetrically about it.

Common misconception: Equilibrium is not the same as rest position—the object passes through it with maximum speed.

How it can be tested: Identify equilibrium position on a force-displacement graph.

Key Vocabulary
  • Simple harmonic motion (SHM) — Oscillatory motion with restoring force proportional to displacement
  • Restoring force — Net force directed toward equilibrium
  • Spring constant k — Stiffness of a spring in N/m
  • Amplitude A — Maximum displacement from equilibrium
  • Equilibrium position — Position where net force is zero
Objectives
  • State the condition for SHM in terms of force and displacement.
  • Apply Hooke's law F = -kx to find force or displacement.
  • Identify equilibrium on force-displacement graphs.
How this can be tested
  • Multiple choice on identifying SHM vs. non-SHM systems.
  • Calculation of spring force given k and stretch.
  • Free-response: describe restoring force for a spring-mass system.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice identifying which systems exhibit SHM.
  • Calculate spring forces for several values of k and x.

Period and Frequency of Oscillation

Learn on this site
Period of a Spring-Mass System

T = 2π√(m/k); period increases with mass and decreases with spring stiffness.

Common misconception: Period does not depend on amplitude or gravitational acceleration for a spring.

How it can be tested: Calculate period given m and k, or solve for m given T and k.

Period of a Simple Pendulum

T = 2π√(L/g); period depends only on length and gravitational field, not mass.

Common misconception: Heavier pendulums do NOT swing faster—mass cancels in the derivation.

How it can be tested: Compare pendulum periods at different gravitational fields.

Frequency and Angular Frequency

Frequency f = 1/T (Hz); angular frequency ω = 2πf = √(k/m) for springs and √(g/L) for pendulums.

Common misconception: Angular frequency ω is in rad/s, not Hz—they are related by 2π.

How it can be tested: Convert between T, f, and ω in problems.

Key Vocabulary
  • Period T — Time for one complete oscillation (s)
  • Frequency f — Oscillations per second (Hz)
  • Angular frequency ω — Rate of oscillation in rad/s; ω = 2πf
  • Simple pendulum — Mass on a string where T = 2π√(L/g)
  • Spring-mass system — Block on spring where T = 2π√(m/k)
Objectives
  • Calculate period and frequency for spring and pendulum systems.
  • Explain why pendulum period is independent of mass.
  • Convert between T, f, and ω.
How this can be tested
  • Multiple choice comparing pendulum periods at different lengths.
  • Calculation: find new period when mass or spring constant changes.
  • Free-response: predict period change if taken to the Moon.
Practice before moving on
  • Derive T for spring-mass and pendulum from memory.
  • Practice unit conversions between T, f, and ω.

Energy in Simple Harmonic Motion

Learn on this site
Energy Conservation in SHM

Total mechanical energy E = ½kA² is constant; it converts between PE = ½kx² and KE = ½mv².

Common misconception: Energy is NOT zero at maximum displacement—it is all potential.

How it can be tested: Find velocity at any position using energy conservation.

Maximum Speed and Acceleration

Maximum speed v_max = Aω occurs at equilibrium; maximum acceleration a_max = Aω² occurs at maximum displacement.

Common misconception: Maximum speed is NOT at maximum displacement—it is at equilibrium.

How it can be tested: Calculate v_max and a_max from amplitude and ω.

Position, Velocity, and Acceleration Functions

x(t) = A cos(ωt), v(t) = -Aω sin(ωt), a(t) = -Aω² cos(ωt); each is a sinusoidal function shifted by 90°.

Common misconception: Velocity and acceleration are not in phase with position—they are 90° and 180° offset.

How it can be tested: Read or sketch position/velocity/acceleration graphs for SHM.

Key Vocabulary
  • Elastic potential energy — PE = ½kx² stored in a spring
  • Total mechanical energy — E = ½kA² = KE + PE in SHM
  • v_max — Maximum speed at equilibrium = Aω
  • a_max — Maximum acceleration at amplitude = Aω²
  • Phase relationship — Velocity leads position by 90° in SHM
Objectives
  • Apply energy conservation to find speed at any position in SHM.
  • Identify positions of maximum KE, PE, speed, and acceleration.
  • Read and interpret x-t, v-t, and a-t graphs for SHM.
How this can be tested
  • Multiple choice: identify where KE and PE are maximum or minimum.
  • Calculation: find speed at x = A/2 using energy methods.
  • Free-response: graph position, velocity, and acceleration over time.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice energy bar charts for SHM at different positions.
  • Calculate v and a at several positions given A, k, m.

Applications: Coupled Oscillators and Real Systems

Learn on this site
Damping and Driven Oscillations

Damping removes energy from an oscillator, reducing amplitude; driven oscillations occur when an external periodic force is applied.

Common misconception: Lightly damped systems still oscillate—they just lose amplitude over time, not frequency.

How it can be tested: Conceptual questions on how damping changes amplitude vs. period.

Resonance

Resonance occurs when the driving frequency equals the natural frequency of the system; amplitude increases dramatically.

Common misconception: Resonance does not mean infinite amplitude in reality—damping limits it.

How it can be tested: Identify resonance conditions in physical scenarios.

Springs in Series and Parallel

Springs in series: 1/k_eff = 1/k₁ + 1/k₂ (softer); springs in parallel: k_eff = k₁ + k₂ (stiffer). Both affect the period.

Common misconception: Series springs are NOT stiffer—they stretch more for the same force.

How it can be tested: Calculate k_eff and new period for spring combinations.

Key Vocabulary
  • Damping — Force opposing motion that removes energy from an oscillator
  • Driven oscillation — Oscillation maintained by a periodic external force
  • Resonance — Maximum amplitude when driving frequency equals natural frequency
  • Natural frequency — Frequency at which a system freely oscillates
  • Series/parallel springs — Combinations that change effective spring constant
Objectives
  • Describe the effects of damping on amplitude and period.
  • Identify conditions for resonance and explain its significance.
  • Calculate effective spring constant for series and parallel combinations.
How this can be tested
  • Multiple choice on resonance in physical systems.
  • Calculation of k_eff and period for springs in series or parallel.
  • Free-response: describe effect of increasing damping on motion.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice k_eff calculations for spring combinations.
  • Research real-world examples of resonance (bridges, instruments).
