Pressure in Fluids

Learn on this site
Fluid Pressure

Pressure P = F/A (Pa = N/m²); in a static fluid P = P₀ + ρgh, where ρ is fluid density, g is gravitational field, and h is depth.

Common misconception: Pressure depends on depth, not the total amount of fluid or container shape.

How it can be tested: Calculate pressure at depth in a fluid column.

Atmospheric Pressure

Atmospheric pressure (≈ 101 325 Pa = 1 atm) acts on all surfaces exposed to the atmosphere; gauge pressure is pressure above atmospheric.

Common misconception: Gauge pressure is relative to atmosphere—absolute pressure includes atmosphere.

How it can be tested: Distinguish gauge from absolute pressure in problems.

Pascal's Law

A pressure change applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally to all parts of the fluid; the basis for hydraulic systems.

Common misconception: Pascal's law applies to static enclosed fluids, not open channels.

How it can be tested: Solve hydraulic lift problems using F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂.

Key Vocabulary
  • Pressure — Force per unit area: P = F/A (Pa)
  • Hydrostatic pressure — Pressure in a fluid at rest: P = P₀ + ρgh
  • Gauge pressure — Pressure above atmospheric pressure
  • Pascal's law — Pressure change transmitted uniformly in enclosed fluid
  • Hydraulic system — Machine using Pascal's law to multiply force
Objectives
  • Calculate pressure at a given depth in a liquid.
  • Distinguish gauge pressure from absolute pressure.
  • Apply Pascal's law to hydraulic lifts.
How this can be tested
  • Multiple choice: compare pressures at different depths or container shapes.
  • Calculation of force output in a hydraulic system.
  • Free-response: explain why pressure depends on depth not volume.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice pressure calculations at multiple depths.
  • Solve hydraulic system problems for force and distance.

Buoyancy and Archimedes' Principle

Learn on this site
Archimedes' Principle

The buoyant force on an object equals the weight of fluid displaced: F_b = ρ_fluid × V_displaced × g.

Common misconception: Buoyant force equals displaced fluid weight, not object weight.

How it can be tested: Calculate buoyant force given displaced volume and fluid density.

Floating and Sinking Conditions

Object floats if ρ_object < ρ_fluid; sinks if ρ_object > ρ_fluid; neutrally buoyant if equal. For floating objects, F_b = weight of object.

Common misconception: A floating object displaces its own weight in fluid, not its own volume.

How it can be tested: Determine fraction of object submerged using density ratio.

Buoyant Force and Net Force

Net upward force on a submerged object = F_b - W_object = (ρ_fluid - ρ_object) × V × g; this determines apparent weight.

Common misconception: Buoyant force does not depend on depth—only on displaced volume and fluid density.

How it can be tested: Calculate apparent weight of submerged object.

Key Vocabulary
  • Buoyant force — Upward force on object equal to weight of displaced fluid
  • Archimedes' principle — F_b = ρ_fluid × V_disp × g
  • Apparent weight — Actual weight minus buoyant force
  • Neutral buoyancy — Object floats within fluid when densities are equal
  • Displacement — Volume of fluid pushed aside by submerged object
Objectives
  • Calculate buoyant force using Archimedes' principle.
  • Determine whether an object floats, sinks, or is neutrally buoyant.
  • Find the fraction of a floating object submerged.
How this can be tested
  • Multiple choice comparing buoyant forces for objects of different densities.
  • Calculation of apparent weight of submerged object.
  • Free-response: explain why a steel ship floats.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice buoyant force calculations for objects in water and oil.
  • Determine submerged fraction for various floating objects.

Pressure Measurement and Applications

Learn on this site
Manometers and Barometers

A manometer measures gauge pressure using a U-tube of fluid; a barometer measures atmospheric pressure using a sealed column of mercury or fluid.

Common misconception: The barometer reading depends on atmospheric pressure, not on the tube diameter.

How it can be tested: Read and interpret pressure from manometer and barometer diagrams.

Pressure in Connected Containers

In connected static fluids, pressure at the same height is equal throughout; this determines fluid levels in U-tubes.

Common misconception: The heavier (denser) fluid settles lower; equal pressure at same depth.

How it can be tested: Solve for unknown fluid heights in U-tube problems.

Surface Tension and Capillarity

Surface tension results from intermolecular cohesion; capillary action is the rise (or fall) of liquid in narrow tubes due to adhesion and cohesion.

Common misconception: Surface tension is not the same as atmospheric pressure—it is a surface property of liquids.

How it can be tested: Qualitative AP® questions on capillarity and surface tension.

Key Vocabulary
  • Manometer — U-tube device measuring gauge pressure using fluid height
  • Barometer — Device measuring atmospheric pressure using a fluid column
  • Surface tension — Inward cohesive force at the surface of a liquid
  • Capillary action — Rise of liquid in narrow tubes due to adhesion > cohesion
  • U-tube — Connected tube used to compare pressures using fluid levels
Objectives
  • Read pressure from manometer diagrams.
  • Apply equal-pressure-at-same-depth principle to connected fluids.
  • Explain surface tension and capillary action qualitatively.
How this can be tested
  • Multiple choice on U-tube fluid heights with different fluid densities.
  • Calculation of atmospheric pressure from barometer reading.
  • Free-response: sketch and analyze a manometer diagram.
Practice before moving on
  • Solve U-tube problems with two different fluids.
  • Explain capillary rise for water in glass vs. mercury in glass.

Fluid Statics in Real-World Contexts

Learn on this site
Submarines and Depth Control

Submarines control depth by adjusting buoyancy with ballast tanks: adding water increases weight, releasing water increases buoyancy.

Common misconception: Submarines do not change volume to control depth—they change mass by flooding tanks.

How it can be tested: Explain submarine buoyancy control qualitatively.

Atmospheric Layers and Pressure

Atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude because there is less air above; the pressure equation P = P₀ + ρgh applies approximately in thin layers.

Common misconception: Air density also decreases with altitude, so pressure drops faster than linearly.

How it can be tested: Qualitative explanation of why mountains have lower air pressure.

Blood Pressure and Medicine

Blood pressure is gauge pressure (mmHg); systolic/diastolic values describe peak/minimum pressure during heartbeats; measured relative to atmospheric pressure.

Common misconception: Blood pressure is gauge pressure, not absolute pressure.

How it can be tested: Apply fluid pressure concepts to biological/medical contexts.

Key Vocabulary
  • Ballast tank — Tank filled with water or air to adjust submarine buoyancy
  • Systolic pressure — Peak blood pressure during heart contraction
  • Diastolic pressure — Minimum blood pressure between heartbeats
  • mmHg — Millimeters of mercury; unit of blood pressure
  • Altitude effect — Air pressure decreases with altitude due to less air above
Objectives
  • Explain how submarines control depth using ballast tanks.
  • Apply P = ρgh to biological and atmospheric contexts.
  • Interpret blood pressure readings as gauge pressure.
How this can be tested
  • Multiple choice applying fluid statics to medical or engineering contexts.
  • Free-response: design a system to measure depth underwater.
  • Conceptual: explain why ears pop in airplane or at altitude.
Practice before moving on
  • Research how diving bells and submarines maintain pressure.
  • Calculate pressure at various blood-vessel heights in the body.
