Ideal Fluids and Fluid Flow

Learn on this site
Ideal Fluid Assumptions

An ideal fluid is incompressible (constant density), non-viscous (no friction), and has steady (laminar) flow. Real fluids deviate from these.

Common misconception: Real fluids have viscosity (internal friction) and can have turbulent flow at high speeds.

How it can be tested: Identify which assumptions are being made in a given fluid problem.

Streamlines and Flow Rate

Streamlines show the path of fluid particles in steady flow; they never cross. Volume flow rate Q = Av (m³/s) measures how much fluid passes a cross-section per second.

Common misconception: Streamlines represent velocity direction, not the speed—closer streamlines mean higher speed.

How it can be tested: Calculate volume flow rate from cross-section area and fluid speed.

Continuity Equation

For incompressible fluids in a pipe, A₁v₁ = A₂v₂ (continuity equation). Narrower pipe → higher speed.

Common misconception: The continuity equation is for volume flow rate, not mass, for incompressible fluids.

How it can be tested: Solve for speed or area at different points in a pipe using continuity.

Key Vocabulary
  • Ideal fluid — Incompressible, non-viscous fluid with laminar flow
  • Viscosity — Internal friction within a real fluid resisting flow
  • Streamline — Path of a fluid element in steady flow; never cross
  • Volume flow rate Q — Q = Av; volume of fluid passing per unit time (m³/s)
  • Continuity equation — A₁v₁ = A₂v₂ for incompressible flow in a pipe
Objectives
  • State the ideal fluid assumptions and identify deviations in real fluids.
  • Calculate volume flow rate from area and speed.
  • Apply continuity to find speed changes in pipes of varying cross-section.
How this can be tested
  • Multiple choice: predict speed change when pipe narrows.
  • Calculation of speed at a constriction using continuity.
  • Free-response: explain why a river flows faster in a narrow canyon.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice continuity equation problems with various pipe shapes.
  • Draw and interpret streamline diagrams.

Bernoulli's Equation

Learn on this site
Bernoulli's Principle

P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant along a streamline. Faster-moving fluid has lower pressure; this arises from energy conservation.

Common misconception: Bernoulli's equation applies along a streamline, not between different streamlines.

How it can be tested: Apply Bernoulli's equation at two points along a flow to find pressure or speed.

Applications of Bernoulli's Principle

Lift on wings (faster air above → lower pressure), Venturi meter (pressure drop in constriction), Pitot tube (speed from pressure difference), and spray atomizers all use Bernoulli's equation.

Common misconception: Airplane lift is more complex than just "Bernoulli"—angle of attack and Newtonian reaction also contribute.

How it can be tested: Identify which Bernoulli applications appear in AP® free-response.

Torricelli's Theorem

Speed of fluid exiting a hole in a tank at depth h below the surface: v = √(2gh). This is derived from Bernoulli's equation with the tank top as the reference.

Common misconception: Torricelli's theorem applies when the hole is small compared to the tank (so surface speed ≈ 0).

How it can be tested: Calculate efflux speed from hole depth using Torricelli's theorem.

Key Vocabulary
  • Bernoulli's equation — P + ½ρv² + ρgh = const; energy conservation in fluid flow
  • Dynamic pressure — ½ρv²; kinetic energy per unit volume of flowing fluid
  • Venturi effect — Pressure drop in a constriction due to increased flow speed
  • Lift — Net upward pressure force on a wing from faster airflow above
  • Torricelli's theorem — Efflux speed v = √(2gh) for fluid exiting a hole at depth h
Objectives
  • Apply Bernoulli's equation between two points in a flow.
  • Explain how faster fluid speed relates to lower pressure.
  • Calculate efflux speed using Torricelli's theorem.
How this can be tested
  • Multiple choice: predict pressure change when fluid speeds up.
  • Calculation using Bernoulli's equation for connected pipes.
  • Free-response: design a Venturi meter and explain how it measures speed.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice Bernoulli's equation with combined continuity.
  • Calculate efflux speed for various hole positions in a tank.

Real Fluids: Viscosity and Turbulence

Learn on this site
Viscosity

Viscosity is a fluid's resistance to flow (internal friction); it causes a velocity gradient across a pipe, with fluid moving fastest at the center.

Common misconception: Viscous flow does not violate continuity — total volume flow rate is still conserved.

How it can be tested: Qualitative questions on how viscosity affects flow in pipes.

Poiseuille's Law (Qualitative)

Flow rate through a pipe Q ∝ r⁴ × ΔP / (μL); flow drops dramatically with smaller radius. Used in medicine (arteries, syringes).

Common misconception: Because Q ∝ r⁴, halving the radius reduces flow to 1/16—a strong effect.

How it can be tested: Predict flow rate changes for different pipe radii qualitatively.

Turbulence and Reynolds Number

Turbulence occurs at high speeds or large dimensions; the Reynolds number Re = ρvL/μ predicts transition. High Re → turbulent; low Re → laminar.

Common misconception: Turbulence is not necessarily bad — it promotes mixing but increases resistance.

How it can be tested: Identify conditions likely to produce turbulent flow.

Key Vocabulary
  • Viscosity μ — Measure of fluid's resistance to flow (Pa·s)
  • Laminar flow — Smooth, layered flow with parallel streamlines
  • Turbulent flow — Chaotic irregular flow at high speed or Reynolds number
  • Poiseuille's law — Q ∝ r⁴ΔP/(μL) for viscous flow in a cylinder
  • Reynolds number Re — Dimensionless ratio predicting laminar vs. turbulent flow
Objectives
  • Describe the velocity profile in viscous pipe flow.
  • Predict qualitatively how radius changes affect flow rate (Poiseuille).
  • Identify conditions leading to turbulent vs. laminar flow.
How this can be tested
  • Multiple choice: predict effect of artery narrowing on blood flow rate.
  • Conceptual: why does reducing pipe radius by half drastically reduce flow?
  • Free-response: explain the significance of Reynolds number.
Practice before moving on
  • Research medical applications of Poiseuille's law (arteries, IV drips).
  • Compare viscosities of common fluids (water, honey, oil).

Fluid Dynamics Applications and Problem Solving

Learn on this site
Combined Bernoulli and Continuity

Most AP® fluid dynamics problems combine both equations. Set up Bernoulli between two points, use continuity to find unknown speeds, then solve for pressure or height.

Common misconception: Students often forget to use continuity to find v₂ before applying Bernoulli.

How it can be tested: Multi-step AP® free-response combining both equations.

Fluid Dynamics in the Human Body

Blood flow obeys continuity (A₁v₁ = A₂v₂) in the circulatory system; blood pressure varies with height in the body (P = P₀ + ρgh); heart acts as a pressure pump.

Common misconception: Blood pressure at the feet is higher than at the heart due to the added hydrostatic column.

How it can be tested: Apply fluid principles to physiological contexts.

Environmental Fluid Dynamics

Wind speed over hills, ocean currents, and river flow all follow fluid principles. Faster wind over a hill → lower pressure → can affect structures (roof lift).

Common misconception: Atmospheric flow is more complex than ideal fluid; Bernoulli is approximate.

How it can be tested: Qualitative application of Bernoulli to wind and weather.

Key Vocabulary
  • Pressure head — ρgh term in Bernoulli; hydrostatic contribution to pressure
  • Stagnation point — Point where fluid velocity is zero; maximum pressure
  • Efflux — Fluid exiting a container through an opening
  • Circulatory system — Closed-loop fluid system; obeys continuity and pressure
  • Draft — Low-pressure region created by fast-moving fluid (Bernoulli)
Objectives
  • Combine continuity and Bernoulli to solve multi-step problems.
  • Apply fluid principles to the circulatory system.
  • Explain qualitatively how wind creates lift on rooftops.
How this can be tested
  • Multi-part free-response combining continuity and Bernoulli.
  • Calculation of blood pressure at foot level given heart-level pressure.
  • Multiple choice on environmental Bernoulli applications.
Practice before moving on
  • Solve 3–4 multi-step pipe problems from past AP® exams.
  • Calculate blood pressure changes due to body position.
