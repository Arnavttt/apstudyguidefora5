Temperature, Thermal Energy, and Heat

Learn on this site
Temperature and Thermal Energy

Temperature is a measure of average kinetic energy of particles; thermal energy (internal energy) is the total kinetic energy of all particles. They are related but not the same.

Common misconception: A large cold object can have more thermal energy than a small hot object.

How it can be tested: Distinguish temperature from thermal energy in conceptual questions.

Heat Transfer

Heat Q is energy transferred between objects due to a temperature difference. Q flows from hot to cold until thermal equilibrium is reached. Sign convention: Q > 0 means heat added to the system.

Common misconception: Heat is a transfer of energy, not a substance — objects don't "contain heat."

How it can be tested: Apply sign conventions for heat in thermodynamics problems.

Thermal Equilibrium and the Zeroth Law

Two objects in thermal contact reach equilibrium when temperatures equalize. Zeroth Law: if A is in equilibrium with C, and B is with C, then A and B are in equilibrium with each other.

Common misconception: Thermal equilibrium means no heat flows — temperatures are equal, not necessarily energies.

How it can be tested: Apply the zeroth law to thermometry and calibration.

Key Vocabulary
  • Temperature — Measure of average random kinetic energy of particles
  • Thermal energy — Total internal kinetic energy of all particles in a substance
  • Heat Q — Energy transferred due to temperature difference (J)
  • Thermal equilibrium — State when no heat flows; temperatures are equal
  • Zeroth law — Two objects each in equilibrium with a third are in equilibrium with each other
Objectives
  • Distinguish temperature, thermal energy, and heat.
  • Apply the sign convention for heat (Q > 0 = added to system).
  • State and apply the zeroth law of thermodynamics.
How this can be tested
  • Multiple choice distinguishing temperature from thermal energy.
  • Conceptual: explain which object has more thermal energy.
  • Free-response: apply zeroth law to thermometer calibration.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice distinguishing thermal energy from temperature in examples.
  • Solve mixing problems using Q_lost = Q_gained.

Specific Heat and Calorimetry

Learn on this site
Specific Heat Capacity

Q = mcΔT; specific heat c (J/kg·K) is the heat per unit mass per degree. Water has an unusually high specific heat (4186 J/kg·K).

Common misconception: The "c" in Q = mcΔT is specific heat, not the speed of light.

How it can be tested: Calculate heat added or removed using Q = mcΔT.

Calorimetry

In a calorimeter, heat lost by hot object = heat gained by cold object: m₁c₁ΔT₁ = m₂c₂ΔT₂. This assumes no heat escapes.

Common misconception: The heat equation uses |ΔT| or sets Q_lost negative and Q_gained positive — don't double-sign.

How it can be tested: Solve mixing calorimetry problems to find equilibrium temperature.

Latent Heat and Phase Changes

During a phase change, temperature is constant; heat is used to change phase: Q = mL. L_fusion (melting) and L_vaporization (boiling) are tabulated values.

Common misconception: During a phase change, adding heat does NOT raise temperature — it converts phase.

How it can be tested: Calculate heat required for phase change using Q = mL.

Key Vocabulary
  • Specific heat c — Heat per unit mass per unit temperature change (J/kg·K)
  • Calorimetry — Measuring heat exchange using Q_lost = Q_gained
  • Latent heat L — Heat per unit mass during a phase change (J/kg)
  • Phase change — Melting, freezing, evaporation, or condensation at constant T
  • Q = mcΔT — Heat for temperature change; Q = mL for phase change
Objectives
  • Calculate heat transfer using Q = mcΔT.
  • Solve calorimetry problems for equilibrium temperature.
  • Calculate heat for phase changes using Q = mL.
How this can be tested
  • Multiple choice: find equilibrium temperature of two mixed materials.
  • Multi-step calculation combining temperature change and phase change.
  • Free-response: design a calorimetry experiment.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice calorimetry problems with different materials.
  • Calculate total heat to convert ice at −20°C to steam at 120°C.

Laws of Thermodynamics

Learn on this site
First Law: Energy Conservation

ΔU = Q - W; change in internal energy equals heat added minus work done by the system. Energy is conserved in all thermal processes.

Common misconception: W is work done BY the system; if system is compressed (work done ON it), W is negative.

How it can be tested: Apply ΔU = Q - W to thermodynamic processes.

Second Law: Direction of Heat Flow

Heat spontaneously flows from hot to cold, never the reverse. Entropy (disorder) of an isolated system always increases or stays the same.

Common misconception: The second law doesn't say heat can never flow from cold to hot—it says it can't do so spontaneously without work input (refrigerators need work).

How it can be tested: Identify processes that violate the second law.

Heat Engines and Efficiency

A heat engine converts heat from a hot reservoir to work, rejecting waste heat to a cold reservoir. Efficiency e = W/Q_H ≤ 1 - T_C/T_H (Carnot limit).

Common misconception: No heat engine can be 100% efficient — the Carnot efficiency is the theoretical maximum.

How it can be tested: Calculate efficiency of heat engines and identify Carnot limit.

Key Vocabulary
  • First law of thermodynamics — ΔU = Q − W; energy conservation for thermal systems
  • Second law of thermodynamics — Entropy of isolated systems never decreases; heat flows hot → cold
  • Entropy S — Measure of disorder or number of microstates in a system
  • Heat engine — Device converting heat to work with efficiency ≤ Carnot
  • Carnot efficiency — e_Carnot = 1 − T_C/T_H; maximum possible efficiency (temperatures in Kelvin)
Objectives
  • Apply the first law ΔU = Q - W to thermal processes.
  • State the second law and identify forbidden processes.
  • Calculate heat engine efficiency and compare to Carnot limit.
How this can be tested
  • Multiple choice: identify which process violates the second law.
  • Calculation of Carnot efficiency given reservoir temperatures.
  • Free-response: apply first and second laws to a cyclic process.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice ΔU = Q - W with signs for various processes.
  • Calculate efficiencies for engines and compare to Carnot maximum.

Heat Transfer Mechanisms

Learn on this site
Conduction

Conduction is heat transfer through direct particle collisions in a material. Rate ∝ k × A × ΔT / L (Fourier's law); k is thermal conductivity.

Common misconception: Metals conduct heat well not because they are "cold" but because free electrons transfer energy rapidly.

How it can be tested: Rank materials by thermal conductivity and explain insulation.

Convection

Convection transfers heat via bulk fluid motion; hot fluid rises (less dense), cool fluid sinks, creating convection currents. Natural and forced convection both occur.

Common misconception: Convection requires a fluid — it cannot occur in solids or a vacuum.

How it can be tested: Explain convection currents qualitatively in weather or heating systems.

Radiation

All objects emit electromagnetic radiation proportional to T⁴ (Stefan-Boltzmann law: P = εσAT⁴). Radiation requires no medium — it travels through vacuum.

Common misconception: All objects above absolute zero emit thermal radiation — even cold objects, just less.

How it can be tested: Compare radiation rates for objects at different temperatures.

Key Vocabulary
  • Thermal conductivity k — Material property determining heat conduction rate
  • Convection — Heat transfer by bulk fluid motion; hot fluid rises
  • Radiation — Heat transfer by electromagnetic waves; requires no medium
  • Stefan-Boltzmann law — P = εσAT⁴; radiated power depends strongly on temperature
  • Emissivity ε — Ratio of actual radiation to blackbody radiation (0 to 1)
Objectives
  • Compare conduction, convection, and radiation mechanisms.
  • Explain convection currents in weather and building heating.
  • Apply Stefan-Boltzmann law qualitatively to radiation problems.
How this can be tested
  • Multiple choice comparing heat transfer mechanisms.
  • Conceptual: identify which mechanism dominates in a given scenario.
  • Free-response: explain why a thermos bottle uses vacuum insulation.
Practice before moving on
  • List everyday examples of each heat transfer mechanism.
  • Explain why a fan makes you feel cooler (forced convection).
