Newton's First and Third Laws

Learn on this site
Newton's First Law (Inertia)

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity, unless acted on by a net external force. Inertia is the resistance to change in motion.

Common misconception: Students think objects need a force to keep moving; only a net force changes velocity — constant velocity requires zero net force.

How it can be tested: Identify scenarios where net force is zero and predict constant-velocity (or rest) motion.

Newton's Third Law (Action-Reaction)

For every force A exerts on B, B exerts an equal and opposite force on A. Action-reaction pairs act on different objects and never cancel each other.

Common misconception: Students think third-law pairs cancel; they act on different objects and cannot be combined into net force on one object.

How it can be tested: Identify Newton's third law pairs; explain why they don't cancel when finding net force on one object.

Identifying Forces

Common forces: gravity (Fg=mg, down), normal (FN, perpendicular to surface), tension (along rope), friction (along surface, opposing relative motion), applied force. All forces require physical contact or a field.

Common misconception: Students invent forces like "the force of motion" or "momentum force"; every force must have a physical agent.

How it can be tested: List and label all real forces on a free-body diagram; identify each force's agent.

Key Vocabulary
  • inertia — Resistance to change in motion; proportional to mass
  • Newton's first law — Zero net force → zero acceleration (constant velocity or rest)
  • action-reaction pair — Equal and opposite forces on different objects (Newton's 3rd law)
  • free-body diagram — Diagram showing all forces acting on a single object as vectors
Objectives
  • Apply Newton's first law to predict motion when net force is zero
  • Identify Newton's third law pairs in physical scenarios
  • Draw complete and accurate free-body diagrams
How this can be tested
  • Free-body diagrams required in almost every AP® Physics FR
  • Third-law pair identification in MC
  • First law scenarios: "why doesn't it keep accelerating?"
Practice before moving on
  • Draw FBD for a book resting on a table
  • Identify all third-law pairs when a horse pulls a cart

Newton's Second Law

Learn on this site
F_net = ma

The net force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration. Direction of acceleration matches direction of net force. Must sum all force components along each axis separately.

Common misconception: Students use the magnitude of individual forces without considering direction; net force requires vector addition.

How it can be tested: Set up ΣF=ma equations for x and y components separately; solve for unknown force or acceleration.

Mass and Weight

Weight W=mg is the gravitational force on an object (in Newtons). Mass is the amount of matter (in kg). Weight varies with g; mass does not.

Common misconception: Students use mass and weight interchangeably; weight is a force (N), mass is a scalar (kg).

How it can be tested: Calculate weight from mass; distinguish conditions where g differs (e.g., on the Moon).

Normal Force

The normal force is perpendicular to the surface and adjusts to prevent penetration. On a horizontal surface, FN=mg for a stationary object. On an incline, FN=mg cosθ. In an elevator, FN changes.

Common misconception: Students always set FN=mg regardless of context; FN varies whenever there is vertical acceleration or the surface is inclined.

How it can be tested: Calculate normal force for inclines, elevators, and curved surfaces.

Key Vocabulary
  • Newton's second law — ΣF = ma; net force causes acceleration
  • weight — W = mg; gravitational force in Newtons
  • normal force — Perpendicular surface force; FN = mg cosθ on incline
  • component method — Decompose forces into x and y components; apply ΣF=ma to each
Objectives
  • Apply F_net=ma to find acceleration or unknown forces
  • Distinguish mass from weight
  • Calculate normal force in various contexts (incline, elevator, curve)
How this can be tested
  • ΣF=ma is the core equation of AP® Physics 1 mechanics
  • Incline problems require force components along and perpendicular to ramp
  • Elevator normal force problems test understanding of apparent weight
Practice before moving on
  • A 4 kg block on a frictionless incline at 30°; find acceleration
  • A 70 kg person in an elevator accelerating up at 2 m/s²; find normal force

Friction

Learn on this site
Static and Kinetic Friction

Static friction (f_s ≤ μ_s·FN) prevents motion; it adjusts up to its maximum. Kinetic friction (f_k = μ_k·FN) acts on sliding objects and is constant. μ_s > μ_k always.

Common misconception: Students use kinetic friction when an object is not sliding; use static friction until the applied force exceeds μ_s·FN.

How it can be tested: Determine which friction applies; calculate maximum static and kinetic friction for given μ and FN.

Friction on Inclines

On an incline: FN=mg cosθ; friction force = μFN=μmg cosθ. Object slides if mg sinθ > μ_s·mg cosθ, i.e., tan θ > μ_s.

Common misconception: Students forget that FN=mg cosθ on an incline, not mg; friction depends on the normal force, which is reduced by the angle.

How it can be tested: Set up and solve friction-on-incline problems using correct FN expression.

Friction in Newton's Second Law Problems

Friction opposes the direction of relative motion (kinetic) or impending motion (static). Draw friction in the correct direction on the FBD, then sum forces along the axis of motion.

Common misconception: Students draw friction in the wrong direction; kinetic friction always opposes actual motion, not applied force direction.

How it can be tested: Correctly identify friction direction and include it in ΣF=ma equations.

Key Vocabulary
  • static friction — f_s ≤ μ_s·FN; prevents motion; adjusts as needed
  • kinetic friction — f_k = μ_k·FN; constant during sliding
  • coefficient of friction — μ; dimensionless ratio relating friction to normal force
  • friction on incline — f = μ·FN = μ·mg cosθ
Objectives
  • Calculate static and kinetic friction forces
  • Determine whether an object slides on an incline
  • Include friction correctly in F_net=ma problems
How this can be tested
  • Friction problems appear in FR as multi-part with FBD + calculation
  • Incline friction tested frequently in MC
  • Must distinguish static vs. kinetic friction based on context
Practice before moving on
  • Find if a 10 kg block slides on 30° incline with μ_s=0.6
  • A 5 kg block pulled at 15 N on μ_k=0.3 surface; find acceleration

Multi-Body Systems and Atwood Machines

Learn on this site
System Approach

For a system of connected objects, apply F_net=m_total·a if they move together. Then use individual object FBDs to find internal forces (like tension).

Common misconception: Students apply F=ma to each object individually for the system acceleration; using the total mass is faster and avoids sign errors.

How it can be tested: Use the system approach to find acceleration, then isolate one object to find the tension.

Atwood Machine

Two masses over a pulley: the heavier mass descends, lighter ascends. Net force = (m₁−m₂)g; total mass = m₁+m₂. Acceleration a=(m₁−m₂)g/(m₁+m₂). Tension T=2m₁m₂g/(m₁+m₂).

Common misconception: Students apply F=ma to the system including the pulley mass; ideal pulley has no mass and only redirects tension.

How it can be tested: Set up and solve Atwood machine problems using system equation and individual object equations.

Connected Objects with Friction

For a horizontal surface system: net force = applied force − friction on surface-contacting objects. Pulley redirects forces but does not change tension magnitude (ideal pulley).

Common misconception: Students add friction from all objects in the system; only objects touching the rough surface experience kinetic friction.

How it can be tested: Identify which objects have friction, set up system equation, and solve for acceleration and tension.

Key Vocabulary
  • system approach — Apply ΣF=m_total·a to find acceleration when objects move as one
  • Atwood machine — Two masses over pulley; a=(m₁−m₂)g/(m₁+m₂)
  • tension — Internal force in a rope/string; same throughout ideal (massless) rope
  • internal vs. external force — External force causes system acceleration; internal forces (tension) are found from individual FBDs
Objectives
  • Apply system method to find acceleration of connected objects
  • Solve Atwood machine for acceleration and tension
  • Find tension between objects in a horizontal two-body system
How this can be tested
  • Connected object problems in FR: system acceleration then tension
  • Atwood machine tested in both MC and FR
  • Must draw FBD for each object in FR
Practice before moving on
  • Two blocks: 8 kg pulled by 40 N, connected to 2 kg on frictionless surface; find a and T
  • Atwood: m₁=6 kg, m₂=4 kg; find a and T
