Work Done by Forces

Learn on this site
Definition of Work

W = F·d·cosθ where θ is the angle between force and displacement. Work is a scalar. Positive work when force has component in direction of motion; negative when opposite.

Common misconception: Students use W=Fd regardless of angle; if force is perpendicular to motion (e.g., normal force, centripetal), W=0.

How it can be tested: Calculate work done by each force in a scenario including gravity, friction, and applied forces.

Work Done by Gravity

W_gravity = −mg·Δh = mg(h_i − h_f). Positive when descending (force and displacement both have downward component), negative when ascending.

Common misconception: Students forget the sign of gravitational work; W_gravity = mgΔh only when downward displacement is positive.

How it can be tested: Calculate gravitational work for objects moving along ramps or arbitrary paths.

Net Work and Work-Energy Theorem

W_net = ΔKE = ½mv_f² − ½mv_i². All forces contributing work can be summed, or alternatively the net force can be used. Forces perpendicular to motion do no work.

Common misconception: Students sum work done by individual forces using incorrect angles; the work-energy theorem provides a powerful scalar alternative to F=ma.

How it can be tested: Apply work-energy theorem to find final speed or work done by an unknown force.

Key Vocabulary
  • work — W = Fdcosθ; scalar quantity, unit: Joules (J)
  • work-energy theorem — W_net = ΔKE = ½mv_f² − ½mv_i²
  • zero work — When force is perpendicular to motion (θ=90°)
  • gravitational work — W_g = −mgΔh; positive when descending
Objectives
  • Calculate work done by each force in various configurations
  • Apply work-energy theorem to find speed or distance
  • Identify forces that do no work
How this can be tested
  • Work by friction in FR: W_friction = f_k·d (negative)
  • Work-energy theorem replaces kinematics in many FR problems
  • Zero-work forces tested in MC
Practice before moving on
  • Find work done by gravity on a 5 kg block sliding 3 m down a 30° ramp
  • Apply W-E theorem to find final speed of pulled block
Key Formulas
  • Work: \( W = \vec{F}\cdot\vec{d} = Fd\cos\theta \)
  • Work–energy: \( W_{\text{net}} = \Delta KE \)

Kinetic and Potential Energy

Learn on this site
Kinetic Energy

KE = ½mv². Depends on both mass and speed. Doubling speed quadruples KE. KE is always ≥ 0.

Common misconception: Students think doubling mass doubles KE equivalently with doubling speed; KE scales linearly with m but quadratically with v.

How it can be tested: Compare kinetic energies of objects with different masses and speeds.

Gravitational Potential Energy

PE_g = mgh where h is height above a chosen reference level. The choice of reference level is arbitrary, but must be consistent. Only changes in PE (ΔPE) are physically meaningful.

Common misconception: Students think the reference level must be at ground level; any consistent reference is valid, and only ΔPE matters in energy conservation.

How it can be tested: Set reference level, calculate PE and ΔPE for objects at different heights.

Elastic Potential Energy

PE_spring = ½kx² where k is spring constant and x is compression/extension from equilibrium. Spring stores energy when compressed or stretched.

Common misconception: Students think PE_spring = kx (linear); it is ½kx², which increases with the square of deformation.

How it can be tested: Calculate elastic PE for compressed/stretched springs; use in energy conservation problems.

Key Vocabulary
  • kinetic energy — KE = ½mv²; energy of motion
  • gravitational PE — PE_g = mgh; energy due to height
  • elastic PE — PE_spring = ½kx²; energy stored in spring
  • reference level — Arbitrary zero for PE; only changes in PE matter
Objectives
  • Calculate KE, gravitational PE, and spring PE
  • Understand that only ΔPE is physically meaningful
  • Compare energies of objects with different masses and speeds
How this can be tested
  • Energy bar diagrams tested in AP® FR (show KE, PE, thermal energy)
  • Spring PE in both MC and FR
  • Reference level: stated or implied in FR problems
Practice before moving on
  • Compare KE of 2 kg at 6 m/s vs. 4 kg at 4 m/s
  • Find PE stored in spring (k=200 N/m) compressed 0.15 m
Key Formulas
  • KE: \( KE = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \)
  • Grav. PE: \( PE_g = mgh \)
  • Spring PE: \( PE_s = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2 \)

Conservation of Mechanical Energy and Power

Learn on this site
Conservation of Mechanical Energy

In the absence of non-conservative forces (friction, air resistance), total mechanical energy E = KE + PE is constant. ΔKE + ΔPE = 0, or KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f.

Common misconception: Students apply energy conservation when friction is present; friction converts mechanical energy to thermal energy, reducing total ME.

How it can be tested: Verify no non-conservative forces are present, then apply energy conservation to find speed or height.

Energy with Non-Conservative Forces

W_non-conservative = ΔE_mech = ΔKE + ΔPE. Friction does negative work equal to −f·d (energy converted to heat). Total energy (mechanical + thermal) is always conserved.

Common misconception: Students think energy is lost due to friction; energy is converted from mechanical to thermal, not lost.

How it can be tested: Apply energy methods with friction: KE_f = KE_i + PE_i − f_k·d (for descending case).

Power

P = W/t = Fv (for constant force and velocity). Unit: Watt (W) = J/s. Average power = ΔE/t. P = Fv when force and velocity are in same direction.

Common misconception: Students use P=Fv even when force and velocity are not parallel; use P = F·v·cosθ = F_parallel·v.

How it can be tested: Calculate power from work and time, and from force and velocity.

Key Vocabulary
  • conservation of ME — KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f (no friction)
  • non-conservative force — Force that converts mechanical energy to thermal (friction, air resistance)
  • thermal energy — ΔE_thermal = f_k·d; mechanical energy lost to friction
  • power — P = W/t = Fv; rate of energy transfer, Watts
Objectives
  • Apply conservation of mechanical energy to find speed or height
  • Account for energy lost to friction in energy equations
  • Calculate power from work/time and from F·v
How this can be tested
  • Energy conservation problems appear in every AP® Physics 1 exam
  • Friction energy losses tested in FR
  • Power calculations in both MC and FR
Practice before moving on
  • Ball rolls off table; find speed at bottom using energy conservation
  • Car engine exerts 3000 N at 20 m/s; find power output
Key Formulas
  • Energy cons.: \( KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f \)
  • Power: \( P = \dfrac{W}{t} = \vec{F}\cdot\vec{v} \)

Momentum and Impulse Preview

Learn on this site
Momentum

p = mv (vector). Unit: kg·m/s. Momentum depends on both mass and velocity. A heavy slow object can have the same momentum as a light fast object.

Common misconception: Students think momentum and kinetic energy are equivalent; p is linear in v, KE is quadratic. Doubling v doubles p but quadruples KE.

How it can be tested: Calculate momentum of objects and compare momenta in different situations.

Impulse-Momentum Theorem

J = FΔt = Δp. Impulse is the product of force and time over which it acts. A large force over short time or small force over long time can produce the same impulse.

Common misconception: Students confuse impulse with work; impulse = FΔt = Δp; work = Fd = ΔKE. Time vs. distance is the key difference.

How it can be tested: Apply impulse-momentum theorem to find change in momentum or average force.

Conservation of Momentum

In an isolated system (no external forces), total momentum is conserved: Σp_i = Σp_f. Applied to collisions and explosions.

Common misconception: Students apply momentum conservation when external forces are present; friction, gravity, or normal forces on the system can change total momentum.

How it can be tested: Verify the system is isolated, then apply momentum conservation to collisions.

Key Vocabulary
  • momentum — p = mv; vector, units kg·m/s
  • impulse — J = FΔt = Δp; units N·s = kg·m/s
  • impulse-momentum theorem — F_avg·Δt = mv_f − mv_i
  • conservation of momentum — Σp_i = Σp_f for isolated systems
Objectives
  • Calculate momentum and relate it to kinetic energy
  • Apply impulse-momentum theorem to find force or time
  • Apply momentum conservation to simple collision problems
How this can be tested
  • Momentum and impulse in Unit 4; preview here connects to energy
  • Impulse from area under F-t graph tested in MC
  • Conservation of momentum in FR every year
Practice before moving on
  • Find impulse needed to stop a 1000 kg car moving at 30 m/s
  • Two 5 kg carts collide; find final velocity after perfectly inelastic collision
Key Formulas
  • Momentum: \( \vec{p} = m\vec{v} \)
  • Impulse: \( \vec{J} = \vec{F}\,\Delta t = \Delta\vec{p} \)
