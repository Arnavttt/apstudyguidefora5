Impulse and Momentum in Depth

Learn on this site
Impulse from Variable Force

When force is not constant, J = area under the F-t graph = ∫F dt. A force-time graph can have triangles, rectangles, or other shapes; total impulse is the total area (signed).

Common misconception: Students use J=FΔt only for constant forces; for variable force, integrate (find area under curve).

How it can be tested: Calculate impulse from F-t graphs by finding the area under the curve.

Impulse and Stopping Time

For a given momentum change (Δp), longer stopping time means smaller average force: F_avg = Δp/Δt. This is the physics of seatbelts and airbags.

Common misconception: Students think more force always means faster stopping; impulse (Δp) is fixed by the collision, but time determines peak force.

How it can be tested: Analyze safety device scenarios using the impulse-momentum relationship.

Rocket Propulsion (Preview)

A rocket gains momentum by expelling mass at high velocity. Even with no external forces, the rocket accelerates because expelled exhaust provides an equal and opposite impulse. This is conservation of momentum.

Common misconception: Students think a rocket needs air to push against; rocket thrust is reaction to exhaust momentum, not air resistance.

How it can be tested: Explain rocket propulsion using Newton's third law and momentum conservation.

Key Vocabulary
  • impulse — J = FΔt = Δp = area under F-t graph
  • average force — F_avg = Δp/Δt; larger time → smaller force for same Δp
  • F-t graph area — Total impulse = signed area under force-time graph
  • safety devices — Extend collision time to reduce peak force for given Δp
Objectives
  • Calculate impulse from constant and variable forces
  • Explain the physics of safety devices using impulse-momentum
  • Relate rocket propulsion to Newton's third law
How this can be tested
  • F-t graph area problems in MC
  • Safety device explanations in FR (seatbelts, airbags)
  • Rocket/explosion momentum problems in FR
Practice before moving on

  • A 1500 kg car stops in 0.1 s vs. 2 s; compare average forces

Conservation of Momentum: Collisions

Learn on this site
Perfectly Inelastic Collisions

Objects collide and stick together. Momentum is conserved (p_i=p_f), but kinetic energy is not — some KE converts to thermal/sound energy. Final velocity: v_f=(m₁v₁+m₂v₂)/(m₁+m₂).

Common misconception: Students apply KE conservation to inelastic collisions; only elastic collisions conserve KE.

How it can be tested: Solve perfectly inelastic collision problems using momentum conservation only.

Elastic Collisions

Both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved. For elastic collision between equal masses: the first stops and the second moves with the first's original velocity (1D).

Common misconception: Students think elastic means colliding objects bounce back with same speed; elastic just means KE conserved, not that objects reverse direction.

How it can be tested: Apply both momentum and energy conservation to elastic collisions.

Explosions

In an explosion, a stationary system breaks apart. Total momentum before = 0 = total momentum after. Fragments move in opposite directions with momenta equal in magnitude.

Common misconception: Students think explosion creates momentum from nothing; internal forces cannot change total momentum; initial p=0 means final p_total=0.

How it can be tested: Apply momentum conservation to explosion problems where initial velocity is zero.

Key Vocabulary
  • perfectly inelastic — Objects stick; KE not conserved; p conserved
  • elastic collision — Both p and KE conserved; objects separate
  • explosion — Object breaks apart; p_initial=0=p_final; KE increases from chemical energy
  • KE conservation — Only valid for elastic collisions
Objectives
  • Solve perfectly inelastic collisions using momentum conservation
  • Apply both conservation laws to elastic collisions
  • Analyze explosions using initial p=0
How this can be tested
  • Collision type identification in MC
  • Multi-part FR: calculate p, determine KE change, state collision type
  • Explosion problems: find fragment velocities from p=0 initial
Practice before moving on
  • 3 kg at 4 m/s hits stationary 2 kg and they stick; find final v
  • 2 kg at 6 m/s hits stationary 2 kg elastically; find both final velocities

Center of Mass

Learn on this site
Center of Mass Position

x_cm = Σ(mᵢxᵢ)/Σmᵢ. The center of mass (CM) is the weighted average position of all masses. For uniform objects, CM is at the geometric center.

Common misconception: Students place the CM at the heavier mass or at the midpoint for all cases; CM is at the midpoint only for equal masses.

How it can be tested: Calculate the CM position for systems of two or more point masses.

Motion of the Center of Mass

The CM of a system moves as if all mass were concentrated there and all external forces acted on it: F_net,ext = M·a_cm. Internal forces don't affect CM motion.

Common misconception: Students think internal forces (collisions, explosions) change CM motion; only external forces move the CM.

How it can be tested: Predict the CM trajectory during a collision or explosion; it continues unchanged by internal events.

Applying CM to Collisions

In a collision, if external forces are negligible, CM velocity is constant. For perfectly inelastic collision, the final velocity equals the CM velocity. For elastic collisions, each object oscillates about the CM.

Common misconception: Students think the CM moves to where the masses stick; CM velocity is the constant final velocity in perfectly inelastic collision.

How it can be tested: Use CM velocity to predict outcome of perfectly inelastic collisions without computing individual momenta.

Key Vocabulary
  • center of mass — x_cm = Σ(mᵢxᵢ)/M; weighted average position
  • CM velocity — v_cm = Σ(mᵢvᵢ)/M = p_total/M
  • CM trajectory — Determined by external forces only; unaffected by internal forces
  • CM and collisions — v_cm before = v_cm after (when external forces negligible)
Objectives
  • Calculate center of mass position for point mass systems
  • Explain why internal forces don't affect CM motion
  • Use CM velocity to predict perfectly inelastic collision outcome
How this can be tested
  • CM problems in both MC and FR
  • CM trajectory during explosion is tested conceptually
  • System CM used in FR to analyze momentum
Practice before moving on
  • Find CM of: 2 kg at x=1 m and 6 kg at x=5 m
  • An isolated system: how does the CM move during a collision?

Two-Dimensional Collisions

Learn on this site
Conservation of Momentum in 2D

In 2D collisions, momentum is conserved in both x and y directions independently. Σp_x (before) = Σp_x (after) and Σp_y (before) = Σp_y (after). Treat each component separately.

Common misconception: Students try to conserve the magnitude of momentum without separating components; momentum conservation is a vector equation.

How it can be tested: Write separate momentum conservation equations for x and y components of a 2D collision.

Glancing Collisions

In a glancing collision, one object is initially moving and the other is at rest. After, both objects move at angles. Use x and y momentum equations plus one more (angle or elasticity).

Common misconception: Students try to solve glancing collisions with one equation; two equations needed (x and y components).

How it can be tested: Set up and identify the equations needed for a 2D glancing collision.

Pool Ball Analogy

Billiard ball collisions approximate elastic 2D collisions. For elastic collision of equal masses where one is at rest, the angle between the two final velocity vectors is exactly 90°.

Common misconception: Students think the 90° rule applies to all 2D collisions; it is specific to equal-mass elastic collisions with one initially at rest.

How it can be tested: Apply the 90° result to equal-mass elastic collisions and verify with momentum/energy conservation.

Key Vocabulary
  • 2D momentum conservation — Separate x and y equations: Σp_x conserved, Σp_y conserved
  • glancing collision — 2D collision where objects scatter at angles
  • elastic 2D collision — Equal masses: angle between final velocities = 90°
  • vector components — Must use cosθ and sinθ to resolve final velocities into components
Objectives
  • Write x and y momentum conservation equations for 2D collisions
  • Set up equations for glancing collisions
  • Apply 90° rule for equal-mass elastic collisions
How this can be tested
  • 2D collisions appear in AP® Physics 1 FR
  • Must write both x and y equations explicitly
  • Equal-mass elastic 2D rule tested in MC
Practice before moving on
  • Set up 2D momentum equations for a 4 kg ball hitting stationary 2 kg ball at an angle
  • Verify 90° rule for a billiard ball collision
