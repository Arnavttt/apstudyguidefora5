Wave Properties

Learn on this site
Transverse and Longitudinal Waves

Transverse waves have particle motion perpendicular to wave propagation (e.g., string waves). Longitudinal waves have particle motion parallel to propagation (e.g., sound). Both transport energy without transporting matter.

Common misconception: Students think the medium moves with the wave; particles oscillate about equilibrium — the wave pattern moves, not the particles.

How it can be tested: Classify waves as transverse or longitudinal; draw the particle oscillation direction relative to wave propagation.

Wave Speed, Frequency, Wavelength

v = fλ. Wave speed depends on the medium, not the source frequency. Frequency f = 1/T where T is the period.

Common misconception: Students think increasing frequency increases wave speed; v depends on the medium (e.g., tension and mass density for strings), not f.

How it can be tested: Apply v=fλ to find any one of the three quantities given the other two.

Amplitude and Energy

The energy carried by a wave is proportional to the square of the amplitude (E ∝ A²). Doubling amplitude quadruples the energy. Intensity I = P/A (power per area).

Common misconception: Students think energy is proportional to amplitude (linear); wave energy scales as A², not A.

How it can be tested: Calculate the ratio of energies or intensities when amplitude or distance changes.

Key Vocabulary
  • transverse wave — Particle motion perpendicular to wave direction
  • longitudinal wave — Particle motion parallel to wave direction (e.g., sound)
  • v = fλ — Wave speed equals frequency times wavelength
  • amplitude — Maximum displacement from equilibrium; energy ∝ A²
Objectives
  • Classify wave types and describe particle motion
  • Apply v=fλ to solve for wave speed, frequency, or wavelength
  • Relate amplitude to energy carried by a wave
How this can be tested

Reflection, Transmission, and Superposition

Learn on this site
Reflection at Boundaries

At a fixed (hard) boundary, a wave inverts on reflection (phase shift of π). At a free (soft) boundary, the wave reflects without inversion. Some energy is transmitted into the new medium.

Common misconception: Students think waves always invert on reflection; inversion only occurs at fixed/hard boundaries, not at free/soft ones.

How it can be tested: Predict whether a wave inverts or not at a given boundary type.

Superposition Principle

When two waves meet, the resultant displacement is the algebraic sum of individual displacements (superposition). After passing through, each wave continues unchanged.

Common misconception: Students think waves collide and are destroyed; waves pass through each other — superposition is temporary and each wave continues independently.

How it can be tested: Apply superposition to find the resultant of two overlapping wave pulses.

Constructive and Destructive Interference

Constructive interference: crests align → amplitude doubles. Destructive interference: crest meets trough → cancellation. For complete cancellation, amplitudes must be equal.

Common misconception: Students think destructive interference destroys energy; energy is redistributed — where destructive interference occurs, energy appears elsewhere.

How it can be tested: Identify constructive and destructive interference from wave diagrams; calculate resultant amplitude.

Key Vocabulary
  • fixed boundary — Rigid end; wave inverts on reflection
  • free boundary — Open end; wave reflects without inversion
  • superposition — Resultant = algebraic sum of individual displacements
  • constructive interference — Crests align; amplitude doubles
  • destructive interference — Crest meets trough; amplitude decreases or cancels
Objectives
  • Predict wave reflection (inverted or upright) based on boundary type
  • Apply superposition to determine resultant displacement
  • Identify constructive and destructive interference
How this can be tested
  • Boundary reflection type tested in MC
  • Superposition of two pulses drawn/described in FR
  • Interference described in MC and explained in FR
Practice before moving on
  • Draw result of two equal pulses meeting: one upright, one inverted
  • Predict what happens when a wave pulse reaches a fixed vs. free end

Standing Waves and Resonance

Learn on this site
Standing Waves on Strings

Standing waves form when a wave and its reflection interfere to create nodes (zero displacement) and antinodes (max displacement). For string fixed at both ends: λₙ=2L/n; fₙ=nv/(2L); n=1,2,3,...

Common misconception: Students memorize the formula without understanding it comes from the condition that L must fit a whole number of half-wavelengths (nodes at both ends).

How it can be tested: Calculate resonant frequencies for standing waves on strings using fₙ=nv/(2L).

Open and Closed Pipes

Open pipe (both ends open): fₙ=nv/(2L), all harmonics. Closed pipe (one closed end): fₙ=nv/(4L) for odd n only (1st, 3rd, 5th harmonics).

Common misconception: Students use the string formula for all wave resonance situations; closed pipes have different boundary conditions and produce only odd harmonics.

How it can be tested: Calculate resonant frequencies for open and closed pipes; state which harmonics are present.

Beats

When two waves of slightly different frequencies f₁ and f₂ interfere, the perceived beat frequency is f_beat=|f₁−f₂|. The sound alternately gets louder and softer at f_beat.

Common misconception: Students think beat frequency is the average of f₁ and f₂; it is the difference.

How it can be tested: Calculate beat frequency from two given frequencies; explain what is heard.

Key Vocabulary
  • node — Point of zero displacement in a standing wave
  • antinode — Point of maximum displacement in a standing wave
  • fundamental frequency — f₁=v/(2L) for string or open pipe; f₁=v/(4L) for closed pipe
  • beat frequency — f_beat=|f₁−f₂|; rate of amplitude variation
Objectives
  • Calculate resonant frequencies for strings and pipes
  • Distinguish open vs. closed pipe harmonics
  • Calculate beat frequency
How this can be tested
  • Standing wave problems appear every year in AP® Physics 1
  • Open vs. closed pipe tested in MC
  • Beat frequency in MC
Practice before moving on
  • Find first three harmonics for a 0.8 m string if v=200 m/s
  • Two tuning forks at 442 Hz and 446 Hz; find beat frequency

Doppler Effect and Wave Intensity

Learn on this site
Doppler Effect

When source and observer move relative to each other, the observed frequency differs from emitted. Source approaches: higher f. Source recedes: lower f. Formula: f_obs = f_s(v ± v_obs)/(v ∓ v_s).

Common misconception: Students think the speed of sound changes with source motion; the speed of sound is determined by the medium, not the source — only the frequency perceived changes.

How it can be tested: Apply qualitative Doppler effect reasoning; state whether observed frequency is higher or lower.

Wave Intensity and Inverse Square Law

Intensity I = P/(4πr²) for a point source radiating spherically. As distance doubles, intensity decreases by factor of 4. Intensity ∝ 1/r².

Common misconception: Students think intensity halves when distance doubles; it quarters because area increases as r².

How it can be tested: Apply inverse square law to compare intensities at different distances.

Shock Waves

When a source moves faster than the wave speed, a shock wave (Mach cone) forms. The Mach number M = v_source/v_wave. For M>1, the source outruns its own waves.

Common misconception: Students think shock waves are only relevant for supersonic aircraft; any wave source exceeding wave speed produces a shock wave.

How it can be tested: Explain the conditions for shock wave formation and the meaning of the Mach number.

Key Vocabulary
  • Doppler effect — Observed frequency change due to relative motion of source and observer
  • intensity — I = P/(4πr²); power per unit area, W/m²
  • inverse square law — I ∝ 1/r²; intensity decreases with distance squared
  • shock wave — Wave front formed when source exceeds wave speed; M>1
Objectives
  • Predict direction of Doppler frequency shift qualitatively
  • Apply inverse square law to find intensity at different distances
  • Explain shock wave formation
How this can be tested
  • Doppler qualitative reasoning in MC
  • Inverse square law ratio problems in MC
  • Shock wave conceptual explanation in FR
Practice before moving on

  • Source has intensity 100 W/m² at 2 m; find intensity at 6 m
