Electric Charge and Coulomb's Law

Learn on this site
Electric Charge

Charge is quantized in units of e=1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C. Protons have +e, electrons −e. Like charges repel, unlike attract. Charge is conserved: net charge of a closed system is constant.

Common misconception: Students think charge can be created or destroyed; it can only be transferred. Rubbing objects transfers electrons, not protons.

How it can be tested: Apply charge conservation to determine final charge distribution after charge transfer.

Coulomb's Law

F = kq₁q₂/r² where k=9×10⁹ N·m²/C². Force is attractive for unlike charges, repulsive for like charges. Force follows inverse-square law.

Common misconception: Students forget to square r; F ∝ 1/r² means doubling distance reduces force by factor of 4, not 2.

How it can be tested: Calculate Coulomb force magnitude; determine direction using sign of charges.

Superposition of Electric Forces

The total electric force on a charge is the vector sum of forces from all other charges. Must find both magnitude and direction of each force, then add as vectors.

Common misconception: Students add forces as scalars; Coulomb forces are vectors — direction depends on the sign of the charges and their positions.

How it can be tested: Find net force on a charge from multiple point charges using vector addition.

Key Vocabulary
  • elementary charge — e = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C; smallest unit of charge
  • Coulomb's law — F = kq₁q₂/r²; k=9×10⁹ N·m²/C²
  • charge conservation — Net charge in a closed system is constant
  • inverse square law — Force decreases as 1/r²; doubles distance → 1/4 force
Objectives
  • Apply charge conservation to charge transfer scenarios
  • Calculate Coulomb force and its direction
  • Find net electric force from multiple charges using superposition
How this can be tested

  • Superposition of forces in FR

Electric Field

Learn on this site
Electric Field Definition

E = F/q (force per unit test charge). Unit: N/C. Electric field exists in space surrounding a charge; a positive test charge placed in the field would experience force in the field direction.

Common misconception: Students think the electric field only exists when a test charge is present; the field exists in space regardless of whether a test charge is there.

How it can be tested: Calculate E from a point charge using E=kQ/r²; determine direction.

Electric Field Lines

Field lines start on + charges and end on − charges. Line density represents field strength. Lines never cross. Between parallel plates: uniform field, parallel lines (fringe fields neglected).

Common misconception: Students draw field lines that cross; field lines NEVER cross because the field has a unique direction at each point.

How it can be tested: Sketch field lines for single charges, dipoles, and parallel plate configurations.

Superposition of Electric Fields

The net electric field is the vector sum of fields from all source charges: E_net = Σ(kQᵢ/rᵢ²)r̂ᵢ. Same principle as force superposition.

Common misconception: Students add field magnitudes without considering direction; fields are vectors and must be added as vectors.

How it can be tested: Find the net electric field at a point due to multiple charges using vector addition.

Key Vocabulary
  • electric field — E = F/q; force per unit positive test charge, N/C
  • field from point charge — E = kQ/r²; direction: away from + charge, toward − charge
  • field lines — Start on + charges, end on − charges; never cross; density ∝ field strength
  • uniform electric field — Constant E between parallel plates of a capacitor
Objectives
  • Calculate electric field at a point from a point charge
  • Sketch electric field lines for various charge configurations
  • Find net electric field from multiple charges using superposition
How this can be tested
  • E field direction from charge sign tested in MC
  • Field line sketches in FR
  • Superposition of E fields in FR and MC
Practice before moving on
  • Find E at 0.3 m from a +5 μC charge
  • Sketch field lines for two equal positive charges

Conductors and Insulators; Charging Methods

Learn on this site
Conductors and Insulators

Conductors allow free electron movement (metals); insulators resist charge flow (rubber, glass). In a conductor, excess charge distributes on the outer surface; the interior has E=0 in electrostatic equilibrium.

Common misconception: Students think charge is distributed throughout a conductor; excess charge resides only on the outer surface in electrostatic equilibrium.

How it can be tested: Explain why E=0 inside a conductor and why charge distributes on the surface.

Charging by Conduction and Induction

Conduction: direct contact transfers charge; object gains same sign as the charging object. Induction: no contact; charging object attracts opposite charges to near side; ground removes excess; same sign charges remain when ground is removed.

Common misconception: Students confuse conduction (contact) with induction (no contact); in induction, the induced charge is opposite to the inducing charge.

How it can be tested: Describe the steps of charging by induction including grounding and what charge results.

Polarization

A charged object can attract a neutral conductor or insulator by polarizing it — shifting charge distributions without transferring net charge. This explains how a comb attracts paper bits.

Common misconception: Students think attraction requires transfer of charge; polarization creates attractive forces without any charge transfer.

How it can be tested: Explain the attraction of a neutral object to a charged one using polarization.

Key Vocabulary
  • conductor — Material with free electrons that can move; charge on outer surface
  • insulator — Material where charge cannot flow freely
  • charging by conduction — Direct contact; object gains same sign as charger
  • charging by induction — No contact; induced charge is opposite to inducing charge
  • polarization — Charge redistribution in neutral object near charged object
Objectives
  • Explain charge distribution in conductors vs. insulators
  • Describe charging by conduction and induction with correct charge signs
  • Explain polarization and the attraction of neutral objects
How this can be tested
  • Charging method identification in MC
  • Induction steps described in FR
  • Conductor interior field=0 tested in FR and MC
Practice before moving on
  • Describe steps to charge a neutral sphere by induction (negative rod available)
  • Why is E=0 inside a conductor at electrostatic equilibrium?

Motion of Charges in Electric Fields

Learn on this site
Acceleration of Charged Particles

A charged particle in a uniform electric field experiences F=qE and accelerates at a=F/m=qE/m. For a positive charge: acceleration in the direction of E. This is analogous to gravity for projectile motion.

Common misconception: Students forget to include the sign of the charge; negative charges accelerate opposite to E.

How it can be tested: Calculate the acceleration of a charge in a uniform field; set up kinematic equations for its motion.

Horizontal Deflection (like CRT)

A charge entering a uniform vertical electric field horizontally is deflected like a projectile: constant horizontal velocity, constant vertical acceleration qE/m. The trajectory is parabolic.

Common misconception: Students apply circular motion equations to this; charge in uniform E-field moves in a parabola like projectile motion in gravity.

How it can be tested: Analyze horizontal beam deflection by a vertical electric field using kinematic equations.

Electric Potential Energy

Work done by electric field on charge q moving through potential difference ΔV: W=qΔV. Energy gained = qΔV (for positive charge moving with field). Unit: Joule; eV = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ J.

Common misconception: Students confuse electric potential (V, volts) with electric potential energy (PE=qV, joules); potential is per-unit-charge, potential energy depends on the specific charge.

How it can be tested: Calculate work done by electric field on moving charges using W=qΔV.

Key Vocabulary
  • acceleration in E field — a = qE/m; direction: with E for positive, against E for negative charge
  • parabolic trajectory — Charge in uniform E field moves in a parabola (like projectile in gravity)
  • electric potential energy — PE = qV; work done by field = qΔV
  • electron-volt — eV = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ J; energy of one electron through 1 V potential difference
Objectives
  • Calculate acceleration of a charge in a uniform electric field
  • Analyze parabolic trajectory of charge entering perpendicular to E field
  • Calculate work done on a charge moving through a potential difference
How this can be tested
  • Charge acceleration in E field in FR
  • Parabolic path analysis in MC
  • Work done by E field using W=qΔV in MC and FR
