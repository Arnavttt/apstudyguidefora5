Ohm's Law and Resistance

Learn on this site
Ohm's Law

V = IR. Voltage (V) drives current (I) through resistance (R). Unit: V=Volts, I=Amperes, R=Ohms (Ω). Ohm's law applies to ohmic resistors where R is constant.

Common misconception: Students rearrange V=IR incorrectly; always identify which two are known and solve for the third. R is not a variable in ohmic resistors.

How it can be tested: Apply V=IR to find voltage, current, or resistance in simple circuits.

Resistance and Resistivity

R=ρL/A where ρ is resistivity, L is length, A is cross-sectional area. Longer wire → more R; thicker wire → less R. Resistivity depends on material and temperature.

Common misconception: Students think resistance equals resistivity; R depends on both material (ρ) and geometry (L and A).

How it can be tested: Predict how resistance changes when length or cross-section changes.

Current and Charge Flow

Current I = ΔQ/Δt (charge per time, Amperes). Conventional current flows from + to − (opposite to electron flow). In a DC circuit, current is the same throughout a series circuit.

Common misconception: Students think current is used up in a resistor; current is conserved (Kirchhoff's current law), but energy (voltage) is used up.

How it can be tested: Distinguish current from voltage; explain why current is the same throughout a series circuit.

Key Vocabulary
  • Ohm's law — V = IR; voltage = current × resistance
  • resistance — R = ρL/A; opposition to current flow, Ohms
  • current — I = ΔQ/Δt; rate of charge flow, Amperes
  • conventional current — Flows from + to − terminal; opposite to electron flow
Objectives
  • Apply V=IR to simple circuits
  • Predict resistance changes from geometry
  • Explain the distinction between current and voltage in a circuit
How this can be tested
  • Ohm's law in every AP® Physics 1 exam
  • Resistance and resistivity ratios in MC
  • Series current conservation in FR
Practice before moving on
  • Find current through 20 Ω resistor with 12 V battery
  • If wire length doubles and diameter doubles, how does R change?
Key Formulas
  • Ohm's law: \( V = IR \)
  • Power: \( P = IV = I^2R = \dfrac{V^2}{R} \)
  • Resistivity: \( R = \dfrac{\rho L}{A} \)

Series and Parallel Circuits

Learn on this site
Series Resistors

In series: same current, voltages add. R_total=R₁+R₂+... Removing one component breaks the entire circuit.

Common misconception: Students add voltages instead of resistances, or think adding resistors in series decreases total resistance; series always increases R.

How it can be tested: Calculate total resistance, current, and voltage drops in a series circuit.

Parallel Resistors

In parallel: same voltage, currents add. 1/R_total=1/R₁+1/R₂+... Adding resistors in parallel always decreases total resistance.

Common misconception: Students use 1/R_total formula incorrectly by taking 1/(1/R₁+1/R₂) = R_total; they often forget to take the reciprocal of the sum at the end.

How it can be tested: Calculate equivalent resistance of parallel resistors; find individual branch currents.

Mixed (Series-Parallel) Circuits

Reduce complex circuits stepwise: combine parallel branches into single equivalent resistance, then combine series. Work from innermost grouping outward.

Common misconception: Students try to analyze the whole circuit at once; always reduce parallel groups first, then series combinations, iteratively.

How it can be tested: Apply stepwise reduction to find total resistance and individual currents/voltages in mixed circuits.

Key Vocabulary
  • series circuit — Same current; voltages add; R_total = ΣRᵢ
  • parallel circuit — Same voltage; currents add; 1/R_total = Σ(1/Rᵢ)
  • voltage divider — In series, voltage divides proportionally to resistance: V₁/V₂=R₁/R₂
  • current divider — In parallel, current divides inversely proportional to resistance
Objectives
  • Calculate series and parallel equivalent resistance
  • Find voltage and current for each resistor in series/parallel circuit
  • Reduce mixed circuits stepwise to find total resistance
How this can be tested
  • Series/parallel circuits guaranteed in AP® FR
  • Stepwise reduction in FR; show all work
  • Voltage divider and current divider tested in MC
Practice before moving on
  • Three 6 Ω resistors: find total R in series and in parallel
  • A 4 Ω and 12 Ω in parallel; series with 6 Ω; find total R
Key Formulas
  • Series R: \( R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + \cdots \)
  • Parallel R: \( \dfrac{1}{R_{eq}} = \dfrac{1}{R_1}+\dfrac{1}{R_2}+\cdots \)

Kirchhoff's Laws and Power

Learn on this site
Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)

At any junction, the sum of currents entering equals the sum of currents leaving: ΣI_in = ΣI_out. This follows from charge conservation.

Common misconception: Students think current is "split evenly" at a junction; current divides inversely proportional to resistance in parallel branches.

How it can be tested: Apply KCL at junctions to write current equations for complex circuits.

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)

Around any closed loop, the sum of voltage changes is zero: ΣΔV = 0. Gains across batteries; drops across resistors (voltage drops in direction of current).

Common misconception: Students don't account for sign conventions; voltage rises across battery (EMF), drops across resistors.

How it can be tested: Apply KVL to closed loops; identify voltage rises (batteries) and drops (resistors) with correct signs.

Electric Power

P = IV = I²R = V²/R. Power is dissipated as heat in resistors. A circuit element with more resistance dissipates more power in series (same I); less power in parallel (same V).

Common misconception: Students apply P=IV in series using the full battery voltage instead of the voltage across that resistor; use P=I²R (same I in series) or P=V²/R (same V in parallel).

How it can be tested: Calculate power dissipated in each resistor and the total power delivered by the battery.

Key Vocabulary
  • KCL — ΣI_in = ΣI_out at any junction
  • KVL — ΣΔV = 0 around any closed loop
  • power — P = IV = I²R = V²/R; Watts
  • power in series — P=I²R; larger R dissipates more power in series
Objectives
  • Apply KCL to write junction equations
  • Apply KVL to write loop equations
  • Calculate power for each element in a circuit
How this can be tested
  • KCL and KVL appear in FR circuit analysis
  • Power calculations in both MC and FR
  • Which resistor dissipates most power: series vs. parallel context
Practice before moving on
  • Write KCL and KVL equations for a two-loop circuit
  • Find power in each resistor: 4 Ω and 8 Ω in series with 12 V

Capacitors (Introductory)

Learn on this site
Capacitance

C = Q/V (charge stored per volt). Unit: Farad (F). A capacitor stores charge on two conducting plates separated by an insulator. Energy stored: U = ½CV² = ½QV = Q²/(2C).

Common misconception: Students think capacitors work like batteries; capacitors store charge (not chemical energy) and discharge quickly.

How it can be tested: Calculate charge stored and energy stored in a capacitor given C and V.

Capacitors in Circuits

In a DC steady state, no current flows through a capacitor (acts as open circuit). Voltage across a capacitor equals the voltage between the nodes it connects.

Common misconception: Students think current flows through capacitors in DC steady state; in DC steady state, capacitor is fully charged and current stops.

How it can be tested: Analyze a simple RC circuit in steady state; find voltage across capacitor.

Charging and Discharging

Capacitor charges exponentially through a resistor: Q(t)=Q_max(1−e^{−t/RC}). Time constant τ=RC. After 5τ, the capacitor is considered fully charged.

Common misconception: Students think charging is instantaneous; charging follows exponential growth; time constant τ=RC determines the charging rate.

How it can be tested: Identify the time constant τ=RC and describe the shape of the charging curve.

Key Vocabulary
  • capacitance — C = Q/V; Farads
  • energy stored — U = ½CV²
  • time constant — τ = RC; time to reach ~63% of final charge
  • steady state — No current through capacitor; fully charged in DC circuit
Objectives
  • Calculate charge and energy stored in a capacitor
  • Analyze capacitor voltage in DC steady-state circuits
  • Describe charging curve and time constant
How this can be tested
  • Capacitor energy in MC
  • Steady-state capacitor voltage in FR
  • Charging curve description in FR
Practice before moving on
  • Find charge and energy stored: C=100 μF, V=12 V
  • RC circuit with R=1000 Ω, C=100 μF; find time constant
Key Formulas
  • Capacitance: \( C = \dfrac{Q}{V},\quad C_{parallel} = \varepsilon_0\dfrac{A}{d} \)
  • Energy stored: \( U = \tfrac{1}{2}QV = \tfrac{1}{2}CV^2 \)
