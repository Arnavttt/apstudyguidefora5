Magnetic Force on Moving Charges

Learn on this site
Magnetic Force

F = qv × B (vector cross product). Magnitude: F = qvBsinθ. Force is perpendicular to both v and B — it does no work (cannot change speed, only direction). Right-hand rule gives direction.

Common misconception: Students think magnetic force does work; since F ⊥ v, the magnetic force is always perpendicular to displacement and W=Fd·cosθ=0.

How it can be tested: Apply F=qvBsinθ to find the magnitude of magnetic force; use RHR for direction.

Circular Motion in Magnetic Field

A charge moving perpendicular to B moves in a circle. Magnetic force provides centripetal force: qvB = mv²/r → r = mv/(qB). Cyclotron motion.

Common misconception: Students think the charge spirals inward due to energy loss; in an ideal field with no energy loss, the radius is constant and the motion is circular.

How it can be tested: Calculate the radius of circular motion for a charge in a magnetic field using r=mv/(qB).

Right-Hand Rule for Magnetic Force

For F = qv × B: point fingers in v direction, curl toward B, thumb points in F direction (for positive charge). For negative charge, reverse the thumb direction.

Common misconception: Students reverse the RHR for negative charges; the RHR gives direction for positive charge — flip for negative charge.

How it can be tested: Apply RHR to determine the direction of magnetic force in various configurations.

Key Vocabulary
  • magnetic force — F = qvBsinθ; perpendicular to both v and B
  • right-hand rule — Fingers: v direction; curl toward B; thumb: F direction (positive charge)
  • circular orbit — r = mv/(qB); magnetic force provides centripetal force
  • zero work — Magnetic force ⊥ velocity → W=0; speed constant

Magnetic Field Sources

Learn on this site
Magnetic Field from Straight Wire

A long straight wire carrying current I produces circular magnetic field lines around it. Direction: right-hand rule (curl fingers in direction of B if thumb points in current direction). B ∝ I/r.

Common misconception: Students think field lines are straight near a wire; they form concentric circles around the wire, decreasing with distance.

How it can be tested: Use RHR to determine the direction of B around a current-carrying wire; state how B varies with distance.

Force Between Parallel Wires

Parallel currents in the same direction attract; in opposite directions they repel. Each wire is in the field of the other, and F=BIL applies.

Common misconception: Students reverse the attraction/repulsion rule; same-direction currents attract (unlike charges which repel for same sign).

How it can be tested: Determine whether two parallel wires attract or repel based on current directions.

Solenoids and Magnetic Dipoles

A solenoid (coil of wire) produces a nearly uniform field inside. B inside ∝ nI where n=N/L. The field resembles a bar magnet with N and S poles. Right-hand rule for solenoid: curl fingers in current direction, thumb points to N pole.

Common misconception: Students think the field is only inside a solenoid; the field also exists outside, but is weaker and non-uniform. Only inside is it uniform.

How it can be tested: Determine the magnetic poles of a solenoid using the right-hand rule for coils.

Key Vocabulary
  • B from wire — B = μ₀I/(2πr); decreases with distance from wire
  • same-direction currents — Attract each other
  • opposite-direction currents — Repel each other
  • solenoid — Coil of wire producing uniform field inside; B ∝ nI
Objectives
  • Apply RHR to find B direction around a wire
  • Determine attraction/repulsion of parallel current-carrying wires
  • Identify poles of a solenoid using RHR
How this can be tested
  • Wire field direction in MC
  • Parallel wire force type (attract/repel) in MC
  • Solenoid pole identification in FR

Electromagnetic Induction and Faraday's Law

Learn on this site
Magnetic Flux and Faraday's Law

Magnetic flux Φ=BAcosθ (B through area A at angle θ). Faraday's law: induced EMF = −ΔΦ/Δt. A changing flux induces an EMF (and current in a closed circuit). Greater rate of change → greater EMF.

Common misconception: Students think a strong magnetic field induces EMF; it is the CHANGING flux that induces EMF, not the static field itself.

How it can be tested: Calculate magnetic flux and determine whether EMF is induced based on whether flux is changing.

Lenz's Law

The induced current flows in a direction that opposes the change in flux that caused it. If flux is increasing, induced current creates field opposing the increase. If decreasing, induced current reinforces the original field.

Common misconception: Students confuse Lenz's law with "the induced current opposes the original current"; it opposes the CHANGE in flux, not the current causing the flux.

How it can be tested: Apply Lenz's law to determine the direction of induced current in various scenarios.

Applications: Generators and Transformers

Generator: rotating coil in magnetic field → changing flux → alternating EMF. Transformer: changing flux in primary coil induces EMF in secondary coil (AC only). V_s/V_p = N_s/N_p.

Common misconception: Students think transformers work with DC; transformers require changing flux (AC) to operate.

How it can be tested: Explain how generators and transformers use electromagnetic induction; apply transformer ratio.

Key Vocabulary
  • magnetic flux — Φ = BAcosθ; Webers (Wb)
  • Faraday's law — EMF = −ΔΦ/Δt; changing flux induces EMF
  • Lenz's law — Induced current opposes the change in flux
  • transformer ratio — V_s/V_p = N_s/N_p; AC only
Objectives
  • Calculate magnetic flux and apply Faraday's law
  • Apply Lenz's law to determine induced current direction
  • Explain generator operation and apply transformer equation
How this can be tested

Applications and Review

Learn on this site
Hall Effect

When a current-carrying conductor is placed in a magnetic field perpendicular to the current, charges are deflected sideways, creating a transverse voltage (Hall voltage). Polarity depends on the sign of the charge carriers.

Common misconception: Students confuse the Hall voltage with the driving voltage of the circuit; the Hall voltage is perpendicular to both current and magnetic field.

How it can be tested: Predict the direction of charge accumulation in the Hall effect and its relevance to determining charge carrier sign.

Mass Spectrometry

Ions are accelerated through a voltage, then enter a magnetic field where they travel in circular arcs. r=mv/(qB). Different masses → different radii → separation.

Common misconception: Students think all ions travel the same arc; radius depends on m/q ratio, so different isotopes take different paths.

How it can be tested: Apply r=mv/(qB) to explain how a mass spectrometer separates isotopes.

Connections Across Units

Magnetic force, electric force, and gravity all follow inverse-square (or similar distance) laws. Energy and momentum are conserved in electromagnetic as well as mechanical interactions. Forces and fields are deeply connected.

Common misconception: Students treat each unit in isolation; AP® FR questions often combine topics (e.g., charge in a gravitational + electric field, or energy stored in capacitor + mechanical work).

How it can be tested: Identify cross-unit connections and apply multiple laws in combination for integrated problems.

Key Vocabulary
  • Hall effect — Transverse voltage from magnetic deflection of charge carriers
  • mass spectrometry — r=mv/(qB); separates ions by mass-to-charge ratio
  • cross-unit connections — E, B, gravity, energy conservation combine in FR problems
  • charge carrier sign — Hall effect polarity reveals whether carriers are + or −
Objectives
  • Explain Hall effect and its application
  • Apply r=mv/(qB) to mass spectrometry
  • Identify connections between mechanics and electromagnetism
How this can be tested
  • Cross-unit integration in AP® FR
  • Mass spectrometry calculation in MC
  • Hall effect conceptual explanation in FR
