Coulomb's Law and Electric Fields

Learn on this site
Coulomb's Law

F = kq₁q₂/r² = q₁q₂/(4πε₀r²); force between point charges. Force is repulsive for like charges, attractive for unlike. k = 8.99×10⁹ N·m²/C².

Common misconception: The force on q₁ due to q₂ is equal and opposite to the force on q₂ — Newton's 3rd law applies.

How it can be tested: Calculate force between point charges; use superposition for multiple charges.

Electric Field

E = F/q₀ = kq/r² (point charge); the electric field E is force per unit positive test charge. Field lines start on + charges and end on − charges.

Common misconception: Electric field exists even where there is no test charge — it is a property of the region, not dependent on q₀.

How it can be tested: Find E from a charge distribution using superposition; draw and interpret field line diagrams.

Superposition Principle

Total E or F from multiple charges = vector sum of individual contributions. Each charge contributes independently; integrate for continuous distributions.

Common misconception: Superposition applies to fields, not potentials squared — E_total = ΣE_i (vector sum).

How it can be tested: Compute E at a point from multiple charges by vector addition.

Key Vocabulary
  • Coulomb's law — F = kq₁q₂/r²; force between point charges
  • Electric field E — E = F/q₀ = kq/r²; force per unit positive charge (N/C)
  • Permittivity ε₀ — ε₀ = 8.85×10⁻¹² C²/(N·m²); vacuum permittivity
  • Field lines — Directed curves showing E direction; density ∝ |E|
  • Superposition — E_total = ΣE_i; fields add as vectors
Objectives
  • Calculate Coulomb force for multiple point charges.
  • Find E at a point using superposition.
  • Draw electric field lines for simple charge configurations.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: find E at midpoint between two charges.
  • Calculation: find E on the axis of a uniformly charged ring by integration.
  • Superposition: find net force on a charge in a system of three.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice superposition problems with 2–3 point charges.
  • Derive E for a ring of charge along the axis.
Key Formulas
  • Coulomb's law: \( \vec{F} = k\dfrac{q_1q_2}{r^2}\hat{r},\quad k=\dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \)
  • E field: \( \vec{E} = \dfrac{\vec{F}}{q_0} = k\dfrac{Q}{r^2}\hat{r} \)

Gauss's Law

Learn on this site
Electric Flux

Φ_E = ∮E·dA = Q_enc/ε₀; flux is the integral of E·dA over a closed surface (Gaussian surface). Positive flux when E points outward.

Common misconception: Flux depends on Q_enclosed, not on the shape or size of the Gaussian surface.

How it can be tested: Calculate flux through various closed surfaces given charge distributions.

Applying Gauss's Law

Choose a Gaussian surface with the same symmetry as the charge distribution. E is constant on the surface: EA = Q_enc/ε₀. Works for spheres, cylinders, and planes.

Common misconception: Gauss's law is always true, but only useful for finding E when symmetry allows E to be pulled out of the integral.

How it can be tested: Apply Gauss's law to find E for spherically, cylindrically, and planar symmetric charge distributions.

Conductors in Electrostatics

In electrostatic equilibrium: E = 0 inside a conductor; all excess charge is on the surface; E is perpendicular to the surface outside. Surface charge density σ relates to external E via E = σ/ε₀.

Common misconception: E = 0 inside a conductor only in STATIC equilibrium — current-carrying conductors have nonzero E inside.

How it can be tested: Apply Gauss's law to find E and charge distribution for conducting surfaces.

Key Vocabulary
  • Electric flux — Φ_E = ∮E·dA; proportional to field lines passing through surface
  • Gaussian surface — Imaginary closed surface chosen to apply Gauss's law
  • Gauss's law — Φ_E = Q_enc/ε₀; total flux equals enclosed charge over ε₀
  • Conductor equilibrium — E = 0 inside; all charge on surface; E perpendicular to surface
  • Surface charge density σ — Charge per unit area (C/m²) on a conductor surface
Objectives
  • Calculate electric flux for closed surfaces.
  • Apply Gauss's law to find E for spherical, cylindrical, and planar symmetry.
  • Explain properties of conductors in electrostatic equilibrium.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: derive E for an infinite line charge using a cylindrical Gaussian surface.
  • Gauss's law: find E inside and outside a uniformly charged sphere.
  • Conductor: find E just outside a conductor with surface charge σ.
Practice before moving on
  • Derive E for all three symmetric distributions from Gauss's law.
  • Practice explaining why each Gaussian surface shape is chosen.
Key Formulas
  • Gauss's law: \( \Phi_E = \oint\vec{E}\cdot d\vec{A} = \dfrac{Q_{enc}}{\varepsilon_0} \)

Electric Potential

Learn on this site
Electric Potential V

V = kq/r for a point charge; V = W/q₀ = -∫E·dl (potential is work per unit positive charge from reference). V is a scalar.

Common misconception: Potential V is not the same as potential energy U; U = qV. V is potential energy per unit charge.

How it can be tested: Calculate V by superposition (sum of kq_i/r_i) and by integrating E.

Relationship Between E and V

E = -∇V; in 1D: E_x = -dV/dx. Equipotential surfaces are perpendicular to field lines. Moving along an equipotential does zero work.

Common misconception: E points from high V to low V (negative gradient), not from positive to negative charge.

How it can be tested: Find E from V by differentiation; find V from E by integration.

Electric Potential Energy

U = qV = kq₁q₂/r for two point charges. For a system of charges, U = sum of all pair potentials. Work done by E field: W = qΔV = -ΔU.

Common misconception: The potential energy is zero at infinite separation — negative U means the system is bound.

How it can be tested: Calculate potential energy of charge configurations and work done by electric fields.

Key Vocabulary
  • Electric potential V — V = kq/r (point charge); work per unit charge (J/C = V)
  • Potential difference ΔV — ΔV = V_B - V_A = -∫_A^B E·dl
  • E = -dV/dx — E is negative gradient of V in the direction of steepest descent
  • Equipotential surface — Surface on which V is constant; perpendicular to field lines
  • Potential energy U — U = qV = kq₁q₂/r; zero at infinite separation
Objectives
  • Calculate V for point charge systems using superposition.
  • Relate E and V via E = -dV/dx.
  • Calculate work done by E field on a charge moving through a potential difference.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: find V at a point from multiple charges; derive E from V.
  • Derive V for a uniformly charged ring on its axis.
  • Work/energy: find speed of charge released from rest at a given potential.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice V superposition for 3–4 charge systems.
  • Derive E from V for several functions V(x).
Key Formulas
  • Potential: \( V = k\dfrac{Q}{r},\quad \Delta V = -\displaystyle\int_a^b\vec{E}\cdot d\vec{l} \)
  • E from V: \( E_x = -\dfrac{dV}{dx} \)
  • Work: \( W = q\Delta V \)

Continuous Charge Distributions: Advanced Integration

Learn on this site
E and V for Lines, Rings, and Disks

For continuous distributions: E = ∫k dq/r² r̂; V = ∫k dq/r. Use symmetry to simplify; identify which components cancel. Results: infinite line (1/r), ring (on axis), disk (on axis).

Common misconception: When integrating for E, always check which components cancel by symmetry before computing the integral.

How it can be tested: Set up and evaluate integrals for E and V from continuous charge distributions.

Volume Charge Distributions

For volume charge density ρ: dq = ρ dV. Use Gauss's law when symmetry permits; otherwise integrate directly. Example: uniformly charged sphere, E ∝ r inside.

Common misconception: dq = ρ dV uses the VOLUME element, not area or length — verify the correct density and element are used.

How it can be tested: Compute E and V for volumetrically distributed charges.

Dipoles

An electric dipole has charges +q and -q separated by distance d; dipole moment p = qd. E on axis: E ≈ 2kp/r³; perpendicular: E ≈ kp/r³ (far field). Torque τ = p × E; PE = -p·E.

Common misconception: Dipole field falls off as 1/r³, faster than monopole 1/r².

How it can be tested: Calculate E from a dipole and the torque on a dipole in a uniform field.

Key Vocabulary
  • Volume charge density ρ — ρ = dq/dV; charge per unit volume (C/m³)
  • Dipole moment p — p = qd; vector from - to + charge; determines far-field behavior
  • Dipole torque — τ = p × E = pE sinθ; torque aligns dipole with field
  • Dipole PE — U = -p·E = -pE cosθ; minimum when p parallel to E
  • Far-field dipole E — E ∝ 1/r³; faster falloff than monopole
Objectives
  • Set up integrals for E and V for line, ring, and disk charge distributions.
  • Apply Gauss's law for volume distributions with symmetry.
  • Calculate torque and PE for a dipole in an external field.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: E and V on axis of a uniformly charged disk by integration.
  • Derive E inside and outside a uniformly charged solid sphere.
  • Dipole: find torque angle for maximum torque; find stable equilibrium.
Practice before moving on
  • Set up and solve the ring and disk E integrals from scratch.
  • Compute E of a dipole at two standard positions.
Key Formulas
  • Superposition: \( V = k\displaystyle\int\dfrac{dq}{r} \)

A is correct. W = qΔV = (1×10⁻⁶)(500-200) = 3×10⁻⁴ J = 0.3 mJ
