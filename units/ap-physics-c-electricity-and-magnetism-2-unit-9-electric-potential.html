Capacitance

Learn on this site
Definition of Capacitance

C = Q/V (F = C/V). Capacitance depends only on geometry, not on Q or V. Parallel plate: C = ε₀A/d; isolated sphere: C = 4πε₀R.

Common misconception: Capacitance does not depend on the charge stored — it is a property of the geometry.

How it can be tested: Calculate C for parallel plates; derive C for cylindrical and spherical capacitors.

Derivation for Cylindrical Capacitor

Coaxial cylinders of radii a and b: E = λ/(2πε₀r) between cylinders; V = λ/(2πε₀) ln(b/a); C = 2πε₀L/ln(b/a).

Common misconception: The logarithm in the cylindrical capacitor formula is essential — it is not linear in the radius ratio.

How it can be tested: Derive C for a cylindrical capacitor by integrating E to find V.

Series and Parallel Capacitors

Parallel: C_eq = Σ C_i (same V); Series: 1/C_eq = Σ1/C_i (same Q). Charge on capacitors in series is equal; voltage divides.

Common misconception: In series, capacitors share the same charge (not voltage); in parallel they share the same voltage (not charge).

How it can be tested: Reduce capacitor networks to a single equivalent capacitance.

Key Vocabulary
  • Capacitance C — C = Q/V; charge stored per unit voltage (F = C/V)
  • Parallel plate capacitance — C = ε₀A/d; increases with area, decreases with separation
  • Cylindrical capacitor — C = 2πε₀L/ln(b/a)
  • Series capacitors — 1/C_eq = Σ1/C_i; same charge on each
  • Parallel capacitors — C_eq = ΣC_i; same voltage across each
Objectives
  • Calculate C for parallel plate, spherical, and cylindrical capacitors.
  • Derive C by integrating E to find V for non-planar geometries.
  • Reduce series/parallel combinations to a single equivalent C.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: derive C for a cylindrical capacitor step by step.
  • Series-parallel network: find equivalent C and charge on each capacitor.
  • Comparison: how does doubling plate separation affect C, Q, V?
Practice before moving on
  • Derive C for cylindrical and spherical capacitors.
  • Practice reducing complex capacitor networks.
Key Formulas
  • Capacitance: \( C = \dfrac{Q}{V} \)
  • Parallel-plate: \( C = \varepsilon_0\dfrac{A}{d} \)
  • Series C: \( \dfrac{1}{C_{eq}} = \sum\dfrac{1}{C_i} \)
  • Parallel C: \( C_{eq} = \sum C_i \)

Energy in Capacitors and Electric Fields

Learn on this site
Energy Stored in a Capacitor

U = ½QV = ½CV² = Q²/(2C). Derived by integrating dW = V dq as charge is added. Energy is stored in the electric field between the plates.

Common misconception: U = ½CV² — the factor of ½ is essential; it arises from the linear relationship Q = CV as Q builds up.

How it can be tested: Calculate energy stored in a capacitor for given C and V or Q.

Energy Density in Electric Field

u = ½ε₀E² (J/m³); energy per unit volume in an electric field. Total energy = ∫u dV.

Common misconception: Energy density depends on E², not E — doubling the field quadruples the energy density.

How it can be tested: Calculate total field energy by integrating u = ½ε₀E² over a volume.

Force Between Capacitor Plates

The force between capacitor plates can be found from U = Q²/(2C) using F = -dU/dx. For parallel plates: F = Q²/(2ε₀A).

Common misconception: The force between plates uses U held at constant Q (isolated) or constant V (connected) — results differ.

How it can be tested: Derive the force between plates using the energy method.

Key Vocabulary
  • Energy stored U — U = ½QV = ½CV² = Q²/(2C)
  • Energy density u — u = ½ε₀E²; energy per unit volume in electric field
  • Parallel plate E — E = σ/ε₀ = Q/(ε₀A) between plates (uniform field)
  • Force from energy — F = -dU/dx; force from spatial derivative of energy
  • Charging work — W = ½CV²; work done to charge a capacitor from zero
Objectives
  • Derive and calculate energy stored in a capacitor.
  • Find energy density in a uniform electric field.
  • Calculate force between plates using energy methods.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: find energy stored in series/parallel capacitor networks.
  • Integration: compute total energy in the field between coaxial cylinders.
  • Force: derive force between plates using F = -dU/dx.
Practice before moving on
  • Compare energy stored at constant V vs. constant Q when plates are moved.
  • Compute u and total field energy for given E.
Key Formulas
  • Energy stored: \( U = \tfrac{1}{2}QV = \tfrac{1}{2}CV^2 = \dfrac{Q^2}{2C} \)
  • Energy density: \( u = \tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2 \)

Dielectrics

Learn on this site
Dielectric Effect

Inserting a dielectric of constant κ: C_new = κC₀; E_new = E₀/κ; V_new = V₀/κ (at constant Q). Dielectric weakens the field and increases capacitance.

Common misconception: Inserting a dielectric INCREASES capacitance (C = κC₀ with κ > 1), reducing E and V for the same charge.

How it can be tested: Predict effects of inserting a dielectric at constant V vs. constant Q.

Molecular Explanation

Polar molecules align with the field; nonpolar molecules develop induced dipoles. The aligned dipoles create an opposing field that reduces the net field inside the dielectric.

Common misconception: The dielectric does not cancel E — it REDUCES E by a factor κ, not to zero.

How it can be tested: Explain qualitatively why κ > 1 for all materials.

Gauss's Law in Dielectrics

Modified Gauss's law: ∮D·dA = Q_free/ε₀ where D = ε₀κE. D is the displacement field; it is determined by free charges only.

Common misconception: D is not the same as E — D accounts for dielectric polarization; E is the actual field in the material.

How it can be tested: Apply Gauss's law with D to find E in the presence of a dielectric.

Key Vocabulary
  • Dielectric constant κ — κ = C/C₀ ≥ 1; dimensionless; ratio of capacitance with/without dielectric
  • Permittivity ε — ε = κε₀; effective permittivity in the dielectric
  • Bound charge — Charge induced on dielectric surface by polarization; reduces E
  • Displacement field D — D = ε₀κE = εE; determined by free charges only
  • Dielectric breakdown — Maximum E before dielectric conducts; dielectric strength (V/m)
Objectives
  • Predict how a dielectric changes C, E, V, Q, and energy.
  • Explain the molecular origin of dielectric polarization.
  • Apply D = εE to find E in a dielectric using Gauss's law.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: capacitor fully filled with dielectric — compare all quantities.
  • Molecular: explain why κ_water >> κ_glass.
  • Calculate new capacitance and charge for a partially filled capacitor.
Practice before moving on
  • Compare dielectric insertion at constant V (battery connected) vs. constant Q.
  • Analyze energy before and after inserting a dielectric at constant V.
Key Formulas
  • Dielectric: \( C = \kappa\varepsilon_0\dfrac{A}{d} \)

Conductors: Advanced Boundary Conditions

Learn on this site
Uniqueness Theorem

The Poisson equation ∇²V = -ρ/ε₀ has a unique solution given boundary conditions. This means any valid solution is THE solution — useful for finding fields by guessing and verifying.

Common misconception: The uniqueness theorem applies to the Poisson equation, not to general electromagnetic scenarios — boundary conditions must fully specify the problem.

How it can be tested: State uniqueness theorem and apply the method of images qualitatively.

Method of Images

A charge q near a grounded conducting plane can be modeled by replacing the conductor with an image charge -q at the mirror position. The field above the plane is the same.

Common misconception: The method of images works only for specific geometries (infinite planes, spheres); it does not generalize to arbitrary shapes.

How it can be tested: Apply the method of images to find the force on a charge near a grounded plane.

Induced Charges and Shielding

Charges near conductors induce surface charges. A Faraday cage (hollow conductor) shields interior from external E. A grounded conductor shields more completely.

Common misconception: A Faraday cage blocks static E fields and transient fields — it does NOT block magnetic fields at low frequency.

How it can be tested: Explain how induced charges on conductors redistribute and shield the interior.

Key Vocabulary
  • Poisson equation — ∇²V = -ρ/ε₀; governs V given charge distribution
  • Laplace equation — ∇²V = 0 in charge-free regions
  • Method of images — Replace conductor with image charge to satisfy boundary conditions
  • Faraday cage — Hollow conductor shielding interior from external E field
  • Uniqueness theorem — Solution to Poisson equation with given BCs is unique
Objectives
  • State the uniqueness theorem and explain its significance.
  • Apply the method of images to a point charge near a grounded plane.
  • Explain Faraday cage shielding qualitatively.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: use method of images to find force on charge q at distance d from grounded plane.
  • Conceptual: explain why interior of a car is safe during a lightning strike.
  • Derive E just outside a conductor using boundary conditions.
Practice before moving on
  • Solve the method-of-images problem for a point charge near a conducting plane.
  • Explain how a Faraday cage works in practical shielding applications.
