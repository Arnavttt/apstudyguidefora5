Current, Resistance, and Ohm's Law

Learn on this site
Electric Current

I = dq/dt (A = C/s); current is the rate of charge flow. Conventional current flows from + to −; electron current flows opposite.

Common misconception: Conventional current direction is historically defined as + charge flow — opposite to actual electron flow in metals.

How it can be tested: Calculate current from dq/dt; apply charge continuity in circuit junctions.

Resistance and Ohm's Law

R = V/I (Ω); for ohmic materials, R is constant. Resistivity: R = ρL/A where ρ depends on material and temperature. Ohm's law: V = IR.

Common misconception: Ohm's law (V = IR with constant R) is a special case, not a fundamental law — many devices are non-ohmic.

How it can be tested: Calculate resistance from geometry and resistivity; apply V = IR.

Power Dissipation

P = IV = I²R = V²/R; power is dissipated as heat in a resistor. In a battery, P = εI (emf × current).

Common misconception: P = IV applies to any circuit element; P = I²R and V²/R are for resistors specifically (Ohm's law assumed).

How it can be tested: Calculate power for resistors and sources in various circuit configurations.

Key Vocabulary
  • Current I — I = dq/dt; rate of charge flow (A = C/s)
  • Resistance R — R = V/I; opposition to current (Ω)
  • Resistivity ρ — R = ρL/A; material property (Ω·m)
  • Ohm's law — V = IR; holds for ohmic materials
  • Power P — P = IV = I²R = V²/R; electrical energy per unit time (W)
Objectives
  • Calculate current using I = dq/dt.
  • Apply R = ρL/A and Ohm's law to circuit elements.
  • Calculate power dissipated by and supplied to circuit elements.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: find current and power in a multi-resistor circuit.
  • Temperature effect: explain how R changes with temperature (R = R₀(1+αΔT)).
  • Power: find efficiency of a real battery with internal resistance.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice V = IR and power calculations for various circuit configurations.
  • Calculate R from material dimensions and resistivity.

Kirchhoff's Laws and Circuit Analysis

Learn on this site
Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)

Sum of currents into a node = sum out; ΣI_in = ΣI_out. Conservation of charge; no charge accumulates at a node.

Common misconception: KCL applies to all circuits — steady-state AND transient. It is a statement of charge conservation.

How it can be tested: Write and solve KCL equations for multi-junction circuits.

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)

Sum of voltage drops around any closed loop = 0: ΣV = 0. This is a statement of energy conservation (path-independent potential).

Common misconception: Sign convention: voltage rises across EMFs in the direction of current; voltage drops across resistors.

How it can be tested: Write and solve KVL equations for multi-loop circuits.

Solving Multi-Loop Circuits

Assign unknown currents to each branch, apply KCL at nodes, and KVL around independent loops. Solve the resulting system of linear equations.

Common misconception: Choose loop directions consistently — currents that come out negative just flow in the opposite direction.

How it can be tested: Solve 2- and 3-loop circuits using KCL and KVL systems of equations.

Key Vocabulary
  • KCL — ΣI_in = ΣI_out at any circuit node (charge conservation)
  • KVL — ΣV = 0 around any closed loop (energy conservation)
  • Branch current — Current through a specific element; assigned as unknown
  • Loop current — Hypothetical current circling a loop in mesh analysis
  • Node voltage — Voltage at a node relative to reference; used in nodal analysis
Objectives
  • Write and apply KCL and KVL to multi-loop circuits.
  • Assign branch currents and set up a system of equations.
  • Solve 2-loop circuits numerically and symbolically.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: solve a 3-element 2-loop circuit using Kirchhoff's laws.
  • Verify solution by checking power balance (total power supplied = total dissipated).
  • Wheatstone bridge: find conditions for zero current through the galvanometer.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice setting up and solving KCL/KVL equations for 3 different circuits.
  • Verify each solution by checking all KCL and KVL conditions.

RC Circuits

Learn on this site
Charging an RC Circuit

When a battery charges a capacitor through R: q(t) = Cε(1 - e^{-t/RC}); I(t) = (ε/R)e^{-t/RC}. Time constant τ = RC. After 5τ, the capacitor is ~99% fully charged.

Common misconception: The time constant τ = RC depends on both R and C — larger R or C means slower charging.

How it can be tested: Derive the charging equation by solving the KVL differential equation.

Discharging an RC Circuit

When a charged capacitor (initial V₀) discharges through R: q(t) = Q₀ e^{-t/RC}; I(t) = (V₀/R)e^{-t/RC}. Current decreases exponentially.

Common misconception: During discharge, I is not constant — it decreases as the capacitor empties and V drops.

How it can be tested: Derive the discharge equation and calculate time for charge to reach a fraction of Q₀.

Energy in RC Circuits

Energy delivered by battery = Cε²; energy stored in capacitor = ½Cε²; energy dissipated in R = ½Cε². Exactly half the battery energy is always dissipated, regardless of R.

Common misconception: The energy lost in R is always ½Cε², regardless of the value of R — changing R only changes the speed of charging, not the energy dissipated.

How it can be tested: Calculate energy balance in RC charging from first principles.

Key Vocabulary
  • Time constant τ — τ = RC; characteristic time scale for RC circuit response
  • Charging equation — q(t) = Cε(1 - e^{-t/τ}); I(t) = (ε/R)e^{-t/τ}
  • Discharging equation — q(t) = Q₀e^{-t/τ}; V(t) = V₀e^{-t/τ}
  • Energy balance — Battery energy Cε²: half stored in C, half dissipated in R
  • Five time constants — After 5τ, capacitor is ≈99.3% fully charged or discharged
Objectives
  • Derive the charging and discharging equations for an RC circuit.
  • Calculate charge, current, and voltage at any time t in an RC circuit.
  • Explain the energy balance: half the battery energy is always dissipated.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: derive q(t) for charging by writing and solving the KVL ODE.
  • Find time for capacitor to reach 90% of final charge.
  • Energy: show that energy dissipated = ½Cε² regardless of R.
Practice before moving on
  • Solve the charging ODE by separation of variables.
  • Practice energy balance calculations for several RC scenarios.

Circuit Theorems and Power Delivery

Learn on this site
Thevenin and Norton Equivalents

Any linear circuit can be replaced by a Thevenin equivalent (V_th in series with R_th) or Norton equivalent (I_N in parallel with R_N). V_th = open-circuit voltage; R_th = resistance with sources zeroed.

Common misconception: To find R_th, replace voltage sources with short circuits and current sources with open circuits, then measure resistance between terminals.

How it can be tested: Find Thevenin and Norton equivalents for simple circuits.

Maximum Power Transfer

Maximum power is delivered to the load when R_load = R_th (Thevenin resistance). Maximum P_max = V_th²/(4R_th).

Common misconception: Maximum power transfer does NOT maximize efficiency — at maximum P transfer, 50% is wasted in R_th.

How it can be tested: Find R_load for maximum power transfer and calculate P_max.

Superposition in Circuits

In a linear circuit with multiple sources, find the contribution of each source separately (deactivate others) and add results. Applies to voltage and current.

Common misconception: Superposition applies to voltages and currents, NOT to power — P = I²R is nonlinear.

How it can be tested: Apply superposition to find current or voltage in a circuit with multiple sources.

Key Vocabulary
  • Thevenin equivalent — V_th and R_th in series; equal to original circuit at terminals
  • Norton equivalent — I_N and R_N in parallel; I_N = V_th/R_th
  • Maximum power transfer — P_max when R_load = R_th; P_max = V_th²/(4R_th)
  • Superposition — Find response from each source separately; sum for total response
  • Efficiency — η = P_load/P_total; at max power transfer η = 50%
Objectives
  • Find Thevenin equivalent of a circuit at a pair of terminals.
  • Calculate R_load for maximum power transfer and P_max.
  • Apply superposition to find current from multiple sources.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: find V_th and R_th for a given two-terminal network.
  • Calculate power delivered to load as function of R_load; find maximum.
  • Superposition: find current through a specific resistor with two batteries.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice Thevenin reduction for 3 different circuit topologies.
  • Verify superposition result using Kirchhoff's laws.

Two resistors (R₁ = 10 Ω, R₂ = 40 Ω) in series with V = 50 V. Find current, power in each, and verify energy balance.
  • A) P_total = 50 W = IV = 1 × 50 ✓
  • B) Energy balance: power in = power out
  • C) I = V/(R₁+R₂) = 50/50 = 1 A
  • D) P₁ = I²R₁ = 10 W; P₂ = I²R₂ = 40 W
C is correct. I = V/(R₁+R₂) = 50/50 = 1 A
Q10
An RC circuit has τ = 2 s. At t = 0 the capacitor is fully charged to V₀ = 10 V. Find V_C at t = 1 s, 2 s, 4 s.
  • A) V_C(2) = 10e^{-1} = 10 × 0.368 = 3.68 V
  • B) V_C(4) = 10e^{-2} = 10 × 0.135 = 1.35 V
  • C) V_C(t) = V₀e^{-t/τ}
  • D) V_C(1) = 10e^{-0.5} = 10 × 0.607 = 6.07 V
C is correct. V_C(t) = V₀e^{-t/τ}
