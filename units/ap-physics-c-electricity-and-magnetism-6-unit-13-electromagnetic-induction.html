Mutual Inductance and Coupled Circuits

Learn on this site
Mutual Inductance

M = NΦ₁₂/I₂; when current in coil 2 changes, EMF is induced in coil 1: ε₁ = -M(dI₂/dt). M is symmetric: M₁₂ = M₂₁.

Common misconception: Mutual inductance M is symmetric — the effect of coil 1 on coil 2 equals the reverse.

How it can be tested: Calculate M for coaxial solenoids and determine induced EMF.

Coupled Circuits and Transformers

Ideal transformer: ε₂/ε₁ = N₂/N₁; I₁/I₂ = N₂/N₁; P₁ = P₂. The coupling coefficient k = M/√(L₁L₂) (k = 1 ideal).

Common misconception: An ideal transformer conserves power (P₁ = P₂) — not charge or current — and assumes no flux leakage.

How it can be tested: Apply transformer ratios; explain step-up and step-down configurations.

Generators and Motors

Generator: mechanical rotation → electrical energy; EMF = NBAω sinωt. Motor: electrical energy → mechanical; torque τ = NIABsinθ. Both use Faraday's law.

Common misconception: Generators and motors are the same device operated in reverse — a motor acts as a generator and vice versa.

How it can be tested: Derive the generator EMF equation; explain back-EMF in a motor.

Key Vocabulary
  • Mutual inductance M — M = NΦ₁₂/I₂; coupling between two coils (H)
  • Induced EMF (mutual) — ε₁ = -M(dI₂/dt); changing current in 2 induces EMF in 1
  • Ideal transformer — V₂/V₁ = N₂/N₁; I₁/I₂ = N₂/N₁; P₁ = P₂
  • Generator EMF — ε = NBAω sinωt; peak EMF = NBAω
  • Back-EMF — EMF generated by motor opposing source voltage; limits current
Objectives
  • Calculate M for coupled coils and find induced EMF.
  • Apply transformer ratios for step-up and step-down configurations.
  • Derive and apply the generator EMF equation.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: derive EMF of a rotating rectangular loop in B field.
  • Transformer: find secondary voltage, current, and power.
  • Motor: explain how back-EMF limits current and why motors draw more current at start.
Practice before moving on

AC Circuits and Phasors

Learn on this site
AC Voltage and Current

V(t) = V₀ sin(ωt); I(t) = I₀ sin(ωt + φ). RMS values: V_rms = V₀/√2; I_rms = I₀/√2. Power: P_avg = V_rms I_rms cosφ.

Common misconception: RMS voltage, not peak voltage, is used for power calculations and is what household "120 V" refers to.

How it can be tested: Convert between peak and RMS values; calculate average power with power factor.

Phasors and Impedance

Resistor: V and I in phase. Inductor: V leads I by 90° (ELI). Capacitor: V lags I by 90° (ICE). Impedance Z: Z_R = R, Z_L = jωL, Z_C = 1/(jωC). Total Z = √(R² + (X_L - X_C)²).

Common misconception: In a phasor diagram, rotating vectors (phasors) represent V and I — their projections onto the real axis give instantaneous values.

How it can be tested: Calculate impedance and phase angle for series RLC circuits.

Power Factor and Resonance

Power factor cosφ = R/Z; average power P = I_rms² R. At resonance (X_L = X_C), cosφ = 1 and P is maximum. Q = ω₀L/R.

Common misconception: Average power is dissipated only in resistors, not in ideal L or C — reactive elements store and return energy.

How it can be tested: Calculate power factor and average power for AC circuits.

Key Vocabulary
  • V_rms — V_rms = V₀/√2 ≈ 0.707V₀; root-mean-square voltage
  • Impedance Z — Z = √(R² + (X_L - X_C)²); total opposition to AC current
  • Inductive reactance X_L — X_L = ωL; increases with frequency
  • Capacitive reactance X_C — X_C = 1/(ωC); decreases with frequency
  • Power factor — cosφ = R/Z; fraction of apparent power that does real work
Objectives
  • Calculate V_rms and I_rms from peak values.
  • Find impedance and phase angle for series RLC circuits.
  • Calculate average power and power factor for AC circuits.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: find I₀, phase angle, and P_avg for a series RLC at given ω.
  • Phasor diagram: draw and label V and I phasors for RLC circuit.
  • Resonance: find ω₀ for max power; calculate Q.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice impedance calculations at different frequencies.
  • Draw phasor diagrams for R, RL, RC, and RLC circuits.

Electromagnetic Wave Energy and Momentum

Learn on this site
Poynting Vector

S = E × B/μ₀ (W/m²); energy flux density of EM wave. Intensity I = |S| = E₀B₀/(2μ₀) = E₀²/(2μ₀c) = cε₀E₀²/2.

Common misconception: The Poynting vector gives power per unit area flowing in the direction of wave propagation.

How it can be tested: Calculate intensity from E₀ or B₀ of an EM wave.

Radiation Pressure

EM waves carry momentum: p = U/c (absorbed) or p = 2U/c (reflected). Radiation pressure P = I/c (absorbed) or 2I/c (reflected).

Common misconception: Radiation pressure is extremely small for normal light intensities — significant only for high-power lasers or in space.

How it can be tested: Calculate radiation pressure and momentum transfer from EM waves.

Electromagnetic Spectrum

EM waves span radio to gamma rays; all travel at c = 3×10⁸ m/s; E = hf (photon energy, quantum). Wavelength-frequency: c = fλ.

Common misconception: All EM waves travel at c in vacuum; speed changes in media (c/n).

How it can be tested: Apply c = fλ to convert between frequency and wavelength; identify spectrum regions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Poynting vector S — S = E×B/μ₀; power per unit area (W/m²)
  • Intensity I — I = |S_avg| = E₀²/(2μ₀c); power per unit area of EM wave
  • Radiation momentum — p = U/c (absorbed); p = 2U/c (reflected)
  • Radiation pressure — P = I/c (absorbed) or 2I/c (reflected)
  • EM spectrum — Radio → microwave → IR → visible → UV → X-ray → gamma; all at c in vacuum
Objectives
  • Calculate intensity and Poynting vector from E₀ of an EM wave.
  • Find radiation pressure for absorbed and reflected waves.
  • Convert between frequency and wavelength across the EM spectrum.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: find intensity and radiation pressure for a laser beam.
  • Calculate force on a solar sail due to radiation pressure.
  • Identify regions of EM spectrum from wavelength or frequency.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice intensity calculations from E₀ and B₀.
  • Calculate solar radiation pressure on spacecraft surfaces.

Applications: Motors, Generators, and Power Systems

Learn on this site
AC Power Generation

Power plants convert mechanical energy → AC power. Three-phase power provides constant total power (no zero crossings). Transmitted at high voltage to minimize I²R losses.

Common misconception: Three-phase power is used because single-phase power is zero twice per cycle — three-phase provides constant power.

How it can be tested: Explain qualitatively why AC is used for power transmission and three-phase for motors.

Induction Motors and Eddy Currents

Induction motors: rotating magnetic field induces currents in the rotor, creating torque. No brushes or commutator. Eddy currents in transformers cause energy loss — laminated cores reduce them.

Common misconception: Eddy currents are induced by changing flux — using laminated cores breaks the current loops and reduces losses.

How it can be tested: Explain induction motor operation and how eddy currents are minimized.

Practical Circuit Applications

Rectifiers convert AC to DC using diodes. Filters smooth ripple. Voltage regulators maintain constant DC output. These are the basis of all electronic power supplies.

Common misconception: A diode allows current in one direction — a bridge rectifier uses four diodes to convert full-wave AC to pulsating DC.

How it can be tested: Trace the path of current through a full-wave bridge rectifier; explain filter capacitor function.

Key Vocabulary
  • Three-phase power — Three AC voltages 120° apart; sum is constant; used industrially
  • Induction motor — Rotating field induces rotor currents; torque without brushes
  • Eddy currents — Currents induced in bulk conductors by changing B; cause power loss
  • Laminated core — Thin insulated sheets break eddy current paths → reduced loss
  • Rectifier — Diode circuit converting AC to pulsating DC
Objectives
  • Explain why AC power is transmitted at high voltage.
  • Describe how an induction motor and eddy current braking work.
  • Trace current through a bridge rectifier and explain the filter capacitor.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: calculate power loss in a transmission line at high vs. low voltage.
  • Multiple choice: identify the effect of increasing transmission voltage.
  • Conceptual: explain why transformer cores are laminated.
Practice before moving on
  • Calculate transmission line losses for different voltages.
  • Research practical applications of induction motors in industry.

  • B) F = P_rad × A = 1.67×10⁻⁶ × 1 = 1.67 μN
  • C) E₀ = √(2Iμ₀c) = √(2 × 500 × 4π×10⁻⁷ × 3×10⁸) = √(376.9) ≈ 614 V/m
  • D) B₀ = E₀/c = 614/(3×10⁸) = 2.05 μT
C is correct. E₀ = √(2Iμ₀c) = √(2 × 500 × 4π×10⁻⁷ × 3×10⁸) = √(376.9) ≈ 614 V/m
Q10
Describe the operation of a three-phase induction motor from first principles.
  • A) Rotating B induces currents in the short-circuited squirrel-cage rotor (Faraday)
  • B) Induced currents and rotating B create torque (Lenz) — rotor follows the field
  • C) Three coil sets in stator, driven by three-phase AC (120° apart in phase)
  • D) At each instant, currents create a resultant rotating magnetic field in the stator
C is correct. Three coil sets in stator, driven by three-phase AC (120° apart in phase)
