Forces and Free-Body Diagrams

Learn on this site
Types of Forces

Contact forces (normal, friction, tension, spring) and field forces (gravity, electric, magnetic). Normal force is perpendicular to the surface; friction is parallel.

Common misconception: Normal force is not always equal to weight — it depends on the surface orientation and other forces.

How it can be tested: Draw FBDs for objects on inclines, in elevators, and in circular motion.

Free-Body Diagram Technique

Represent each object as a point; draw all forces acting ON the object (not by it); label with symbols; choose a coordinate system aligned with the acceleration.

Common misconception: Common error: including velocity or acceleration as forces — these are not forces.

How it can be tested: Draw and label FBDs for multi-body problems with correct force identification.

Newton's Third Law

For every force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force acting on a different object. Newton's 3rd law pairs always act on different objects.

Common misconception: 3rd law pairs act on different objects and are always of the same type; do not include both in one object's FBD.

How it can be tested: Identify Newton's 3rd law pairs in complex systems.

Key Vocabulary
  • Normal force N — Contact force perpendicular to a surface
  • Tension T — Pulling force exerted by a rope or string along its length
  • Friction f — Contact force parallel to surface opposing relative motion
  • Free-body diagram (FBD) — Diagram showing all external forces on one object
  • Newton's 3rd law pair — Equal and opposite forces on two different objects
Objectives
  • Draw complete, accurate FBDs for objects in various scenarios.
  • Identify all forces acting on each object in a system.
  • Apply Newton's 3rd law to identify action-reaction pairs.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: draw FBD for box on an incline and an Atwood machine.
  • Multiple choice: which forces are present on an object on a curved surface?
  • Identify which forces belong to 3rd-law pairs in a given system.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice FBDs for at least 5 different scenarios.
  • Identify all 3rd-law pairs in a system of two connected blocks.

Newton's Second Law: Applications

Learn on this site
ΣF = ma in Components

Apply Newton's 2nd law separately in x and y (and z) directions. Align coordinate axes with the acceleration direction for simplicity.

Common misconception: The net force ΣF must be the vector sum of all forces — do not omit any.

How it can be tested: Solve for unknown forces and accelerations using component equations.

Inclined Planes

On an incline at angle θ: a = g(sinθ - μcosθ) for sliding down with kinetic friction. Choose x along incline, y perpendicular.

Common misconception: On an incline, weight must be resolved into components — mg is not along or perpendicular to the incline.

How it can be tested: Derive and apply the acceleration formula for an incline with friction.

Atwood Machine

Two masses over a frictionless pulley: a = (m₁ - m₂)g/(m₁ + m₂); T = 2m₁m₂g/(m₁ + m₂). Treat rope as single constraint.

Common misconception: In an Atwood machine, the two masses have the SAME magnitude of acceleration (connected by inextensible rope).

How it can be tested: Set up and solve the Atwood machine equations with and without friction.

Key Vocabulary
  • Component equations — ΣFx = max; ΣFy = may applied separately
  • Incline angle θ — Angle of slope; W_parallel = mg sinθ; W_perp = mg cosθ
  • Kinetic friction — f_k = μ_k N; opposes direction of sliding motion
  • Static friction — f_s ≤ μ_s N; adjusts to prevent motion up to maximum
  • Atwood machine — Two masses on a pulley; constraint: |a₁| = |a₂|
Objectives
  • Apply ΣF = ma in component form to incline and pulley problems.
  • Derive acceleration of an Atwood machine from first principles.
  • Distinguish static and kinetic friction and apply correctly.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: two blocks on a table connected by a string over a pulley.
  • Incline: find minimum μ_s to prevent sliding.
  • Atwood: find T and a given m₁ and m₂.
Practice before moving on
  • Solve at least 5 incline problems with varying friction conditions.
  • Derive the Atwood machine equations independently.

Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation

Learn on this site
Gravitational Force

F_g = Gm₁m₂/r² where G = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg². Force is attractive, along the line joining the masses, inversely proportional to r².

Common misconception: Gravitational force decreases as r increases — doubling r reduces force by a factor of 4 (inverse square law).

How it can be tested: Calculate gravitational force and field strength at various distances.

Gravitational Field

Gravitational field g = GM/r² at distance r from mass M. Near Earth's surface, g ≈ 9.8 m/s². Field lines point toward the center of the source mass.

Common misconception: The "g" in F = mg is the local gravitational field strength, not a universal constant.

How it can be tested: Compute g at different altitudes above Earth's surface.

Shell Theorem

A uniform spherical shell exerts no gravitational force on objects inside it; outside, it acts as if all mass is concentrated at its center.

Common misconception: Inside a solid uniform sphere, g ∝ r (not constant) — students often wrongly apply 1/r² inside.

How it can be tested: Apply shell theorem to objects inside and outside spherical mass distributions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Universal gravitation — F = Gm₁m₂/r²; attractive inverse-square law
  • G — Universal gravitational constant = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg²
  • Gravitational field g — g = GM/r²; force per unit mass at a point
  • Shell theorem — Spherical shell: zero field inside; acts as point mass outside
  • Inverse square law — Force ∝ 1/r²; doubles distance → quarter force
Objectives
  • Calculate gravitational force between two masses.
  • Find g at a given altitude above Earth's surface.
  • Apply the shell theorem to inside/outside sphere problems.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: find g at altitude h above Earth's surface.
  • Calculate the gravitational force between two specified masses.
  • Shell theorem: find force on a mass inside a hollow sphere.
Practice before moving on
  • Compute g at the surface, 1 R_E, and 2 R_E above Earth.
  • Compare gravitational force at different orbital radii.

Dynamics: Circular Motion and Drag

Learn on this site
Circular Dynamics

The net centripetal force ΣF = mv²/r must be provided by real forces (gravity, tension, normal, friction). Identify which force(s) provide centripetal force in each geometry.

Common misconception: Centripetal force is not a new force — it is the name for the net inward force provided by real forces.

How it can be tested: Solve circular dynamics problems at the top and bottom of loops, banked curves.

Banked Curves

For a frictionless banked curve: tan θ = v²/(rg). For a curve with friction, there is a range of speeds. Choose axes along and perpendicular to the bank.

Common misconception: On a frictionless bank, the horizontal component of N provides centripetal force — no friction needed at the correct speed.

How it can be tested: Derive and apply the banked curve formula.

Linear Drag (Stokes' Law)

In viscous fluids, drag force F_d = bv (linearly proportional to velocity) or F_d = ½ρCₐAv² (quadratic). Terminal velocity when drag equals weight.

Common misconception: Terminal velocity is reached asymptotically — object never truly reaches it instantaneously.

How it can be tested: Write and solve the differential equation for motion with linear drag.

Key Vocabulary
  • Centripetal force — Net inward force = mv²/r; provided by gravity, tension, N, or friction
  • Banked curve — Inclined road that allows circular motion without friction at design speed
  • Terminal velocity — Speed at which drag force = weight; net force = 0
  • Linear drag — F_d = bv; drag proportional to velocity in viscous flow
  • Quadratic drag — F_d = ½ρCₐAv²; dominant at high speeds in air
Objectives
  • Identify the force(s) providing centripetal acceleration in circular dynamics problems.
  • Derive and apply the frictionless banked curve formula.
  • Set up and solve the differential equation for linear drag.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: car at top and bottom of a circular loop — find N at each.
  • Derive terminal velocity for linear drag from F_net = 0.
  • Multi-part: banked curve problem with and without friction.
Practice before moving on
  • Solve loop-the-loop problems at top and bottom.
  • Practice the banked curve derivation from scratch.
