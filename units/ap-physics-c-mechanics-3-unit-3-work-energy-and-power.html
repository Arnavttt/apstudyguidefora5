Work as a Line Integral

Learn on this site
Work Definition

W = ∫F·ds = ∫F cosθ ds; for constant force and straight path, W = F·d cosθ. Work is a scalar; positive work increases KE.

Common misconception: Work can be negative if force opposes displacement (e.g., friction does negative work).

How it can be tested: Calculate work for constant and variable forces using integrals.

Work Done by Variable Forces

W = ∫F(x) dx from x₁ to x₂. For a spring: W = ½kx₂² - ½kx₁² (or -ΔPE_spring). Graphically, work = area under F-x graph.

Common misconception: The work done BY a spring on a block is negative if the spring is pushing the block back to equilibrium.

How it can be tested: Compute work using F(x) integration and F-x graph areas.

Work-Energy Theorem

W_net = ΔKE = ½mv₂² - ½mv₁². Net work equals the change in kinetic energy — this is a fundamental result derived from Newton's 2nd law.

Common misconception: W_net includes all forces; individual force work can be positive or negative — only the net determines ΔKE.

How it can be tested: Apply work-energy theorem to find speeds after forces act.

Key Vocabulary
  • Work W — W = ∫F·ds; scalar quantity (J = N·m)
  • Work-energy theorem — W_net = ΔKE; net work equals change in KE
  • Variable force work — W = ∫F(x)dx; area under F-x graph
  • Spring work — W = ½kx² for spring compressed/stretched by x from natural length
  • Negative work — Work done by friction or opposing forces that decrease KE
Objectives
  • Calculate work for constant forces using W = Fd cosθ.
  • Compute work for variable forces using integration.
  • Apply the work-energy theorem to find final speed.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: find work done by a spring using integration.
  • Calculation: use W-E theorem to find speed after a net force acts.
  • F-x graph: find work from area under the curve.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice ∫F(x)dx for several force functions.
  • Verify work-energy theorem using kinematics as a check.

Potential Energy and Conservation of Energy

Learn on this site
Conservative Forces and Potential Energy

A force is conservative if the work done is path-independent. PE is defined as ΔU = -W_conservative. For gravity: U_g = mgh; for spring: U_s = ½kx².

Common misconception: Only conservative forces can be associated with potential energy — friction is not conservative.

How it can be tested: Identify conservative forces and compute their potential energies.

Conservation of Mechanical Energy

When only conservative forces act: KE + PE = constant. E_mech = ½mv² + U is conserved when friction is absent.

Common misconception: Mechanical energy is NOT conserved when friction or other non-conservative forces do work.

How it can be tested: Apply energy conservation to find speed at any point in a conservative system.

Work by Non-Conservative Forces

W_nc = ΔKE + ΔPE = ΔE_mech. Non-conservative forces (friction, drag) change the total mechanical energy of the system.

Common misconception: Energy is always conserved (1st law); mechanical energy decreases only because it converts to thermal energy via friction.

How it can be tested: Calculate energy lost to friction using W_nc = ΔE_mech.

Key Vocabulary
  • Conservative force — Force for which work is path-independent; associated with PE
  • Non-conservative force — Force (friction, drag) for which work is path-dependent
  • Gravitational PE — U_g = mgh; reference point chosen arbitrarily
  • Elastic PE — U_s = ½kx²; stored in a compressed/stretched spring
  • Energy dissipation — Mechanical energy converted to thermal by friction: ΔE_thermal = |W_friction|
Objectives
  • Define conservative forces and compute associated potential energies.
  • Apply energy conservation when only conservative forces act.
  • Use W_nc = ΔE_mech to account for friction.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: ball on a curved track — find speed at various heights.
  • Calculation: spring-launched block with friction — find speed at specific point.
  • Identify energy transformations throughout a given process.
Practice before moving on
  • Solve at least 5 conservation of energy problems with and without friction.
  • Practice energy bar charts at multiple points in a system.

Power and Energy in Complex Systems

Learn on this site
Power

P = dW/dt = F·v; average power P_avg = W/t. SI unit: watt (W = J/s).

Common misconception: Power depends on force AND velocity — a slow, large force can have the same power as a fast, small force.

How it can be tested: Calculate instantaneous and average power for various situations.

Center of Mass Energy

The kinetic energy of a system has a center-of-mass component (½Mv_cm²) and an internal component (KE relative to CM). Only the CM KE is affected by external forces.

Common misconception: In a collision, only CM kinetic energy changes if external forces are absent — internal KE is unchanged by external impulse.

How it can be tested: Decompose KE into CM and relative components for two-body systems.

Energy in Gravitational Fields

Gravitational PE: U = -GMm/r (negative for bound orbits). At r → ∞, U = 0. Escape velocity: v_esc = √(2GM/r).

Common misconception: Gravitational PE is negative for bound systems — binding energy is how much must be added to free the object.

How it can be tested: Calculate escape velocity and binding energy for orbital systems.

Key Vocabulary
  • Power P — P = dW/dt = F·v; rate of energy transfer (W = J/s)
  • Average power — P_avg = W_total/Δt
  • Instantaneous power — P = F·v at a specific instant
  • Escape velocity — v_esc = √(2GM/r); minimum speed to escape gravity
  • Gravitational PE (orbit) — U = -GMm/r; negative for bound orbit
Objectives
  • Calculate instantaneous power using P = Fv.
  • Find escape velocity from a planet using energy conservation.
  • Explain why U = -GMm/r is negative and what that means physically.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: find power output of a motor lifting a mass at constant velocity.
  • Calculation: find orbital KE, PE, and total energy at radius r.
  • Derive escape velocity from energy conservation.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice power problems with varying force and velocity.
  • Compute total orbital energy at various radii.

Potential Energy Functions and Force

Learn on this site
Force from Potential Energy

F = -dU/dx in 1D; the force is the negative gradient of potential energy. Equilibrium where dU/dx = 0; stable if d²U/dx² > 0.

Common misconception: The minus sign is essential: force points "downhill" on a potential energy curve.

How it can be tested: Derive force from a given U(x) by differentiation.

Potential Energy Diagrams

A U(x) graph (energy landscape) shows equilibrium points, turning points (where KE = 0), and stability. Total energy E is a horizontal line on the diagram.

Common misconception: At a turning point, v = 0 but a ≠ 0 (force still acts); the object reverses direction.

How it can be tested: Analyze motion qualitatively from a U(x) diagram.

Multiple Conservative Forces

Total mechanical energy E = KE + U_gravity + U_spring + U_other. Each conservative force contributes its own PE term.

Common misconception: Do not mix PE and W_by_force in the same equation — use either all PE or all work, not both.

How it can be tested: Apply energy conservation with combined gravitational and elastic PE.

Key Vocabulary
  • F = -dU/dx — Force is the negative derivative of potential energy
  • Equilibrium — Point where F = 0; dU/dx = 0
  • Stable equilibrium — F restores object after displacement; d²U/dx² > 0 (U is minimum)
  • Turning point — Position where KE = 0; object momentarily at rest, then reverses
  • Energy landscape — Graph of U(x) with total energy E shown as horizontal line
Objectives
  • Derive force from a given potential energy function.
  • Identify stable and unstable equilibria from a U(x) diagram.
  • Find turning points and describe motion using U(x) graphs.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: given U(x), find F(x), equilibria, and describe motion.
  • Graph: sketch U(x) for a spring-mass system and gravity combined.
  • Multiple choice: interpret energy landscape for a particle.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice F = -dU/dx for several U(x) functions.
  • Analyze energy diagrams for 2–3 different physical systems.
