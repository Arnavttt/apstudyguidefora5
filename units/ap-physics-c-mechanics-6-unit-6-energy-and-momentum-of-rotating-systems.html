The SHM Differential Equation

Learn on this site
Deriving the SHM Equation

From F = -kx and F = ma: m(d²x/dt²) = -kx → d²x/dt² = -(k/m)x = -ω²x where ω = √(k/m). The solution is x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ).

Common misconception: The angular frequency ω is not chosen — it is determined by the physical parameters k and m (or g and L for a pendulum).

How it can be tested: Verify that x = A cos(ωt + φ) satisfies d²x/dt² = -ω²x.

General Solution and Initial Conditions

x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ); the constants A (amplitude) and φ (phase angle) are determined by initial conditions x(0) and v(0).

Common misconception: A and φ are not always 0 — they depend on when and how the motion was initiated.

How it can be tested: Determine A and φ from initial conditions x₀ and v₀.

Velocity and Acceleration in SHM

v(t) = -Aω sin(ωt + φ); a(t) = -Aω² cos(ωt + φ) = -ω²x. The relationships v_max = Aω and a_max = Aω² follow.

Common misconception: v and a are 90° out of phase: v is maximum when x = 0; a is maximum (magnitude) when x = ±A.

How it can be tested: Write and use v(t) and a(t) for a given SHM system.

Key Vocabulary
  • ω = √(k/m) — Angular frequency of spring-mass SHM
  • x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ) — General SHM position function
  • Phase angle φ — Determined by initial conditions; shifts the cosine function
  • v_max = Aω — Maximum speed at x = 0
  • a = -ω²x — SHM: acceleration proportional to and opposite to displacement
Objectives
  • Derive that x = A cos(ωt + φ) satisfies d²x/dt² = -ω²x.
  • Determine A and φ from initial conditions.
  • Compute v(t) and a(t) by differentiation.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: given x(0) = 0.1 m and v(0) = 0, write x(t), v(t), a(t).
  • Verify x = A sin(ωt) also satisfies the SHM ODE.
  • Find maximum speed and acceleration for a given A and ω.
Practice before moving on
  • Solve for A and φ for various initial conditions.
  • Practice differentiating x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ) twice to verify a = -ω²x.

Energy in SHM and Pendulums

Learn on this site
Energy in SHM

E = ½kA² = ½mv² + ½kx² (constant). At x = 0: all KE; at x = ±A: all PE. Speed at position x: v = ω√(A² - x²).

Common misconception: Total energy E = ½kA² is fixed by amplitude; energy continuously converts between KE and PE.

How it can be tested: Apply energy conservation to find speed at any position in SHM.

Simple Pendulum as SHM

For small θ: F_restoring ≈ -mg sinθ ≈ -mgθ. Setting F = m(d²(Lθ)/dt²) gives d²θ/dt² = -(g/L)θ → ω = √(g/L). Valid only for small angles.

Common misconception: The pendulum is SHM only for small angles (θ < ~10°); for large angles, the period increases and the problem requires elliptic integrals.

How it can be tested: Derive the pendulum ODE and apply it for small oscillations.

Physical Pendulum

A rigid body oscillating about a pivot: τ = -MgD sinθ ≈ -MgDθ for small θ; d²θ/dt² = -(MgD/I)θ → ω = √(MgD/I) where D is distance from pivot to CM.

Common misconception: The physical pendulum ODE requires knowing both I and the CM distance from the pivot.

How it can be tested: Derive ω for a physical pendulum (disk, rod) about a given pivot.

Key Vocabulary
  • Energy in SHM — E = ½kA² = const; converts between KE and PE
  • Speed in SHM — v = ω√(A² - x²) from energy conservation
  • Simple pendulum — ω = √(g/L) for small oscillations
  • Physical pendulum — ω = √(MgD/I); D = CM distance from pivot
  • Small angle approximation — sinθ ≈ θ (in radians) for θ < 10°
Objectives
  • Apply energy conservation to find speed at any x in SHM.
  • Derive the pendulum equation of motion and identify ω.
  • Calculate ω for a physical pendulum given I and geometry.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: derive d²θ/dt² + (g/L)θ = 0 for a simple pendulum.
  • Physical pendulum: find period for a rod pivoting at one end.
  • Energy: find position where KE = PE in SHM.
Practice before moving on
  • Derive v = ω√(A² - x²) from energy conservation.
  • Calculate ω for a uniform disk pendulum about its rim.

Damped and Driven Oscillations

Learn on this site
Damped Oscillations

With linear damping F_d = -bv: md²x/dt² + b(dx/dt) + kx = 0. Solution: x(t) = Ae^(-bt/2m) cos(ω't + φ); ω' = √(ω₀² - (b/2m)²). Three regimes: underdamped, critically damped, overdamped.

Common misconception: Underdamping → oscillates with decreasing amplitude; critical damping → fastest approach to equilibrium without oscillating; overdamping → slow return without oscillating.

How it can be tested: Classify damping regime given b and identify qualitative behavior.

Driven Oscillations and Resonance

When driven by F₀ cos(ω_d t): amplitude peaks at ω_d ≈ ω₀ (resonance). Amplitude A = F₀/(m√((ω₀²-ω_d²)² + (bω_d/m)²)). At resonance with low damping, A can be very large.

Common misconception: Resonance occurs at ω_d ≈ ω₀, not at ω_d = 0 or infinity — the driving frequency must MATCH the natural frequency.

How it can be tested: Explain how damping limits resonance amplitude and identify resonance conditions.

Energy in Damped Systems

Energy of a damped oscillator: E(t) = ½kA² e^(-bt/m) — decays exponentially. Power dissipated = bv². Quality factor Q = ω₀m/b measures sharpness of resonance.

Common misconception: Energy decays at twice the rate of amplitude (since E ∝ A²).

How it can be tested: Calculate energy dissipation rate and quality factor for damped oscillators.

Key Vocabulary
  • Underdamped — Oscillates with exponentially decreasing amplitude; ω' < ω₀
  • Critical damping — Fastest return to equilibrium without oscillation; b = 2√(km)
  • Overdamped — Returns to equilibrium slowly without oscillating; b > 2√(km)
  • Resonance — Maximum amplitude when ω_d = ω₀ (natural frequency)
  • Quality factor Q — Q = ω₀m/b; higher Q → sharper resonance peak
Objectives
  • Classify oscillators as under-, over-, or critically damped.
  • Explain resonance and how damping affects maximum amplitude.
  • Calculate energy dissipation in a damped oscillator.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: sketch amplitude vs. ω_d for driven oscillator with different damping.
  • Calculate Q factor for a given oscillator; predict resonance peak width.
  • Identify which damping regime applies given b, m, k.
Practice before moving on
  • Compare the three damping regimes on position-time graphs.
  • Research real-world resonance examples (MRI, musical instruments, suspension bridges).

Coupled Oscillators and Normal Modes

Learn on this site
Two Coupled Oscillators

Two masses connected by springs can oscillate in normal modes: in-phase (both move together, lower ω) and out-of-phase (move oppositely, higher ω). Each mode has a characteristic frequency.

Common misconception: Coupled oscillators have TWO natural frequencies (modes), not one — choosing initial conditions determines which mode is excited.

How it can be tested: Identify normal modes and frequencies for a symmetric two-mass system.

Energy Transfer Between Coupled Oscillators

When two identical oscillators are weakly coupled and one is started, energy transfers periodically to the other and back (beats). Beat frequency = |ω₊ - ω₋|.

Common misconception: Beat frequency is the DIFFERENCE of the two mode frequencies, not their sum.

How it can be tested: Describe qualitatively how energy transfers between coupled oscillators.

Superposition and Normal Mode Analysis

Any motion of a coupled system can be written as a superposition of normal modes. Each mode oscillates independently; complex motions are sums of simple normal modes.

Common misconception: Normal modes are independent: exciting one mode does not put energy into the other.

How it can be tested: Express initial conditions as superposition of normal modes.

Key Vocabulary
  • Normal mode — Collective oscillation pattern where all parts move at same frequency
  • In-phase mode — Masses move together; frequency ω₁ = √(k/m) for outer springs only
  • Out-of-phase mode — Masses move oppositely; frequency ω₂ = √(k/m + 2k_c/m) with coupling
  • Beat frequency — |ω₊ - ω₋|; rate of energy transfer between coupled oscillators
  • Superposition — General motion = linear combination of normal modes
Objectives
  • Identify in-phase and out-of-phase modes for coupled oscillators.
  • Calculate normal mode frequencies for a symmetric system.
  • Explain energy transfer as a beat phenomenon.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: find normal mode frequencies for two masses and three springs.
  • Qualitative: describe motion if only one mass is initially displaced.
  • Beat period calculation given two mode frequencies.
Practice before moving on
  • Analyze a symmetric two-mass, three-spring system for both modes.
  • Simulate coupled oscillator motion and identify beat pattern.

  • C) Lower damping b → larger amplitude at resonance
  • D) In the limit b → 0: A → ∞ (unrealistic; materials fail first)

Show that the power dissipated by linear damping is P = bv².
