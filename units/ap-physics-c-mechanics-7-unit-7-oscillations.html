Gravitational Force and Field

Learn on this site
Newton's Law of Gravitation

F = -Gm₁m₂/r² r̂ (vector form). The force is attractive, along the line joining the masses, and obeys an inverse square law.

Common misconception: The inverse square law means FORCE drops as 1/r², not 1/r — doubling distance quarters the force.

How it can be tested: Calculate gravitational force and field at various positions; apply superposition for multiple masses.

Gravitational Field and Potential

g = -GM/r² r̂; gravitational potential V = -GM/r (J/kg). Potential energy U = mV = -GMm/r. ΔV = -∫g·dr.

Common misconception: Gravitational potential V is negative (bound system); gravitational field g = -∇V.

How it can be tested: Compute V and g at a given point; use V to find PE for orbital problems.

Shell Theorem via Integration

The shell theorem states that a uniform spherical shell exerts no force inside (field = 0) and acts as a point mass outside. This is proved by integrating contributions from all shell elements.

Common misconception: The shell theorem requires a UNIFORM spherical shell — irregularly shaped shells do not have zero interior field.

How it can be tested: Apply the shell theorem result; describe qualitatively how to prove it by integration.

Key Vocabulary
  • Gravitational field g — g = -GM/r² r̂; force per unit mass (N/kg = m/s²)
  • Gravitational potential V — V = -GM/r; potential energy per unit mass (J/kg)
  • Potential energy U — U = -GMm/r; negative for bound orbit
  • Superposition — Total field = vector sum of individual mass contributions
  • Shell theorem — Uniform sphere: g = 0 inside shell; point mass outside
Objectives
  • Calculate g and V at a given distance from a mass.
  • Apply superposition to find total field from multiple masses.
  • State and apply the shell theorem for inside and outside a sphere.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: find g and V at altitude h above Earth.
  • Superposition: find total gravitational field at midpoint between two masses.
  • Shell theorem: state and explain why field = 0 inside a shell.
Practice before moving on
  • Practice g and V calculations at several radii.
  • Compute total gravitational PE for a two-body system.

Kepler's Laws

Learn on this site
Kepler's First Law: Elliptical Orbits

Planets orbit the Sun in ellipses with the Sun at one focus. For circular orbits, the Sun is at the center. Ellipse parameters: semi-major axis a, eccentricity e.

Common misconception: The Sun is at a FOCUS of the ellipse, not the center — the planet is sometimes closer, sometimes farther.

How it can be tested: Identify periapsis, apoapsis, and semi-major axis from orbital parameters.

Kepler's Second Law: Equal Areas

A line from the Sun to a planet sweeps equal areas in equal times. This is a consequence of conservation of angular momentum (no torque acts along orbital plane).

Common misconception: Kepler's 2nd law does NOT say the planet moves at constant speed — it moves faster at periapsis.

How it can be tested: Apply the second law: if orbital radius decreases, speed must increase proportionally.

Kepler's Third Law: Period-Radius

T² = (4π²/GM)r³ for circular orbits (r = semi-major axis a for elliptical). Derived from F_grav = F_centripetal. T² ∝ a³ for all planets orbiting the same star.

Common misconception: T² ∝ a³ only applies when orbiting the SAME central body — comparing planets in different solar systems requires different GM.

How it can be tested: Apply T² = (4π²/GM)a³ to find orbital periods and radii.

Key Vocabulary
  • Semi-major axis a — Half the longest diameter of an ellipse; average orbital radius
  • Periapsis/Apoapsis — Closest/farthest points in the orbit from the focus
  • Eccentricity e — e = 0 is circular; e → 1 is highly elongated ellipse
  • Kepler's 3rd law — T² = (4π²/GM)a³; T ∝ a^{3/2}
  • Angular momentum in orbit — L = mvr (constant for circular orbit; v×r = const generally)
Objectives
  • Identify orbital parameters (a, e, periapsis, apoapsis) from given data.
  • Apply Kepler's 2nd law to compare orbital speeds at different positions.
  • Use T² = (4π²/GM)a³ to find period or orbital radius.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: derive Kepler's 3rd law for circular orbit.
  • Comparison: find ratio of speeds at periapsis and apoapsis using L conservation.
  • Calculate T for a given orbit radius using Kepler's 3rd law.
Practice before moving on
  • Derive Kepler's 3rd law from Newton's law for circular orbits.
  • Apply L = mvr to find speed ratio at two orbital points.

Orbital Energy and Escape Velocity

Learn on this site
Total Orbital Energy

For a circular orbit: E = KE + PE = GMm/(2r) - GMm/r = -GMm/(2r). Negative E means bound orbit. Larger orbit → higher (less negative) total energy.

Common misconception: A satellite in a higher orbit has MORE total energy (less negative), not less — this is counterintuitive.

How it can be tested: Calculate KE, PE, and total energy for circular orbits at various radii.

Escape Velocity

E = 0 at escape (v = 0 at r = ∞). Energy conservation: ½mv_esc² - GMm/R = 0 → v_esc = √(2GM/R). Independent of the escaping mass.

Common misconception: Escape velocity is a minimum speed at launch, not a requirement for all altitudes — above atmosphere, any constant speed eventually escapes.

How it can be tested: Calculate escape velocity from planets and moons.

Elliptical Orbit Energy

For an elliptical orbit, E = -GMm/(2a) where a is the semi-major axis. T = 2π√(a³/GM). E depends only on a, not on eccentricity.

Common misconception: Two orbits with the same semi-major axis a have the same energy and period, regardless of eccentricity.

How it can be tested: Apply E = -GMm/(2a) and T = 2π√(a³/GM) to elliptical orbits.

Key Vocabulary
  • Orbital energy — E = -GMm/(2r) for circular orbit; negative → bound
  • Binding energy — Energy needed to escape from orbit: |E| = GMm/(2r)
  • Escape velocity — v_esc = √(2GM/R); set E_total = 0
  • Elliptical orbit energy — E = -GMm/(2a); period T = 2π√(a³/GM)
  • Virial theorem — For circular orbit: |PE| = 2KE; E = -KE = PE/2
Objectives
  • Calculate KE, PE, and total energy for circular orbits.
  • Derive and apply escape velocity formula.
  • Apply E = -GMm/(2a) and T = 2π√(a³/GM) to elliptical orbits.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: calculate energy needed to transfer from low orbit to geostationary orbit.
  • Derive v_esc from energy conservation.
  • Elliptical orbit: find period and energy given periapsis and apoapsis radii.
Practice before moving on
  • Compute orbital energy at several radii for Earth orbit.
  • Find orbital speed and period for GPS satellites (r = 26 560 km).

Gravitational Potential and Advanced Applications

Learn on this site
Gravitational Potential Energy in Extended Systems

U = -Gm₁m₂/r for two point masses; for a system of N bodies, U_total = -G Σ_{i

Common misconception: The total potential energy of three bodies has THREE pair terms — not just one.

How it can be tested: Calculate total PE for a system of point masses.

Tidal Forces

Tidal forces arise because the gravitational field is not uniform; the near side of a body is pulled more than the far side, stretching the body. Magnitude ∝ M/r³.

Common misconception: Tidal forces are differential forces — they are the difference in gravity across the body, not the average gravity.

How it can be tested: Explain qualitatively why tides have two bulges and why r³ dependence matters.

Lagrange Points

Lagrange points (L1–L5) are positions in a two-body orbital system where a third small body can orbit with the same period as the two main bodies. L4 and L5 are stable.

Common misconception: Lagrange points are special solutions, not a general property — they exist only for small mass third bodies.

How it can be tested: Identify the L1 point location qualitatively and explain its significance for space missions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Gravitational PE system — U_total = -G Σ m_i m_j/r_{ij} summed over all pairs
  • Tidal force — Differential gravity across a body; stretches objects toward/away from source
  • Roche limit — Distance within which tidal forces overcome self-gravity of a satellite
  • Lagrange point — Location where third body orbits with same period as binary system
  • Tidal locking — Rotation period equals orbital period; Moon is tidally locked to Earth
Objectives
  • Calculate total gravitational PE for a system of three masses.
  • Explain tidal forces and why they scale as 1/r³.
  • Identify Lagrange points qualitatively and their significance.
How this can be tested
  • Free-response: find total energy of a three-body system.
  • Conceptual: explain why the Moon shows only one face to Earth.
  • Calculation: estimate tidal force on an astronaut near a black hole.
Practice before moving on
  • Calculate binding energy of Earth-Moon-Sun system.
  • Research how tidal forces affect geological processes on Io and Earth.

  • D) Each star orbits at r = d from CM; gravity provides centripetal force

Q10
Explain physically why satellites closer to Earth orbit faster but have less total energy.
  • A) PE = -GMm/r is more negative (lower) at smaller r
