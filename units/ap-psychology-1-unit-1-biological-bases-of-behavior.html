Neurons and Neural Communication

Learn on this site
Neuron Structure and Function

Neurons consist of dendrites (receive signals), cell body (integrates signals), axon (transmits signals), and myelin sheath (speeds transmission); the action potential is an all-or-nothing electrochemical signal traveling down the axon.

Common misconception: Neurons do not touch—the synapse is a gap; signals cross chemically via neurotransmitters, not electrically.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests neuron parts and their functions; FRQ asks you to trace a neural signal from stimulus to response.

Neurotransmitters

Key neurotransmitters include dopamine (reward/movement), serotonin (mood/sleep), acetylcholine (muscle movement/memory), GABA (inhibition), glutamate (excitation), and norepinephrine (arousal/attention).

Common misconception: More neurotransmitter does not always mean more activation—reuptake, receptor sensitivity, and inhibition all modulate effects.

How it can be tested: MCQ frequently matches neurotransmitters to behaviors or disorders; FRQ asks you to explain drug effects via NT systems.

The Synapse and Drug Effects

Agonist drugs mimic or enhance neurotransmitter effects; antagonist drugs block receptors; reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) increase NT availability by preventing reabsorption.

Common misconception: Agonists don't just add more NT—they can act on receptors even without the NT present, which is why their effects differ from natural NT activity.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to explain how a specific drug affects behavior by naming the NT system and mechanism.

Key Vocabulary
  • Neuron
  • Action potential
  • Synapse
  • Neurotransmitter
  • Reuptake
Objectives
  • Trace the path of a neural signal from dendrite to postsynaptic neuron.
  • Match neurotransmitters to associated behaviors and disorders.
  • Explain how agonist and antagonist drugs alter neural communication.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests neuron structures and NT functions.
  • FRQ traces neural signals or explains drug mechanisms.
  • Scenario questions ask you to apply NT knowledge to real behaviors.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw a synapse and label the parts involved in NT release and reuptake.
  • Create a table: NT, function, associated disorder, and one drug that affects it.

Brain Structures and Functions

Learn on this site
Hindbrain and Midbrain

The brainstem (medulla, pons, reticular formation) controls vital functions like breathing and heart rate; the cerebellum coordinates movement and balance; together these structures maintain basic survival functions.

Common misconception: The cerebellum coordinates movement but does not initiate it—the motor cortex initiates, the cerebellum fine-tunes.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to match brain damage locations to behavioral deficits.

Limbic System

The limbic system (hippocampus, amygdala, hypothalamus) regulates emotion and memory; the hippocampus forms new explicit memories; the amygdala processes fear and emotional responses; the hypothalamus regulates hunger, thirst, and body temperature.

Common misconception: The hippocampus stores memories in formation but is not where long-term memories are permanently held—they are distributed across the cortex.

How it can be tested: FRQ frequently uses patient case studies (like H.M.) to ask about memory systems.

Cerebral Cortex and Lobes

The cerebral cortex has four lobes: frontal (executive function, motor control, Broca's area), parietal (somatosensory processing), temporal (auditory processing, Wernicke's area), and occipital (visual processing).

Common misconception: Broca's area (speech production) and Wernicke's area (speech comprehension) are both in the left hemisphere of most people—damaging either causes aphasia but of different types.

How it can be tested: MCQ and FRQ use lesion studies and brain imaging to test lobe functions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Hippocampus
  • Amygdala
  • Frontal lobe
  • Broca's area
  • Corpus callosum
Objectives
  • Match brain structures to their primary functions.
  • Explain what behavioral deficits result from damage to specific brain areas.
  • Distinguish Broca's and Wernicke's aphasia using brain structure knowledge.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ matches brain damage to behavioral deficits.
  • FRQ uses case studies (H.M., split-brain patients) to test structure knowledge.
  • Imaging studies shown in MCQ to identify active brain regions.
Practice before moving on
  • Draw a brain diagram labeling all four lobes and key limbic structures.
  • Research H.M. and explain what his case revealed about hippocampal function.

The Nervous System and Endocrine System

Learn on this site
Central and Peripheral Nervous Systems

The central nervous system (brain and spinal cord) processes information; the peripheral nervous system includes the somatic (voluntary movement) and autonomic (automatic functions) divisions; the autonomic has sympathetic (fight-or-flight) and parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) branches.

Common misconception: The sympathetic system does not just activate during genuine danger—it activates in response to any perceived threat, including social anxiety or exciting news.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to classify nervous system components; FRQ may ask you to trace a stress response through the system.

Endocrine System and Hormones

The endocrine system communicates via hormones released into the bloodstream; key glands include the pituitary (master gland), adrenal glands (cortisol/adrenaline for stress), thyroid (metabolism), and gonads (sex hormones).

Common misconception: Hormones are slower than neural signals but longer-lasting—they affect widespread body systems rather than specific target neurons.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests hormone-gland-function associations; FRQ connects endocrine function to behavior.

Genetics and Behavior

Behavioral genetics studies how genes and environment interact; twin studies (identical vs. fraternal) and adoption studies separate genetic from environmental influences; heritability estimates describe how much variation in a trait is due to genetic differences in a population.

Common misconception: Heritability does not mean a trait is fixed or cannot be changed by environment—heritability describes population variance, not individual destiny.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to evaluate twin and adoption study designs as methods for studying genetic influences.

Key Vocabulary
  • Sympathetic nervous system
  • Parasympathetic nervous system
  • Cortisol
  • Heritability
  • Twin study
Objectives
  • Distinguish the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems by function.
  • Match endocrine glands to their hormones and behavioral effects.
  • Evaluate what twin and adoption studies reveal about genetic influences on behavior.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ classifies nervous system divisions.
  • FRQ traces stress response through nervous and endocrine systems.
  • Heritability questions appear in genetics and intelligence units.
Practice before moving on
  • Create a table of endocrine glands, their hormones, and behavioral effects.
  • Explain a twin study design and why identical twins are compared to fraternal twins.

Sleep, Consciousness, and Psychoactive Drugs

Learn on this site
Sleep Stages and Dreams

Sleep cycles through NREM stages (1-3, progressively deeper) and REM sleep; REM is associated with dreaming, memory consolidation, and brain activity similar to waking; sleep deprivation impairs attention, memory, and immune function.

Common misconception: Dreams occur primarily in REM sleep but also in NREM—the idea that all dreams happen in REM is an oversimplification.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests sleep stage characteristics; FRQ may ask you to explain the function of REM sleep.

Consciousness and Altered States

Consciousness ranges from focused alertness to sleep; hypnosis creates a state of heightened suggestibility but does not give the hypnotist control over the subject; meditation reduces arousal and may alter neural activity patterns.

Common misconception: Hypnosis does not create genuine amnesia or access repressed memories—research shows it increases suggestibility and the production of false memories.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests consciousness spectrum and hypnosis effects.

Psychoactive Drugs

Psychoactive drugs are classified as depressants (alcohol, barbiturates—slow CNS), stimulants (cocaine, amphetamines—speed CNS), hallucinogens (LSD, marijuana—distort perception), and opiates (heroin, morphine—reduce pain via endorphin receptors).

Common misconception: Marijuana is sometimes classified separately—it has hallucinogenic, depressant, and stimulant properties and does not fit neatly into one category.

How it can be tested: MCQ and FRQ ask you to classify drugs, explain their NT mechanisms, and describe tolerance and withdrawal.

Key Vocabulary
  • REM sleep
  • NREM sleep
  • Psychoactive drug
  • Tolerance
  • Depressant
Objectives
  • Describe sleep stages and explain REM's function.
  • Evaluate hypnosis as an altered state of consciousness.
  • Classify psychoactive drugs and explain their neural mechanisms.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests sleep stage characteristics and drug classification.
  • FRQ explains drug effects via NT mechanisms.
  • Scenario questions apply drug categories to real-world examples.
Practice before moving on
  • Create a chart: drug class, example, NT affected, behavioral effect, and withdrawal symptom.
  • Research one sleep disorder (insomnia, narcolepsy, sleep apnea) and its treatment.
