Prenatal Development and Infancy

Learn on this site
Prenatal Development

Prenatal development occurs in three stages: germinal (fertilization to 2 weeks), embryonic (2-8 weeks, organ formation), and fetal (8 weeks to birth, growth and refinement); teratogens (alcohol, drugs, toxins) are most harmful during the embryonic period when organs are forming.

Common misconception: Fetal alcohol syndrome is the most common preventable cause of intellectual disability—the embryonic period is most critical because organs are forming, not just growing.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests prenatal stages and teratogen effects.

Infant Sensory and Motor Development

Newborns have reflexes (rooting, grasping, sucking) that aid survival; motor development follows a cephalocaudal (head-to-tail) and proximodistal (center-outward) pattern; habituation (decreased response to repeated stimuli) shows infants can distinguish stimuli.

Common misconception: Motor milestones have a sequence but not a fixed schedule—culture and environment influence the timing of walking and other motor skills.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests infant reflexes and habituation as a research method.

Attachment Theory

Harlow's experiments with infant monkeys showed that contact comfort (not feeding) drives attachment; Ainsworth's Strange Situation identified secure, anxious-ambivalent, and avoidant attachment styles; early attachment patterns predict later social and emotional outcomes.

Common misconception: Secure attachment does not require a single primary caregiver—multiple consistent caregivers can support secure attachment.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to apply attachment research to explain adult relationship patterns.

Key Vocabulary
  • Teratogen
  • Rooting reflex
  • Habituation
  • Contact comfort
  • Secure attachment
Objectives
  • Identify teratogens and explain why the embryonic period is most critical.
  • Explain Harlow's findings and what they reveal about the basis of attachment.
  • Distinguish secure, avoidant, and anxious-ambivalent attachment styles.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests teratogen timing and infant reflexes.
  • FRQ applies attachment theory to real-world parenting scenarios.
  • Scenario questions test Ainsworth attachment types.
Practice before moving on
  • Research Harlow's monkey experiments and explain the ethical controversy.
  • Design a Strange Situation scenario and explain what each infant response reveals.

Cognitive Development: Piaget and Vygotsky

Learn on this site
Piaget's Stages of Cognitive Development

Piaget proposed four stages: sensorimotor (0-2, object permanence develops), preoperational (2-7, symbolic thought but egocentrism and lack of conservation), concrete operational (7-11, conservation and logical thinking about concrete objects), and formal operational (12+, abstract reasoning).

Common misconception: Piaget underestimated children's abilities—research shows object permanence develops earlier than he claimed, and formal operational thinking is not universal in adulthood.

How it can be tested: FRQ gives a child behavior and asks you to identify the Piagetian stage and justify with evidence.

Schema, Assimilation, and Accommodation

Schemas are cognitive frameworks for organizing knowledge; assimilation fits new information into existing schemas; accommodation modifies schemas to fit new information that doesn't fit; cognitive development involves constant interplay between assimilation and accommodation.

Common misconception: Schemas are not rigid—they are dynamic and constantly updated through experience; the terms assimilation and accommodation are frequently confused on the exam.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests assimilation vs. accommodation; FRQ applies these concepts to real learning scenarios.

Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development

Vygotsky emphasized social and cultural influences on cognitive development; the Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) is the gap between what a learner can do independently and what they can achieve with guidance; scaffolding provides temporary support that is gradually removed as competence grows.

Common misconception: Vygotsky's ZPD is not the same as simply teaching above a child's level—it requires the learner to be close to mastery, needing only guided support.

How it can be tested: MCQ and FRQ apply ZPD to educational scenarios.

Key Vocabulary
  • Sensorimotor stage
  • Preoperational stage
  • Conservation
  • Zone of Proximal Development
  • Scaffolding
Objectives
  • Identify Piaget's four stages and match behaviors to each stage.
  • Distinguish assimilation from accommodation using specific examples.
  • Apply Vygotsky's ZPD and scaffolding to an educational scenario.
How this can be tested
  • FRQ identifies Piagetian stage from behavior description.
  • MCQ tests assimilation vs. accommodation.
  • Scenario questions apply ZPD to teaching contexts.
Practice before moving on
  • Test conservation (liquid, number) with a child and record observations.
  • Compare Piaget's stage theory to Vygotsky's sociocultural theory on one key dimension.

Social Development: Erikson and Adolescence

Learn on this site
Erikson's Psychosocial Stages

Erikson proposed 8 stages of psychosocial development, each involving a crisis to resolve: trust vs. mistrust (infancy), autonomy vs. shame (toddler), initiative vs. guilt (preschool), industry vs. inferiority (school age), identity vs. role confusion (adolescence), and later adult stages.

Common misconception: Erikson's stages are not strictly sequential in the sense that failing one stage doesn't permanently prevent progress—people can revisit and resolve earlier crises later.

How it can be tested: MCQ matches Erikson's stages to age groups; FRQ may ask you to apply a stage to a case study.

Adolescent Development

Adolescence features puberty (physical maturation), formal operational thinking (abstract reasoning), and identity formation; Marcia extended Erikson's identity stage into four statuses: diffusion, foreclosure, moratorium, and achievement.

Common misconception: Identity formation is not complete in adolescence—it continues into adulthood; identity achievement requires both exploration and commitment.

How it can be tested: SAQ asks you to apply identity status theory to adolescent case studies.

Parenting Styles and Social Development

Baumrind identified three parenting styles: authoritative (high warmth + high control, best outcomes), authoritarian (low warmth + high control), and permissive (high warmth + low control); authoritative parenting is consistently linked to positive outcomes across many cultures.

Common misconception: Parenting style effectiveness is not universal—in some cultural contexts, authoritarian parenting is associated with better outcomes than Western research predicts.

How it can be tested: MCQ matches parenting style descriptions to outcomes.

Key Vocabulary
  • Erikson's stages
  • Identity vs. role confusion
  • Identity achievement
  • Authoritative parenting
  • Puberty
Objectives
  • Match Erikson's psychosocial stages to age groups and their core conflicts.
  • Apply Marcia's identity statuses to adolescent case studies.
  • Evaluate parenting styles and their associated developmental outcomes.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ matches Erikson stages to age/conflict.
  • SAQ applies Marcia's identity statuses.
  • MCQ matches parenting style to outcome.
Practice before moving on
  • Identify which of Erikson's stages you believe you are in and justify with evidence.
  • Research the cultural limitations of Baumrind's parenting style research.

Adulthood and Aging

Learn on this site
Physical and Cognitive Changes in Adulthood

Physical development peaks in early adulthood; middle adulthood brings menopause, sensory decline, and slowed processing; older adulthood shows fluid intelligence decline (processing speed) but crystallized intelligence (accumulated knowledge) is maintained or increases.

Common misconception: Cognitive decline in aging is not universal or inevitable—social engagement, exercise, and mental stimulation can slow age-related cognitive changes significantly.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests fluid vs. crystallized intelligence in aging.

Death, Dying, and Grief

Kübler-Ross proposed five stages of grief: denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance; research shows these stages are not universal or sequential—grief is highly individual and nonlinear.

Common misconception: Kübler-Ross's stages describe grief reactions but are not prescriptive—not everyone goes through all stages or in order; the model is descriptive, not normative.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests Kübler-Ross stages; FRQ may apply them to a case study with a critical evaluation.

Cross-Sectional vs. Longitudinal Research

Cross-sectional research compares different age groups at one time (faster but confounded by cohort effects); longitudinal research follows the same group over time (more valid but costly and subject to attrition); cross-sequential designs combine both approaches.

Common misconception: Cross-sectional studies showing older adults perform worse than younger adults may reflect cohort differences (different education, nutrition) rather than aging effects.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests research design appropriateness for developmental questions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Fluid intelligence
  • Crystallized intelligence
  • Kübler-Ross stages
  • Cross-sectional research
  • Longitudinal research
Objectives
  • Distinguish fluid and crystallized intelligence and explain aging effects on each.
  • Evaluate Kübler-Ross's grief stages as a model.
  • Compare cross-sectional and longitudinal research designs for developmental questions.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests fluid vs. crystallized intelligence.
  • FRQ evaluates Kübler-Ross stages critically.
  • MCQ tests research design strengths and weaknesses.
Practice before moving on
  • Research one study comparing cross-sectional and longitudinal findings on the same developmental question.
  • Apply the fluid/crystallized distinction to explain why older adults often outperform younger adults on certain tasks.
