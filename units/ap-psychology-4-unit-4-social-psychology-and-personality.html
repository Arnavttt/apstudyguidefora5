Social Influence: Conformity and Obedience

Learn on this site
Asch's Conformity Experiments

Asch's line experiments showed that 75% of participants conformed to incorrect group answers at least once; conformity was driven by normative social influence (wanting to belong) and informational social influence (assuming the group has correct information).

Common misconception: Asch's conformity was not permanent—when participants had even one ally who gave the correct answer, conformity dropped dramatically.

How it can be tested: FRQ describes an Asch-like scenario and asks you to explain conformity using social influence concepts.

Milgram's Obedience Study

Milgram's shock experiments showed 65% of participants delivered maximum (450V) shocks when ordered by an authority figure; obedience was influenced by authority legitimacy, proximity of victim, and presence of other disobedient peers.

Common misconception: Milgram's participants were not sadists or unusually obedient—they were ordinary people; the study demonstrated the power of situational forces over character.

How it can be tested: FRQ and MCQ apply Milgram's findings to explain real-world obedience to authority.

Social Facilitation and Group Dynamics

Social facilitation: the presence of others improves performance on easy/well-practiced tasks but impairs performance on difficult tasks (Zajonc); social loafing: people exert less effort in groups; deindividuation: anonymity in groups reduces personal accountability.

Common misconception: Social facilitation does not always help—it depends on task difficulty; this nuance is frequently tested in MCQ.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests social facilitation vs. loafing vs. deindividuation in scenario questions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Normative social influence
  • Informational social influence
  • Obedience
  • Social facilitation
  • Deindividuation
Objectives
  • Distinguish normative from informational social influence.
  • Apply Milgram's findings to explain factors that increase or decrease obedience.
  • Distinguish social facilitation, social loafing, and deindividuation.
How this can be tested
  • FRQ applies conformity and obedience to new scenarios.
  • MCQ tests social influence concepts.
  • Scenario questions require classifying social influence type.
Practice before moving on
  • Research the ethical controversies of Milgram's study and how it changed research ethics.
  • Design a modification of Asch's study that would reduce conformity and explain why.

Attitudes, Attribution, and Prejudice

Learn on this site
Attribution Theory

Attribution theory (Heider, Kelley) explains how we assign causes to behavior; the fundamental attribution error (FAE) is overestimating dispositional (personality) causes and underestimating situational causes for others' behavior; the actor-observer bias shows we make the opposite error for our own behavior.

Common misconception: The FAE is not universal—collectivist cultures show weaker FAE because they are more attuned to situational context.

How it can be tested: MCQ and FRQ identify FAE and actor-observer bias in scenario descriptions.

Attitudes and Cognitive Dissonance

Attitudes have cognitive, affective, and behavioral components; cognitive dissonance (Festinger) is the discomfort from holding conflicting beliefs or acting against one's beliefs; people reduce dissonance by changing attitudes, changing behavior, or rationalizing.

Common misconception: Dissonance reduction typically changes attitudes to match behavior, not the reverse—we often justify past actions rather than changing our behavior.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to apply cognitive dissonance to a scenario where behavior and attitude conflict.

Prejudice and Discrimination

Prejudice is a negative attitude toward a group; discrimination is behavior against a group; stereotypes are cognitive schemas about groups; in-group/out-group bias leads people to favor their own group; the just-world hypothesis leads people to blame victims for their misfortune.

Common misconception: Prejudice does not require conscious hostility—implicit biases operate automatically outside awareness and influence behavior without conscious intent.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to explain persistence of prejudice using social psychology concepts.

Key Vocabulary
  • Fundamental attribution error
  • Cognitive dissonance
  • Prejudice
  • In-group bias
  • Just-world hypothesis
Objectives
  • Identify fundamental attribution error and actor-observer bias in scenarios.
  • Apply cognitive dissonance theory to explain attitude change.
  • Explain how stereotypes and biases contribute to prejudice and discrimination.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ identifies FAE and actor-observer bias.
  • FRQ applies cognitive dissonance.
  • FRQ explains prejudice persistence.
Practice before moving on
  • Identify a recent personal example of cognitive dissonance and explain how you resolved it.
  • Research implicit bias tests (IAT) and evaluate what they measure.

Personality Theories

Learn on this site
Psychoanalytic and Neo-Freudian Theories

Freud proposed personality arises from conflict among id (instinctual drives), ego (rational mediator), and superego (moral standards); defense mechanisms (repression, rationalization, projection) protect the ego from anxiety; neo-Freudians (Adler, Jung, Horney) emphasized social factors over sexuality.

Common misconception: The scientific evidence for specific Freudian concepts (repression, unconscious conflict) is weak—Freud's clinical insights were influential but many specific claims are not empirically supported.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests defense mechanisms; FRQ may ask you to evaluate Freudian theory's strengths and limitations.

Humanistic and Social-Cognitive Theories

Maslow's hierarchy of needs proposes five levels from physiological to self-actualization; Rogers emphasized the self-concept and unconditional positive regard for healthy development; Bandura's social-cognitive theory highlights reciprocal determinism—person, behavior, and environment mutually influence each other.

Common misconception: Maslow's hierarchy is widely cited but poorly supported by cross-cultural research—the sequence is not universal across all cultures.

How it can be tested: MCQ and FRQ apply humanistic and social-cognitive concepts to scenarios.

Trait Theories and the Big Five

Trait theories describe personality in terms of stable dispositions; the Big Five (OCEAN: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism) is the most empirically supported trait model; traits are relatively stable across time but can change gradually.

Common misconception: Traits predict behavior on average across situations but not in every specific situation—the person-situation debate (Mischel) challenged the consistency of traits.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests Big Five dimensions; FRQ may evaluate trait theory as a personality explanation.

Key Vocabulary
  • Id, ego, superego
  • Defense mechanisms
  • Self-actualization
  • Reciprocal determinism
  • Big Five
Objectives
  • Identify Freudian defense mechanisms in scenario descriptions.
  • Apply Maslow's hierarchy and Rogers's self-concept to real-world scenarios.
  • Describe the Big Five personality traits and evaluate trait theory.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests defense mechanisms.
  • FRQ applies humanistic theory to scenarios.
  • MCQ tests Big Five dimensions.
Practice before moving on
  • Take a Big Five personality quiz and reflect on the accuracy of your results.
  • Identify two limitations of Freudian theory from a scientific perspective.

Social Cognition and Groups

Learn on this site
Group Decision Making

Groupthink occurs when group cohesion overrides realistic evaluation of alternatives; groupthink is characterized by illusion of invulnerability, collective rationalization, and self-censorship; group polarization shows groups make more extreme decisions than individuals after discussion.

Common misconception: Groupthink does not require a powerful leader to force conformity—it arises from the group's own dynamics of cohesion and desire for consensus.

How it can be tested: FRQ describes a group decision-making scenario and asks you to identify groupthink symptoms.

Prosocial Behavior and the Bystander Effect

The bystander effect shows that people are less likely to help in emergencies when others are present; diffusion of responsibility (assuming someone else will help) and pluralistic ignorance (interpreting ambiguous situations as non-emergencies because others seem calm) drive this effect.

Common misconception: The bystander effect is not simply selfishness—it results from genuine cognitive processes about responsibility and ambiguity that operate across all people.

How it can be tested: MCQ applies bystander effect to emergency scenarios.

Aggression and Altruism

Aggression has biological (testosterone, amygdala), social learning (Bandura), and situational (frustration-aggression hypothesis, heat, alcohol) causes; altruism is helping others even at cost to oneself; kin selection and reciprocal altruism offer evolutionary explanations for helping behavior.

Common misconception: The frustration-aggression hypothesis has been revised—frustration increases the likelihood of aggression but is not sufficient; other factors moderate the relationship.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests causes of aggression and explanations for altruistic behavior.

Key Vocabulary
  • Groupthink
  • Bystander effect
  • Diffusion of responsibility
  • Altruism
  • Frustration-aggression hypothesis
Objectives
  • Identify groupthink symptoms in group decision scenarios.
  • Apply bystander effect explanations (diffusion, pluralistic ignorance) to emergencies.
  • Evaluate biological, social, and situational explanations for aggression.
How this can be tested
  • FRQ identifies groupthink from scenario.
  • MCQ applies bystander effect.
  • MCQ evaluates aggression explanations.
Practice before moving on
  • Research the Kitty Genovese case and explain how it illustrates the bystander effect.
  • Design a strategy a leader could use to prevent groupthink in a decision-making team.
