Chesapeake Colonies: Virginia and Maryland

Learn on this site
Jamestown and Early Virginia

Jamestown (1607) was England's first permanent settlement; early colonists focused on finding gold rather than farming and faced "starving time" before tobacco cultivation under John Rolfe transformed Virginia into a profitable colony.

Common misconception: Jamestown survived not because of good planning but despite terrible planning—disease, starvation, and conflict with the Powhatan Confederacy nearly destroyed it multiple times.

How it can be tested: SAQ asks you to analyze how tobacco shaped Virginia's social and economic development.

Headright System and Indentured Servitude

Virginia's headright system granted 50 acres of land for each person whose passage a planter paid; indentured servants worked 4–7 years in exchange for passage, then received freedom dues (land, tools, food).

Common misconception: Indentured servants were not slaves—they were temporarily unfree but legally persons with rights; their labor shortage drove the eventual shift to African slavery.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates the transition from indentured servitude to chattel slavery.

Bacon's Rebellion (1676)

Bacon's Rebellion was a 1676 uprising of backcountry farmers and former indentured servants against Virginia's elite; it revealed class tensions and led planters to shift toward African slave labor as a more controllable workforce.

Common misconception: Bacon's Rebellion was not a democratic revolution—it targeted both the colonial elite AND Native Americans; its significance lies in its aftermath: the shift to racial slavery.

How it can be tested: DBQ and LEQ frequently use Bacon's Rebellion as evidence of colonial social tensions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Headright system
  • Indentured servitude
  • Tobacco economy
  • Bacon's Rebellion
  • Powhatan Confederacy
Objectives
  • Explain how tobacco shaped Chesapeake colonial development.
  • Describe the headright and indentured servitude systems.
  • Analyze how Bacon's Rebellion accelerated the shift to chattel slavery.
How this can be tested
  • SAQ analyzes tobacco's role in Virginia's development.
  • LEQ evaluates labor system transitions.
  • DBQ uses Bacon's Rebellion as a causation source.
Practice before moving on
  • Trace how Virginia went from starvation colony to profitable tobacco economy.
  • Explain what a former indentured servant received as freedom dues and why it created problems.

New England Colonies

Learn on this site
Puritan Migration and Massachusetts Bay

Puritan settlers founded Massachusetts Bay Colony (1630) under John Winthrop seeking to create a "city upon a hill"—a godly model community; the colony was a theocracy where church membership influenced political participation.

Common misconception: Puritans came to New England for religious freedom for themselves, not religious tolerance in general—they banished dissenters like Roger Williams and Anne Hutchinson.

How it can be tested: SAQ asks you to compare New England and Chesapeake colonial motivations and social structures.

Roger Williams, Anne Hutchinson, and Dissent

Roger Williams was banished from Massachusetts for advocating separation of church and state and fair purchase of Native land; he founded Rhode Island as a haven for religious dissenters; Anne Hutchinson was banished for antinomianism—claiming direct communication with God.

Common misconception: The banishment of dissenters shows that Massachusetts Bay was not pluralistic—it enforced a specific Puritan orthodoxy.

How it can be tested: DBQ and SAQ use Williams and Hutchinson as evidence of Puritan intolerance.

Puritan Economics and King Philip's War

New England's economy centered on small farms, fishing, timber, and trade rather than plantation agriculture; King Philip's War (1675–76) was the bloodiest per-capita conflict in American history, with Metacom (King Philip) leading a Native coalition against English expansion.

Common misconception: King Philip's War ended effective Native resistance in New England and opened land for English settlement, not just limited it.

How it can be tested: LEQ and SAQ evaluate consequences of King Philip's War for New England.

Key Vocabulary
  • Puritans
  • City upon a hill
  • Antinomianism
  • King Philip's War
  • Metacom
Objectives
  • Explain Puritan religious goals and how they shaped Massachusetts Bay governance.
  • Analyze the cases of Williams and Hutchinson as evidence of Puritan intolerance.
  • Evaluate the consequences of King Philip's War for New England's development.
How this can be tested
  • SAQ compares New England and Chesapeake motivations.
  • DBQ uses dissenter sources to assess Puritan religious freedom.
  • LEQ evaluates King Philip's War consequences.
Practice before moving on
  • Read Winthrop's "City upon a Hill" speech and identify key Puritan values.
  • Compare King Philip's War and Bacon's Rebellion as examples of colonial conflict.

Middle Colonies and Southern Colonies

Learn on this site
Middle Colonies: Diversity and Commerce

The Middle Colonies (New York, Pennsylvania, New Jersey, Delaware) were the most ethnically and religiously diverse region, featuring Dutch, German, Scots-Irish, and English settlers; Pennsylvania under William Penn offered religious tolerance and attracted Quakers and other dissenters.

Common misconception: The Middle Colonies' diversity was not always harmonious—ethnic and religious tensions existed, but the region lacked the rigid social hierarchies of New England and the South.

How it can be tested: SAQ and MCQ compare the three colonial regions on economy, religion, and social structure.

Southern Colonies: Rice, Indigo, and Slavery

South Carolina and Georgia developed plantation economies based on rice and indigo, using enslaved African labor with specialized agricultural knowledge; the Stono Rebellion (1739) was the largest slave revolt in colonial America.

Common misconception: Rice cultivation in South Carolina depended on enslaved Africans' specific agricultural expertise from West Africa—planters deliberately selected enslaved people from rice-growing regions.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates how the plantation economy shaped Southern colonial society.

Development of Chattel Slavery

Chattel slavery—the legal ownership of enslaved persons as property—was gradually codified in colonial law through slave codes; Virginia's 1705 slave code established that the status of enslaved Africans followed the mother (partus sequitur ventrem), making slavery hereditary.

Common misconception: Slavery became racial and hereditary through specific legal decisions—it was not an inevitable or natural development but a constructed legal system.

How it can be tested: DBQ and LEQ use slave codes and related documents to analyze the construction of racial slavery.

Key Vocabulary
  • Chattel slavery
  • Slave codes
  • Stono Rebellion
  • Partus sequitur ventrem
  • Quakers
Objectives
  • Compare the three colonial regions in economy, religion, and social structure.
  • Explain how and why chattel slavery became hereditary and racial through colonial law.
  • Analyze the Stono Rebellion as evidence of enslaved resistance.
How this can be tested
  • Three-region comparison is a classic SAQ and LEQ topic.
  • DBQ uses slave codes to analyze construction of racial slavery.
  • MCQ tests specific colonial regional characteristics.
Practice before moving on
  • Create a three-column comparison chart of New England, Middle, and Southern colonies.
  • Research the 1705 Virginia slave code and identify three key provisions.

Colonial Society, Culture, and Governance

Learn on this site
Salutary Neglect and Colonial Self-Governance

Britain's policy of salutary neglect allowed colonial assemblies to govern with minimal British interference, creating traditions of self-government; colonial legislatures like Virginia's House of Burgesses gained power over taxation and local affairs.

Common misconception: Salutary neglect was not an intentional policy—it resulted from British preoccupation with European affairs; when it ended after 1763, colonists saw British assertion of authority as tyranny.

How it can be tested: LEQ and SAQ connect salutary neglect to growing colonial expectations of self-governance.

Great Awakening and Colonial Identity

The Great Awakening (1730s–40s) was a religious revival led by preachers like George Whitefield and Jonathan Edwards that emphasized personal conversion, challenged church hierarchy, and created a shared inter-colonial cultural experience.

Common misconception: The Great Awakening had political consequences beyond religion—its anti-hierarchical emphasis and shared inter-colonial experience contributed to a colonial identity separate from Britain.

How it can be tested: DBQ and SAQ analyze the Great Awakening as evidence of growing colonial unity.

Enlightenment and Colonial Intellectual Life

The Enlightenment emphasized reason, natural rights, and the social contract; colonial thinkers like Benjamin Franklin applied Enlightenment ideas to practical problems; Enlightenment thought later justified revolution against Britain.

Common misconception: Enlightenment ideas about natural rights were selectively applied in colonial America—they coexisted with slavery and exclusion of women without contradiction in most colonists' minds.

How it can be tested: LEQ connects Enlightenment ideology to the ideological foundations of the American Revolution.

Key Vocabulary
  • Salutary neglect
  • House of Burgesses
  • Great Awakening
  • Enlightenment
  • Natural rights
Objectives
  • Explain how salutary neglect developed traditions of colonial self-governance.
  • Analyze the Great Awakening's role in creating inter-colonial identity.
  • Connect Enlightenment ideas to later revolutionary ideology.
How this can be tested
  • SAQ connects salutary neglect to colonial expectations.
  • DBQ uses Great Awakening sermons and responses.
  • LEQ evaluates Enlightenment ideas' influence on Revolution.
Practice before moving on
  • Compare a Great Awakening sermon to an Enlightenment text and identify key differences.
  • List three powers the Virginia House of Burgesses held by 1750.
