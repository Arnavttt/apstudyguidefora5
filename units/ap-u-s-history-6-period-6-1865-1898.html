Industrialization and the Gilded Age

Learn on this site
Rise of Big Business

Gilded Age industrialists (Carnegie, Rockefeller, Morgan) used vertical integration, horizontal integration, and trusts to dominate industries; Social Darwinism and the Gospel of Wealth justified wealth concentration as natural and beneficial.

Common misconception: Robber barons vs. captains of industry is a genuine historical debate—these men built transformative industries while engaging in exploitative practices; the APUSH exam expects you to evaluate both sides.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates whether Gilded Age industrialists helped or harmed American society.

Labor and the Worker

Industrialization created dangerous working conditions, 12-hour days, and child labor; labor organizations (Knights of Labor, AFL under Gompers) sought better conditions; the Homestead Strike (1892) and Pullman Strike (1894) showed the limits of labor power against business and government.

Common misconception: The federal government consistently sided with business over labor in this era—using courts, injunctions, and federal troops to break strikes.

How it can be tested: SAQ asks you to evaluate labor movement strategies and their effectiveness.

Social Darwinism and Reform Responses

Social Darwinism (Herbert Spencer) applied natural selection to human society, justifying inequality as natural; the Social Gospel movement (Jane Addams, settlement houses) countered with Christian obligation to address poverty; the Mugwumps sought political reform against corruption.

Common misconception: Social Darwinism was not Darwin's idea—Spencer applied evolutionary concepts to justify the status quo; Darwin himself did not endorse this application.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses Social Darwinist and reformer documents to analyze competing responses to industrialization.

Key Vocabulary
  • Vertical integration
  • Trust
  • Social Darwinism
  • Gospel of Wealth
  • Knights of Labor
Objectives
  • Compare Social Darwinism and the Social Gospel as responses to industrialization.
  • Evaluate labor organizations' strategies and their effectiveness.
  • Analyze how big business practices concentrated economic power.
How this can be tested
  • LEQ evaluates Gilded Age industrialists as robber barons vs. captains of industry.
  • SAQ evaluates labor movement strategies.
  • DBQ uses competing reform documents.
Practice before moving on
  • Compare Carnegie's "Gospel of Wealth" to Jane Addams's Hull House philosophy.
  • Research the Homestead Strike and identify what it revealed about labor-business-government relations.

Immigration, Urbanization, and the New South

Learn on this site
New Immigration and Nativism

The "New Immigration" (1880s–1920s) brought millions from Southern and Eastern Europe (Italians, Poles, Jews) and Asia; nativists responded with the Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) and calls for literacy tests; immigrants settled in ethnic urban enclaves.

Common misconception: The distinction between "old" (Northern/Western European) and "new" (Southern/Eastern European) immigration was a nativist construction used to justify restrictions—both groups faced discrimination.

How it can be tested: SAQ and DBQ use immigration documents and restriction debates to analyze nativist responses.

Urbanization and Political Machines

Rapid urbanization created overcrowded tenements, inadequate sanitation, and political machines (Tammany Hall) that traded services for votes; Jacob Riis's How the Other Half Lives (1890) exposed urban poverty through photography and journalism.

Common misconception: Political machines were corrupt but provided real services (jobs, housing assistance) to immigrant communities that government otherwise ignored—they were simultaneously exploitative and functional.

How it can be tested: MCQ and SAQ ask you to evaluate political machines' role in immigrant communities.

The New South and Jim Crow

New South advocates (Henry Grady) promoted industrial development while maintaining white supremacy; Jim Crow laws codified racial segregation after Reconstruction; Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) upheld "separate but equal," giving constitutional cover to segregation.

Common misconception: The "New South" was economically still primarily agrarian and poor—industrialization lagged far behind the North despite boosters' claims.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates the contrast between New South ideology and the reality of Black life under Jim Crow.

Key Vocabulary
  • New Immigration
  • Chinese Exclusion Act
  • Political machines
  • Jim Crow
  • Plessy v. Ferguson
Objectives
  • Analyze nativist responses to New Immigration using specific policies as evidence.
  • Evaluate political machines as simultaneously exploitative and functional.
  • Explain how Jim Crow and Plessy v. Ferguson constructed legal racial segregation.
How this can be tested
  • SAQ evaluates political machines' role.
  • DBQ uses immigration restriction and nativist documents.
  • LEQ evaluates New South ideology vs. Jim Crow reality.
Practice before moving on
  • Research Jacob Riis's photos and identify three specific conditions they documented.
  • Compare Booker T. Washington's and W.E.B. Du Bois's responses to Jim Crow.

Farmers, Populism, and the West

Learn on this site
Western Settlement and Native Americans

The Homestead Act (1862) promoted westward settlement; railroad expansion enabled agricultural commercialization; the destruction of the bison and the reservation system devastated Native peoples; Wounded Knee (1890) marked the end of organized Native resistance.

Common misconception: The reservation system was designed to assimilate Native peoples, not preserve their cultures—the Dawes Act (1887) broke up tribal land to "civilize" individual Native families.

How it can be tested: SAQ and LEQ evaluate the impact of western expansion on Native peoples.

Farmers' Problems and the Grange Movement

Commercial farmers faced falling crop prices, rising railroad freight costs, credit problems, and deflation; the Grange movement organized farmers for political action; the Farmers' Alliance and cooperatives sought collective solutions to market problems.

Common misconception: Farmers' economic problems were structural (global overproduction, railroad monopolies, credit scarcity), not due to individual failure—Populists correctly identified systemic causes.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses Populist documents to analyze agrarian reform arguments.

Populist Party and Election of 1896

The People's (Populist) Party (1892) demanded a graduated income tax, government ownership of railroads, direct election of senators, and currency inflation (free silver); William Jennings Bryan's "Cross of Gold" speech united silver Democrats and Populists in 1896, but Bryan lost to McKinley.

Common misconception: Bryan's 1896 defeat marked the end of significant agrarian political challenge to industrial capitalism—the alliance of eastern workers and western farmers never materialized.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates Populism's significance and why it ultimately failed.

Key Vocabulary
  • Dawes Act
  • Homestead Act
  • Populist Party
  • Free silver
  • Cross of Gold speech
Objectives
  • Analyze the impact of western expansion on Native peoples using the Dawes Act.
  • Explain farmers' structural economic problems and their organizational responses.
  • Evaluate Populism's platform and explain why it failed in 1896.
How this can be tested
  • SAQ evaluates Native American policy.
  • DBQ uses Populist documents.
  • LEQ evaluates Populism's significance.
Practice before moving on
  • Research Bryan's "Cross of Gold" speech and identify its main economic argument.
  • Compare the Dawes Act's stated goals with its actual effects on Native communities.

American Imperialism in the 1890s

Learn on this site
Causes of American Imperialism

American imperialism in the 1890s was driven by: economic interests (new markets for surplus goods), strategic concerns (naval bases, coaling stations), Social Darwinism and the "White Man's Burden," and the Turner Thesis (frontier closure requiring new outlets for expansion).

Common misconception: Imperialism was not simply economic—racial ideology (belief in Anglo-Saxon superiority) and strategic anxiety about global competition were equally important motivations.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates the relative importance of different causes of American imperialism.

Spanish-American War and Its Consequences

The Spanish-American War (1898) began with the explosion of the USS Maine and yellow journalism stoking public opinion; the U.S. quickly defeated Spain, acquiring Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines for $20 million; the Philippines acquisition triggered a brutal guerrilla war.

Common misconception: The Philippines was not a passive acquisition—Filipino independence fighters (Emilio Aguinaldo) had fought Spain and expected independence, then fought the U.S. in the Philippine-American War (1899–1902) that killed hundreds of thousands.

How it can be tested: DBQ and SAQ use anti-imperialist and imperialist documents to analyze the debate over Philippine annexation.

Anti-Imperialism and Debate

The Anti-Imperialist League (Mark Twain, Andrew Carnegie) argued that imperialism violated American democratic principles, the Constitution didn't follow the flag, and that governing subject peoples without consent contradicted the Declaration of Independence.

Common misconception: Anti-imperialists were not necessarily allies of colonial peoples—some opposed imperialism for racist reasons (not wanting to incorporate nonwhite peoples as citizens), while others opposed it on democratic principle.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses anti-imperialist and imperialist writings to analyze competing visions of American identity and purpose.

Key Vocabulary
  • Yellow journalism
  • Spanish-American War
  • Philippine-American War
  • Anti-Imperialist League
  • Turner Thesis
Objectives
  • Identify and evaluate multiple causes of American imperialism in the 1890s.
  • Analyze the Spanish-American War's origins and consequences, including the Philippine-American War.
  • Compare imperialist and anti-imperialist arguments about Philippine annexation.
How this can be tested
  • LEQ evaluates imperialism causes.
  • DBQ uses imperialist vs. anti-imperialist documents.
  • SAQ analyzes Spanish-American War consequences.
Practice before moving on
  • Read an excerpt from Alfred Thayer Mahan's The Influence of Sea Power on History.
  • Research the Philippine-American War's scale and compare it to the Spanish-American War.
