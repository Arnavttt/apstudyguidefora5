The Progressive Era

Learn on this site
Progressive Reform Goals

Progressivism (1890s–1920s) sought to address industrial society's problems: regulating big business (Sherman Antitrust Act enforcement), protecting workers (labor laws), expanding democracy (direct election of senators, initiative/referendum), and social welfare (settlement houses, prohibition).

Common misconception: Progressivism was not a unified movement—it included contradictions: championing democracy while supporting racist disfranchisement; promoting social welfare while imposing middle-class Protestant values.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates Progressive achievements and limitations.

Muckrakers and Reform

Muckraking journalists exposed corruption and abuses: Ida Tarbell investigated Standard Oil, Upton Sinclair's The Jungle exposed meatpacking conditions, Lincoln Steffens documented municipal corruption; their work built public support for reform legislation.

Common misconception: Sinclair's The Jungle was intended to build sympathy for workers but instead sparked outrage about contaminated food—he said he aimed for the public's heart but hit its stomach.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses muckraker documents to analyze Progressive reform motivations.

Progressive Amendments and Limits

Constitutional amendments reflected Progressive goals: 16th (income tax), 17th (direct Senate election), 18th (Prohibition), 19th (women's suffrage); however, Progressivism largely excluded Black Americans—Theodore Roosevelt dis-invited Booker T. Washington from the White House after one dinner.

Common misconception: The Progressive movement's racial exclusion was not incidental—many Progressives supported racial segregation as "scientific" and "progressive" based on eugenics.

How it can be tested: SAQ evaluates which groups were included in and excluded from Progressive reforms.

Key Vocabulary

  • Sherman Antitrust Act
  • 16th Amendment
  • 19th Amendment
  • Eugenics

World War I and the 1920s

Learn on this site
U.S. Entry into WWI and Home Front

The U.S. entered WWI in 1917 after German unrestricted submarine warfare and the Zimmermann Telegram; the war transformed the home front: the Espionage Act (1917) and Sedition Act (1918) suppressed dissent; the Great Migration accelerated as Black Americans moved north for factory jobs.

Common misconception: WWI civil liberties suppression (prosecution of Eugene Debs, Schenck v. U.S.) set dangerous precedents; the Supreme Court ruled "clear and present danger" could limit free speech.

How it can be tested: SAQ evaluates civil liberties trade-offs during wartime.

Wilson's 14 Points and the Treaty of Versailles

Wilson's 14 Points proposed a peace based on self-determination and a League of Nations; the Treaty of Versailles (1919) imposed harsh terms on Germany; the Senate rejected the treaty (Article X controversy); the U.S. returned to "normalcy" and isolation.

Common misconception: The Senate did not reject the treaty because of isolationism alone—Henry Cabot Lodge had specific constitutional objections to Article X's collective security commitment.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates Wilson's idealism vs. European power politics in shaping the treaty.

1920s: Prosperity, Culture, and Tension

The 1920s brought mass consumer culture (automobiles, radio, movies), the Harlem Renaissance, and women's new social roles (flappers); but also the Red Scare, Ku Klux Klan revival, immigration restriction (National Origins Act 1924), and Prohibition.

Common misconception: The 1920s were not uniformly prosperous—farmers remained economically depressed throughout the decade, and rural/urban cultural conflict was intense.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses 1920s cultural documents to analyze tensions between tradition and modernity.

Key Vocabulary
  • Zimmermann Telegram
  • Great Migration
  • Wilson's 14 Points
  • League of Nations
  • Harlem Renaissance
Objectives
  • Explain U.S. entry into WWI and analyze home front civil liberties suppression.
  • Evaluate Wilson's 14 Points and why the Senate rejected the Treaty of Versailles.
  • Analyze 1920s cultural tensions between modernity and tradition.
How this can be tested
  • SAQ evaluates WWI civil liberties.
  • LEQ evaluates Wilson's 14 Points and treaty.
  • DBQ uses 1920s cultural documents.

Great Depression and the New Deal

Learn on this site
Causes of the Great Depression

The Great Depression (1929–39) resulted from multiple causes: overspeculation in the stock market, weak banking system, overproduction in agriculture and industry, high tariffs (Smoot-Hawley), and the Federal Reserve's contractionary monetary policy.

Common misconception: The stock market crash (October 1929) did not cause the Depression—it was a symptom of deeper structural weaknesses; the Depression resulted from policy failures as much as market failures.

How it can be tested: SAQ asks you to evaluate multiple causes of the Great Depression.

Hoover's Response and FDR's New Deal

Hoover believed in "voluntarism"—private charity rather than federal relief—and refused direct aid; FDR's New Deal (1933–39) used federal power for relief (FERA), recovery (NRA, AAA), and reform (FDIC, SEC, Social Security); the Second New Deal (1935) was more liberal.

Common misconception: The New Deal did not end the Depression—WWII did; but it permanently expanded the federal government's role in the economy and created the modern social safety net.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates whether the New Deal was a radical departure or a conservative preservation of capitalism.

New Deal's Limits and Critics

The New Deal excluded many Black Americans (AAA displaced sharecroppers, Social Security excluded domestic workers), faced criticism from the right (Liberty League) and left (Huey Long's Share Our Wealth), and FDR's court-packing plan (1937) damaged his coalition.

Common misconception: The New Deal's racial exclusions were largely deliberate bargains with Southern Democratic congressmen whose support FDR needed—racial equality was traded for economic reform.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses New Deal documents and critic writings to analyze its successes and failures.

Key Vocabulary

World War II

Learn on this site
U.S. Entry and Military Mobilization

Japan's attack on Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941) brought the U.S. into WWII; industrial mobilization transformed the economy, achieving full employment and massive production; the war required sacrifice (rationing, war bonds) but ended the Depression.

Common misconception: The U.S. did not enter WWII purely for moral reasons—strategic interests (controlling the Pacific, preserving European allies) drove policy; moral condemnation of fascism and the Holocaust came later and incompletely.

How it can be tested: SAQ evaluates how WWII mobilization transformed the American economy and society.

Home Front: Women, Minorities, and Civil Liberties

WWII opened new opportunities for women ("Rosie the Riveter") and Black Americans (Double V campaign—victory against fascism abroad and racism at home); but Japanese American internment (Executive Order 9066) and Korematsu v. U.S. showed civil liberties violations.

Common misconception: Women's wartime work in factories was largely temporary—employers expected women to return home after the war; real structural change in women's workforce participation came later.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates WWII's contradictory impact on American civil liberties and minority rights.

Allied Strategy and Atomic Bombs

Allied strategy prioritized Europe first (D-Day, June 6, 1944) while fighting Japan in the Pacific (island-hopping); the U.S. dropped atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki (August 1945), ending the Pacific war; the bombs' use remains debated.

Common misconception: Truman's decision to use atomic bombs is genuinely debated—defenders cite invasion casualty projections, critics note Japan was near surrender and the bombs killed hundreds of thousands of civilians.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses documents about the atomic bomb decision to analyze competing historical interpretations.
