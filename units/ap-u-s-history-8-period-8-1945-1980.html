Cold War Origins and Containment

Learn on this site
Truman Doctrine and Marshall Plan

The Truman Doctrine (1947) committed U.S. support to any nation threatened by communist expansion; the Marshall Plan ($13 billion to rebuild Western Europe) prevented economic desperation that communists might exploit; both reflected the containment strategy.

Common misconception: Containment was not a military-first strategy—Kennan originally envisioned political and economic containment; the militarization of containment (NSC-68) was a later development.

How it can be tested: SAQ evaluates the Truman Doctrine and Marshall Plan as complementary containment strategies.

Korean War and McCarthyism

The Korean War (1950–53) tested containment in Asia with 36,000 American deaths; McCarthyism (Senator Joseph McCarthy's anti-communist crusade) created a climate of fear and destroyed careers through unsubstantiated accusations; the Red Scare suppressed dissent.

Common misconception: McCarthy did not start the Red Scare—it began before him with HUAC and loyalty oaths; McCarthy exploited an existing climate of fear and was eventually discredited by Edward R. Murrow's journalism.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates the domestic consequences of Cold War anti-communism.

Nuclear Deterrence and Korea-to-Vietnam

Massive retaliation and mutual assured destruction (MAD) created Cold War stability through terror; the Vietnam War (1955–75) showed containment's limits—the U.S. committed 500,000 troops and lost to North Vietnamese forces, revealing overextension of containment logic.

Common misconception: Vietnam was not primarily a military failure—U.S. forces won most military engagements; it was a political failure because the South Vietnamese government lacked legitimacy and popular support.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses Vietnam War documents to analyze containment's evolution and limits.

Key Vocabulary

  • Truman Doctrine
  • Marshall Plan
  • McCarthyism
  • Mutual assured destruction
Objectives

  • Evaluate the domestic consequences of Cold War anti-communism through McCarthyism.

Postwar Prosperity and Social Change

Learn on this site
Baby Boom and Suburban Growth

The postwar baby boom and GI Bill (education, mortgages) drove suburban growth; Levittown-style suburbs provided affordable homes for white middle-class families; the highway system (Interstate Highway Act, 1956) enabled suburban commuting but reinforced racial segregation.

Common misconception: Suburban growth was racially exclusive—FHA loan policies and restrictive covenants denied mortgages to Black families, creating white suburbs and Black urban neighborhoods.

How it can be tested: SAQ asks you to analyze how postwar prosperity was unevenly distributed.

Conformity and Counter-Culture Seeds

The 1950s featured cultural conformity pressure (corporate culture, suburban domesticity) but also seeds of dissent: Beat Generation (Kerouac, Ginsberg), early rock 'n' roll, and youth culture challenging conformity; the decade's consensus masked deep tensions.

Common misconception: The 1950s were not uniformly conformist—the decade saw enormous cultural ferment beneath the surface that erupted in the 1960s.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates the 1950s as an era of conformity vs. emerging dissent.

Eisenhower and the Military-Industrial Complex

Eisenhower's Farewell Address (1961) warned against the "military-industrial complex"—the dangerous alliance between defense contractors and the military that could distort national priorities; his warning reflected his own experience managing Cold War defense spending.

Common misconception: Eisenhower was not an anti-military isolationist—he expanded the nuclear arsenal significantly; his warning was about institutional capture, not military spending per se.

How it can be tested: DBQ may use Eisenhower's Farewell Address to analyze Cold War domestic effects.

Key Vocabulary
  • GI Bill
  • Levittown
  • Baby boom
  • Military-industrial complex
  • Interstate Highway Act
Objectives
  • Analyze how postwar prosperity was racially uneven.
  • Evaluate 1950s conformity and emerging cultural dissent.
  • Explain Eisenhower's warning about the military-industrial complex.

Civil Rights Movement

Learn on this site
Legal Strategies: Brown and the Courts

The NAACP's legal strategy culminated in Brown v. Board of Education (1954), which overturned Plessy v. Ferguson and declared school segregation unconstitutional; Little Rock (1957) tested implementation when Eisenhower sent federal troops to enforce integration.

Common misconception: Brown did not immediately integrate American schools—massive resistance, white flight to private schools, and de facto segregation through housing patterns meant integration was slow and incomplete.

How it can be tested: SAQ evaluates Brown v. Board's significance and limitations.

Direct Action: Montgomery to Birmingham

The Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–56), sit-ins, Freedom Rides, and Birmingham demonstrations used nonviolent direct action to expose southern segregation's brutality; televised images of police violence built northern white support for federal legislation.

Common misconception: Martin Luther King Jr.'s nonviolent strategy was deliberately provocative—it was designed to force a violent response that would expose segregation's reality to outside observers.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses Civil Rights Movement primary sources to analyze strategy and ideology.

Civil Rights Act, Voting Rights Act, and Beyond

The Civil Rights Act (1964) banned racial discrimination in public accommodations and employment; the Voting Rights Act (1965) dismantled barriers to Black voting; but King's Chicago Campaign (1966) showed de facto northern segregation was harder to address than southern legal segregation.

Common misconception: The Civil Rights Movement had a northern problem—de facto segregation through housing, schools, and employment discrimination could not be addressed by simple legal prohibition.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates the Civil Rights Movement's achievements and unfinished agenda.

Key Vocabulary
  • Brown v. Board
  • Montgomery Bus Boycott
  • Civil Rights Act of 1964
  • Voting Rights Act
  • Nonviolent direct action
Objectives

  • Analyze why northern de facto segregation proved more resistant to civil rights strategies.
How this can be tested

Great Society, Vietnam, and Social Upheaval

Learn on this site
LBJ's Great Society

Lyndon Johnson's Great Society programs (1964–68) created Medicare, Medicaid, federal education funding (ESEA), the Voting Rights Act, and immigration reform (Hart-Celler Act); the War on Poverty used community action programs; this was the most ambitious domestic legislation since the New Deal.

Common misconception: The Great Society was undermined by Vietnam War spending that created inflation and diverted political capital—LBJ's foreign policy destroyed his domestic legacy.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates the Great Society's achievements and limitations.

Vietnam War's Domestic Impact

The Vietnam War created a "credibility gap" between government statements and reality; the antiwar movement grew from campus teach-ins to massive demonstrations; the My Lai massacre (1968) and Pentagon Papers (1971) confirmed the war's moral and strategic failures.

Common misconception: The antiwar movement was not uniformly counter-cultural—it included veterans (Vietnam Veterans Against the War), mainstream liberals, and eventually the majority of the American public.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses antiwar movement and pro-war documents to analyze Vietnam's domestic divisions.

Social Movements of the Late 1960s-70s

The late 1960s–70s saw second-wave feminism (NOW, Ms. magazine, Title IX), the Chicano Movement (César Chávez, United Farm Workers), American Indian Movement (AIM), and the gay rights movement (Stonewall 1969); these movements challenged traditional hierarchies and expanded rights claims.

Common misconception: These movements were partly inspired by the Civil Rights Movement but also criticized its limitations—many questioned why their groups had been left out of mainstream civil rights gains.

How it can be tested: SAQ analyzes how late 1960s–70s social movements built on and extended civil rights traditions.

Key Vocabulary

  • Medicare/Medicaid
  • Antiwar movement
  • Second-wave feminism
  • Title IX
