Reagan Revolution and Conservative Ascendancy

Learn on this site
Rise of the New Right

The New Right coalition (Reagan 1980) united economic conservatives (tax cuts, deregulation), social conservatives (evangelical Christians opposing abortion and gay rights), and Cold Warriors; Reagan's "Morning in America" optimism contrasted with Carter's malaise.

Common misconception: Reagan's coalition was not simply a white backlash to civil rights—it included genuine ideological commitments to free markets, limited government, and Cold War anti-communism.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates the causes and significance of the conservative ascendancy.

Reaganomics

Reagan's economic program (supply-side economics/"trickle-down") cut top income tax rates from 70% to 28%, reduced social spending, increased military spending, and deregulated industries; the economy recovered after a 1982 recession but inequality grew significantly.

Common misconception: Supply-side economics promised that tax cuts for the wealthy would "trickle down" to all Americans—critics note that while GDP grew, income inequality increased significantly during and after Reagan's presidency.

How it can be tested: SAQ evaluates Reaganomics using economic evidence.

Social Conservatism and Culture Wars

The Moral Majority (Jerry Falwell) organized evangelical Christians politically around opposition to abortion, feminism, and gay rights; the culture wars produced intense social conflict over values, identity, and the role of religion in public life.

Common misconception: The culture wars were not simply about social issues—they reflected deeper conflicts over what America was and whose values would define it.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses culture war documents to analyze competing visions of American identity.

End of the Cold War and Post-Cold War America

Learn on this site
Reagan's Cold War Policy and Soviet Collapse

Reagan's massive military buildup and Reagan Doctrine (arming anti-communist guerrillas) pressured the Soviet Union; Gorbachev's reforms (glasnost, perestroika) unleashed forces that collapsed the USSR (1991); the Cold War ended not through military confrontation but Soviet internal collapse.

Common misconception: Reagan's role in ending the Cold War is debated—internal Soviet contradictions and Gorbachev's reforms were arguably more important than U.S. military pressure.

How it can be tested: SAQ evaluates Reagan's role in the Cold War's end.

Gulf War and Post-Cold War Foreign Policy

The Gulf War (1991) established a "new world order" of multilateral action under Bush; Clinton navigated a post-Cold War world with humanitarian interventions (Bosnia, Kosovo) but no clear strategic framework; the U.S. emerged as the sole superpower without a clear new foreign policy doctrine.

Common misconception: The Gulf War's success created false confidence in U.S. military superiority—the "Powell Doctrine" of overwhelming force with clear objectives was not always followed in later conflicts.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates the challenges of U.S. foreign policy after the Cold War ended.

September 11 and the War on Terror

The September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks reshaped U.S. foreign policy; the USA PATRIOT Act expanded surveillance; the invasions of Afghanistan (2001) and Iraq (2003) began costly wars; the Iraq War's justification (WMD) proved false, reviving Vietnam-era credibility gaps.

Common misconception: The Iraq War was more consequential than 9/11 itself—it destabilized the Middle East, produced ISIS, and damaged U.S. credibility in ways that still shape global politics.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses post-9/11 policy documents to analyze foreign policy decisions.

Key Vocabulary
  • Reagan Doctrine
  • Soviet collapse
  • Gulf War
  • September 11
  • PATRIOT Act
Objectives
  • Evaluate Reagan's role in ending the Cold War using evidence.
  • Analyze post-Cold War foreign policy challenges under Bush and Clinton.
  • Assess the consequences of post-9/11 foreign policy decisions.
How this can be tested
  • SAQ evaluates Reagan's Cold War role.
  • LEQ evaluates post-Cold War foreign policy.
  • DBQ uses post-9/11 documents.
Practice before moving on

  • Compare the Gulf War's multilateral approach to the Iraq War's unilateral approach.

Economic Globalization and Domestic Inequality

Learn on this site
NAFTA and Globalization

NAFTA (1994) created a free trade zone among the U.S., Canada, and Mexico; globalization accelerated deindustrialization as manufacturing moved to low-wage countries; technology (internet, automation) disrupted labor markets; the benefits of globalization were unevenly distributed.

Common misconception: Globalization's benefits (cheaper goods, corporate profits) were concentrated, while its costs (job loss, wage stagnation) were spread across specific communities and workers.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates globalization's economic and social consequences.

Inequality and the Great Recession

Income inequality grew from the 1970s through the 2000s as wages stagnated for most workers while incomes soared for the top 1%; the 2008 Great Recession (caused by housing bubble and financial deregulation) produced the worst economic crisis since the Depression.

Common misconception: The Great Recession's causes included deregulation decisions made by both parties over decades—blaming a single party or president misses the long-term structural causes.

How it can be tested: SAQ evaluates the causes and consequences of the 2008 financial crisis.

Technological Revolution and Social Change

The internet, smartphones, and social media transformed communication, commerce, and politics; the "knowledge economy" rewarded education while displacing routine jobs; globalization and technology together restructured American labor markets in ways that increased inequality.

Common misconception: Technology is not politically neutral—it has amplified existing inequalities while creating new opportunities; access to technology is itself unequally distributed.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses economic and technological change documents to analyze inequality's causes.

Key Vocabulary

21st Century Challenges and American Identity

Learn on this site
Obama and Political Polarization

Barack Obama's election (2008) as the first African American president reflected civil rights progress; his presidency saw passage of the Affordable Care Act but intense partisan polarization; the Tea Party movement reflected backlash to both Obama's policies and demographic change.

Common misconception: Obama's presidency demonstrated both how far America had come on race and how far it had to go—his election did not usher in "post-racial America" as some claimed.

How it can be tested: SAQ evaluates what Obama's election and presidency reveal about race in contemporary America.

Immigration and Demographic Change

The Hart-Celler Act (1965) transformed immigration sources from Europe to Latin America and Asia; the U.S. became increasingly diverse; debates over undocumented immigration, DACA, and border policy reflected anxiety about demographic and cultural change.

Common misconception: Immigration debates conflate economic, cultural, and legal issues—separating them reveals that most economic evidence shows immigration benefits the economy, while cultural anxieties drive much of the political opposition.

How it can be tested: LEQ evaluates how immigration has shaped American identity debates.

21st Century Social Movements

Black Lives Matter (2013) emerged to protest police violence; #MeToo (2017) addressed sexual harassment and assault; environmental justice movements connected climate change to racial and economic inequality; these movements used social media for unprecedented organizing.

Common misconception: Social media amplified these movements' reach but also created backlash communities—technology's role in social movements is complex and cuts both ways.

How it can be tested: DBQ uses 21st century social movement documents to analyze continuity and change in American reform traditions.

Key Vocabulary

  • Tea Party
  • DACA

  • #MeToo
