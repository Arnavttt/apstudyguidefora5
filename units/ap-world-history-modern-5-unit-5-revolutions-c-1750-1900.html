Revolutions, c. 1750–1900

The Enlightenment and Atlantic Revolutions

Learn on this site
Enlightenment Ideas

The Enlightenment (c.1650–1800) applied reason to human affairs, challenging the divine right of kings and asserting natural rights (Locke), separation of powers (Montesquieu), and popular sovereignty (Rousseau). These ideas provided the intellectual foundation for the Atlantic revolutions.

Common misconception: The Enlightenment did not immediately abolish hierarchy — most Enlightenment thinkers excluded women, the enslaved, and the poor from their vision of rational governance.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests specific Enlightenment thinkers and their ideas; SAQ asks you to trace Enlightenment influence on specific revolutions.

The American and French Revolutions

The American Revolution (1776) created a republic based on Enlightenment principles of natural rights and representation, though it preserved slavery. The French Revolution (1789) was more radical, abolishing aristocracy, executing the king, and inspiring nationalist movements worldwide.

Common misconception: The American Revolution was more conservative than the French — it preserved the social order for white property-owning men while the French Revolution challenged social hierarchy more broadly.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to compare the American and French Revolutions using specific Enlightenment concepts.

The Haitian Revolution

The Haitian Revolution (1791–1804) was the only successful slave revolution in history, establishing the first Black republic (Haiti) after defeating French, British, and Spanish armies. It was inspired by the French Revolution's rhetoric of liberty, which French colonizers refused to extend to enslaved people.

Common misconception: The Haitian Revolution terrified slaveholding societies worldwide — it was deliberately suppressed in historical memory by the United States and European powers because it challenged the legitimacy of slavery.

How it can be tested: SAQ asks you to explain the Haitian Revolution's relationship to the French Revolution's ideals.

Key Vocabulary
  • Enlightenment — Intellectual movement applying reason to human affairs; foundation for political revolutions
  • natural rights — John Locke's concept of inherent rights (life, liberty, property) that governments cannot legitimately take
  • popular sovereignty — Principle that legitimate political authority derives from the consent of the governed
  • Haitian Revolution — Slave revolution (1791–1804) that created the independent republic of Haiti
  • nationalism — Political ideology asserting that people sharing culture, language, and history should form sovereign states
Objectives
  • Explain how Enlightenment ideas challenged the divine right of kings.
  • Compare the American, French, and Haitian Revolutions' applications of Enlightenment principles.
  • Analyze why the Haitian Revolution had a limited immediate global impact.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests Enlightenment thinkers and their key ideas.
  • SAQ asks you to trace Enlightenment influence on one Atlantic revolution.
  • FRQ asks you to compare two Atlantic revolutions using Enlightenment concepts.
Practice before moving on
  • Chart three Enlightenment thinkers: idea, which revolution it influenced, and how.
  • Explain why the Haitian Revolution challenged slaveholder societies more than the American Revolution did.

The Industrial Revolution

Learn on this site
Origins in Britain

Britain industrialized first due to: abundant coal and iron deposits, a colonial empire supplying raw materials and markets, a banking system providing investment capital, enclosure movements pushing peasants into factory labor, and relative political stability. The steam engine (Watt, 1769) was the key enabling technology.

Common misconception: Industrialization required not just technology but a specific social and economic context — without enclosure, urbanization, and capital markets, the steam engine alone would not have industrialized Britain.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests why Britain industrialized first; SAQ asks you to explain which factor was most important.

Social Consequences of Industrialization

Industrialization created an urban working class (proletariat) dependent on wages, living in overcrowded industrial cities with poor sanitation. Child labor, 14-hour workdays, and factory accidents were common. A new middle class (bourgeoisie) of factory owners and professionals also emerged.

Common misconception: Industrial conditions were genuinely terrible by modern standards — students should not normalize 19th-century labor conditions by applying the standards of a later era that created worker protections.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to analyze the social consequences of industrialization using specific examples.

Ideological Responses

Socialism emerged as a critique of industrial capitalism: Robert Owen proposed cooperative communities; Karl Marx argued that capitalism's contradictions would lead to proletarian revolution. Labor unions formed to collectively bargain for better conditions. Liberals defended free market capitalism as the engine of progress.

Common misconception: Marx predicted revolution in the most industrialized countries — it actually occurred in agrarian Russia, not Britain or Germany, which he did not predict. AP® World asks you to evaluate this discrepancy.

How it can be tested: SAQ asks you to compare socialist and liberal responses to industrial capitalism.

Key Vocabulary
  • Industrial Revolution — Transition to machine-based manufacturing, beginning in Britain c.1750–1850
  • steam engine — James Watt's improved engine (1769) using steam power to drive machinery and locomotives
  • proletariat — Marx's term for the urban industrial working class dependent on wages
  • bourgeoisie — Marx's term for the capitalist class owning the means of production
  • socialism — Ideology advocating collective or state ownership of production to reduce inequality
Objectives
  • Explain three reasons why Britain industrialized before other countries.
  • Describe the social consequences of industrialization for the working class.
  • Compare socialist and liberal ideological responses to industrial capitalism.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests causes of British industrialization.
  • SAQ asks you to explain the social impact of industrialization using specific examples.
  • FRQ asks you to evaluate ideological responses to capitalism.
Practice before moving on
  • List the four main causes of British industrialization and rank them by importance.
  • Compare working-class conditions in an early industrial city to a pre-industrial rural village.

Nationalism and State Building

Learn on this site
European Nationalism

Nationalism — the belief that people sharing language, culture, and history should form sovereign states — drove the unification of Germany (1871) and Italy (1870) and challenged multinational empires like Austria-Hungary and the Ottoman Empire. It emerged from Enlightenment ideas about popular sovereignty.

Common misconception: Nationalism created new states but also enabled new forms of exclusion — ethnic and racial nationalism defined "the nation" in ways that excluded minorities and justified aggression against neighbors.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests German unification and its consequences; FRQ asks you to evaluate nationalism's impact on 19th-century European states.

Meiji Japan and Non-European Industrialization

Japan's Meiji Restoration (1868) launched a state-directed program of industrialization, creating a Western-style army, navy, constitution, and education system. Japan deliberately adopted Western technologies and institutions to avoid being colonized like China.

Common misconception: Meiji Japan is the clearest example of deliberate, state-directed industrialization — but it was not unique; Germany also used the state to accelerate industrialization rather than relying on market forces alone.

How it can be tested: SAQ asks you to explain how Japan industrialized without Western control.

Latin American Independence

Between 1808 and 1825, most of Spain's American colonies achieved independence, inspired by Enlightenment ideas and the disruption caused by Napoleon's invasion of Spain. Leaders like Simón Bolívar and José de San Martín led military campaigns. However, independence did not end the hierarchical social order.

Common misconception: Latin American independence changed who governed but not how — creole elites replaced Spanish-born rulers while indigenous people and the enslaved remained at the bottom of the social hierarchy.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to evaluate the extent to which Latin American independence was a genuine social revolution.

Key Vocabulary
  • nationalism — Ideology asserting people sharing culture and history should form their own sovereign states
  • Meiji Restoration — Japanese state-directed modernization (1868) adopting Western technology to prevent colonization
  • creole — Person of European descent born in the Americas; led most Latin American independence movements
  • Otto von Bismarck — Prussian chancellor who unified Germany through "blood and iron" diplomacy and war
  • Simón Bolívar — South American independence leader known as "the Liberator" of Venezuela, Colombia, and Peru
Objectives
  • Explain how nationalism contributed to German unification.
  • Analyze how the Meiji Restoration enabled Japan to industrialize while avoiding colonization.
  • Evaluate the extent to which Latin American independence movements created genuine social change.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests German unification and Meiji Restoration.
  • SAQ asks you to explain Latin American independence movements.
  • FRQ asks you to compare nationalism's effects across two different regions.
Practice before moving on
  • Compare Japan's Meiji Restoration with China's response to Western pressure — what accounts for the different outcomes?
  • Explain why Latin American independence led by creole elites did not eliminate social inequality.

The New Imperialism

Learn on this site
Causes of New Imperialism

European industrial powers expanded rapidly into Africa and Asia after 1870, driven by: industrial capitalism's demand for raw materials and markets, Social Darwinist ideology justifying European racial superiority, nationalist competition among European powers, and new military technologies (Maxim gun, steamships, quinine for malaria). The Berlin Conference (1884–85) partitioned Africa.

Common misconception: The "civilizing mission" was ideology, not benevolent policy — it justified extraction and control while denying African and Asian agency.

How it can be tested: MCQ tests causes of imperialism; FRQ asks you to evaluate which cause was most important.

Methods of Colonial Control

European powers used direct rule (France in West Africa — replacing local institutions), indirect rule (Britain in Nigeria — governing through local chiefs), sphere of influence (China — no formal annexation but foreign control of trade and law), and settler colonialism (South Africa, Algeria). Each method reflected different colonial goals.

Common misconception: Not all imperialism was the same — direct rule eliminated local governance; indirect rule preserved it while extracting resources. AP® World asks you to compare these approaches.

How it can be tested: SAQ asks you to compare direct and indirect colonial rule with specific examples.

Resistance to Imperialism

Colonized peoples resisted through armed revolt (Zulu resistance, Ethiopian victory at Adwa 1896, Boxer Rebellion in China), passive resistance, religious movements, and preservation of cultural practices. Ethiopia and Japan were the only two non-Western states that successfully resisted European imperialism.

Common misconception: Anti-colonial resistance was widespread but mostly unsuccessful due to the military gap created by industrialization — not because colonized peoples accepted European rule.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to evaluate why most anti-colonial resistance failed in this period.

Key Vocabulary
  • New Imperialism — Wave of European territorial expansion into Africa and Asia after 1870
  • Berlin Conference — Meeting (1884–85) at which European powers divided Africa among themselves
  • Social Darwinism — Pseudo-scientific ideology applying natural selection to justify European racial superiority and empire
  • direct rule — Colonial policy replacing local institutions with European administrators
  • indirect rule — Colonial policy governing through existing local rulers as intermediaries
Objectives
  • Identify economic, ideological, and political causes of the New Imperialism.
  • Compare direct and indirect colonial rule using specific examples.
  • Explain why most anti-colonial resistance failed in the late 19th century.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests causes and methods of imperialism.
  • SAQ asks you to compare direct and indirect rule.
  • FRQ asks you to evaluate the causes of European imperialism or forms of resistance.
Practice before moving on
  • Map Africa before and after the Berlin Conference and note which country claimed which territory.
  • Compare Ethiopia's successful resistance at Adwa with the Zulu resistance to Britain — what accounts for the different outcomes?
