Global Conflict, c. 1900–Present

Causes and Course of World War I

Learn on this site
MAIN Causes of WWI

Historians use the MAIN acronym — Militarism, Alliance systems, Imperialism, and Nationalism — to explain WWI's underlying causes. The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand in Sarajevo (1914) was the immediate trigger that activated the alliance system, pulling the great powers into war.

Common misconception: The alliance system is a cause of WWI's scale and speed, not its underlying cause — it explains how a regional assassination became a world war, not why European powers were so tense.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to identify MAIN causes from scenarios; FRQ asks you to evaluate which cause was most significant.

Trench Warfare and Total War

WWI introduced industrial-scale warfare: machine guns, poison gas, tanks, and aerial bombing transformed combat. Trench warfare on the Western Front created a bloody stalemate. Total war mobilized entire economies and societies — women entered the workforce, civilians were targeted, and propaganda shaped public opinion.

Common misconception: WWI was not primarily a war of cavalry charges — the industrial technologies that caused such high casualties are what distinguished it from earlier conflicts and define "modern war."

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to explain how industrial technology transformed the nature of warfare in WWI.

Consequences of WWI: The Paris Peace Settlement

The Treaty of Versailles (1919) imposed harsh terms on Germany: the War Guilt Clause, reparations, territorial losses, and military restrictions. New nation-states were created from collapsed empires (Austria-Hungary, Ottoman Empire), but self-determination was applied inconsistently — colonial peoples were denied independence.

Common misconception: The Treaty of Versailles did not cause WWII by itself — Hitler exploited German grievances, but many interwar factors (Great Depression, weak Weimar Republic, global fascism) also contributed.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about Versailles terms; FRQ evaluates whether Versailles made WWII inevitable.

Rise of Totalitarianism and World War II

Learn on this site
Rise of Fascism and Nazism

The Great Depression, Versailles resentment, fear of communism, and weak democratic institutions created conditions in which fascist movements seized power. Mussolini's Italy (1922) and Hitler's Nazi Germany (1933) used mass propaganda, scapegoating of minorities, and paramilitary violence to consolidate authoritarian rule.

Common misconception: Fascism was not a single unified ideology — Italian Fascism and German Nazism shared authoritarian and nationalist characteristics but differed significantly in racial ideology, with Nazism being far more explicitly genocidal.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to identify conditions enabling fascist rise; FRQ asks you to explain how economic crisis contributed to authoritarian regimes.

Causes of World War II

WWII resulted from multiple converging factors: the unresolved grievances of WWI, the Great Depression destabilizing democracies, the failure of collective security (appeasement at Munich 1938), and Hitler's aggressive expansionism. Japan's imperial expansion in Asia and attack on Pearl Harbor (1941) made the war truly global.

Common misconception: Appeasement was not simply cowardice — British and French leaders feared another devastating war and believed Hitler's demands were limited; they misjudged his intentions.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to evaluate the relative causes of WWII and compare the European and Pacific theaters.

The Pacific Theater and Decolonization Pressures

Japan's expansion in Asia — Manchuria (1931), China (1937), Southeast Asia (1941) — was driven by resource scarcity and nationalist ideology. Japan's initial defeats of European colonial powers (Britain, France, Netherlands) demonstrated that Western colonizers were not invincible, accelerating post-war decolonization movements.

Common misconception: Japan did not present itself as a liberator in conquered territories — its brutal occupation of China and Southeast Asia contradicted its "Asia for Asians" rhetoric of the Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about Japan's expansion motivations; FRQ asks you to analyze how WWII undermined European colonialism.

Key Vocabulary
  • fascism
  • totalitarianism
  • appeasement
  • Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere
  • Blitzkrieg
Objectives
  • Explain economic and political conditions that enabled the rise of fascism.
  • Evaluate multiple causes of WWII and compare them to WWI causes.
  • Analyze how Japan's expansion in Asia affected European colonialism.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ identifies causes of WWII and characteristics of totalitarian regimes.
  • FRQ compares causes of WWI and WWII or evaluates appeasement.
  • SAQ analyzes a primary source from Hitler or Mussolini.
Practice before moving on

  • Explain how Japan's victories over Britain and France in Southeast Asia (1941–42) affected colonial subjects.

The Holocaust and Genocide

Learn on this site
Origins and Implementation of the Holocaust

The Holocaust was the systematic, state-sponsored murder of six million Jews by Nazi Germany and its collaborators, along with millions of Roma, disabled people, Soviet POWs, and political prisoners. Implementation proceeded in stages: legal discrimination (Nuremberg Laws 1935), forced emigration, ghettoization, and the Final Solution's death camps (Auschwitz, Treblinka) after 1941.

Common misconception: The Holocaust was not a spontaneous outburst of violence — it was methodically planned and executed by a modern bureaucratic state using industrial technology, which is what makes it distinct in the history of genocide.

How it can be tested: MCQ and FRQ ask you to explain how the Holocaust escalated in stages and what conditions made it possible.

Collaboration, Complicity, and Resistance

The Holocaust required collaboration: Nazi-aligned governments (Vichy France, Croatia), local police forces, railway workers, and ordinary citizens participated in or enabled the genocide. Jewish resistance occurred in ghettos (Warsaw Ghetto Uprising 1943) and partisan groups despite overwhelming odds.

Common misconception: The Warsaw Ghetto Uprising was a military failure but a significant act of defiance — students should not evaluate resistance solely by whether it succeeded militarily.

How it can be tested: FRQ may ask you to evaluate the role of bystanders, collaborators, and resisters in the Holocaust.

Genocide in Global Context

The Holocaust was the largest but not the only genocide of the 20th century. The Armenian Genocide (1915–16, Ottoman Empire), Cambodian Genocide (1975–79), and Rwandan Genocide (1994) share characteristics: state power, dehumanizing ideology, and deliberate mass killing of a targeted group. The UN Genocide Convention (1948) defined genocide and obligated signatories to prevent and punish it.

Common misconception: Genocide requires intent to destroy a group "in whole or in part" — students often confuse mass killings or war crimes with genocide, which has a specific legal definition.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks you to compare genocides; FRQ asks you to explain common conditions that enable genocide.

Key Vocabulary

  • Nuremberg Laws
  • Final Solution
  • genocide
  • UN Genocide Convention

Nationalism and Decolonization after WWII

Learn on this site
Rise of Nationalism in Asia and Africa

WWII accelerated anti-colonial nationalism. Western ideals of self-determination (Atlantic Charter 1941) contradicted continued colonial rule. Educated nationalist leaders — Gandhi in India, Nkrumah in Ghana, Ho Chi Minh in Vietnam — organized mass independence movements that imperial powers could no longer suppress as they had before the war.

Common misconception: Nationalism in the colonies was not simply imported from Europe — it developed indigenously in response to colonial exploitation, but nationalists also strategically used Western democratic ideals to demand independence.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about leaders and methods of decolonization; FRQ asks you to explain how WWII accelerated decolonization.

Methods of Decolonization

Decolonization occurred through multiple paths: peaceful negotiation (India 1947, Ghana 1957), prolonged armed struggle (Algeria 1954–62, Vietnam 1945–75), and rapid withdrawal leaving fragile states (Belgian Congo 1960). The Cold War complicated decolonization as superpowers backed rival factions in former colonies.

Common misconception: Decolonization was not uniformly peaceful or violent — the method depended on how entrenched the colonial power was and whether European settlers formed a politically powerful minority.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to compare decolonization paths across two regions.

Legacies of Colonialism

Newly independent states inherited arbitrary colonial borders (especially in Africa), extractive economic structures, limited infrastructure, and ethnic tensions artificially created or exacerbated by colonial rule. These structural legacies — not African or Asian inherent inadequacy — explain many post-independence governance challenges.

Common misconception: Post-colonial instability was not inevitable or caused by independence — it resulted from deliberate colonial choices about borders, resource extraction, and denied political development.

How it can be tested: AP® World History FRQ asks you to explain how colonial legacies shaped post-independence challenges.

Key Vocabulary
  • nationalism
  • self-determination
  • decolonization
  • Atlantic Charter
  • neo-colonialism
Objectives

  • Compare peaceful and armed paths to decolonization with specific examples.
  • Analyze how colonial legacies shaped post-independence challenges.

  • FRQ compares decolonization across two regions.
  • SAQ analyzes a primary source by an anti-colonial leader.
