Cold War and Decolonization, c. 1900–Present

Cold War Origins and Superpower Competition

Learn on this site
Origins of the Cold War

The Cold War (1947–1991) emerged from ideological conflict between US capitalism and Soviet communism, mutual suspicion built during WWII, and competition for influence over post-war Europe. The Truman Doctrine (1947), Marshall Plan (1948), and Berlin Blockade (1948–49) defined the early Cold War's European theater.

Common misconception: The Cold War was not simply the result of Soviet aggression — historians debate whether US or Soviet actions bear more responsibility for the conflict's escalation.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about Cold War origins and early events; FRQ asks you to explain how WWII created conditions for Cold War competition.

Containment and the Arms Race

The US strategy of containment sought to prevent Soviet expansion beyond its existing sphere of influence. The nuclear arms race produced mutually assured destruction (MAD) — the doctrine that nuclear war would destroy both sides, paradoxically preventing direct superpower conflict. NATO and the Warsaw Pact formalized military blocs.

Common misconception: MAD did not eliminate the risk of nuclear war — several near-misses (Cuban Missile Crisis 1962, Able Archer 1983) showed how close the superpowers came to nuclear conflict.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to explain the logic of containment and mutually assured destruction.

Cultural and Ideological Competition

Beyond military rivalry, the Cold War was fought through propaganda, cultural diplomacy, and ideological competition. The space race (Sputnik 1957, Moon landing 1969) served as demonstrations of technological superiority. Both superpowers used foreign aid, alliances, and cultural programs to win the allegiance of newly decolonized nations.

Common misconception: The Non-Aligned Movement — led by India, Egypt, and Yugoslavia — attempted to remain outside the Cold War blocs, but true neutrality was difficult as both superpowers pressured developing nations to choose sides.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about Cold War competition beyond military conflict; FRQ asks you to evaluate how ideological competition shaped superpower policy toward decolonizing nations.

Proxy Wars and Cold War Conflicts

Learn on this site
The Korean War

The Korean War (1950–53) was the first major proxy conflict of the Cold War. North Korea, backed by the USSR and China, invaded South Korea; the US-led UN coalition intervened. The war ended in an armistice near the original border, with Korea permanently divided at the 38th parallel — a costly war with no territorial change.

Common misconception: The Korean War is often called the "Forgotten War" because it is overshadowed by WWII and Vietnam, but it established key Cold War precedents — including the willingness to fight limited wars rather than seek nuclear victory.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about Korean War outcomes and Cold War precedents; FRQ asks you to analyze it as a Cold War proxy conflict.

The Vietnam War

The Vietnam War (1955–75) grew from France's colonial war against Ho Chi Minh's nationalist-communist Viet Minh into a major US intervention aimed at preventing South Vietnam from falling to communism. Despite massive military power, the US could not achieve a decisive victory against guerrilla warfare; the fall of Saigon (1975) marked North Vietnam's victory.

Common misconception: The Vietnam War was not simply a Cold War conflict — it was simultaneously an anti-colonial war for Vietnamese independence, which helps explain why the US, despite military superiority, lost the political war.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to explain why the US failed in Vietnam despite military advantages.

Cold War in Latin America and the Developing World

The Cold War extended into Latin America (Cuban Revolution 1959, CIA-backed coups in Guatemala 1954 and Chile 1973), Africa (Angola civil war), and Asia. The Cuban Missile Crisis (1962) — when Soviet missiles in Cuba brought the superpowers to the brink of nuclear war — was the Cold War's most dangerous moment.

Common misconception: US interventions in Latin America (Guatemala, Chile, Nicaragua) contradicted American rhetoric about freedom and democracy, a tension that shaped global perceptions of US foreign policy.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about Cold War in the developing world; FRQ asks you to evaluate the impact of superpower rivalry on post-colonial states.

  • Vietnam War
  • Cuban Missile Crisis
  • domino theory

Indian Independence and the Chinese Communist Revolution

Learn on this site
Indian Independence and Partition

India gained independence from Britain in 1947 after decades of nationalist struggle led by Gandhi (non-violent resistance, Salt March 1930) and the Indian National Congress. Partition created the separate states of India and Pakistan along religious lines, triggering massive communal violence and the displacement of ~14 million people.

Common misconception: Partition was not a clean solution — the religious boundary could not neatly separate Hindu and Muslim communities that had lived intermingled for centuries, resulting in one of history's bloodiest mass migrations.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to evaluate Gandhi's non-violent methods as a model for independence movements and to analyze the causes and consequences of partition.

The Chinese Communist Revolution

Mao Zedong's Chinese Communist Party defeated Chiang Kai-shek's Nationalist forces in 1949, establishing the People's Republic of China. The CCP gained popular support through land reform promises and its resistance to Japanese invasion; the Nationalists were weakened by corruption and inflation during WWII.

Common misconception: The Communist victory in China was not inevitable — it resulted from specific wartime conditions, CCP organization, and Nationalist failures, not from some inherent Chinese communist destiny.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about causes of the Communist victory; FRQ asks you to explain how the Chinese Revolution compared to other 20th-century revolutions.

Mao's China: Great Leap Forward and Cultural Revolution

Mao's Great Leap Forward (1958–62) forced rapid industrialization and agricultural collectivization, causing a famine that killed 15–55 million people — the deadliest famine in history. The Cultural Revolution (1966–76) mobilized youth (Red Guards) to attack "bourgeois" culture and political rivals, causing massive social disruption and deaths.

Common misconception: The Great Leap Forward famine was caused by policy, not natural disaster — the government continued grain exports and denied the scale of starvation, making the death toll a political as much as agricultural failure.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to evaluate the human costs of Mao's revolutionary programs.

Decolonization in Africa and the Middle East

Learn on this site
African Decolonization

Most African nations gained independence in the 1950s–60s — Ghana (1957), Guinea (1958), and a wave of independence in 1960 ("Year of Africa"). African independence leaders — Nkrumah (Ghana), Kenyatta (Kenya), Nyerere (Tanzania) — sought pan-African unity but faced Cold War pressure to align with one superpower bloc.

Common misconception: African decolonization was not a single event but a process that varied dramatically by colony — some transitions were peaceful while others (Algeria, Kenya's Mau Mau, Angola) involved brutal violence.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about African decolonization leaders and paths; FRQ asks you to compare decolonization across African regions.

Middle East: Mandate System and Arab Nationalism

The collapse of the Ottoman Empire after WWI created the mandate system, placing Arab territories under British and French control while promising both Arab independence and a Jewish homeland in Palestine (Balfour Declaration 1917). Arab nationalism, exemplified by Egypt's Nasser and his 1956 Suez Crisis, challenged Western imperialism during the Cold War.

Common misconception: The mandate system was imperialism by another name — Britain and France controlled these territories economically and politically, despite the League of Nations' theoretical oversight.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to analyze how WWI's settlement created ongoing conflicts in the Middle East.

Apartheid in South Africa

South Africa's apartheid system (1948–1994) institutionalized racial segregation through legislation, creating separate and unequal systems for Black, Coloured, and White South Africans. The African National Congress, led by Nelson Mandela, organized resistance; international sanctions contributed to apartheid's eventual end.

Common misconception: Apartheid ended through a combination of internal resistance, economic sanctions, and negotiated transition — not military conquest. This makes South Africa's transition relevant to debates about how to end authoritarian systems.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about apartheid's characteristics and end; FRQ asks you to compare apartheid to other systems of racial oppression.
