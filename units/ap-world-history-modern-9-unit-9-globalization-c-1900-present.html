Unit 9 of AP® World History: Modern

Globalization, c. 1900–Present

Accelerating globalization after 1945 transformed economies, communication, food production, and the environment while generating resistance movements and deepening inequality between and within societies.

Economic Globalization and Inequality

Learn on this site
Global Trade and Economic Integration

Post-WWII economic institutions — the IMF, World Bank, and GATT (later WTO) — created a rules-based global trading system that accelerated economic integration. Multinational corporations (MNCs) shifted production to low-wage countries, creating global commodity chains that connect raw materials, manufacturing, and consumers across continents.

Common misconception: Economic globalization has reduced extreme poverty in some regions (especially East Asia) while increasing inequality within countries — both effects are real and students should avoid one-sided evaluations.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about global economic institutions and their functions; FRQ asks you to evaluate globalization's effects on economic inequality.

The Rise of East Asia

Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and Singapore industrialized rapidly in the postwar decades through export-led growth, state investment in education and infrastructure, and targeted industrial policy. China's market reforms (Deng Xiaoping from 1978) transformed it from an impoverished agrarian state to the world's second-largest economy within 40 years.

Common misconception: East Asian economic success challenged both dependency theory (which predicted continued underdevelopment) and pure free-market models — state direction of the economy was central, not peripheral, to success.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to explain East Asian economic development and evaluate whether it challenges or confirms development theories.

Global Supply Chains and Labor

Global supply chains distribute production across multiple countries to minimize costs — a smartphone may involve raw materials from Congo, components from South Korea, assembly in China, and design in the United States. This model reduced consumer prices but exploited low-wage labor in developing countries and enabled companies to evade national labor and environmental regulations.

Common misconception: Global supply chains are not naturally occurring — they were deliberately constructed through trade agreements, structural adjustment programs, and corporate strategies that shifted risk and costs onto vulnerable workers.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about global supply chains; FRQ asks you to evaluate the effects of global supply chains on workers in developing countries.

Key Vocabulary
  • globalization
  • multinational corporation
  • global supply chain
  • WTO
  • export-led growth
Objectives
  • Explain how post-WWII institutions promoted economic globalization.
  • Analyze East Asian economic development as a model and evaluate its applicability elsewhere.
  • Evaluate effects of global supply chains on workers and environments in developing countries.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests global economic institutions, MNCs, and supply chains.
  • FRQ evaluates globalization's effects on inequality or compares development models.
  • SAQ analyzes a primary source on globalization's benefits and costs.
Practice before moving on

  • Compare South Korea's export-led development to a country that followed IMF structural adjustment advice.

The Green Revolution and Food Security

Learn on this site
Green Revolution Technologies and Spread

The Green Revolution (1940s–1960s) introduced high-yield variety (HYV) seeds, chemical fertilizers, pesticides, and irrigation to agriculture in Asia and Latin America. Led by Norman Borlaug, it dramatically increased food production in Mexico, India, and Pakistan, averting predicted famines and feeding rapidly growing populations.

Common misconception: The Green Revolution was not a single event but a continuous process of agricultural innovation that continued through the 1970s and beyond — students often treat it as a one-time miracle rather than an ongoing program.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about Green Revolution technologies and geographic impact; FRQ asks you to evaluate Green Revolution benefits and costs.

Critiques and Consequences of the Green Revolution

The Green Revolution increased inequality between wealthy farmers who could afford inputs and poor subsistence farmers who could not. Chemical-intensive farming degraded soil, depleted aquifers, and reduced biodiversity through monoculture. The Green Revolution's benefits were geographically uneven — Sub-Saharan Africa received far fewer benefits than Asia.

Common misconception: The Green Revolution prevented hundreds of millions from starving while simultaneously creating long-term environmental vulnerabilities — this tension defines the debate about its legacy.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to evaluate Green Revolution trade-offs using specific evidence from Asia and Africa.

Food Security in the 21st Century

Despite the Green Revolution, nearly 800 million people still face chronic hunger — food insecurity results from poverty, conflict, distribution failures, and climate change rather than absolute food scarcity. The global food system is highly globalized: price shocks in one region (Ukraine's wheat exports disrupted by war) immediately affect food security globally.

Common misconception: Food insecurity is primarily a poverty and access problem, not a production problem — the world produces sufficient calories, but they are not equitably distributed.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about causes of food insecurity; FRQ asks you to analyze connections between globalization, climate, and food security.

Key Vocabulary
  • Green Revolution
  • high-yield variety
  • food security
  • monoculture
  • aquifer depletion
Objectives
  • Explain Green Revolution technologies and evaluate their geographic impact.
  • Analyze environmental and social costs of Green Revolution agriculture.
  • Explain why food insecurity persists despite increased global food production.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests Green Revolution causes, technologies, and consequences.
  • FRQ evaluates Green Revolution trade-offs with geographic evidence.
  • SAQ compares food security in Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa.
Practice before moving on
  • Compare food insecurity in South Asia before and after the Green Revolution.
  • Explain how monoculture farming increases vulnerability to disease and climate disruption.

The Digital Revolution and Global Communication

Learn on this site
The Digital Revolution

The digital revolution — from personal computers (1970s–80s) to the internet (1990s) and smartphones (2000s) — transformed global communication, commerce, and culture. The internet enabled instantaneous global communication, e-commerce, and the rise of platform corporations (Google, Amazon, Alibaba) that operate globally across national jurisdictions.

Common misconception: The digital revolution has not eliminated geography — internet access remains deeply unequal globally (the "digital divide"), and digital infrastructure (undersea cables, server farms) has its own geography.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about digital revolution characteristics and impacts; FRQ asks you to evaluate how the digital revolution has changed global connectivity and inequality.

Digital Divide and Global Inequality

The digital divide refers to unequal access to digital technology between wealthy and poor nations, and between urban and rural populations within nations. High-income countries have near-universal internet access while many African, South Asian, and rural populations lack reliable connectivity, limiting their participation in the digital economy.

Common misconception: The digital divide reflects and reinforces existing economic inequality — it is not a separate phenomenon but an extension of the same global structures that have generated economic inequality for centuries.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to analyze how the digital divide relates to broader patterns of global economic inequality.

Social Media and Political Mobilization

Social media (Facebook, Twitter/X, WhatsApp, WeChat) has enabled unprecedented political mobilization — Arab Spring (2010–12), Black Lives Matter (2013–present), and pro-democracy movements globally used social media to organize across geographic boundaries. Authoritarian governments have responded with internet censorship, surveillance, and platform control.

Common misconception: Social media's political effects are dual-edged — it enables both democratic mobilization and authoritarian surveillance and disinformation, making it simultaneously a tool of liberation and control.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about social media's political effects; FRQ asks you to evaluate social media as a tool for both resistance and authoritarian control.

Key Vocabulary
  • digital revolution
  • internet
  • digital divide
  • social media
  • platform economy
Objectives
  • Explain how the digital revolution transformed global communication and commerce.
  • Analyze the digital divide as a dimension of global inequality.
  • Evaluate social media's dual role in enabling both democratic mobilization and authoritarian control.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests digital revolution characteristics and the digital divide.
  • FRQ evaluates the digital revolution's effects on global inequality.
  • SAQ analyzes social media's role in a specific political movement.
Practice before moving on
  • Map global internet access rates and identify the regions with the lowest connectivity.
  • Analyze how China's "Great Firewall" reflects authoritarian responses to the digital revolution.

Resistance Movements, Climate Change, and the Environment

Learn on this site
Resistance to Globalization

Globalization has generated diverse resistance movements: anti-globalization protests (Seattle WTO protests 1999), indigenous rights movements defending traditional lands against multinational extraction (Zapatista uprising Mexico 1994), and religious fundamentalist movements rejecting Western cultural influence. These movements share a critique of global capitalism's effects on local communities and cultures.

Common misconception: Resistance to globalization is not a single unified movement — it includes both left-wing critiques of corporate power and right-wing nationalist movements rejecting immigration and international institutions.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about forms of resistance to globalization; FRQ asks you to evaluate resistance movements and their effectiveness.

Climate Change as a Global Challenge

Human-caused climate change — driven by fossil fuel combustion and deforestation since industrialization — is raising global temperatures, causing sea level rise, increasing extreme weather events, and threatening agricultural systems and biodiversity. The 1997 Kyoto Protocol and 2015 Paris Agreement represent international attempts to coordinate emissions reductions.

Common misconception: Climate change is not a future problem — its effects are already measurable and disproportionately harm the poorest nations that contributed least to emissions, raising issues of global justice.

How it can be tested: FRQ asks you to explain causes and consequences of climate change and evaluate international responses.

Environmental Movements and Sustainable Development

The modern environmental movement grew from industrialization's visible consequences: Rachel Carson's Silent Spring (1962) exposed pesticide harms, the 1970 Earth Day and US Environmental Protection Agency marked institutional responses, and the 1987 Brundtland Report defined sustainable development as "meeting present needs without compromising future generations." Global environmental governance remains weak — sovereignty limits binding international agreements.

Common misconception: Sustainable development is not just an environmental concept — it integrates economic development, social equity, and environmental protection, recognizing that poverty and ecological destruction are linked.

How it can be tested: MCQ asks about environmental movements and agreements; FRQ asks you to evaluate international environmental governance.

Key Vocabulary
  • anti-globalization movement
  • climate change
  • Paris Agreement
  • sustainable development
  • Brundtland Report
Objectives
  • Explain diverse forms of resistance to economic globalization.
  • Analyze human causes and global consequences of climate change.
  • Evaluate international efforts to address climate change and environmental degradation.
How this can be tested
  • MCQ tests resistance movements, climate agreements, and environmental history.
  • FRQ evaluates effectiveness of climate change governance.
  • SAQ analyzes a primary source on environmental challenges.
Practice before moving on
  • Compare the 1997 Kyoto Protocol and 2015 Paris Agreement as different approaches to climate change.
  • Research the Zapatista uprising as an example of indigenous resistance to economic globalization.
