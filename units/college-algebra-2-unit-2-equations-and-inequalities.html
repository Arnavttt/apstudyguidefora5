Solving Equations and Inequalities

Learn on this site
Linear and Quadratic Equations

Linear equations (ax + b = c) are solved by isolating x. Quadratic equations (ax^2+bx+c=0) can be solved by factoring, completing the square, or the quadratic formula: x = [-b ± √(b^2-4ac)]/(2a). The discriminant D = b^2-4ac tells you the number of real roots: D>0 (two distinct real roots), D=0 (one repeated root), D<0 (no real roots, two complex roots).

Common misconception: Students forget the ± in the quadratic formula, getting only one solution instead of two.

How it can be tested: Solve linear equations; apply the quadratic formula; interpret the discriminant.

Inequalities and Absolute Value Equations

Solving inequalities follows the same steps as equations, with one critical difference: multiplying or dividing by a negative number reverses the inequality sign. Compound inequalities (a < x < b or x < a or x > b) require careful interval notation. Absolute value equations |f(x)| = k require splitting into two cases: f(x) = k and f(x) = -k. Absolute value inequalities: |x| < k means -k < x < k.

Common misconception: Students do not flip the inequality sign when multiplying by a negative — this is the most common error in inequality solving.

How it can be tested: Solve linear inequalities; write solutions in interval notation; solve absolute value equations and inequalities.

Key Vocabulary
  • discriminant — b^2 - 4ac from the quadratic formula; determines the number and type of roots
  • completing the square — A method of rewriting ax^2+bx+c as a(x-h)^2+k by adding and subtracting (b/2a)^2
  • interval notation — A way to write solution sets: [a,b] = a≤x≤b; (a,b) = a
  • compound inequality — Two inequalities joined by "and" (intersection) or "or" (union)
  • absolute value inequality — |x| < k means -k < x < k (and); |x| > k means x < -k or x > k (or)

Q2

  • A) one repeated real solution
  • B) two distinct real solutions
  • C) three solutions
  • D) no real solutions — only two complex (imaginary) solutions
