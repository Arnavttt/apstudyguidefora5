Polynomials, Division, and Rational Functions

Polynomial Functions and Their Zeros

A polynomial of degree n has at most n real zeros and at most n-1 turning points. The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra: every degree-n polynomial has exactly n zeros (counting multiplicity, in the complex numbers). The Rational Root Theorem: for integer-coefficient polynomials, any rational zero p/q has p dividing the constant term and q dividing the leading coefficient.

Common misconception: Students assume every polynomial of degree n has exactly n real zeros — they can have complex (non-real) zeros that come in conjugate pairs.

How it can be tested: Find zeros by factoring or the Rational Root Theorem; apply synthetic division; sketch polynomials using end behavior and zeros.

Rational Functions and Asymptotes

A rational function is a ratio of polynomials p(x)/q(x). Vertical asymptotes occur where q(x)=0 (and p(x)≠0). Horizontal asymptotes depend on degrees: if deg(p)deg(q), no horizontal asymptote (oblique or polynomial behavior). A hole (removable discontinuity) occurs where a factor cancels between numerator and denominator.

Common misconception: Students place vertical asymptotes wherever the denominator is zero even if the factor cancels — canceling factors create holes, not asymptotes.

How it can be tested: Find vertical and horizontal asymptotes; identify holes; sketch the graph of a rational function.

Key Vocabulary
  • zeros (roots) — The x-values where a function equals zero; also called x-intercepts when real
  • multiplicity — How many times a factor (x-r) appears; even multiplicity: graph touches x-axis; odd: crosses
  • synthetic division — A shorthand method for dividing a polynomial by (x - c)
  • vertical asymptote — A vertical line x = a where the rational function grows without bound
  • horizontal asymptote — A horizontal line y = k that the function approaches as x → ±∞
  • hole — A removable discontinuity in a rational function where a common factor cancels

  • Apply synthetic division to divide polynomials

Q1
The degree of the polynomial 4x^5 - 3x^2 + 7 is
  • A) 2
  • B) 7
  • C) 4
  • D) 5 — the degree is the highest power of the variable
D is correct. 5 — the degree is the highest power of the variable
Q2
The end behavior of f(x) = -2x^3 + 5x is
  • A) as x→+∞, f→-∞; as x→-∞, f→+∞ — odd degree, negative leading coefficient
  • B) as x→+∞, f→+∞; as x→-∞, f→-∞
  • C) both ends go to +∞
  • D) both ends go to -∞
A is correct. as x→+∞, f→-∞; as x→-∞, f→+∞ — odd degree, negative leading coefficient
Q3
For f(x) = (x+1)^2(x-3), the zero x=-1 has multiplicity 2, which means
  • A) the graph crosses the x-axis at x=-1
  • B) x=-1 is not actually a zero
  • C) the function has two values at x=-1
  • D) the graph touches but does not cross the x-axis at x=-1
D is correct. the graph touches but does not cross the x-axis at x=-1
Q4
The horizontal asymptote of f(x) = 3x^2/(x^2-1) is
  • A) there is no horizontal asymptote
  • B) y = 3 — equal degrees, so divide leading coefficients: 3/1 = 3
  • C) y = 0
  • D) y = 1
B is correct. y = 3 — equal degrees, so divide leading coefficients: 3/1 = 3
Q5
The Rational Root Theorem says that possible rational zeros of 2x^3 - x + 6 are
  • A) only integers that divide 6
  • B) only ±1 and ±2
  • C) of the form ±p/q where p divides 6 (constant) and q divides 2 (leading coefficient)
  • D) any rational number
C is correct. of the form ±p/q where p divides 6 (constant) and q divides 2 (leading coefficient)
